# Interview Questions — Engineering Roles

**50 questions per role, 10 per category (Culture, Technical, Collaboration, Leadership, Depth).**

Questions are written for a remote multiplayer games studio (~55 people, UK + Greece).

---

### Lead Full Stack Developer

**Culture**
1. You join a games studio where the engineering team has normalised shipping code without tests, reviewing PRs in under five minutes, and deploying on Fridays before a weekend event. Nobody thinks anything is wrong. What do you do — and how do you distinguish between a team that has bad habits and a team that has made conscious trade-offs you do not yet understand?
2. When you strongly disagree with an architectural decision that has already been made and shipped, what do you do? Give a real example.
3. What frustrates you most about working in games infrastructure specifically, as opposed to pure web development or SaaS?
4. Do you prefer working in a team that moves fast and fixes later, or one that is methodical and ships less often? Why -- and has that preference ever cost you?
5. Describe a time you were wrong about a technical approach and someone else's idea was better. How did that feel, and what did you do about it?
6. You have been at a studio for two years. The codebase is yours, the team trusts you, but the technical challenges have plateaued — you are maintaining, not building. A recruiter reaches out with a role at a studio working on a technically harder problem. What actually goes through your head, and what would tip the decision?
7. How do you handle it when a non-technical stakeholder overrides your technical recommendation? Walk us through a specific instance.
8. What kind of manager brings out your best work? What kind makes you shut down?
9. Tell me about a colleague you learned the most from. What made them effective, and how did they change how you work?
10. You are the most senior backend person in a games studio where the "real product" is the game, not your services. How do you handle being in a support function when everyone else's work gets the spotlight?

**Technical**
1. Describe how you would approach breaking a 10,000-line monolithic server.js into a modular route structure without breaking the live application. What is your migration strategy, and how do you ensure the game client is not affected during the transition?
2. Walk us through building a real-time data sync system for a multi-user internal dashboard used by a game studio. The team needs to see project status, task updates, and bug reports updating live across multiple sessions. What is your approach to polling versus WebSockets versus SSE, and how do you handle stale state and conflict resolution?
3. Our backend handles authentication via Azure MSAL, serves game studio management data, and needs to support both internal team dashboards and client-facing views with different permission levels. How do you architect role-based access control that is maintainable as the number of user types grows?
4. You inherit a PostgreSQL database with 50+ migrations, no test data seeding, and production queries that were written ad hoc. How do you introduce query performance monitoring and indexing discipline without rewriting the entire data layer?
5. Walk us through designing an API versioning strategy for a backend that serves both a monolithic SPA and potentially future mobile or third-party consumers. What trade-offs do you make between URL versioning, header versioning, and just not breaking things?
6. The SPA makes 40 API calls on initial load because each widget fetches its own data independently. Page load is slow. How do you restructure the backend to reduce round trips without creating a single mega-endpoint that returns everything?
7. Describe how you would implement rate limiting and abuse prevention on a backend that serves both authenticated internal users and potentially public-facing endpoints. What is your approach when the abuser is an internal script someone wrote?
8. How do you handle database migrations that need to be backward-compatible with the currently running server version? Walk us through a migration that adds a non-nullable column to a table with active writes.
9. You need to add full-text search across work items, bug reports, and comments in a PostgreSQL-backed system. What are the trade-offs between pg_trgm, tsvector, and bolting on Elasticsearch, and how do you decide for a team of this size?
10. Describe your approach to structured logging and error handling in a Node.js/Express application. How do you make logs useful for debugging production issues without flooding the log stream or leaking sensitive data?

**Collaboration**
1. When a gameplay engineer asks you to build an API endpoint that returns data in a shape you know will be painful to maintain or extend, how do you push back while still unblocking their work? Give an example.
2. Your backend serves data to a large single-page application where the frontend developer is embedding everything inline. The SPA is 20,000+ lines. How do you work with them on API contracts when they cannot easily refactor their side?
3. Describe how you have handled a production incident on a service that a game team depended on during crunch. How did you communicate, triage, and run the post-mortem when tensions were already high?
4. A designer asks you to "just add a field" to the work item API. You know that field will need validation, permissions, audit history, and UI changes. How do you scope the real work without making them feel like you are sandbagging?
5. You are building internal tools for a studio where the users are 30 metres away on Slack, not anonymous customers. How does building for colleagues who can message you at any time change your approach to documentation, error messages, and API design?
6. The studio hires a contractor to build a new frontend feature. They need to integrate with your API but have no context on the data model, authentication flow, or naming conventions. How do you onboard them without becoming their full-time support?
7. You discover that the gameplay team has been scraping your database directly because your API does not expose what they need. How do you handle the conversation and the migration?
8. A producer wants a weekly automated report pulled from your database. The query is expensive and the data is sensitive. How do you deliver what they need without giving them raw database access or building a one-off script that nobody maintains?
9. The gameplay team needs a new API endpoint for a feature demo happening in two days. The producer needs you to fix a data export that the client is waiting on. The art team's Perforce server is misconfigured and you are the only person who understands it. All three hit your Slack within the same hour. Walk me through the next 60 minutes — who do you talk to, what do you do first, and what do you let fail?
10. Walk us through coordinating a breaking API change with a frontend developer who is in a different timezone and cannot test the same day you deploy.

**Leadership**
1. How do you establish code review standards that catch real issues without becoming a bottleneck, especially when your team includes engineers who primarily think about game systems rather than web architecture?
2. When you inherit a codebase with no tests and a live game studio depending on it daily, how do you introduce testing without stopping feature delivery? What do you test first?
3. How do you make architectural decisions that your team actually follows? Documentation that gets ignored is not a standard. What has worked for you in practice?
4. You are hiring a junior backend developer for your team. What do you look for that is different from hiring a senior? How do you evaluate potential versus current ability?
5. A team member consistently writes code that works but is hard for others to maintain -- clever abstractions, sparse comments, complex one-liners. How do you address this when they see it as a strength?
6. How do you decide when to build something yourself versus adopting a third-party library or service? What has burned you in either direction?
7. Your team has grown from two to five engineers. The informal "just ask me" communication model is breaking down. What structure do you introduce, and how do you prevent it from becoming bureaucracy?
8. How do you handle the situation where you are the bottleneck? Every PR needs your review, every architecture question comes to you, every production issue is escalated to you. What do you delegate first?
9. Describe how you would run a blameless post-mortem for an outage that was clearly caused by one person's mistake. How do you make it genuinely blameless without being dishonest about what happened?
10. When a production crisis hits and you need to drop everything to write code yourself — not review it, not delegate it, but sit down and build it — what was the last time that happened, what did you build, and what did it tell you about how far your hands-on skills have drifted from where they need to be?

**Depth**
1. If we asked you to build the backend for a studio management platform that tracks work items across a strict hierarchy (Client > Project > Feature > Story > Task), supports prerequisite blocking between items, handles optimistic concurrency for multi-user editing, and syncs incrementally to avoid full-page refreshes, walk us through the data model and the sync mechanism. Where are the hard problems?
2. Our database has 50+ migrations applied in production. A new feature requires a schema change that touches a table with hundreds of thousands of rows and active concurrent writes. Walk us through how you would plan and execute that migration with zero downtime.
3. Describe how you would build a metrics and observability layer for a game studio's internal backend. What do you instrument, what alerting do you set up, and how do you make the data useful to non-engineers who need to understand system health?
4. Design a notification system for a studio management tool where different users need different notification channels (Slack, email, in-app), different granularity (every change vs. daily digest), and different scopes (my tasks only vs. my team vs. everything). How do you architect this without the notification logic infecting every feature?
5. Walk us through building a backup and disaster recovery strategy for a PostgreSQL database that is the single source of truth for a 55-person studio's project management. What is your RPO, what is your RTO, and how do you test that your backups actually work?
6. You need to build an audit trail that records every change to every work item -- who changed what, when, and what the previous value was. The system has 50 concurrent users and thousands of daily updates. How do you design this without killing write performance?
7. Describe how you would architect a plugin or extension system for an internal dashboard, allowing different teams to add custom views, reports, or integrations without modifying the core codebase. What are the security and stability boundaries?
8. The studio wants to add AI-assisted features to the internal tools -- automatic work item categorisation, effort estimation from descriptions, anomaly detection in project metrics. How do you integrate LLM capabilities into an existing Express/Postgres backend without creating a maintenance burden?
9. Walk us through designing a multi-tenant architecture if this studio management tool needed to serve multiple game studios as a SaaS product. What changes in the data model, authentication, and deployment?
10. You are asked to make the backend horizontally scalable -- multiple server instances behind a load balancer. The current architecture assumes a single process with in-memory state. What breaks first, and how do you fix it?

### Lead Gameplay Developer

**Culture**
1. What does a healthy gameplay engineering team look like to you? What is the first sign that iteration velocity is masking accumulating chaos?
2. When you strongly disagree with a designer's creative direction but the game director has approved it, what do you do? Where is the line between advocating and undermining?
3. Describe a time you were wrong about how a gameplay system would feel in players' hands. Your prototype worked exactly as designed but players hated it. How did that experience change your process?
4. What frustrates you most about gameplay engineering specifically? Not game development generally -- what about the gameplay programmer's life makes you want to throw your keyboard?
5. Do you prefer working on a game where design is locked and you are executing, or one where design is fluid and you are discovering the game alongside the designers? Why -- and what breaks when you get the wrong one?
6. What makes you want to stay at a studio? What would make you leave one where you loved the game you were building?
7. How do you handle it when a producer cuts a feature you have spent two months building because playtest feedback was negative? Walk us through the emotional and professional response.
8. What kind of creative director brings out your best engineering work? What kind makes you write the worst code of your career?
9. Tell me about a designer you had the best working relationship with. What made it work, and what did you learn about collaboration from them?
10. Gameplay code gets rewritten more than any other code in a game. Features get cut, mechanics pivot, entire systems get replaced. How do you stay emotionally invested in code that might not ship?

**Technical**
1. Describe your approach to building an ability system for a multiplayer action game. How do you structure it so designers can author new abilities through data (cooldowns, targeting, effects, costs) without engineering involvement, while keeping the system debuggable and maintainable? What patterns have burned you?
2. Walk us through how you handle authority and prediction for a melee combat ability in a multiplayer game. The player presses attack, the animation starts, the hit needs to feel instant, but the server is 80ms away. What gets predicted client-side, what waits for server confirmation, and how do you handle the rollback when the server disagrees?
3. You are building a gameplay system where 50 players can simultaneously trigger area-of-effect abilities that interact with each other (slows stack, damage zones overlap, knockbacks chain). How do you structure the gameplay simulation to handle this without the server tick rate collapsing?
4. Describe your approach to building a hit detection system that handles both melee and projectile combat in the same game. How do you decide between ray casts, overlap tests, and projectile simulation? How does lag compensation interact with each approach?
5. Walk us through implementing a status effect system where effects can stack, override, combine, or conflict (a freeze and a burn cancel each other, two slows stack multiplicatively up to a cap, a shield absorbs the next N damage instances). How do you make this extensible without it becoming an untestable combinatorial nightmare?
6. How do you approach building a camera system for a multiplayer action game? The camera needs to serve combat readability, spatial awareness, ability targeting, and cinematic moments. What are the specific technical challenges when multiple cameras (spectator, death cam, ability cam) need to coexist?
7. Describe your experience with gameplay scripting versus hard-coded systems. When do you expose functionality to designers via Blueprint/visual scripting/Lua, and when do you keep it in C++/C#? Where have you drawn that line wrong?
8. You need to build an AI director that dynamically adjusts PvE encounter difficulty based on player performance metrics. What signals do you read, what do you adjust, and how do you prevent the system from creating a "rubber band" feel where skilled play is punished?
9. Walk us through building a respawn system for a multiplayer game with multiple modes. Deathmatch uses instant respawn with spawn protection. Objective modes use wave-based respawn. Battle royale has no respawn. How do you architect a single system that serves all three?
10. How do you profile and optimise gameplay code when the bottleneck is not a single expensive function but thousands of small gameplay tick updates across 50 entities? What tools do you use, and what patterns do you refactor toward?

**Collaboration**
1. How do you build designer-facing tools that designers actually use? Every studio has examples of "we built a node graph editor and designers still ask us to hard-code things." What has worked and what has not in your experience?
2. A single ability requires animation events to trigger VFX spawns, VFX to trigger audio cues, audio timing to match hit reactions, and all of it to replicate across the network. How do you manage that cross-discipline integration so it does not become a coordination nightmare every time someone adjusts a timing?
3. Describe a time you worked with a network engineer upfront to ensure a gameplay feature was multiplayer-viable before you built it. What did that conversation look like, and what changed in your design because of it?
4. A designer hands you a one-page spec for a new ability. It covers the happy path but says nothing about edge cases: what happens if the target dies mid-cast, if the caster is stunned during the wind-up, if two players cast it simultaneously on each other. How do you handle the gap between design spec and implementation reality?
5. How do you work with QA when a bug report says "the ability feels wrong" but the behaviour matches the design spec exactly? How do you translate feel feedback into actionable engineering work?
6. The animation team has delivered a set of combat animations with timings that do not match the frame data your combat system expects. Re-exporting the animations would take a week. Adjusting your frame data would change the combat feel. How do you negotiate a solution?
7. A junior gameplay programmer on your team has built a feature that works but is architecturally inconsistent with the rest of the ability system. Fixing it now would delay the milestone. Leaving it creates a precedent. How do you handle this?
8. Walk us through how you communicate technical constraints to a game director who thinks in terms of player experience, not system architecture. How do you say "this is expensive" in a way that leads to a productive design conversation rather than a shutdown?
9. You discover that the UI team has been duplicating gameplay state on the client side because your gameplay code does not expose what they need cleanly. How do you resolve the duplication and prevent it from recurring?
10. How do you handle the situation where two gameplay programmers have built overlapping systems because they were working in parallel and neither knew about the other's approach? Who decides which one survives?

**Leadership**
1. How do you manage gameplay code quality when the team is prototyping three different combat ideas simultaneously and leadership will kill two of them next month? When does "quick and dirty" become unacceptable, and how do you draw that line with your team?
2. How do you prioritise gameplay bugs? A designer files "the dash doesn't feel responsive enough" alongside a crash in the ability queue and a desync in the projectile system. How do you weight feel bugs against functional bugs when both affect the player experience?
3. Describe how you would onboard a new gameplay programmer onto a project with an established ability system, a custom gameplay framework, and 18 months of accumulated design decisions. What do they need to know in week one versus month one?
4. How do you balance giving your team autonomy to solve problems their own way versus enforcing consistency across the gameplay codebase? Where do you mandate patterns and where do you let people choose?
5. A gameplay programmer on your team wants to rewrite a system that works but is hard to extend. You agree it needs rewriting but the timing is terrible -- you are six weeks from alpha. How do you handle the request?
6. How do you run playtest sessions as a gameplay engineering lead? What are you observing that is different from what the designers are observing? What data do you capture?
7. You need to hire a gameplay programmer. The candidate has shipped a AAA single-player game but has no multiplayer experience. The other candidate has shipped a multiplayer indie game but has no AAA pipeline experience. How do you evaluate them?
8. How do you prevent your gameplay code from becoming a dumping ground for "it doesn't fit anywhere else" logic? What architectural boundaries do you enforce?
9. Describe how you handle the end of a prototyping phase when two of three prototypes need to be killed. How do you manage the team members who built the killed prototypes?
10. Tell me about the last gameplay system you prototyped yourself from scratch — not reviewed, not spec'd, not delegated. What was it, how long ago was it, and when you got your hands dirty did you discover that your instincts about how the engine or the genre had evolved were wrong in any way?

**Depth**
1. If we asked you to build the combat system for a multiplayer action game that supports 50+ simultaneous players with responsive melee and ranged combat, walk us through the architecture. How do you handle hit detection across latency, ability interactions (can a shield block a projectile mid-flight?), and the state machine complexity of characters that can attack, dodge, block, use abilities, and be interrupted?
2. Walk us through designing a data-driven progression system that designers can tune without code changes. What are the data structures, the authoring tools, the validation rules, and the safety rails that prevent a designer from accidentally making level 2 require more XP than level 50?
3. You need to build bot AI for a multiplayer action game that serves two purposes: filling empty player slots in matchmaking and providing PvE opponents in a co-op mode. The bots need to use the same ability system as players and navigate the same combat spaces. How do you approach this, and what changes between the two use cases?
4. Design the architecture for a match replay system that allows players to rewatch a full 50-player match from any perspective. What do you record, how do you handle the data volume, and how do you deal with replay playback when the game has been patched since the match was played?
5. Walk us through building a modding or custom game mode system where players can modify gameplay rules (double damage, no cooldowns, custom objectives) using the same ability and game mode infrastructure the core game uses. What do you expose, what do you lock down, and how do you prevent mods from crashing the server?
6. Describe how you would architect a training or practice mode where a single player can test abilities, combos, and mechanics against configurable dummies, with real-time stat displays and frame data. How does this interact with the multiplayer gameplay systems?
7. You are asked to add a new damage type to the game (e.g., "void damage") that interacts differently with every existing resistance, shield, and healing system. Walk us through the impact analysis, the implementation approach, and how you ensure nothing breaks across the existing 30 abilities.
8. Design an event system for time-limited gameplay modifiers (weekend events with altered rules, seasonal mechanics, tournament-specific rulesets) that can be deployed without a client patch. What is the architecture, and how do you test combinations of events?
9. Walk us through building a spectator mode for competitive play that needs to show ability cooldowns, ultimate charge, team economy, and kill feed -- information that is hidden from players during the match. How do you architect the spectator client's relationship to the game simulation?
10. You inherit a gameplay codebase where the combat system, movement system, and ability system are all tightly coupled. Changing the dash ability breaks the jump, which breaks the wall-climb. How do you incrementally decouple these without a full rewrite during active development?

### Snr Network Engineer

**Culture**
1. What does a healthy relationship between networking engineers and gameplay programmers look like? What is the first sign that the two groups are drifting into an adversarial dynamic?
2. When you strongly disagree with a gameplay feature that you know will be a networking nightmare, but the creative director has approved it, what do you do? Where is the line between raising concerns and being obstructionist?
3. Describe a time you were wrong about a networking assumption -- you predicted a system would scale and it did not, or you vetoed a feature as impossible and someone found a way. How did that experience change your approach?
4. What frustrates you most about being a network engineer in game development specifically? Not software engineering generally -- what about games networking makes you want to scream?
5. Do you prefer working on a game where the networking model is defined upfront and you are executing, or one where creative experiments keep changing what you need to replicate? Why?
6. What makes you want to stay at a games studio? What would make you leave one where you were working on technically interesting networking problems?
7. How do you handle it when a player-facing community manager publicly blames "netcode" for an issue that is actually caused by the player's ISP or hardware? Do you correct the record or let it go?
8. What kind of engineering lead brings out your best work? What kind of management makes you stop caring about code quality?
9. Tell me about a gameplay programmer you had the best working relationship with. What made it work, and what did they understand about networking that most gameplay programmers do not?
10. Networking code is the most thankless code in a game -- invisible when it works, blamed for everything when it does not. How do you find professional satisfaction in a discipline where your best work is unnoticeable?

**Technical**
1. Describe your netcode architecture for a multiplayer action game with 50+ concurrent players in a single zone. Walk us through your tick rate decision, your snapshot compression strategy, your delta encoding approach, and how you handle the bandwidth ceiling when 50 players are all in combat simultaneously.
2. Walk us through how you would implement lag compensation for hit detection in a game where melee and ranged combat coexist. The attacker sees a hit, the victim was already behind cover on their screen, and the server has a third version of reality. How do you resolve this? What is your rewind window, and how do you prevent it from being exploitable?
3. At 50+ players, you are sending potentially thousands of entity updates per second. Walk us through your prioritisation system. How do you decide what gets replicated at full fidelity, what gets downsampled based on distance or relevance, what gets predicted locally, and what gets interpolated? How do you handle priority inversion when a distant player suddenly becomes relevant?
4. Describe your approach to building a custom reliable-ordered channel on top of UDP. When do you need it (ability activation, state transitions) versus when unreliable-unordered is fine (position updates)? How do you handle the sequencing and ack system without reinventing TCP?
5. Walk us through implementing a deterministic lockstep simulation versus a server-authoritative model with client prediction. For a 50-player action game, why would you choose one over the other? What are the specific failure modes of each?
6. How do you handle physics replication in a multiplayer game? Objects that need to be physically simulated on the server but look smooth on the client -- explosions that scatter debris, ragdolls, destructible environments. What gets replicated and what gets faked?
7. Describe your approach to bandwidth profiling and optimisation. You have a 256kbps upstream budget per client. Walk us through how you measure current usage, identify the most expensive replicated properties, and reduce bandwidth without visible quality loss.
8. How do you handle the transition between gameplay states from a networking perspective -- lobby to loading to gameplay to post-match? What state needs to be synchronised at each transition, and what are the race conditions that cause players to see different things?
9. Walk us through implementing voice chat integration in a multiplayer game. Do you route through your game server or a separate VOIP service? How do you handle spatial audio, team channels, proximity chat, and the bandwidth implications of each?
10. Describe your approach to anti-cheat from the networking layer. What can you detect server-side (speed hacks, teleportation, impossible hit rates), what requires client-side instrumentation, and how do you handle the false positive problem?

**Collaboration**
1. A gameplay programmer has built an ability that spawns 30 physics-driven projectiles that all need to be authority-checked. It works perfectly in single-player testing. How do you have the conversation about why this will not scale to 50 players, and what do you propose instead?
2. How do you work with QA to create meaningful network testing scenarios? Clamp tools and artificial latency are a start, but they do not capture real-world patterns like jitter spikes, route changes, and asymmetric packet loss. What is your approach to testing networking at scale before you have real players?
3. A designer wants an ability that "freezes time for everyone except the caster for 3 seconds." Explain how you would communicate the networking implications of that design before they build an entire mechanic around it.
4. How do you explain networking constraints to an art team that wants to add client-side cosmetic effects that accidentally affect gameplay state? The particle effect looks like it should deal damage but does not, confusing players. Whose problem is this?
5. Walk us through how you work with the DevOps or IT team on server infrastructure decisions. What hosting requirements are non-negotiable for your networking architecture, and how do you communicate them to someone who manages servers but does not understand tick rates?
6. A producer asks you to estimate how long it will take to "add multiplayer" to a feature that was built single-player first. The feature works locally. How do you scope the networking work and communicate why it is not just "syncing the data"?
7. How do you handle the situation where a gameplay programmer has been writing replication code that works but is wildly inefficient -- sending full state every frame, not using delta compression, replicating cosmetic data with gameplay priority? Do you rewrite it, mentor them, or build better abstractions?
8. You need to coordinate a networking architecture change that affects every gameplay system. The change is necessary but disruptive. How do you plan the rollout with the gameplay team so they are not constantly fighting merge conflicts?
9. A live ops team wants to deploy a server-side hotfix that changes gameplay values without a client update. How do you architect the system to support this, and what are the networking implications of server and client running different game versions?
10. How do you communicate the difference between "possible" and "advisable" to a creative director who hears "yes, we can technically do that" as "yes, we should do that"?

**Leadership**
1. Gameplay engineers write replication code for every new feature, and most of them do not think about bandwidth, ordering, or authority. How do you establish networking patterns and abstractions that make it hard for non-network engineers to write broken netcode? What has worked for you?
2. It is Friday evening, players are reporting rubber-banding on EU servers, your metrics show normal server tick time but elevated round-trip times. Walk us through your triage process. How do you distinguish between server-side load, client-side performance, ISP routing issues, and hosting provider problems?
3. You are the only person who understands the replication graph, the snapshot system, and the reconciliation logic. How do you document and teach your networking architecture so the team is not completely dependent on you?
4. How do you evaluate a networking middleware or library (Photon, Mirror, Fish-Net, custom) for a new project? What are the evaluation criteria that matter and which marketing claims do you ignore?
5. A junior engineer has joined the team and needs to write netcode. The learning curve for your networking architecture is steep. How do you structure their ramp-up so they are productive without creating landmines?
6. How do you make the case for investing in networking infrastructure (better profiling tools, automated network testing, stress test frameworks) when the feature backlog is already full and leadership sees infrastructure as not shipping content?
7. Describe how you would run a network playtest -- not a gameplay playtest, but a session specifically designed to stress-test your networking under realistic conditions. How do you simulate 50 real players, and what data do you collect?
8. When a live game has a networking incident that affects match quality for thousands of players, how do you balance the urgency of fixing it right now versus understanding the root cause? When do you apply the band-aid and when do you take the game offline?
9. How do you establish networking budgets (bandwidth per entity, tick rate requirements, maximum latency tolerance) early in development so that gameplay programmers design within constraints rather than discovering them in beta?
10. You need to hire another network engineer. The role requires deep knowledge that is hard to assess in interviews. What do you actually ask to distinguish someone who understands networking at a systems level from someone who has only used high-level networking frameworks?

**Depth**
1. If we asked you to build the networking layer for a 100-player multiplayer game with real-time combat, walk us through your architecture from the wire protocol up. UDP with custom reliability? What is in your packet header? How do you structure your snapshot system? Where does interest management live? What are the hard scaling limits, and at what player count do you need to start spatially partitioning the simulation?
2. A player disconnects mid-match. They had active abilities, were carrying objectives, and other players were targeting them. Walk us through your reconnection system: how do you preserve their state, handle the gap period, resync them when they return, and deal with the edge case where they reconnect after the match has progressed significantly.
3. Describe how you would architect a dedicated server fleet for a multiplayer game. Cover server allocation, region selection, match lifecycle management, graceful shutdown during deploys, and how you handle the situation where a physical host fails mid-match. What hosting model do you prefer and why?
4. Design a network replay system that allows server-side recording of match state for anti-cheat review, tournament adjudication, and player-facing kill cams. What data do you capture, how do you compress it, and how do you handle the playback when the game client has been patched since the match occurred?
5. Walk us through building a cross-region matchmaking system where players in different geographic regions can play together. How do you handle the asymmetric latency, where do you place the server, and how do you communicate to players that cross-region play will feel different?
6. Describe your approach to building a networking stress test framework that can simulate hundreds of concurrent players with realistic input patterns, varying network conditions, and emergent gameplay scenarios. How do you make the results repeatable and the metrics actionable?
7. You are tasked with adding client migration -- the ability to transfer a player's connection from one server to another without disconnecting them (for seamless world transitions or server load balancing). Walk us through the handoff protocol.
8. Design the networking architecture for a spectator system that supports broadcast-quality esports viewing with 10-second delay, configurable camera angles, and overlay data (player stats, ability cooldowns). How does the spectator stream differ from the player stream?
9. Walk us through how you would handle a scenario where your hosting provider announces a 30% price increase with 90 days notice. What is your migration strategy, how do you evaluate alternatives, and how do you ensure zero impact on players during the transition?
10. A cheating ring has figured out how to manipulate your packet ordering to gain a 50ms advantage in hit registration. Describe how you would investigate, confirm, and close the exploit without breaking legitimate players on high-jitter connections.

### Tools Engineer

**Culture**
1. What does a healthy relationship between a tools team and the content creators they serve look like? What is the first sign that tools engineers have lost touch with their users?
2. When you strongly disagree with an artist's workflow -- you think their process is fundamentally wrong and no tool will fix it -- what do you do? Give a real example of navigating that tension.
3. Describe a time you built a tool that nobody used. What went wrong -- was it a UX problem, a trust problem, a discoverability problem, or did you solve the wrong problem entirely? How did that failure change your approach?
4. What frustrates you most about being a tools engineer in game development specifically? Not software engineering generally -- what about building tools for creative teams drives you mad?
5. Do you prefer building polished tools that cover 80% of use cases, or flexible tools that power users can extend but casual users find intimidating? Why -- and where has each approach failed you?
