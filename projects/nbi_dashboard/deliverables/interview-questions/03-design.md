# Interview Questions — Design Roles

**50 questions per role, 10 per category.** Written for Couch Heroes — a fully remote multiplayer games studio (~55 people, UK + Greece).

---

### Game Design Lead

**Culture**
1. You own the design vision, but your level designers, systems designers, and economy designers all have strong opinions about how the game should feel. When two of your designers fundamentally disagree on how a core system should work, how do you resolve it without just pulling rank?
2. In a multiplayer game, player behaviour often reveals that your intended design was wrong — players find exploits, ignore systems you thought were critical, or create emergent play patterns you never anticipated. How do you decide when to correct the players versus redesigning around their behaviour?
3. Design documentation at most studios ranges from "ignored completely" to "treated as law long after the game has moved on." How do you keep your design vision alive and evolving across a remote team without it either fossilising in a wiki or evaporating into Slack conversations?
4. Remote game design means you cannot overhear a conversation between two designers who are quietly interpreting the same system differently. How do you detect design drift — where different team members are building towards subtly different visions — before it shows up as conflicting implementations weeks later?
5. You are the most senior design voice in the room. Junior designers are more likely to defer to your opinion than challenge it. How do you create an environment where people push back on your ideas with genuine critique rather than polite agreement?
6. Game design lead is a role where you spend more time in documents, meetings, and feedback sessions than actually designing systems yourself. How do you stay creatively sharp and maintain genuine design instinct when your daily work has shifted towards leadership?
7. When the studio ships a feature you know is mediocre — it passed review, it works, but it is not what you envisioned — how do you process that? Do you push for iteration post-launch, accept the shipping reality, or treat it as a failure of your process?
8. Multiplayer games generate intense player feedback — Reddit threads dissecting your balance decisions, Discord arguments about your systems choices, content creators publicly calling your design philosophy wrong. How does community sentiment factor into your design decisions, and where do you draw the line?
9. You have shipped a design pillar document that the team has been building against for six months. New information — a competitor release, a playtest insight, a technical limitation — makes you believe one of the pillars is wrong. How do you handle a major course correction that invalidates months of work?
10. How do you handle the creative tension between designing for launch (a complete, polished experience) and designing for live service (systems that support years of content updates)? These goals often conflict at the architecture level. Where do you make the trade-offs?

**Technical**
1. Walk us through how you would design and balance an asymmetric multiplayer system where different player roles have fundamentally different abilities, win conditions, or power curves. What is your methodology for ensuring no role feels unfair to play against?
2. Describe your framework for designing a core gameplay loop for a multiplayer game that needs to sustain engagement across thousands of hours. What are the specific systems you layer (moment-to-moment, session, meta), and how do you validate that each layer is doing its job?
3. How do you approach designing systems that interact with each other in a multiplayer game — for example, a progression system that feeds into matchmaking that affects combat balance? How do you predict and test second-order effects before they reach players?
4. Describe your process for writing a game design document that engineers can actually implement. What level of detail do you specify for core systems, what do you deliberately leave open for engineering to solve, and how do you handle the sections where your design depends on technical feasibility you cannot confirm?
5. How do you use data to inform design decisions in a multiplayer game without becoming a slave to metrics? Walk us through a specific example where the data told you one thing, your design instinct told you another, and how you resolved the conflict.
6. Walk us through your approach to designing a matchmaking system that balances queue times, skill fairness, party support, and connection quality. What trade-offs do you make at different player population levels, and how do you prevent the system from creating negative experiences at the edges?
7. How do you design reward schedules and engagement hooks without crossing the line into exploitative or manipulative design? Where do you personally draw that ethical line, and how does that affect the systems you build?
8. Describe how you prototype and validate a new game mechanic before committing engineering resources to a full implementation. What fidelity do you need, what tools do you use, and how many iterations do you typically go through before you are confident enough to greenlight production?
9. How do you approach designing a combat system that supports both skilled mechanical play and strategic decision-making? Walk us through the specific levers you tune — time-to-kill, ability cooldowns, resource management, positioning advantage — and how they interact.
10. When you inherit an existing design that has fundamental problems — the economy is broken, the progression is too flat, the core loop has a dead spot at the 20-hour mark — how do you diagnose root causes versus symptoms? Walk us through your analytical process for systems-level design problems.

**Collaboration**
1. When your engineering lead tells you a core system you have designed is architecturally expensive, but the cheaper alternative fundamentally changes the player experience, how do you evaluate the trade-off? Give a specific example of how you have navigated this.
2. How do you align your level design lead, economy designer, and systems designers around a unified player experience when each discipline naturally optimises for different things? What artefacts or processes make that alignment concrete?
3. Describe how you work with production to scope a design-heavy milestone. Designers are notorious for underestimating complexity — how do you translate design ambition into honest effort estimates?
4. How do you collaborate with the art director when your design requirements conflict with their visual vision? The art director wants atmospheric, moody environments; your game design needs bright, readable spaces for competitive play. Who wins, and how do you reach a resolution that serves both?
5. When a systems designer on your team proposes a design that is technically elegant but you believe will not be fun for players, how do you give that feedback? How do you distinguish between "this will not work" and "this is not how I would do it"?
6. Describe how you manage the design-engineering handoff for a complex system. You have written the design, engineering has questions, the implementation reveals edge cases your document did not cover. What does the ongoing collaboration look like during the build phase?
7. How do you work with the live ops team to ensure your game systems support the content cadence they need post-launch? What design decisions do you make early in development specifically to enable sustainable live service?
8. When the QA team discovers an interaction between two of your systems that produces an unintended result — not a bug, but an emergent behaviour you did not design for — how do you decide whether to embrace it, patch it, or redesign around it?
9. How do you handle feedback from external playtesters who tell you the game is not fun but cannot articulate why? What is your process for translating vague player sentiment into specific design actions?
10. Describe a time you had to present a controversial design decision to studio leadership — a decision you believed in but knew would face resistance. How did you build the case, what evidence did you bring, and what did you do if they said no?

**Leadership**
1. How do you build a design team culture where designers critique each other's systems designs rigorously — not just "I like it" or "I don't like it," but structured analysis of how a system will behave at scale? What does a productive design review look like under your leadership?
2. When a major feature you championed fails in playtesting — not a small tweak needed, but fundamentally not fun — how do you handle the pivot with your team and with leadership? How do you distinguish between "needs iteration" and "needs killing"?
3. How do you evaluate the work of a systems designer versus a level designer versus an economy designer? They produce very different outputs — what does "excellent" look like in each, and how do you calibrate your feedback?
4. How do you develop junior designers who have strong creative instincts but weak analytical skills — they can ideate but cannot model how their system will behave at scale, under edge cases, or across thousands of hours of play? What does that development path look like?
5. When two features compete for the same engineering bandwidth, and both are designed by members of your team, how do you make the prioritisation call without the losing designer feeling their work was dismissed? How do you handle the politics of internal prioritisation?
6. How do you maintain design authority without becoming a bottleneck? Every system design review goes through you, every mechanic needs your sign-off — at some point that model breaks. How do you delegate design authority while maintaining coherent vision?
7. Describe how you run a design retrospective after a major feature ships. What do you evaluate — the design document quality, the playtesting process, the engineering collaboration, the final player reception — and how do you turn those learnings into process improvements?
8. How do you handle a designer on your team who consistently overscopes their designs? The ideas are good, but every feature they design takes three times longer than estimated because they cannot resist adding complexity. What is your coaching approach?
9. When you hire a new systems designer, what do you look for beyond portfolio and experience? What signals in an interview tell you someone can actually design multiplayer systems that work at scale versus someone who has good ideas but poor systems thinking?
10. How do you keep your design team motivated during the long middle of production — past the excitement of pre-production, before the energy of ship — when the work is iteration, bug fixing, and balance tuning rather than inventing new systems?

**Depth**
1. Design a meta-progression system for a multiplayer action game that keeps competitive players engaged for months without creating pay-to-win perception or insurmountable gaps between new and veteran players. What are your core design pillars and what mechanics do you explicitly avoid?
2. If you inherited a multiplayer game where the community has identified a "dominant strategy" that makes 80% of available options unviable, walk us through your diagnostic and correction process. How do you fix the balance without alienating the players who built their skill around the dominant approach?
3. How do you design for the tension between depth and accessibility in a multiplayer game? Competitive players want mechanical mastery; casual players want immediate satisfaction. Walk us through a specific system design that serves both without diluting either.
4. Design a social systems layer for a multiplayer game that encourages cooperative play without forcing it. Solo players should not feel punished; grouped players should feel rewarded. Walk us through the specific mechanics — matchmaking, communication, shared objectives, social progression — and how they interact.
5. You are designing the economy for a free-to-play multiplayer game. Walk us through your sink-and-faucet model, your monetisation touch points, your approach to cosmetic versus functional items, and how you prevent inflation from undermining the economy within the first year.
6. Design a seasonal content model for a multiplayer game that keeps engagement high across a three-month season. Walk us through the content cadence, the progression arc, the reward structure, and how you handle the "dead zone" in the final weeks before a new season starts.
7. If you were tasked with designing an anti-cheat-friendly game from the design layer — not the technical anti-cheat, but game design decisions that make cheating less impactful or less appealing — what systems-level choices would you make? How does game design reduce the motivation to cheat?
8. Walk us through designing the onboarding experience for a multiplayer game with complex systems. New players need to learn combat, movement, abilities, economy, progression, and social features. How do you sequence that learning, what do you surface first, and how do you prevent information overload without patronising experienced gamers?
9. Design a competitive ranking system for a team-based multiplayer game where individual performance varies wildly within a team. How do you handle the tension between team outcome (win/loss) and individual contribution? How do you prevent ranking systems from creating toxic behaviour?
10. You are designing a multiplayer game that will launch with 8 characters and grow to 40 over two years. How do you design the initial character roster to establish clear design space, ensure each new character adds meaningful options without power creep, and maintain balance as the roster triples in size? What are your design constraints from day one?

### Level Design Lead

**Culture**
1. Level design has the longest feedback loop of any design discipline — a map takes months to validate, and you often cannot tell whether spatial decisions work until dozens of playtests in. How do you manage your team's morale and momentum through that uncertainty?
2. Competitive multiplayer maps live or die on fairness, but your environment artists want landmark moments and your art director wants visual variety. How do you establish a studio-wide policy for when visual spectacle can override competitive symmetry?
3. Your maps will be played thousands of times by the same players. Designing for spectacle is easy; designing for repeated discovery is hard. What is your philosophy on creating spaces that still surprise on the hundredth play?
4. Level design is often invisible when done well — players notice a bad map immediately but rarely articulate why a great map works. How do you advocate for level design quality and investment in a studio where flashier disciplines get more attention?
5. A map that tests well internally can fall apart when the wider player base gets their hands on it — players find routes, exploits, and strategies your internal team never discovered. How do you plan for the gap between internal playtesting and live player behaviour?
6. Remote level design means you cannot physically walk someone through a space and point at problems. How do you give and receive spatial feedback asynchronously when screenshots lose the sense of scale and video walkthroughs take time to produce?
7. The games industry has a tendency to ship fewer, more polished maps rather than more, rougher ones. Where do you stand on that trade-off for a multiplayer game, and how does that philosophy affect your team structure and production approach?
8. When a map you designed and championed performs poorly in live — players dislike it, it gets voted out of rotation, the data shows problems you did not anticipate — how do you handle the professional and emotional reality of that? Do you fix it or move on?
9. Level design requires deep concentration — spatial problem-solving that benefits from long, uninterrupted blocks. How do you protect your team's focus time in a remote studio with constant Slack messages, meetings, and cross-discipline requests?
10. How do you balance investing in new maps versus iterating on existing ones? Players want fresh content, but your shipped maps still have known issues. How do you allocate your team's time between creation and refinement?

**Technical**
1. Walk us through how you establish a blockout-to-ship pipeline for a multiplayer game with multiple maps in simultaneous development. What are the stage gates, who has approval authority at each stage, and how do you prevent the pipeline from becoming a bottleneck?
2. Describe your approach to designing map geometry that scales across different player counts — the same map needs to feel populated at 16 players and not overcrowded at 50. What spatial techniques and dynamic systems do you use?
3. How do you approach sightline management in a multiplayer map? Long sightlines favour ranged play, short ones favour melee — and different areas of the same map might need different combat dynamics. Walk us through your methodology for designing sightline language across a map.
4. How do you approach spawn system design for a multiplayer map? Walk us through your methodology for placing spawn points that prevent spawn camping, maintain competitive fairness, and avoid the feeling of randomness that makes deaths feel unfair.
5. Describe your approach to designing vertical space in multiplayer maps. Height advantage is one of the most powerful spatial tools, but poorly designed verticality creates frustrating one-way encounters. How do you balance elevation advantage with counterplay options?
6. How do you use spatial pacing to create distinct emotional beats across a multiplayer map — tension in corridors, release in open spaces, intensity at objectives? Walk us through the spatial vocabulary you use to control player experience.
7. Walk us through how you design navigation and wayfinding in a complex multiplayer map without explicit UI markers. How do you use geometry, lighting, colour, and landmark placement so players develop an intuitive mental map within their first three matches?
8. Describe your approach to designing destructible or dynamic environments in a multiplayer map. What can change during a match, what stays fixed, and how do you prevent dynamic elements from creating unfair advantages or making the map unreadable?
9. How do you approach map optimisation for network performance — ensuring that map complexity does not create problems for the server's interest management, entity replication, or physics simulation at full player counts?
10. Walk us through designing cover systems within a multiplayer map. How do you place cover to create readable combat spaces where players can make informed decisions about exposure, and how do you prevent cover from being so abundant that engagements stalemate?

**Collaboration**
1. When your environment art lead has invested significant time making a space beautiful but your playtest data shows players consistently avoid that area, how do you handle the conversation? What is your framework for these trade-offs at scale, not just one-off negotiations?
2. How do you work with your game design lead to ensure level layouts actually support the game's core mechanics? Give an example of discovering that a mechanic and a map layout were working against each other and how you resolved it.
3. Describe how you coordinate with your audio and lighting leads to ensure spatial audio cues, lighting-based navigation hints, and level geometry all tell the same story about where players should go and what threats exist.
4. When the network engineer tells you that a specific area of your map is causing server performance issues — too many players converging, too many physics objects, too much simultaneous combat — how do you adjust the spatial design without destroying the gameplay intent of that area?
5. How do you work with the game design lead on objective placement? The systems designer defines what objectives do; you define where they live. When the "where" fundamentally changes the "what" — an objective in a tight corridor plays completely differently from one in an open field — who drives the iteration?
6. Describe how you collaborate with QA on map testing. What specific test cases do you ask QA to run beyond "play the map and report bugs"? How do you brief them on what spatial problems to look for that automated testing cannot catch?
7. How do you manage the relationship between your level design team and external outsource studios who may be doing environment art for your maps? How do you ensure outsourced art does not break spatial gameplay intent?
8. When a player-facing community manager reports that players are complaining about a specific map, but your data shows the map performs within normal parameters, how do you reconcile qualitative feedback with quantitative data?
9. How do you coordinate map development timelines with the broader production schedule? Maps are long-lead items that depend on almost every other discipline. How do you prevent map development from becoming the critical path that holds up the entire milestone?
10. Describe how you work with your game design lead to define the "map identity" for a set of multiplayer maps. Each map needs to feel mechanically distinct — not just visually different — while still supporting the same core game modes. How do you create that variety without fragmenting the player experience?

**Leadership**
1. How do you establish spatial design standards across a team of level designers so that all maps feel like they belong in the same game? What does a "level design style guide" contain, and how do you enforce it without killing individual creativity?
2. When a junior level designer's blockout is structurally sound but spatially uninteresting — correct but boring — how do you teach spatial instinct versus spatial correctness? What does that mentoring process look like?
3. How do you scope and schedule level design work when one map might take two months and another might take six, and you will not know which until the first playtest? What is your estimation approach?
4. How do you run playtest reviews with your level design team? A playtest session generates dozens of observations — movement patterns, engagement hotspots, player deaths, confusion points. How do you structure the debrief so it produces actionable changes rather than a pile of observations?
5. When you need to cut a map from the launch lineup because it is not meeting quality bar, how do you make and communicate that decision? The team has invested months in it. What is your process?
6. How do you build a level design team that can work independently? In a remote studio, you cannot stand behind someone and guide their spatial decisions. What processes, reviews, and documentation enable your designers to make good spatial choices without you reviewing every blockout iteration?
7. Describe how you onboard a new level designer onto a project with established spatial standards, an existing map set, and specific combat metrics they need to design around. What do they need in their first week versus first month?
8. How do you handle disagreements between level designers who have different spatial philosophies? One prefers open, arena-style combat; another prefers tight, corridor-based encounters. Both approaches are valid. How do you maintain variety without inconsistency?
9. How do you track and improve level design quality over the life of a project? What metrics do you use — playtest completion rates, player navigation heatmaps, combat engagement distances — and how do you turn data into design standards?
10. When production asks you to reuse an existing map layout with a visual reskin instead of designing a new map, how do you evaluate whether that serves the players? When is a reskin legitimate, and when is it a false economy?

**Depth**
1. Design a competitive multiplayer map for 40-60 players with at least three distinct engagement zones, meaningful elevation play, and strategic chokepoints that create predictable combat hotspots without feeling formulaic. Walk us through from initial concept to first playable blockout.
2. How do you design a map set for a game that supports multiple modes — the same geometry needs to work for team deathmatch, objective control, and an asymmetric attack/defend mode? What spatial design principles make multi-mode maps viable versus ones that only work for one mode?
3. Describe how you would audit an underperforming map in a live multiplayer game. Player data shows one side wins 60% of the time and one area of the map has almost zero traffic. Walk us through your diagnostic process and proposed fixes.
4. Design a tutorial map for a multiplayer game that teaches movement, combat, and objective play without feeling like a tutorial. The space needs to introduce mechanics organically through spatial design rather than pop-up text. Walk us through the spatial progression and how each area teaches a specific skill.
5. You are designing a map that will be used for an esports tournament. The requirements are stricter than casual play — perfect symmetry, predictable spawns, zero exploitable geometry, broadcast-friendly sightlines. How does designing for competitive broadcast change your spatial decisions compared to a standard multiplayer map?
6. Describe how you would design a map set for a seasonal content model where one new map ships every three months. How do you plan the spatial variety across a year of releases so each map fills a gap in the rotation rather than overlapping with existing maps?
7. Walk us through designing a multiplayer map for a game mode that has never existed before — a mode the game design lead has just defined on paper. You have the rules but no spatial precedent. How do you approach the blank canvas when there are no reference points?
8. Design a map that supports 50+ players and has meaningful environmental storytelling — a space with a narrative history that players can piece together through spatial details — without that narrative ever interfering with competitive readability. How do you layer story into competitive geometry?
9. You are tasked with redesigning the most popular map in a live game. Players love it, but it has known spatial problems (one dominant strategy, a dead zone, a spawn trap). How do you improve it without losing what makes it beloved? What do you change and what do you preserve?
10. Describe your process for building a map that needs to support both PvP and PvE encounters in the same space. The spatial requirements are different — PvE needs encounter staging, spawn closets, and difficulty pacing; PvP needs fairness, flow, and counterplay. How do you design one space that serves both?

### Senior Level Designer

**Culture**
1. You have been iterating on the same map for three months — rebuild, playtest, feedback, rebuild. The fifth version is better than the first, but you are not sure it is good yet. How do you maintain the creative conviction to push through versus the humility to accept it might need a fundamentally different approach?
2. After months of daily playtesting your own maps, you know every sightline, every flank route, every spawn timing. How do you compensate for the fact that you can no longer experience your own level as a new player? What techniques do you use to maintain fresh perspective?
3. Grey-box playtesting is where most level design decisions are actually made. Walk us through how you organise and run a productive grey-box playtest session — who plays, what you observe, how you capture data, and how you turn observations into actionable changes.
4. You have spent three weeks building a blockout you believe is genuinely innovative, but two senior colleagues independently tell you it is 'fine but safe' and suggest you are playing it too conservatively. They might be right, or they might be projecting their own taste. How do you evaluate whether you are being appropriately cautious or creatively timid, and what do you do with that self-assessment?
5. When you receive contradictory playtest feedback — half the testers say an area is too open, the other half say it is too cluttered — how do you parse that? What is your framework for turning conflicting feedback into a spatial decision?
6. How do you handle the emotional investment in a space you have built when the level design lead decides to change the direction significantly? You have spent weeks on a blockout you believe in, and the lead wants a fundamentally different approach. How do you navigate that?
7. Multiplayer map design requires you to think simultaneously as a player, a designer, and a performance-conscious developer. How do you balance the instinct to create a beautiful, complex space against the practical reality of what runs at 60fps with 50 players?
8. Your map gets a negative community reception after release. Players are vocal on social media about specific areas they dislike. Some of the criticism is valid; some misunderstands your design intent. How do you process and respond to public criticism of your spatial work?
9. Level designers at different studios have different philosophies — some are metric-driven, some are instinct-driven, some are theory-driven. What is your design philosophy, and how has it been shaped by specific successes and failures in your career?
10. Your lead consistently praises your spatial work but you privately feel your maps lack a distinctive voice compared to the best designers in the industry. You are technically proficient but creatively uncertain. How do you confront that gap honestly, and what does your relationship with creative ambition look like when no one else is pushing you to be better?

**Technical**
1. Describe your scripting and implementation workflow within levels — triggers, spawn logic, objective sequencing, dynamic events. What is your comfort level with scripting tools, and how do you debug a trigger chain that is not firing correctly during a playtest?
2. Walk us through how you construct a combat encounter space in a multiplayer map. What are the spatial dimensions you control (cover density, engagement range, flank exposure, height advantage), and how do you tune them to create the combat feel the game design lead has specified?
3. How do you approach performance optimisation from a level design perspective? What spatial decisions directly affect rendering cost (draw distance, occlusion, density), and how do you work with artists and engineers to hit frame rate targets without sacrificing spatial quality?
4. Describe how you approach the transition between indoor and outdoor spaces in a multiplayer map. The shift in scale, lighting, engagement distance, and cover language changes dramatically. How do you make that transition feel natural while serving gameplay?
5. Walk us through your blockout methodology. What tools do you use, what scale do you work at initially, how do you validate measurements against player metrics (movement speed, jump height, weapon range), and at what point do you consider a blockout ready for its first playtest?
6. How do you design spawn logic for a multiplayer map? Walk us through your placement methodology — weighted spawns, influence maps, safety checks — and how you prevent the spawn system from creating unfair experiences without making spawns predictable.
7. Describe your approach to designing objective locations within a map. What spatial properties make an objective location interesting to fight over? How do you ensure the approach routes, sightlines, and cover create tactical choices rather than a single optimal strategy?
8. How do you handle map variants or modifications — night versions, weather variants, layout changes for different game modes — within a single base map? What stays consistent across variants, and what changes? How do you manage the additional complexity?
9. Walk us through how you use metrics from playtesting — heatmaps, kill-distance data, movement flow data, time-in-area statistics — to inform specific spatial changes. Give an example of data revealing a problem your instinct missed.
10. Describe your approach to designing spaces that create "moments" in multiplayer — a long-range sniper duel, a tense close-quarters fight, a dramatic objective push. How do you use spatial design to create conditions where memorable gameplay emerges naturally?

**Collaboration**
1. When you give feedback on another level designer's map, "this area does not flow well" is not actionable. What does specific, useful level design critique look like? Walk us through how you would give feedback on a blockout that has structural problems.
2. You need a specific art treatment for a gameplay-critical area — a sniper perch that needs to be visually prominent, or a flanking route that needs to feel hidden. How do you brief an environment artist so they understand the gameplay intent, not just the visual request?
3. Describe a situation where you discovered during implementation that a game mechanic did not work in the space you had designed for it. How did you diagnose whether the problem was in the mechanic or the space, and what did the resolution look like?
4. How do you work with the systems designer when they change a core mechanic — movement speed, ability range, time-to-kill — that affects the spatial assumptions your map was built around? At what point does a systems change require a map rework versus a map adjustment?
5. When the lighting artist's pass on your map changes how players read the space — a previously visible flank is now in shadow, a previously dark corner is now lit — how do you collaborate to ensure the lighting serves both visual quality and spatial gameplay?
6. Describe how you coordinate with sound designers on audio design for your maps. How do you communicate which areas should feel sonically distinct, where audio cues are critical for gameplay (footsteps, ability sounds), and how audio contributes to spatial awareness?
7. When a QA tester reports that a specific area of your map has an exploit — an unintended sightline, a geometry clip, a movement trick that bypasses your intended routing — how do you evaluate whether to fix it or leave it as emergent depth?
8. How do you work with the network engineer when your map design creates server performance problems — too many players converging in one area, too many physics objects, too many breakable elements? What spatial compromises do you make?
9. Describe how you handle the moment when an environment artist tells you that an area of your blockout cannot be art-passed at the quality bar required — the geometry is too complex, the scale is wrong for modular kit pieces, or the shapes do not support the studio's visual style.
10. How do you collaborate with the game design lead when playtest data suggests one of your maps favours a specific play style or character archetype? Is that a level design problem, a game design problem, or an expected outcome of spatial variety?

**Leadership**
1. When you mentor a junior level designer, what are the hardest skills to teach? What separates a junior who can place geometry correctly from a senior who can make a space feel compelling?
2. You inherit a half-finished map from a designer who has left the project. The blockout is approved, but the implementation is incomplete. How do you pick up someone else's spatial vision without starting over?
3. You are working on three maps simultaneously at different stages — one in blockout, one in art pass, one in final polish. How do you manage your attention and energy across them, and how do you prevent the most urgent one from consuming all your time?
4. How do you approach documentation for your maps? What information do you leave behind so that someone else — another level designer, an environment artist, a QA tester — can understand your spatial intent without having to ask you?
5. When you identify a systemic problem across multiple maps — spawns are too predictable, cover is too dense, objective areas are too similar — how do you raise it as a team-level issue rather than a map-level fix? How do you advocate for standards changes?
6. How do you approach knowledge sharing with other level designers on your team? What have you learned about effective spatial design communication — is it walkthroughs, annotated top-down views, design pillars documents, or something else?
7. Describe how you handle a situation where your lead's spatial feedback conflicts with your professional judgement. You believe the map plays better your way; the lead disagrees. How do you decide whether to advocate, experiment, or defer?
8. When a less experienced designer asks you for help with a spatial problem, how do you coach them towards a solution rather than just giving them the answer? What is your teaching philosophy for spatial design?
9. How do you contribute to the team's spatial design standards beyond your own maps? What do you do to elevate the level design quality across the team rather than just within your own work?
10. Describe a time you proactively identified a risk or problem on someone else's map before it became a crisis. How did you raise it, and how did you handle the interpersonal dynamics of critiquing a peer's work unsolicited?

**Depth**
1. Walk us through designing a multiplayer map from the first blank canvas to a validated, playtest-approved blockout. What constraints do you start with, what are your first spatial decisions, how many iterations do you expect, and how do you know when the blockout is "done"?
2. Design a map that works for both casual and competitive play. Casual players need readable spaces and forgiving geometry; competitive players need skill-expression opportunities and strategic depth. What specific spatial design techniques let you serve both?
3. Pick the best-designed multiplayer map you have ever played. Explain what makes it great from a spatial design perspective — routing, pacing, sightlines, landmark hierarchy, and how the geometry creates memorable moments. What principles from that map would you carry into your own work?
4. Walk us through redesigning a specific combat area in an existing map. Playtest data shows the area has a 70/30 win rate for attackers, the cover layout funnels players into predictable paths, and the objective is visible from too many angles. What changes do you make, and how do you validate that the redesign solves the problems without creating new ones?
5. Design a multiplayer map for a game mode that involves escorting a payload along a fixed path. The path creates an inherent advantage for defenders who can set up positions. How do you design the surrounding space to give attackers viable strategies without making defence feel pointless?
6. Describe how you would design a map that uses elevation as its primary design axis — not just a flat map with some ledges, but a genuinely vertical space where height control is the core strategic element. What are the unique challenges of designing for vertical play?
7. You are given a real-world location as reference — a specific neighbourhood, a train station, a harbour — and asked to design a multiplayer map inspired by it. How do you translate a real space into a competitive multiplayer layout? What do you keep, what do you change, and what do you invent?
8. Walk us through designing a multiplayer map that supports vehicle and infantry combat in the same space. Vehicles need wide lanes and long sightlines; infantry needs cover and tight spaces. How do you design a map where both coexist without one dominating?
9. Describe your process for designing a map that will be revealed to the competitive community at a major event. The stakes are high — the map needs to make a strong first impression, demonstrate spatial innovation, and be competitively viable from day one. How does that pressure change your design process?
10. Design a multiplayer map that changes significantly during the course of a match — a bridge that collapses, a wall that opens, a section that floods. How do you ensure that the map remains fair and readable as its geometry shifts, and how do you prevent the transformations from feeling like arbitrary map changes?

### Mid General Designer

**Culture**
1. As a mid-level designer at a multiplayer studio, you will own individual features that plug into systems designed by more senior designers. How do you take creative ownership of your feature while respecting the constraints and vision someone else has set?
2. You have designed a feature and you are convinced it is the right approach, but your lead disagrees and wants you to try a different direction. When do you advocate harder, when do you execute their vision, and how do you tell the difference?
3. You join a remote studio where everyone seems to already know each other and have established relationships. Design discussions reference old decisions you were not part of, and inside jokes fly over your head. Six weeks in, you still feel like an outsider. What do you do about it, and how much of that responsibility sits with you versus the team?
4. A senior designer gives you blunt feedback that your last feature design was 'overthought and underfelt' — technically correct but missing the soul of what makes multiplayer games fun. You are not sure they are right, but the comment stings because it touches something you worry about privately. How do you sit with that feedback, and what do you do with it?
5. How do you handle the frustration of seeing your carefully designed feature reduced in scope or simplified during implementation? Engineering constraints, schedule pressure, and production realities mean the shipped version is rarely the full vision. How do you stay motivated?
6. When you are the least experienced designer in a room full of senior and lead designers, how do you contribute value? What perspective does a mid-level designer bring that more experienced designers might miss?
7. Game design is one of the few disciplines where almost every player thinks they could do your job. How do you handle feedback from non-designers — engineers, artists, producers — who have strong opinions about your feature design?
8. What is a game you love that most designers in your peer group dismiss or have never played? Why does it resonate with you, and what does your affection for it reveal about what you value in game design that might differ from mainstream design thinking?
9. Your feature is one of twenty in the game. Individually it works well, but as a player you can feel that it does not connect to the features around it. How do you evaluate whether the integration problem is yours to fix or a broader design coherence issue?
10. How do you handle the reality that some of your features will be cut before they ever reach players? You invest creative energy into a design that gets cancelled mid-development. How do you process that without becoming defensive about your work in future prioritisation discussions?

**Technical**
1. Walk us through how you would write a design specification for a new multiplayer game mode. What sections are essential, what level of detail does engineering need to implement without daily check-ins, and how do you handle edge cases you cannot predict?
2. Describe a specific game system you have balanced using spreadsheets or data modelling. What were the variables, what was your methodology, and how did the theoretical model compare to actual player behaviour once implemented?
3. How do you approach designing a feature that intersects with other designers' systems — for example, a reward system that touches economy, progression, and matchmaking? How do you identify and manage the dependencies before implementation?
4. Walk us through your process for creating a prototype to test a game mechanic. What fidelity level do you aim for, what tools do you use (paper, digital prototype, in-engine), and how do you decide when a prototype has answered the questions you needed it to answer?
5. Describe how you approach writing edge case documentation for a multiplayer feature. What happens when a player disconnects mid-interaction, when two abilities trigger simultaneously, when a timer expires during a specific state? How do you think through the unhappy paths?
6. How do you use player data and analytics to evaluate a feature you designed after it ships? What metrics do you look at, what tells you the feature is working as intended versus being used but not enjoyed, and how do you distinguish between low engagement and poor discoverability?
7. Walk us through how you would design a UI/UX flow for a complex feature — for example, a loadout customisation system. How do you work with the UI designer to ensure the interface communicates the underlying game design clearly?
8. Describe your approach to writing balance proposals. When you want to change a number — a cooldown, a damage value, a resource cost — how do you predict the ripple effects, document your reasoning, and present the change to the team?
9. How do you handle designing for a feature where the technical constraints are unclear? Engineering has not confirmed whether something is feasible, but you need to progress the design. How do you design speculatively without wasting time on an impossible implementation?
10. Walk us through how you document a design decision that you expect to be controversial. The team will have questions. How do you structure the document to anticipate objections, present alternatives you considered, and explain your reasoning?

**Collaboration**
1. You receive conflicting feedback on your feature design from the game design lead, the level design lead, and the engineering lead. Each has a valid perspective. How do you synthesise their input into a coherent direction, and when do you escalate versus resolve it yourself?
2. Describe how you work with a level designer to ensure a feature you have designed — say, a capture-point mechanic — actually works spatially in the maps they are building. What does that collaboration look like in practice?
3. Your feature is in playtesting and it is not working. Players are confused, engagement is low. How do you diagnose whether the problem is in your design, the implementation, the tutorialisation, or the players just needing more time to learn it?
4. How do you work with an engineer who is implementing your design when they suggest changes during the build? The engineer sees technical opportunities or constraints you did not consider. How do you evaluate their suggestions without losing design intent?
5. Describe a time you needed to convince a sceptical team member — someone who thought your feature was unnecessary or poorly conceived — that the design was worth pursuing. What evidence or approach did you use?
6. How do you handle design reviews where multiple stakeholders are present? The game design lead, the art director, the tech lead, and the producer all have different concerns. How do you present your design so it addresses each perspective without the review becoming unfocused?
7. When a QA tester files a bug against your feature that is technically "working as designed" but clearly confuses players, how do you handle it? Is that a bug, a design problem, or a communication problem?
8. Describe how you collaborate with the economy designer when your feature creates or consumes in-game resources. How do you ensure your feature does not break the economy without having to understand the full economic model?
9. How do you handle a situation where the implementation of your design is technically correct but does not feel right when you play it? The engineer built exactly what you specified, but the experience is wrong. How do you give feedback that is actionable rather than just "it feels off"?
10. Describe how you work with production to communicate the status of your design work. Design progress is harder to track than engineering or art progress — there is no commit history or asset count. How do you make your work visible and trackable?

**Leadership**
1. How do you take ownership of a feature through its full lifecycle — from initial concept through implementation, playtesting, iteration, and live tuning? What does "owning" a feature mean to you day-to-day?
2. You spot a significant design problem in a system outside your assigned feature — a progression cliff, an economy exploit, a broken interaction between two systems. How do you raise it constructively without overstepping into another designer's ownership?
3. How do you manage your own workload when you have a new feature design due, three bugs from your last feature, and iteration notes from a playtest all competing for your time? What is your prioritisation framework?
4. When you are asked to mentor a more junior designer on their first feature, how do you balance giving them room to learn from mistakes against preventing them from wasting time on approaches you know will not work?
5. How do you advocate for design quality when production pressure pushes towards shipping something that works but is not good? What does "good enough" mean to you as a designer, and how do you make that case?
6. You have been the sole designer on a feature for two months when a new mid-level designer joins and is assigned to help you. They immediately start suggesting changes to your core design decisions. Some suggestions are good; some show they do not understand the constraints you worked through. How do you share ownership of something you have been building alone without either steamrolling them or letting them undo informed decisions?
7. When your feature ships and the reception is lukewarm — not broken, not beloved, just adequate — how do you evaluate what went wrong? Was it the design, the execution, the context, or your expectations?
8. You notice that the design team's post-mortems have become performative — everyone lists safe lessons learned, nobody mentions the real failures. You are mid-level and it is not your meeting to run. Do you say something, and if so, how do you challenge the format without overstepping your seniority?
9. Describe a time you took initiative on something that was not explicitly your responsibility. What prompted you to act, how did you handle the ambiguity of ownership, and what was the outcome?
10. How do you approach receiving critical feedback on your design work? Walk us through a specific example of receiving feedback that was hard to hear but ultimately made your design better. What made the difference between feedback you accepted and feedback you pushed back on?

**Depth**
1. Design a multiplayer game mode from scratch for a team-based action game. Walk us through your starting point: what questions do you answer first, how do you define the win condition and pacing, how do you account for different team sizes, and how do you validate the concept before committing to full production?
2. Design a player progression system for a multiplayer game that motivates engagement without gating power. Players who have played 500 hours should not have a mechanical advantage over new players, but they should feel their investment is meaningfully rewarded. What are your design principles?
3. Describe a feature you have designed that went through significant iteration. What was the original design, what changed after playtesting, and what specifically did you learn about the gap between theory and player reality?
4. Design a daily challenge system for a multiplayer game that drives player retention without feeling like a chore. How do you structure the challenges, what are the reward hooks, how do you prevent players from gaming the system, and how do you handle the player who misses a day?
5. Walk us through designing a contextual ping or communication system for a team-based multiplayer game. Players need to communicate tactical information without voice chat. What information can they share, how do you prevent spam, and how do you make the system useful enough that players actually use it instead of ignoring it?
6. Design a spectator mode for a multiplayer game. Walk us through what information spectators see, how you handle information asymmetry (spectators should not give advantages to living players), and how you make watching as engaging as playing for broadcast and social scenarios.
7. You are tasked with designing a tutorial that teaches the game's most complex system — one that experienced players love but new players bounce off. Walk us through your approach to scaffolding complexity, and how you validate that the tutorial is teaching understanding rather than button-press memorisation.
8. Design a post-match flow for a multiplayer game — the screen players see after a match ends. What information do you show, in what order, and how do you use this moment to drive re-engagement, social connection, and progression investment? What do you deliberately not show?
9. Walk us through designing a crafting or loadout system for a multiplayer game where the number of meaningful choices needs to grow over time as new items are added. How do you design the underlying structure so that 50 items feel like interesting choices rather than 47 bad options and 3 mandatory ones?
10. Describe how you would approach designing a live event for a multiplayer game — a limited-time mode, a community challenge, or a seasonal event. Walk us through the design from concept through measurement: what makes a live event successful, how do you scope it for a small design team, and how do you evaluate whether it achieved its goals?
