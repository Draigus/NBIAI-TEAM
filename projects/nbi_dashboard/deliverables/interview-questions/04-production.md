# Production Roles -- Interview Questions

---

### Shared Producer Questions (Art Producer, Assoc Producer, Tech Producer)

**Culture**
1. You are producing a remote team and you realise you have no idea what one of your workstreams has actually accomplished in the last two weeks. The updates have been positive but vague. You have a gut feeling something is wrong but no evidence. What do you do, and how do you do it without signalling distrust to the team member who has been reporting everything is fine?
2. How do you handle a milestone that's clearly going to slip? Walk us through the specific sequence: when do you raise it, who do you tell first, and what do you bring to that conversation?
3. Describe how you build trust with a new development team that's been burned by process-heavy production before. They see producers as overhead. What do you actually do in your first two weeks?
4. Tell me about a team where production was genuinely adding value and the developers knew it. What specifically was happening day to day that made it work? Now tell me about a team where production was tolerated but not valued. What was different, and was any of it within the producer's control to change?
5. How do you personally handle crunch pressure? Not in terms of what you say to the team, but your own response when the hours are long, the milestone is close, and the risk of cutting corners feels real.
6. You've joined a studio where production and development have an adversarial relationship — producers are seen as enforcers, not partners. No single incident caused it; it's been years in the making. How do you start shifting that without it seeming like a PR exercise?
7. What is your approach to mistakes — your own and the team's? When something goes wrong on your watch, how do you respond and what do you want the team to take from watching how you handle it?
8. When the team disagrees with a decision that's already been made above them — a feature is being cut, a date is fixed, a direction has changed — how do you hold the line while keeping people genuinely engaged rather than just compliant?
9. You're working with a team split across the UK and Greece. Time zones overlap for only a few hours, async communication is the norm, and cultural norms around directness and disagreement differ. What specifically do you do differently in your day-to-day production to account for this?
10. A highly capable team member is chronically negative in team settings — not abusive, but draining. Their technical contribution is excellent, but their energy affects the people around them. How do you approach this as a producer rather than escalating straight to a lead or manager?

**Collaboration**
1. When you inherit a project mid-development and discover the existing tracking is a mess — tasks without owners, outdated statuses, no consistent naming — how do you clean it up without halting everyone's work?
2. How do you handle external stakeholders who change requirements mid-milestone? What is your actual process for absorbing scope change without blowing up the committed plan?
3. A cross-discipline dependency is blocking progress: engineering is waiting on art, art is waiting on design sign-off, design says they already signed off. How do you untangle this in a remote team where you can't put everyone in a room?
4. Describe how you manage the relationship between two leads who have conflicting priorities — both legitimate, both time-sensitive. You don't have authority over either of them. What is your actual move?
5. You are coordinating a milestone delivery that requires sign-off from QA, a discipline lead, and a stakeholder in a different time zone. The stakeholder is slow to respond and has blocked the last two reviews by raising new feedback on the day of submission. How do you change the dynamic?
6. A producer on another team is handling a dependency your team relies on, and they're behind. You have no direct authority over them. How do you manage upward to get what your team needs without creating a political incident?
7. How do you run a sprint planning or milestone planning session with a remote team where some people are highly vocal and others say almost nothing? What specifically do you do to get real estimates and commitment rather than head-nods?
8. Engineering estimates are consistently 40% longer than the milestone schedule can absorb. Design says they've reduced scope as far as they can. How do you facilitate the conversation that needs to happen without it becoming a blame session?
9. You are producing a feature that has a hard external deadline — a platform certification window — and two internal teams are each waiting for the other to finish before they start their portion. How do you break the deadlock?
10. A game director and a studio technical director disagree on a technology choice that affects your milestone. The disagreement has gone on for two weeks and no one has made a decision. You're not senior enough to decide for them. What do you do?

**Leadership**
1. How do you identify when someone on the team is struggling before they tell you? What signals do you watch for when your only contact is Slack messages and stand-up updates?
2. When a project needs to cut scope to hit a date, how do you decide what gets cut? Walk us through your framework and how you present it to the team without destroying morale.
3. A team member consistently delivers work late but insists their estimates were realistic. How do you diagnose whether it is an estimation problem, a capacity problem, a dependency problem, or a motivation problem?
4. How do you give critical feedback to a team member who is technically capable but resistant to process? They see your feedback as bureaucracy rather than as something that helps the team. What is your approach across multiple conversations?
5. You're a producer without formal authority over the people you work with. How do you actually lead? Not in principle, but in practice — what do you do week to week that makes people follow your lead rather than ignore you?
6. A team member tells you in confidence that they're thinking of leaving the studio. They don't want you to tell anyone. How do you handle it, and how do you think about the tension between their confidence and your responsibilities?
7. How do you maintain team focus and morale during the long, unglamorous middle of a project — after the energy of early production but before the excitement of visible progress? What do you actually do?
8. You have two team members in conflict — a disagreement that has become personal and is affecting stand-ups and shared work. Neither has escalated it formally. How do you handle it as the producer?
9. A junior producer or coordinator is struggling. They're capable but making the same mistakes repeatedly and seem to be hiding it. How do you structure support for them without undermining their confidence in front of the team?
10. When do you escalate a team problem to a lead or HR versus handling it yourself? Where is your line, and has it ever moved based on experience?

### Art Producer (additional questions)

**Technical**
1. You join a project and discover that concept-to-engine turnaround for characters is averaging 14 weeks when the schedule assumes 8. Where do you start investigating? Walk us through how you'd diagnose whether the bottleneck is in concepting, modelling, texturing, rigging, or review cycles.
2. How do you write an outsource art brief that gets consistent quality back? Walk us through what goes into the brief for a batch of environment props: reference material, technical specs, naming conventions, delivery format. What do you include that most people forget?
3. An art director wants to raise the visual quality bar mid-production, which means reworking assets that already passed review. How do you scope the impact across character, environment, and VFX pipelines, and what data do you bring to the conversation about whether it is feasible?
4. How do you manage art asset versioning across an internal team and an outsource vendor when both are working in the same project simultaneously? What breaks when the naming and versioning conventions aren't agreed upfront, and how do you enforce compliance after the fact?
5. Describe your approach to setting up and maintaining a Shotgrid (or equivalent) pipeline for a production with 500+ assets across multiple disciplines. What are the minimum fields and statuses you need, and where do Shotgrid implementations typically go wrong?
6. A character art batch comes back from an outsource vendor technically correct — meets the poly budget, textures are within spec, LODs are present — but the silhouettes don't read well in-engine and the art director is unhappy. How do you handle the feedback loop without wasting another round-trip?
7. Your internal environment art team uses Perforce; your outsource vendor works in their own source control and delivers via a shared drive. Assets need to land in the depot without corrupting the folder structure or overwriting in-progress internal work. Walk us through the intake and integration process you'd set up.
8. How do you track and manage art technical debt — assets that passed review at the time but now violate updated technical specs, texture budgets, or platform requirements? How do you prioritise remediation against the active feature backlog?
9. A VFX artist is producing effects that look great in isolation but consistently cause GPU spikes in-engine when combined with existing scene content. You're not a technical artist yourself. How do you facilitate the conversation between the VFX artist and the engine programmer to resolve this without becoming a relay station?
10. Animation is blocking character integration: rigs are being revised after skins are already done, and the changes ripple through to game code. How do you restructure the handoff sequence to reduce re-work, and what agreements do you put in place between rigging, animation, and engineering?

**Depth**
1. You're managing art production for a multiplayer game with 12 playable characters, 8 maps, and a VFX budget of 200 unique effects. How do you build the master asset tracker, and what columns and views does each discipline lead actually need versus what just creates noise?
2. The internal art team can produce 60% of the assets needed for the next milestone. You need to outsource the other 40%. Walk us through your vendor selection, onboarding, and quality control process. How do you handle a vendor whose work is technically correct but stylistically off?
3. Describe how you coordinate a major art milestone review where environment, character, lighting, VFX, and UI art all need to be evaluated together in-engine. How do you structure the review so it is useful rather than a two-hour screenshot slideshow?
4. You're producing art for a multiplayer game that needs to ship simultaneously on PC and two console platforms. Each platform has different texture compression formats, memory budgets, and performance targets. How do you structure the art pipeline to handle multi-platform deliverables without tripling the work?
5. Midway through production your studio wins a second art outsource contract with a studio you haven't worked with before. They're large, capable, and come with their own pipeline preferences that conflict with yours. You need to integrate them quickly without disrupting the existing outsource relationship or the internal team. How do you manage the onboarding?
6. The art director leaves the project six weeks before a major milestone. Their replacement has a different visual direction in mind and wants to revisit approved assets. You have fixed delivery dates, a committed team, and a Shotgrid board full of assets that were signed off under the previous AD. Walk us through how you handle the transition.
7. You're managing art production on a game with significant live service ambitions — cosmetics, seasonal content, battle pass assets. The base game hasn't shipped yet. How do you structure art production to support the live service cadence post-launch while still hitting the day-one deliverables?
8. A senior character artist on your team is producing exceptional quality work but is extremely slow and resists scope conversations. The art director defends them. The schedule is suffering and junior artists are starting to notice the double standard. How do you resolve this without losing the artist or alienating the art director?
9. Describe how you'd approach the art production plan for a new map in a hero shooter: from first block-out through to final cert-ready state. What are the decision gates, who has sign-off at each stage, and where in the pipeline do most delays actually occur?
10. You've just completed a major production cycle and you have two weeks before the next one begins. How do you run the art production retrospective, what do you actually change in the pipeline based on what you learned, and how do you document it so the knowledge persists when people leave?

### Tech Producer (additional questions)

**Technical**
1. You're managing an engineering team that ships a new client build every Tuesday and a server deploy every Thursday. Walk us through your release checklist: what gets validated before each goes out, who signs off, and what is your rollback process when a Thursday deploy breaks matchmaking at 6pm?
2. Engineering leads are asking for two weeks to refactor the networking layer before building the next major feature. The milestone deadline has no slack. How do you evaluate whether the refactor is genuinely blocking or a preference? What evidence do you ask for, and how do you present the trade-off to stakeholders?
3. Your engineering team is split across gameplay, backend, and tools. Each sub-team estimates differently: gameplay uses story points, backend uses hours, tools doesn't estimate at all. How do you create a coherent capacity plan and milestone forecast across all three?
4. How do you track and communicate the state of a live game's technical backlog — platform compliance issues, deprecated SDK dependencies, unresolved performance regressions — when engineering leadership is focused entirely on the next feature and doesn't want to hear about maintenance?
5. You're managing a build system that produces client builds for PC, PS5, and Xbox Series X from a single codebase. The build team is two engineers. Build times are increasing and flaky tests are masking real failures. How do you prioritise build health improvements against the feature roadmap?
6. A third-party middleware licence is expiring in six weeks and the replacement path is unclear — some engineers want to build in-house, others want a different vendor. The middleware touches physics, audio, and UI. You're not an engineer. How do you drive the decision to a conclusion on time?
7. Describe how you'd manage the certification (cert) submission process for a multiplayer game on PlayStation and Xbox simultaneously. What's your submission timeline, who owns each platform's requirements, and how do you handle a cert failure two weeks before the launch date?
8. Your backend team uses a microservices architecture and deploys to AWS. You're not a backend engineer. How do you develop enough fluency to track progress, spot risks, and run meaningful engineering reviews without pretending to understand code you can't read?
9. Engineering is running a two-week sprint. Halfway through, a critical bug surfaces in the live game that needs two engineers and three days to fix. How do you manage the sprint re-planning — who decides what gets dropped, how do you communicate the slip, and what does your revised forecast look like?
10. You're onboarding a new engineer who joins mid-sprint. The team is under pressure and nobody has time to pair with them properly. How do you structure their first two weeks to get them productive without interrupting the team or setting them up to fail?

**Depth**
1. Describe how you'd manage the engineering work for a major multiplayer feature that touches client, server, and infrastructure: a new ranked matchmaking system. What are the technical risks you'd track, what cross-team dependencies would you surface early, and how do you stage the rollout?
2. A critical production incident takes the game offline during a weekend event. Walk us through your role as tech producer during and after: triage communication, who you pull in, how you keep leadership informed without distracting the engineers fixing it, and what your post-mortem process looks like.
3. The build pipeline is slow — a full build takes 90 minutes and breaks twice a week on average. Engineers are losing half a day each time. How do you build the case for investing engineering time in build infrastructure when the feature backlog is already behind?
4. You're producing a live multiplayer game that needs to pass platform compliance reviews for a major content update — new modes, new progression systems, new cosmetics. Walk us through how you manage the compliance and cert track as a parallel workstream to the main feature delivery track.
5. The engineering team you're producing for is 60% remote contractors and 40% full-time employees. Knowledge is siloed, documentation is sparse, and contractor turnover is running at 30% per year. How do you structure the production environment to reduce the impact of turnover and prevent critical knowledge from walking out the door?
6. Your game has a seasonal content model with a new season every eight weeks. Engineering support for each season is about two weeks of work, but the team is also building a major new feature for the next base game update. How do you manage the competing demands without either the season or the feature slipping?
7. A game director wants to make a significant design change to the matchmaking logic based on player feedback from a beta. Engineering says it's a three-week rework. The next major milestone is in four weeks. How do you facilitate the decision — what options do you put on the table and who makes the call?
8. Describe how you'd manage the engineering and production side of launching a new platform port — taking an existing PC multiplayer game to console — with a team that has never done a console port before. What do you not know at the start, and how do you find out fast enough?
9. You're producing a cross-functional team building a new social feature: clans, friends lists, cross-play invites. The feature touches game client, backend services, platform SDKs, and UI. Each sub-team has its own sprint cadence, tooling, and lead. How do you coordinate delivery across four different working rhythms without becoming the bottleneck for every decision?
10. Post-launch, your live game has accumulated six months of engineering decisions that made sense at the time but are now slowing the team down: performance regressions nobody has time to fix, a test suite that takes 45 minutes to run, platform compliance gaps flagged in the last cert review. How do you structure a technical health programme that runs in parallel with live ops without pulling engineers off the content roadmap?

### Assoc Producer (additional questions)

**Technical**
1. You're asked to set up the JIRA board for a new feature team of eight people (3 engineers, 2 artists, 1 designer, 1 QA, 1 animator). What issue types, workflow statuses, and board views do you create? What do you deliberately keep simple at the start?
2. A lead asks you to compile a status report for a milestone review tomorrow. You have 40 tasks across three boards, some are clearly outdated, and two people haven't updated their tickets in a week. Walk us through how you get an accurate picture in four hours.
3. You're taking notes in a cross-discipline planning meeting where engineering, art, and design are all talking past each other about priorities. How do you capture decisions and action items in real time when the conversation is moving fast and half the decisions are implicit?
4. You've been asked to set up the Shotgrid project for a new art track. You've used Shotgrid before but never as the person doing the initial configuration. How do you approach it — what do you research first, what do you set up yourself, and what do you ask the art lead to define before you configure it?
5. A sprint ends and three of the eight stories in it are still in progress. The lead wants to understand why and whether the team's velocity estimate for the next sprint is realistic. How do you prepare the data and the conversation?
6. You're responsible for tracking external dependencies — assets coming from an outsource vendor, a middleware update, a platform SDK from a third party. How do you keep your JIRA board accurate when the external parties don't update your ticketing system and communicate primarily via email?
7. Your studio head asks for a weekly update. Last week, the milestone was on track. This week, you have early signals that two features are at risk but no confirmed slip yet. What do you include in the update, what do you leave out, and why? What changes if the studio head tends to intervene heavily when they hear bad news early?
8. You're onboarding onto a project that has been running for eight months and has no centralised documentation — process is in people's heads, the JIRA board is inconsistent, and the wiki hasn't been updated since pre-production. How do you build an accurate picture of the current state of the project without spending two weeks in meetings?
9. A game feature your team has been building for three sprints gets descoped. Forty JIRA tickets need to be closed, labelled, and archived in a way that lets the team find them if the feature gets revived. How do you handle the administrative side of this without it taking a full day?
10. You have five things competing for your attention this afternoon: a milestone report due tomorrow, a vendor who has gone quiet on a delivery due Monday, a team member whose onboarding is stalling because nobody is pairing with them, a process doc your SP asked for last week, and a Slack thread where an engineer is clearly frustrated about a blocked task. You can only properly handle three before end of day. Which three, in what order, and what do you do about the other two?

**Depth**
1. Describe a situation where you realised a process you were running — a stand-up, a report, a tracking method — was not actually helping the team. How did you figure that out, and what did you do about it?
2. As an associate producer, you're often the person closest to the day-to-day work. A senior team member consistently bypasses the agreed workflow: doesn't update tickets, ships without QA sign-off, ignores the sprint commitment. How do you handle it when you're junior to them?
3. Think about the best producer you have worked with or observed. What specifically did they do that made them effective, and what is the gap between how you work now and how they work? What are you actively doing to close that gap?
4. You're the AP on a team where the senior producer is frequently pulled into cross-studio work and is largely unavailable. You're running the day-to-day effectively, but there's a decision that needs to be made today that's above your authority. Your senior producer hasn't responded to messages. What do you do?
5. Describe a time when you spotted a risk that nobody else on the team had noticed. How did you identify it, how did you communicate it, and what happened?
6. You're new to the studio and you're asked to observe a sprint retrospective and then suggest improvements to how it's run. What do you look for, and what specifically would you change — not in principle, but in the actual structure of the meeting?
7. The production team at this studio is two senior producers and you. Both SPs are working on different projects and you're pulled across both. How do you manage your time and attention when both projects are simultaneously in a crunch phase and both SPs expect full support?
8. An engineer on your team tells you informally that they think the sprint goal is unrealistic and they don't want to say so in the planning meeting because the last time they raised it they felt dismissed. How do you handle this — what do you do before the meeting, during it, and after?
9. You've been in the role for six months. Your senior producer asks you to take ownership of managing the outsource vendor relationship for a batch of 30 UI assets. You've never managed an outsource relationship before. How do you prepare, what questions do you ask the SP before starting, and what does your first communication to the vendor look like?
10. Looking back at your career so far, what's the production mistake you've learned the most from? What happened, what did you get wrong, and what is specifically different about how you work now as a result?

### Executive Producer

**Culture**
1. As EP, you define how production works across the entire studio. What is your production philosophy, and how do you establish it with a team that already has ingrained habits — some good, some not?
2. How do you balance studio-level imperatives — burn rate, publisher milestones, headcount targets — with the team's daily reality of what they can actually build? When those two views of the world diverge, whose reality wins?
3. When a project is in genuine trouble — not just behind schedule, but the team has lost confidence in the direction — what are the first three things you do? Walk us through a real example if you can.
4. What does psychological safety mean to you in a production context, and how do you create the conditions for it without it becoming a performance? Be specific about what you do, not just what you believe.
5. You've inherited a studio culture where overtime is normalised, crunch is expected, and the production team is seen as the people who schedule the crunch rather than the people who prevent it. How do you change this without being naive about the commercial realities of game development?
6. A significant portion of your studio is based in one country and the rest are distributed across another. Cultural differences are creating friction — communication styles, attitudes to hierarchy, approaches to giving feedback. How do you build a genuinely unified culture across that divide?
7. When you make a decision that turns out to be wrong — a call that cost the studio time, money, or a team member — how do you handle it with the people affected? What does accountability look like at EP level?
8. What is your approach to transparency with the team? Where is your line between information the team needs to function and information that creates anxiety without giving them anything they can act on?
9. You're at a studio with a strong creative culture where developers are proud of their craft and deeply attached to the product. How do you make hard commercial decisions — feature cuts, scope reductions, platform compromises — without destroying the creative energy that makes the studio good?
10. A game you've shipped is being reviewed negatively by players for reasons you and the team anticipated internally but were overruled by leadership. How do you handle the post-launch period with a team that is demoralised and feeling vindicated and resentful at the same time?

**Technical**
1. How do you evaluate whether a game is on track when you're not in the weeds every day? What leading indicators do you watch, and how do you get that information without creating a reporting tax on the production team?
2. Describe your approach to greenlight and stage-gate decisions. What evidence do you need to approve continued investment at each gate, and what does a "no" look like — have you ever killed or paused a project?
3. You're overseeing two projects sharing engineering resources. One is in pre-production and needs rapid prototyping; the other is in beta and needs stability. How do you allocate shared talent, and what happens when both leads escalate to you on the same day?
4. How do you structure your production metrics and reporting across multiple projects? What data do you actually look at each week, how do you get it without it being gamed, and what is the gap between what the reports tell you and what you actually believe about the state of the work?
5. A major technical risk materialises on a project you're overseeing — the netcode architecture the team has built cannot support the player counts the game requires. The team has known for six weeks and escalated it internally but it didn't reach you. How do you handle the immediate problem and the systemic failure that let it stay hidden?
6. You're evaluating whether to bring a technology in-house versus licensing middleware versus using an open-source solution for a critical system — say, a dedicated server backend or a matchmaking framework. What is your process, who do you involve, and how do you make the decision when your technical leads disagree?
7. How do you approach the build and release infrastructure across a studio shipping one or more live multiplayer games? What is the minimum investment in CI/CD and build engineering you consider non-negotiable, and how do you make the case for it when it competes with feature work?
8. A project in your portfolio is chronically optimistic in its milestone estimates — the team is capable, motivated, and confident, but they've missed the last three milestones by 20-30%. Improved estimation training hasn't helped. What is your diagnosis and what do you actually change?
9. Describe how you evaluate technical talent at the lead and senior level. What does a production-ready senior engineer or lead look like to you, and what red flags do you watch for that don't show up in a code review?
10. You're overseeing a live game that is technically healthy but commercially underperforming. The data team says player retention is the problem; engineering says the servers are fine; design says the content cadence is the issue. You need to make a call about where to invest the next three months of engineering capacity. Walk us through how you decide.

**Collaboration**
1. How do you work with a game director whose creative ambition consistently outpaces the team's capacity? You respect the vision, but the current trajectory means shipping 18 months late. What does that conversation look like?
2. Describe how you manage the relationship between internal teams and external partners — publishers demanding milestone demos, platform holders requiring cert compliance, outsource studios needing clear briefs. How do you shield the team without becoming a bottleneck?
3. Information flow between leadership and the development floor is your responsibility. What gets shared transparently, what gets filtered, and how do you handle the situation where the team finds out about a major decision through rumour rather than you?
4. You're working with a publisher who has contractual milestone approval rights and a tendency to use them to drive scope changes. How do you manage the relationship so the studio retains creative control without triggering a contractual dispute?
5. How do you manage co-development relationships — situations where your studio is building a game alongside another studio or for a client — when the other studio's production culture, tooling, and communication norms are very different from yours?
6. A senior creative lead and a senior technical lead have fundamentally different views on the direction of a feature. Both are respected, both have valid points, and they've been talking past each other for three weeks. You need a decision. How do you facilitate it?
7. Describe your approach to managing upward — keeping studio leadership, board, or publisher stakeholders informed and confident without creating unnecessary noise or giving them reason to intervene in day-to-day production decisions.
8. Your studio has an external QA partner that consistently reports the same categories of bugs across multiple milestones, suggesting the bugs are a process problem rather than a code quality problem. How do you run the conversation between QA, engineering, and production to actually fix the root cause?
9. A key outsource partner your studio depends on for a significant volume of art production has financial problems and may not be able to fulfil the contract. You've heard this through an informal channel. How do you validate the risk, when do you tell your team, and what is your contingency?
10. You're building a new multiplayer game for a client who has a strong product vision but limited games production experience. They want to be involved in every milestone decision and have approval rights on content. How do you structure the relationship so they feel in control without their involvement becoming the single biggest risk to the schedule?

**Leadership**
1. How do you build a production department from scratch? What roles do you hire first — internal producers, outsource managers, project coordinators? How do you define what production culture looks like before those people arrive?
2. You need to restructure a team mid-project: moving a senior engineer off a struggling feature, shifting an artist between projects, potentially letting a contractor go. Walk us through how you approach these decisions and the communication around them.
3. Post-mortems at most studios produce documents that nobody reads and nothing changes. How do you run a post-mortem that actually drives improvement in the next milestone or project?
4. How do you develop the producers on your team? What does deliberate career development look like at EP level — are you running one-to-ones, creating stretch assignments, sponsoring people for leadership opportunities? What specifically do you do, and what have you produced?
5. A lead producer on your team is technically excellent but struggles to give difficult feedback, avoids conflict, and as a result has a team with unresolved interpersonal issues and silently failing processes. How do you develop them — or at what point do you conclude the role isn't right for them?
6. You're the most senior production person in the studio. There is no production lead above you to escalate to. When you're uncertain, overwhelmed, or wrong, how do you find the perspective and support you need without destabilising the team's confidence in production leadership?
7. Describe a time you had to make a decision that was commercially correct but personally uncomfortable — cutting a feature you believed in, ending a contractor engagement, overruling a lead you respected. How did you make the decision and live with it?
8. How do you manage a studio-wide change — a new process, a new tool, a shift in how milestones are structured — when you know there will be genuine resistance from experienced people who have valid reasons for their scepticism?
9. What does your approach to hiring look like at the producer level? How do you assess cultural fit, production instinct, and the ability to work in a remote-first studio specifically, rather than relying on experience from a traditional co-located environment?
10. When you look at the production failures you've seen across your career — projects that shipped late, went over budget, lost key people, or shipped broken — what do they have in common, and what specifically do you do differently now as a result?

**Depth**
1. You inherit a project that is six months behind schedule with low team morale and a publisher losing patience. What is your 30-day plan? Be specific about what you assess, what you change, and what you deliberately leave alone.
2. Walk us through building a multi-year development plan for a new multiplayer game from concept through live service. How do you phase development, where are the critical decision gates, and how do you plan for the live ops team you'll need post-launch?
3. You've shipped and now you're running a live service. Content cadence is slipping, the team is fatigued, and player retention is declining. How do you restructure production for sustainable live ops without burning out the team that just crunched to ship?
4. Describe how you would structure production for a 55-person remote multiplayer studio split across two countries, with a game in production and a live game in maintenance simultaneously. How do you organise the production team, what systems do you put in place, and how do you prevent the live game from consuming all available resource?
5. A project you're overseeing has been in pre-production for ten months. The prototype is technically impressive but the core loop has not been validated with players, the team is growing restless, and leadership is asking whether the concept is actually viable. How do you structure the conversation and what production levers do you use to force a decision?
6. You're EP on a game that ships, performs well critically, but underperforms commercially. The studio needs to make decisions about the sequel, the team, and the live service investment. Walk us through how you approach the six months after launch: the analysis, the conversations, the decisions, and what you'd preserve versus wind down.
7. Describe how you've built or significantly improved a production function at a studio — not just the processes, but the people, the tools, the culture, and the relationship between production and the rest of development. What did it look like when you arrived and what did it look like when you left or when the project shipped?
8. Your studio has been acquired. The new parent company has its own production methodology, reporting cadence, and tooling preferences. Some of it is better than what you're doing; some of it is worse; some is just different. You need to integrate without losing what makes your studio's production culture effective.
