# Specialist Role Interview Questions

50 questions per role, 10 per category (Culture, Technical, Collaboration, Leadership, Depth).
Context: Couch Heroes — 55-person fully remote game studio, UK and Greece, building a multiplayer title.

---

### CTO

**Culture**
1. You are joining a 55-person remote studio split between the UK and Greece. Engineering is roughly 15-20 people. How do you build a shared engineering culture — coding standards, review norms, architectural principles — across two countries and multiple time zones, without being physically present?
2. Game studios often treat tech debt as something to fix "after we ship," except there is always another milestone. Walk us through a real situation where you had to make a hard call between shipping on time and addressing technical risk that could bite you in live operations. What did you decide and what happened?
3. At a studio this size, the CTO is still close enough to the codebase to have strong opinions about implementation. How do you decide when to be in the code versus when to step back? How has that boundary shifted as teams you have led have grown?
4. Engineering talent in the games industry is fiercely competitive. How do you create an engineering environment that retains senior talent who could easily leave for a FAANG salary? What makes a studio worth staying at beyond compensation?
5. The games industry has a complicated relationship with crunch. As CTO, how do you set engineering norms around working hours and sustainability while still meeting milestone commitments that have real financial consequences?
6. How do you handle a situation where the engineering team disagrees with a studio-wide decision — for example, a tool choice or a process imposed by production? Do you shield the team, advocate upward, comply, or something else?
7. Tell me about a time an engineer on your team publicly embarrassed the company — a bad conference talk, an ill-judged blog post, a Twitter argument with a competitor. How did you handle it, and what did it teach you about where the line is between encouraging visibility and protecting the studio?
8. You are inheriting an engineering team with existing leads, existing norms, and existing relationships. How do you earn credibility as a new CTO without dismantling what the team has built before you arrived?
9. Remote engineering teams can become siloed — backend engineers never talk to gameplay engineers, tools engineers are forgotten. How do you create cross-team visibility and shared identity across engineering subdisciplines?
10. Your studio ships a multiplayer update and within two hours a server-side bug is wiping player progression data. The engineer who wrote the code is in the room. The community manager is asking for a statement. The CEO is asking how this happened. Walk me through the next four hours — what you do, what you say to each of those people, and how you handle the engineer who is visibly devastated.

**Technical**
1. Our game is a multiplayer title targeting PC and console. Walk us through the key architectural decisions you would make in the first 90 days: engine choice, networking topology, backend services, build infrastructure. How do you make these decisions stick when the team will live with them for three to five years?
2. Platform certification (Sony TRC, Microsoft XR, Nintendo Lotcheck) is a gating function that can delay a launch by weeks. How do you build cert compliance into the development process from day one rather than treating it as a pre-launch checklist? What are the most common cert failures you have seen, and how do you prevent them?
3. A multiplayer game as a service needs backend infrastructure that handles matchmaking, player progression, live events, analytics, and server orchestration. Third-party backend platforms promise to handle all of this, but they introduce vendor lock-in, opaque pricing changes, and the risk of a critical dependency shutting down or pivoting. For a studio of our size, how do you evaluate whether to build core backend services in-house versus accepting that dependency? What has burned you or a team you have worked with in either direction?
4. Describe your approach to engine architecture for a multiplayer game that needs to support both PvP and PvE content with potentially different networking models. How do you avoid building two separate games under one roof?
5. Security in a live multiplayer game is an ongoing battle — cheating, data breaches, DDoS attacks, account theft. How do you build a security posture that is proportionate to a 55-person studio's resources without leaving critical vulnerabilities open?
6. Walk us through how you would design the data pipeline for a live service game — from client telemetry through ingestion, storage, and surfacing insights to the design and production teams. What do you instrument from day one versus what can wait?
7. How do you approach cross-platform play technically? What are the networking, identity, and certification challenges of shipping a game that works across PC and multiple consoles simultaneously?
8. Describe your experience with server infrastructure scaling. How do you design backend services that handle launch-day spikes, regional distribution, and cost efficiency during off-peak hours? What cloud providers and orchestration tools have you used?
9. How do you approach build infrastructure for a game project? A full build taking 90 minutes kills engineering velocity. Walk us through your ideal CI/CD pipeline — build times, test automation, deployment gates, and artifact management.
10. Describe your approach to live operations from a technical perspective. When the game is running and players are online, how do you deploy updates, manage hotfixes, handle rollbacks, and communicate technical incidents to the team and to players?

**Collaboration**
1. The Game Director wants a feature that requires server-authoritative physics simulation for 50+ players. Your lead network engineer says it is technically possible but will require six months of R&D with uncertain outcomes. How do you facilitate that conversation between creative ambition and technical reality without becoming the "no" person?
2. External partners — platform holders, middleware vendors, outsource studios — each have their own SDK timelines and technical requirements. Describe how you manage these relationships so that external dependencies do not become internal blockers. Give a specific example.
3. The CEO asks: "Why is this taking so long? It is just a matchmaking system." How do you explain technical complexity to non-technical leadership in a way that builds trust rather than creating an adversarial dynamic?
4. As CTO you sit between engineering and every other discipline. How do you ensure that art, design, and production have realistic expectations about what engineering can deliver without creating a culture of defensive estimation?
5. How do you work with the Head of Production on capacity planning? Engineering time is the most contested resource in a game studio. Describe how you negotiate allocation between features, tech debt, tools, and infrastructure.
6. When a vendor relationship is not working — their SDK has critical bugs, their support is slow, or their pricing has changed — how do you manage the transition to an alternative without disrupting the development schedule?
7. Describe how you collaborate with the QA Lead to establish engineering quality standards. What role should QA play in engineering processes beyond end-of-pipeline testing?
8. How do you handle the relationship between your in-house engineering team and external development partners or outsource studios? What technical governance do you put in place to ensure consistency?
9. The Art Director says the character pipeline is too slow because of engine limitations. The Lead Engineer says the art is not optimised for the target hardware. How do you mediate a cross-discipline technical dispute where both sides have valid points?
10. How do you communicate engineering strategy to the board or investors? Describe how you translate technical investment into business value for an audience that cares about ship dates and revenue, not architecture.

**Leadership**
1. How do you structure an engineering organisation of 15-20 people? What teams do you create, how do you define ownership, and how do you handle the tension between discipline-based teams (gameplay, backend, tools) and feature-based teams?
2. Describe your approach to hiring engineers for a game studio. What do you test in interviews that is specific to game engineering versus general software engineering? How do you evaluate someone's ability to work in a game codebase?
3. How do you develop engineering leads? Not everyone wants to be a manager. Describe how you create growth paths for both people who want to lead teams and people who want to go deeper technically.
4. When you need to make a technology decision that the team will disagree with — for example, switching engines, adopting a new middleware, or rewriting a core system — how do you make the call and bring the team along?
5. Describe a time you inherited a codebase with significant architectural problems. How did you assess the situation, prioritise what to fix, and communicate the plan without demoralising the engineers who built it?
6. How do you evaluate engineering performance? Code output is a poor proxy. What do you actually look at, and how do you handle an engineer who is technically excellent but not contributing to the team's overall velocity?
7. Describe your approach to engineering budgets. How do you make the case for tools, infrastructure, and middleware investment when the studio's priority is always "more features, faster"?
8. When a live service game has a critical outage at 2am on a Saturday, what is your incident response process? How do you build an on-call culture that is sustainable in a studio that values work-life balance?
9. How do you keep your own technical skills sharp while carrying CTO-level responsibilities? What is your approach to staying hands-on enough to make good decisions without becoming a bottleneck?
10. The studio wants to grow engineering from 18 to 30 people in the next year. How do you scale the team without breaking velocity, culture, or code quality? What does your hiring plan look like and what organisational changes do you make along the way?

**Depth**
1. You are hired as CTO of Couch Heroes. The game is in early production, the engineering team has 18 people, the tech stack was chosen before you arrived, and there are some architectural decisions you disagree with. Walk us through your first 90 days: what you assess, what you change, what you leave alone, and how you communicate with the team and studio leadership throughout.
2. Design the complete server architecture for a multiplayer game that needs to support 50-player sessions, persistent player progression, live events, a cosmetic shop, matchmaking, and analytics. Walk us through each component: what you build in-house, what you buy, how you handle scaling, and how you plan for a live service that runs for years.
3. The game is six months from launch and the backend is not meeting its target performance — server tick rate drops under load, matchmaking has intermittent failures, and the player save system has data integrity issues. You cannot delay launch. Walk us through how you triage, prioritise, and execute a technical recovery plan while keeping the team functional.
4. Platform holders (Sony, Microsoft, Nintendo) each have their own certification requirements, SDK schedules, and technical relationship managers. Walk us through how you manage simultaneous multi-platform development and certification for a live service multiplayer game. What goes wrong and how do you prevent it?
5. The studio is evaluating whether to continue with the current game engine or migrate to another. Both options have significant trade-offs. Walk us through your evaluation framework: what criteria you assess, how you quantify the cost of migration versus the cost of staying, how you involve the team in the decision, and how you present the recommendation to the CEO and board.
6. Design a technical hiring process for game engineers that effectively evaluates candidates for multiplayer game development. Walk us through each stage — from job description through technical screens, practical assessments, and final interview. What specifically do you test for that generic software engineering interviews miss?
7. You discover that the engineering team has accumulated significant technical debt in the networking layer — workarounds, hardcoded values, poorly documented state machines. The team knows it but has never had time to address it. How do you build a sustainable tech debt reduction programme that does not require pausing feature development?
8. The studio wants to build a modding or user-generated content system for the multiplayer game. Walk us through the technical architecture: what you expose to modders, how you sandbox user content for security and stability in a multiplayer context, how you handle versioning, and how you moderate content.
9. A competitor has shipped a similar multiplayer game with a feature set that significantly exceeds yours technically — better netcode, better load times, better server stability. The CEO is alarmed. Walk us through how you assess the competitive technical gap, communicate it honestly, and build a plan that is realistic rather than reactionary.
10. Walk us through how you would build the engineering team's live operations capability from scratch — monitoring, alerting, incident response, post-mortem processes, deployment automation, and player communication during outages. What do you need in place before launch, and what can evolve after?

### Head of Finance

**Culture**
1. Couch Heroes is a 55-person remote game studio where people care deeply about making games, not spreadsheets. How do you build a finance function that the team sees as enabling their work rather than controlling it?
2. Game development has unpredictable timelines and scope changes. How do you bring financial rigour to an environment where the creative team pushes back on anything that feels like corporate overhead?
3. You are the first dedicated finance hire in a studio that has been managed on founder intuition. How do you introduce financial processes without overwhelming a team that has never had them?
4. Remote work across the UK and Greece means dealing with multiple currencies, tax jurisdictions, and employment structures. How have you managed financial operations across borders before, and what surprised you about it?
5. The games industry operates on long investment cycles with uncertain returns. How do you stay pragmatic about financial discipline while understanding that the product is inherently speculative?
6. How do you communicate financial health to a team that is not financially literate? What do people at a game studio need to understand about the company's finances, and what is none of their business?
7. Studio founders often mix personal spending with business spending, or make financial commitments without understanding the full cost. How do you handle that diplomatically while protecting the company's financial integrity?
8. You discover that a well-liked senior developer has been expensing personal items — not large amounts, maybe 200 pounds over several months. They are a critical hire the studio cannot afford to lose. What do you do, and what does your answer tell us about how you balance relationships with integrity?
9. A team lead accidentally shares the company's runway figure in a Slack channel visible to the whole studio. People are worried. The CEO is furious at the leak. How do you handle the next 48 hours — the team's anxiety, the CEO's anger, and the question of whether the information should have been shared more openly in the first place?
10. When a project is over budget and leadership wants to keep going, how do you present the financial reality without being the person who kills the dream? Walk us through a real situation.

**Technical**
1. What accounting systems and financial tools have you used for companies of this size? Describe your ideal finance tech stack for a 55-person remote studio with UK and Greek entities.
2. Walk us through how you would set up financial reporting for a game studio. What reports does the CEO need monthly, what does the board need quarterly, and what does the team never need to see?
3. Describe your experience with R&D tax credits for game development. What qualifies, how do you structure claims, and what documentation practices do you put in place from day one?
4. How do you approach revenue recognition for a game that uses in-app purchases, season passes, and virtual currency? What accounting standards apply and what are the common pitfalls?
5. Walk us through how you build a financial model for a game studio. What are the key assumptions, cost drivers, and revenue scenarios? How do you model the gap between development costs and first revenue?
6. Describe your experience with payroll across multiple jurisdictions. How do you manage UK PAYE, Greek social contributions, and contractor payments in a single finance function?
7. How do you approach cash flow management for a studio that has long development cycles and lumpy revenue? What reserves do you maintain and how do you forecast runway?
8. Walk us through your approach to transfer pricing and intercompany transactions between a UK parent and a Greek subsidiary. What structures have you seen work, and what creates problems?
9. You are preparing for the studio's first external audit and you discover that the previous year's books have inconsistencies — intercompany transactions between the UK and Greek entities that do not reconcile, contractor payments categorised inconsistently, and R&D tax credit claims that may not withstand scrutiny. The audit is in six weeks. Walk me through how you triage, what you fix versus what you disclose, and how you prevent the CEO from panicking.
10. How do you approach financial controls for a growing company? What segregation of duties and approval workflows are essential at 55 people versus what becomes necessary at 100?

**Collaboration**
1. How do you work with producers on project budgets? Game production estimates are notoriously unreliable. How do you build a budget that is useful without being fiction?
2. Describe how you collaborate with HR on compensation planning. How do you translate market benchmarking data into a compensation framework that the studio can afford and talent will accept?
3. When engineering wants to buy expensive middleware or infrastructure, how do you evaluate the request? What financial analysis do you do beyond just checking whether there is budget?
4. How do you work with the CEO and board on fundraising or investment decisions? Describe your role in preparing financial materials for investors or publishers.
5. The studio is negotiating a publishing deal. What financial terms do you focus on, what do you push back on, and how do you model the economics of different deal structures?
6. Your external accountant has been filing the studio's VAT returns and managing year-end compliance for two years. You join and discover they have been applying the wrong VAT treatment to digital services revenue, creating a potential liability. They are a long-standing relationship of the CEO. How do you handle the correction, the conversation with the CEO, and the decision about whether to keep the firm?
7. The art director has overspent their outsource budget by 40% because they approved additional character variants without checking with you. The work is already done and delivered — you cannot claw it back. The CEO does not know yet. How do you handle this: the conversation with the art director, the disclosure to the CEO, and the system you put in place so it does not happen again without making every discipline lead feel like they need your permission to spend?
8. The studio is about to sign a publishing deal. The publisher's contract includes a recoupment clause, a revenue split that changes at thresholds, platform fee deductions before the split, and a marketing spend commitment that comes off the studio's share. The CEO thinks it is a good deal. Walk me through the financial analysis you would do and what red flags you would raise before signing.
9. Describe how you support the studio's hiring decisions from a financial perspective. When a lead says they need three more people, what analysis do you do before agreeing?
10. How do you handle a situation where the founders disagree about financial priorities — one wants to invest in growth, the other wants to conserve cash? What role does finance play in resolving that?

**Leadership**
1. How do you build a finance function from scratch in a company that has been operating without one? What do you put in place in the first month, the first quarter, and the first year?
2. Describe your approach to financial planning for a company with two revenue streams: client services work (consulting) and product development (the game). How do you allocate costs and measure profitability for each?
3. When the studio needs to cut costs, how do you identify where to cut without damaging the team's ability to ship? What is your framework for distinguishing essential spend from discretionary spend?
4. How do you approach scenario planning for a game studio? What does your "base case," "upside," and "downside" financial model look like, and how do you decide which scenario to plan against?
5. Describe your experience with grant funding, tax incentives, or government programmes relevant to game development. What have you successfully accessed and how did you structure the application?
6. How do you measure and communicate the studio's financial health beyond basic P&L? What metrics matter for a game studio that might not matter for a typical SME?
7. When the board asks for financial projections for a game that has not launched yet, how do you build credible forecasts? What comparable data do you use and how transparent are you about the uncertainty?
8. Describe how you would handle discovering a financial irregularity — misclassified expenses, undeclared benefits, or potential fraud. What is your process from discovery through investigation to resolution?
9. How do you build financial literacy across the studio? What do department heads need to understand to manage their budgets effectively?
10. The studio is considering taking external investment. Walk us through how you prepare the company financially — cleaning up the books, structuring the entity, preparing the data room. What do investors typically scrutinise?

**Depth**
1. The studio has a UK entity and a Greek entity with 35 and 20 employees respectively. The UK entity funds the Greek entity. Walk us through how you structure the intercompany relationship, manage transfer pricing, handle VAT, and ensure compliance in both jurisdictions. What are the tax optimisation opportunities and what are the risks?
2. Build a financial model for a multiplayer game with a three-year development cycle, projected launch revenue from sales and in-app purchases, and ongoing live service costs. Walk us through your assumptions for development costs, marketing spend, platform fees, server costs, and revenue ramp. How do you model the breakeven point?
3. The studio has been offered a publishing deal with a 70/30 revenue split after recoupment of a $5M development advance, or alternatively a co-development deal with a platform holder that provides $3M in funding but requires platform exclusivity for 12 months. Walk us through your financial analysis of both options. What additional information do you need, and which do you recommend?
4. The studio needs to raise additional funding to complete the game. Options include venture capital, publisher funding, government grants, and debt financing. Walk us through the financial implications of each: dilution, control, repayment terms, and impact on the company's capital structure. What do you recommend for a studio of this size and stage?
5. You discover that the studio's Greek entity has been misclassifying some contractors as employees (or vice versa) and there is potential exposure to back taxes, penalties, and reclassification claims. Walk us through how you assess the exposure, engage legal counsel, remediate the situation, and prevent recurrence.
6. Design the annual budgeting process for the studio. Walk us through how you gather input from department heads, reconcile competing priorities, build the consolidated budget, get board approval, and track actuals against budget through the year. What format and cadence works for a creative studio?
7. The studio is growing and needs to decide between remaining bootstrapped with client services revenue cross-subsidising game development, or raising a Series A to fund dedicated game development. Walk us through the financial analysis you would prepare for the CEO to make this decision.
8. A key revenue stream — the client services business — is declining because Glen is spending more time on Couch Heroes. Walk us through how you model the impact, forecast the cash flow gap, and present options to the founders: hire to backfill, wind down services, or restructure the business.
9. Walk us through how you would set up a financial dashboard for the CEO and board. What metrics do you display, what cadence do you update, and how do you handle the tension between transparency and not overwhelming non-financial stakeholders with data?
10. The game launches and underperforms initial projections by 40%. The live service has ongoing costs (servers, content team, platform fees) but revenue is not covering them. Walk us through the financial analysis: at what point do you recommend scaling back live service investment, what does a "minimum viable live ops" budget look like, and how do you present the options to leadership?

### HR Operations

**Culture**
1. Couch Heroes has a solo HR person (Lorenza) who has been managing everything from recruitment to compliance for a 55-person remote team. You are joining to professionalise operations. How do you complement her work without undermining what she has built?
2. The studio operates across the UK and Greece with different employment laws, holiday entitlements, and cultural expectations. How do you build HR processes that feel fair and consistent to everyone despite the legal asymmetries?
3. Remote HR operations means you cannot read the room — you cannot see who looks stressed, who is eating lunch alone, who is staying late. How do you maintain an accurate pulse on team wellbeing without invasive monitoring?
4. Game studios often resist "corporate HR" processes because they associate them with large publishers that impose bureaucracy. How do you introduce necessary structure while respecting the studio's independent identity?
5. Couch Heroes is growing. What HR processes that work for 55 people will break at 80, and what do you put in place now to prepare for that growth without over-engineering for today?
6. How do you handle the cultural differences between UK and Greek work norms? Different attitudes to hierarchy, communication style, holiday patterns, and employment expectations. How do you create unity without forcing uniformity?
7. The games industry has a retention problem — people leave for competitors, burnout is real, and senior talent gets poached constantly. What HR operational practices actually improve retention beyond competitive salary?
8. A manager asks you to share an employee's personal circumstances — a health issue, a family situation — because they think it explains the employee's recent performance. The employee told you in confidence. What do you do, and where do you think the line is between supporting the manager and protecting the employee?
9. You are writing the studio's first proper employee handbook. The CEO wants it to feel 'startup cool' — informal tone, minimal rules. The employment lawyer says it needs specific clauses to protect the company. An employee in Greece points out that the UK-centric draft feels exclusionary. How do you navigate these three competing pressures, and which one do you prioritise if you cannot satisfy all three?
10. When an employee has a problem with their manager but does not want to file a formal complaint, how do you handle it operationally? What systems do you put in place for informal resolution?

**Technical**
1. What HRIS platforms have you implemented for distributed teams of this size? Walk us through your evaluation criteria and what you would recommend for a UK-Greece remote studio.
2. Describe your experience setting up onboarding workflows for remote employees. What happens before day one, during the first week, and during the first 90 days? How much of this is automatable?
3. Walk us through how you manage leave and absence tracking across UK and Greek employment law. Different statutory minimums, different public holidays, different sick pay rules. How do you make this work in a single system?
4. Describe your approach to building an employee handbook for a remote games studio. What sections are legally required, what sections are culturally important, and what do you deliberately leave out?
5. How do you manage the contractor-versus-employee classification across jurisdictions? What are the risks of misclassification in the UK and Greece, and what operational controls do you put in place?
6. The studio has never had a formal performance review. Creative leads say reviews will "kill the vibe." Engineers want clear promotion criteria. The CEO wants something lightweight. Design a review process that actually works for a 55-person remote game studio — and explain how you would handle the first cycle when some people discover they are not performing as well as they thought.
7. Describe your experience with benefits administration across borders. What benefits can you harmonise across UK and Greece, and what must be different? How do you communicate a fair total rewards package when the components differ?
8. The CEO asks you why turnover is high in the Greek office. You pull the data and discover it is not actually high by industry standards — it is two departures in six months from a team of 20, both voluntary, both for higher-paying roles. But the perception of a problem is real and affecting morale. How do you present the data honestly while addressing the emotional reality that the team feels unstable?
9. You review the existing employment contracts and discover that the Greek contracts are missing IP assignment clauses, three UK contractors should probably be classified as employees, and one senior hire's non-compete is likely unenforceable. The studio has been operating like this for two years. How do you prioritise remediation, and how do you handle the conversation with the CEO about the exposure?
10. The studio wants to hire a brilliant technical artist who is based in Turkey. There is no entity in Turkey, the candidate cannot relocate, and the lead says they are the only person who can do the job. The options are contractor arrangement, employer of record, or asking them to set up a company and invoice. Each has compliance, tax, and IP risks. Walk me through how you advise the CEO and what you recommend.

**Collaboration**
1. How do you work with hiring managers who have never written a job description, never conducted a structured interview, and never made a formal offer? Describe how you enable them without doing their job for them.
2. Describe how you collaborate with the existing HR person (who has been doing everything alone) to divide responsibilities. How do you avoid stepping on toes while bringing new capability?
3. How do you partner with finance on headcount planning, compensation budgets, and payroll operations? What information flows between HR ops and finance, and at what cadence?
4. A lead engineer wants to put a mid-level programmer on a performance improvement plan. You investigate and discover the engineer's work is fine — the real issue is a personality clash. The lead is framing a relationship problem as a performance problem. How do you redirect the conversation without undermining the lead's authority, and what do you do if the lead insists?
5. The studio's Greek payroll provider has made an error that resulted in three employees being underpaid for two months. The provider blames a miscommunication about a regulatory change. The employees are upset. The provider is the only one in the market who handles your specific setup. How do you resolve the immediate problem, manage the employee relationships, and decide whether to keep or replace the provider?
6. How do you support production leads through difficult people situations — team conflict, underperformance, personal issues affecting work? What is the boundary between HR support and line management?
7. When the CEO wants to make a people decision quickly — a new hire, a salary increase, a role change — and you need to slow them down because of compliance or process reasons, how do you handle it?
8. An employee is terminated and you discover 48 hours later that their Perforce access, GitHub access, and Slack account are all still active — IT was never notified because the offboarding checklist did not exist. The terminated employee was working on unreleased game content. Walk me through how you handle the immediate security risk and what you build to prevent it from happening again.
9. How do you handle requests from employees that are reasonable but fall outside existing policy — for example, an extended leave request, a remote work arrangement from a different country, or a flexible schedule? What is your decision-making framework?
10. A Greek employee raises a complaint that their UK-based manager makes culturally insensitive comments during team calls — not overtly offensive, but consistently dismissive of Greek work norms around holidays and communication style. The manager thinks they are joking. The employee does not want a formal grievance. You need to resolve this across two jurisdictions with different employment law frameworks and cultural expectations. Walk me through your approach.

**Leadership**
1. How do you prioritise HR operational improvements when everything needs attention? The onboarding is inconsistent, the handbook is outdated, the performance process does not exist, and compliance documentation has gaps. What do you fix first and why?
2. Describe your approach to building HR processes that scale. What works at 55 people that needs to be redesigned at 80 or 100? How do you build with growth in mind without over-engineering?
3. When you discover a compliance gap — for example, right-to-work checks that were not completed, or employment contracts missing required clauses — how do you remediate without creating panic?
4. How do you measure whether HR operations are actually working? What metrics indicate that your processes are serving the studio versus just existing on paper?
5. Describe your experience with redundancy or restructuring processes. How do you manage the legal requirements across jurisdictions, the communication to affected employees, and the impact on the remaining team?
6. How do you build trust with employees who see HR as "management's enforcers"? What specific actions demonstrate that HR operations serve the team, not just the company?
7. The studio is growing from 55 to 80 people over six months. You are the only HR ops person. The CEO says "hire when you need to" but will not proactively approve headcount until you are visibly drowning. How do you build the case before you are underwater, and what do you do if the answer is still no — do you keep absorbing, lower your standards, or draw a line?
8. Describe your approach to succession planning for critical roles. In a 55-person studio, losing one key person can be devastating. How do you identify and mitigate single points of failure?
9. A new UK employment regulation comes into effect that requires changes to how the studio handles flexible working requests. The Greek team assumes it applies to them too and starts making requests. The regulation does not apply in Greece, but refusing the Greek team's requests while granting the UK team's will create a perception of unfairness. How do you handle a situation where legal compliance and perceived fairness are in direct conflict?
10. You introduce a mandatory probation check-in process for new hires. Six months later, you discover that most managers are treating the check-ins as rubber stamps — they fill in the form but do not have genuine conversations. Two people who should have been flagged during probation are now underperforming with no documentation trail. The process exists but it is not working. What do you do — fix the process, fix the managers, or accept that some processes will be gamed?

**Depth**
1. Design the complete onboarding process for a new employee joining Couch Heroes — from offer acceptance through their first 90 days. Cover UK and Greek variations, remote setup, equipment provisioning, access management, team introductions, probation milestones, and feedback checkpoints. What does a world-class remote onboarding look like for a game studio?
2. The studio is opening a new role in a country where it has no entity — say, Germany or Spain. Walk us through the operational options: employer of record, contractor arrangement, entity setup. What are the HR operational implications of each, and what do you recommend for a single hire?
3. An employee in Greece raises a grievance against their UK-based manager. The allegation is serious enough to require investigation but not criminal. Walk us through the operational process: who investigates, what documentation is required, what interim measures do you take, and how do you handle the cross-border jurisdictional complexity?
4. Design the annual compensation review process for a 55-person studio with employees in two countries. Walk us through data gathering, benchmarking, budget allocation, manager calibration, communication to employees, and handling the inevitable appeals from people who feel undervalued.
