# Estimate Inflation Control: "Shenanigans" Culture and Scope Capitulation Prevention

## Key Content

Two interconnected production problems and their solutions, observed at a ~55-person game studio entering early production:

**Estimate inflation:**
- Estimates balloon when unchallenged. One lead's vertical slice estimate went from 1,600 days to 800 days to approximately 3 months when challenged in sequence -- the final number was the real one.
- Art estimates specifically default to "fully shipped" quality rather than prototype tiers, inflating all downstream planning.
- Fix: codify tier language (e.g. tier 2/3/4) agreed across all disciplines so "done" means the same thing everywhere.
- Studio adopted "shenanigans" as the call-out word for inflated claims -- a non-confrontational signal that forces justification without personal conflict.
- Parallel model: Gary's blockout stages (a visual, staged approach to level art) became the template others are asked to replicate for their discipline.

**Scope capitulation:**
- Design intent is quietly altered in one-off conversations without visibility to leadership. The scope change is only discovered after the deadline slip.
- Fix: stop capitulating at the point of pressure. The correct response is to state a constraint explicitly ("I need another month if you want X") so it can be escalated to producers, interrogated, and either accepted or rejected by leadership.
- Shared document logging scope "flashpoints" gives leadership a monitoring layer without requiring them to attend every conversation.

## Decisions / Insights

- Glen concluded: estimate challenges must be sequential and persistent, not one-time. Leads will compress estimates under repeated pressure even after initial resistance.
- Glen decided: producers get a deadline (mid-next-week) to deliver combat broken into stages with blockers and timeline -- outputs then feed arena meetings with date-specific commitments.
- Glen observed: art estimates consistently banking to shipped quality is a systemic pattern, not individual error. Tier language fixes it at the definition level.

## Applicability

- Relevant when: a studio is entering production and estimates feel too large -- apply sequential challenge; the compression is almost always real.
- Relevant when: art or any creative discipline is inflating estimates -- check whether the estimate standard defaults to shipped quality rather than the intended prototype tier.
- Relevant when: scope drift is occurring in one-off meetings -- implement a flashpoint log and make escalation with a stated constraint the cultural norm.
- Relevant when: introducing a call-out culture for inflated claims -- a non-confrontational shared vocabulary ("shenanigans") reduces personal friction while maintaining accountability.
- Relevant when: planning arena or sprint meetings -- lock in date-specific commitments from each discipline, not open-ended "we're working on it" status.
