# Poisoned Phrase Problem: When Studio Terminology Carries Baggage

## Key Content
Studio leadership pattern: a phrase becomes toxic due to a prior failure associated with it. Using the phrase in any future context — even with different intent — triggers defensive or allergic reactions from leadership, regardless of merit.

**The pattern:**
- A term is coined around a specific initiative that consumes significant resource and fails
- Senior leadership (particularly founders/CEOs) develop a strong negative association with the term
- When someone later proposes a superficially similar effort using the same language, the reaction is rejection of the language, not evaluation of the substance
- Other team members may continue using the phrase unaware of its toxicity, creating misalignment

**Intervention approach:**
1. Identify the phrase is poisoned (note who introduced it originally and what the failure was)
2. Understand the underlying concept the stakeholder objects to vs. what they actually want
3. Find an alternative phrase that describes the desired outcome without referencing the failure
4. Introduce the reframed concept to the stakeholder proactively before others use the old language in front of them
5. Brief the broader team on the new language before the next cross-functional session

**Example structure:** A phrase associated with "months spent, build never ran" became taboo with a CEO. The underlying desire (a high-fidelity showcase space) remained valid. Reframe: "proof of completion" or "fully dressed map." Specific vehicle proposed: a contained, single-purpose display room that could demonstrate final art quality without being tied to a gameplay area.

## Decisions / Insights
- Glen decided: never use the original phrase with the CEO; use "proof of completion" or "fully dressed map" instead
- Pattern recognised: the guild hall (or equivalent contained space) is a reliable vehicle for demonstrating final quality without triggering scope-expansion concerns
- Glen concluded: the reframe must happen before someone else uses the old language in front of leadership — reactive reframing after the trigger is harder than proactive introduction

## Context
1:1 between Glen and a level design lead at a ~70-person MMO studio, 2026-06-22. The specific term had been associated with a failed multi-month initiative; the CEO had strong negative reactions to it. Glen identified the need to reframe before the next all-hands or investor session.
