# S-Curve Change Management Model for Studio Transformation

## Key Content
Glen's framework for managing structural change in a studio undergoing significant operational transformation:

**S-curve model:** Introduce a cluster of significant changes (personnel, process, structure) as a defined "curve." Allow the organisation to absorb and stabilise before the next curve. Repeat. By the third curve, the studio has built change tolerance and shifts into a sprint-walk rhythm -- capable of absorbing smaller improvements continuously without a full stabilisation pause between each.

Key principle: changes are batched, not drip-fed. Drip-feeding keeps the organisation in permanent disruption mode. Batching creates clear before/after states the team can orient to.

In application: the current curve at this studio includes major personnel changes (several exits, multiple senior hires), process resets (estimation methodology, pipeline health governance, scope governance), and leadership upgrades (EP, Head of Finance, People Partner). The next curve begins only after the studio has absorbed the first.

**Risk signal:** A new senior hire making independent structural changes during the stabilisation phase of a curve disrupts absorption and resets the timeline. This is an early-warning indicator, not a trivial friction.

## Decisions / Insights
- Glen concluded: S-curve batching is more effective than continuous incremental change for studios in transformation.
- Glen noted: a new senior hire making structural changes in their first two weeks is a disruption risk signal that threatens the current stabilisation phase.
- Dino's read: the S-curve model, if followed, positions the studio for a sprint-walk rhythm by the third curve -- roughly 12 months out.
