# Early Probation Termination: Grounds When Employee Declares Own Incapacity

## Key Content

Pattern derived from a CH level design hire resolved 17 June 2026. Anonymised.

**The mismatch pattern:**
A designer with strong creative instincts, world/quest design experience, and team management background was hired for a senior individual contributor role where ~60% of the core function (technical blockout work) was explicitly stated in the job description. Within three weeks, the hire admitted they had never performed that core function and could not do so. Simultaneously they expressed disagreement with the discipline lead's approach and began positioning for a lead role not offered.

**UK employment law position during probation:**
No right to unfair dismissal during a probation period. An employee's own verbal statement constituting a declaration of incapacity is the strongest possible documentation ground — it eliminates the need for an observation period. Additionally, if competencies were misrepresented during hiring (e.g., retroactively changed job titles on professional profiles, claims in interview not borne out in role), the grounds extend to misrepresentation at hire.

**Counter-risk:** Terminating very early (under 3 weeks of a 4-month probation) creates an argument that the probation period was not given proper time. Counter: the employee's own declaration removes the need for that observation. Legal alignment needed before proceeding.

**Documentation plan:**
- Chronological timeline: recruitment approach → role offered at level X → candidate accepted level Y → competencies claimed in interview → role requirements in signed contract → employee's own statements (verbatim from call)
- 2-3 pages of structured feedback with dates each issue was raised
- Include verbatim statements — the employee's own words are the core of the record

**Misalignment signal in the 1:1:**
The designer was more engaged when given unstructured creative work (prototype exploration, concept iteration) than structured blockout delivery. They wanted lead-level autonomy from a role that required individual technical contribution. This mismatch — creative generalist in a technical specialist role — emerged clearly within two weeks of start. Studios should probe this distinction during technical assessment, not just portfolio review.

## Decisions / Insights

- [Glen/Aris] decided: proceed with termination; employee's own declaration of incapacity is sufficient grounds
- [Glen] concluded: no unfair dismissal rights in UK probation — verbal declaration of incapacity is the documentation, not extended observation
- [Glen] observed: wide-coverage creative designers (quest, world, narrative, management) do not automatically transfer to technical blockout work — role descriptions must be tested in hire assessment, not assumed
- [Glen] observed: when a hire begins positioning for a higher role or challenging a peer lead within the first three weeks, it is a signal of misalignment, not ambition

## Applicability

Relevant when: a studio hires a generalist creative with management experience for a technical individual contributor role — the gap may not surface until probation begins.
Relevant when: a hire admits within the first month that they cannot perform the core technical function of the role — this is grounds for early probation exit, not a basis for role restructuring.
Relevant when: an employee has misrepresented technical competencies during hiring (post-hire retroactive profile changes, interview claims not borne out) — this strengthens the termination grounds beyond simple role-fit failure.
Relevant when: advising a studio on probation documentation — a chronological evidence record with verbatim statements is more valuable than a performance review form.
Relevant when: a new hire begins positioning for a more senior role or contradicting a peer lead within weeks of starting — address immediately; left unchecked it creates team confusion and undermines the existing lead.
