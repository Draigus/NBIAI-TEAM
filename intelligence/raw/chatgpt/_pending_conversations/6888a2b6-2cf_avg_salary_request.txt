## Role Breakdown and Sources

### Network Engineer (Contract) – Mid vs. Senior  
**Mid-level network engineers** contracting in games (e.g. those handling online infrastructure or multiplayer networking) typically earn **£400–£500 per day**【17†L89-L93】. This rate reflects solid demand for network skills in game development, as studios rely on stable online services. **Senior network engineers** (or highly specialized network architects) can command around **£600–£800+ per day**【17†L89-L93】. These higher rates align with broader tech contractor trends and reward extensive experience and certifications. In fact, industry data shows certifications like CCNP/CCIE can boost network engineering pay by **15–20%** on average【17†L125-L133】. Overall, network specialists remain valuable in gaming as multiplayer and live-service features expand, and companies often pay a premium for contractors who can “hit the ground running” on critical network projects【17†L117-L125】.

### Full Stack Engineer (Contract) – Mid vs. Senior  
Contracted **full stack engineers** in the games sector (e.g. working on game web services, tools, or infrastructure) see mid-level day rates around **£350–£450** on average. Recent UK job data puts the median full-stack contractor rate about **£358/day**【36†L25-L33】, with many mid-level postings in the £375–£425 range. **Senior full stack engineers** with strong experience are offered higher premiums, often **£600+ per day**. For example, a 6-month London contract for a senior full-stack developer was advertised at **£600–£640/day (inside IR35)**【41†L270-L278】. This reflects the high demand for seasoned developers who can design both front-end and back-end systems. Full stack roles in gaming are influenced by general tech market conditions – meaning skilled contractors can sometimes command rates comparable to other tech industries, especially if working on critical backend services or cloud-based game features. 

### Client/Feature Engineer (Contract) – Mid vs. Senior  
“Client/Feature Engineer” typically refers to a **gameplay or client-side developer** who implements game features. In the UK games industry, **mid-level gameplay engineers** on contract earn roughly **£300–£400 per day**. Industry salary surveys show a median of about **£320/day** for “game developer” contractors in recent months【44†L26-L34】. Senior or lead gameplay engineers can see higher rates in the **£450–£500/day** range, with top-tier contractors occasionally reaching ~£500+ for complex AAA projects【44†L42-L49】. It’s worth noting that historically, game programming roles have paid slightly lower than some enterprise software roles; however, skilled game engineers are still in demand. Recent trends (e.g. the push for more content updates and live ops in games) mean experienced feature developers are valued for short-term contracts to deliver specific gameplay systems. Additionally, studios facing hiring freezes or uncertain project scopes often turn to contractors for flexibility, which can sustain or increase day rates for these specialized engineers. 

## Industry Trends Impacting Contractor Pay  
**Industry-wide factors:** The games sector has seen significant shifts in 2024–2025, including layoffs and a rise in outsourcing【53†L249-L258】. Large studios have become cautious about permanent headcount, sometimes preferring contractors or external teams for certain pipelines (especially art, QA, etc.)【53†L249-L258】. **Core engineering roles** like networking and full-stack development, however, are less easily outsourced, keeping their contractors in steady demand. As a result, experienced engineers in these areas often maintain strong bargaining power on pay. 

**IR35 and contract terms:** In the UK, many game studios engage contractors via umbrella companies or inside IR35 arrangements. The day rates quoted above are **gross (pre-tax) figures** – contractors may take home less after taxes and fees, but studios often adjust pay upward to offset this. For example, a senior contract might be advertised at £600+ inside IR35【41†L270-L278】, reflecting the need to remain attractive despite the tax implications. Contractors should thus consider the **net** pay, but the **gross market rates** remain a useful benchmark for comparison. 

**Skills demand:** Lastly, specific skills can drive higher rates. For network engineers, expertise in cloud networking and security (AWS, Cloudflare, etc.) or low-latency MMO infrastructure can justify top-end pay. Full stack developers with knowledge of modern frameworks and scalable architecture are highly sought after in gaming backends. Gameplay/feature engineers who can work across engines (Unity, Unreal) or implement complex systems (AI, networking, physics) might negotiate above-average rates due to their niche expertise. In summary, **contractor salaries in the UK games industry are healthy**, especially for senior talent, and tend to follow overall tech trends while reflecting the unique demands of game development【17†L89-L93】【44†L26-L34】. The **passion-driven nature** of the industry can temper salaries slightly versus finance or fintech, but ongoing competition for skilled engineers (and the rise of remote work opportunities) is gradually narrowing that gap, ensuring that mid-level and senior contractors are well-compensated for their contributions.
