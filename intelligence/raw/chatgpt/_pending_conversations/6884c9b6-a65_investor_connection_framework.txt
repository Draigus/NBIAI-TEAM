## 2. Studio & Game Readiness – Investor Questions and Assessment Tool

Before reaching out, a studio must *think like an investor*. This section provides an **investigation process**: a set of key questions and tasks to evaluate the game and studio from an investor’s perspective. By answering these questions, you can identify strengths and gaps. We also propose a **grading methodology** to score the readiness of the studio on each aspect and determine next steps. The goal is to ensure the studio meets common investor requirements – increasing the likelihood of securing investment. 

Investors will perform due diligence on several areas, so your self-assessment should cover all potential concerns. Below are major categories of investor questions, with specific questions and what “good” looks like. For each question, we suggest grading your answer on a simple scale (for example: 5 = excellent/ready, 3 = moderate/needs work, 1 = poor/not addressed). After scoring, any item below a threshold (say <3) should be improved before pitching. **Success means** most areas score strong (green), or at least no critical red flags (and a plan to mitigate any remaining risks). 

**a. Vision & Market Need:**  
- **What problem or player desire is your game addressing?** Investors want to know that your game fulfills a real market demand or niche. In other words, *why do players want this game?* Be specific – for example, *“There’s a growing audience for social-cooperative RPGs, but current offerings lack XYZ feature – our game fills that gap.”* A strong answer demonstrates a clear *value proposition* to players.  
- **How large and attractive is the market?** This refers to your game’s **target audience** and **market segment**. Investors will ask about your *TAM (Total Addressable Market)* – e.g. number of potential players or revenue in your genre globally【32†L81-L89】【32†L137-L145】. Provide research: *“Our target is the action-adventure console market (~50 million players worldwide, $X billion/year).”* Also mention growth trends (e.g. genre is growing, or players spending more time in similar games). A top-notch answer includes credible stats and shows you understand the gaming landscape. *Grade 5:* you’ve identified a sizable, growing market and positioned your game uniquely within it; *Grade 1:* unclear who the game is for or you claim “everyone is a potential player” without evidence.  
- **“Why now” – what market trends make this the right time for your game?** Timing can be critical【45†L215-L224】【45†L225-L233】. Maybe a new platform has emerged (VR, AR), a certain genre is on the rise, or tech advances (e.g. AI, crossplay) enable your concept now. If you’re too early or too late, investors may worry. A good answer might be: *“The popularity of esports is booming, and our game capitalizes on that trend with a competitive feature set – we’re entering as demand peaks, not after the market is saturated.”* Show you’ve thought about timing risks (e.g., if the market is crowded or if any external factors – platform changes, regulations – could impact you). 

**b. Product & Gameplay:**  
- **Do you have a clear vision of the game and its unique hook?** Before pitching, **ensure you can describe your game compellingly in one minute or less**【22†L87-L95】. Investors will ask *“What is the game and why is it special?”*. Practice a concise elevator pitch: genre, setting, core gameplay, and the unique selling point. Grade yourself on clarity: if an outsider struggles to understand or find it interesting, refine the vision. Investors see hundreds of pitches – a *clear, memorable hook* is crucial.  
- **Is there a playable demo or prototype? What does it demonstrate?** At early stages, a **prototype is often the single most convincing proof** of your game’s potential. Expect investors to request a demo build or at least a gameplay video. Ideally, you should have a **vertical slice** or prototype that shows the core gameplay loop and fun factor【22†L95-L103】. *Grade 5:* a polished, stable demo that quickly showcases the most interesting gameplay (within the first 1–2 minutes of play)【22†L95-L103】; *Grade 3:* a rough prototype or video that conveys the idea but needs explanation; *Grade 1:* no demo – just concept art or GDD (in this case, strongly consider creating at least a small playable sample before approaching investors). Investors will ask themselves *“Does the gameplay feel good and is it fun/engaging?”* – so gather feedback: have neutral testers play it and see if the intended experience comes across【22†L91-L99】. If your demo is lacking, work on improving it or simplifying the scope to better convey the core fun factor.  
- **Content and IP questions:** What is the **content roadmap** for the game? (Especially if it’s not a one-off: e.g. plans for updates, expansions, sequels, or user-generated content.) Do you own the **IP (Intellectual Property)** for all content and tech in the game? Investors will be cautious if, for example, you are using unlicensed IP or if a key technology belongs to someone else. Ensure you can answer IP ownership questions (owning your code, art, etc., or having proper licenses). If your game has unique technology (AI, engine modifications), be ready to highlight that as a proprietary advantage【32†L99-L107】. Conversely, if tech isn’t your focus (maybe it’s the game design/creative side), emphasize what makes the *experience* unique. A good answer might also mention any **proprietary tech or patents** you have, or how you leverage existing tech smartly.  

**c. Team & Execution Capability:**  
- **Who are the founders and key team members?** Outline each core team member’s *relevant experience* (game industry background, past titles shipped, specific domain expertise like art, programming, marketing)【36†L269-L277】【36†L270-L274】. - **Team Gaps and Plans:** No early team is perfect. Investors may ask: *“What are your team’s weaknesses or missing pieces, and how will you address them?”*【36†L269-L277】【36†L271-L274】. **e. Monetization & Financials:**  
- **What is the business model? - **Use of Funds (The Ask):** Investors will absolutely ask *“How much funding are you seeking, and what will you use it for?”*【36†L278-L286】【36†L312-L320】. **g. Exit Strategy (Long-Term Vision):**  
This is more relevant for VC-type investors (who eventually need a return via exit). They may ask: *“What’s your long-term plan? Do you aim to be acquired, or build a new independent publisher, etc.?”*. You don’t need an exact answer (startups evolve), but show you understand the end-game. Perhaps: *“Our vision is to build a franchise. If successful, potential acquirers could be larger publishers like X or platform holders like Y – there is precedent (e.g., studio Z was acquired by BigPub for $50M). We’ll keep that option open, while also preparing to scale as an independent studio if needed.”* If you’re not comfortable discussing exit, focus on the *vision*: e.g. *“We want to become the leading studio in the VR horror genre in 5 years.”* A big, ambitious vision can reassure investors that their upside is large if they back you. Grade this on ambition and coherence with the rest of your plan.

**Grading Methodology:** Create a checklist or spreadsheet with all the above questions grouped by category. For each question, write down your answer and score it 1 (poor), 3 (adequate), or 5 (excellent). For example:

- *Team Experience:* Founders have 10+ years in AAA studios – score 5 (strong).  
- *Prototype Fun:* Playtesters are confused by the game loop – score 2 (weak; needs iteration).  
- *Market Size:* Well-documented niche of 10M players – score 4 (good).  

After scoring, **highlight any low-scoring areas**. These are the concerns an investor would have – now you have a chance to address them *before* you pitch. For each weak point, decide an action: improve it or prepare a convincing explanation. For instance, if “no marketing expert” is a low score, plan to recruit a marketing advisor or co-founder (so you can tell investors that plan). If “no demo” is a low score, allocate time to build a small demo or at least a video trailer, since *having something to show is practically a requirement in game pitches*【22†L95-L103】. If a certain risk is high, maybe you can do a test now to reduce uncertainty (e.g. run a beta to get retention data).

Finally, when you feel you can honestly answer most questions with a confident “Yes” or a solid plan (as Elena Lobova of Achievers Hub puts it, if you can answer *“YES” to all the pre-pitch questions* about vision, demo, uniqueness, team, timeline, budget, etc.【22†L69-L77】【22†L75-L83】), then you are *investor-ready*. At that point, your self-assessment might “grade” as mostly green/high – meaning the studio is meeting the common criteria that make investors likely to say “yes.” This preparation and grading process effectively lets you **step into the investor’s shoes**, so there will be fewer surprises during actual pitches. 

*(Note: You can also use this question set during pitch practice. Have a colleague or advisor play the role of an investor and fire some of these questions at you. See if your answers are compelling. Iterate until they are. This mimics the Q&A in real investor meetings and ensures you’re prepared with clear, concise responses【32†L39-L47】【32†L54-L62】.)* 

## 3. Required Artifacts – What You Need to Raise Investment

Once the studio is prepared and questions answered, you must compile **professional artifacts** for the fundraising process. These are the materials investors expect to see before they can commit funding. Below is a list of key artifacts needed, each with details on how to tailor them to a game studio:

- **Pitch Deck (Presentation):** The pitch deck is the centerpiece of your fundraising toolkit. It should be a **concise (10–15 slides)** visual presentation of your game and business【22†L139-L147】. For game studios, a pitch deck typically includes:
  - **Intro Slide:** Studio name & logo, and a one-line tagline or vision for the game.
  - **Problem/Opportunity:** Briefly, what player need or market opportunity you identified (e.g. *“Players want a co-op experience in genre X, but current games don’t deliver – we will.”*).
  - **Solution/Game Concept:** Screenshots, concept art, or short GIFs of your game. **Use visuals liberally** – show the art style, characters, or a key scene. Games are a visual medium, and a static wall of text will lose investor interest【22†L139-L147】. Keep text minimal and let images/videos illustrate the gameplay.
  - **Market & Competition:** Who is the target player and how many are out there? Include a chart or data point about market size/growth【41†L463-L471】. Show a competitor landscape (maybe logos of similar games or a positioning chart). Highlight how your game stands out (e.g. *“Unlike Game A and B, our game offers multiplayer plus user-generated content”*).
  - **Team Slide:** Photos/names/titles of key team members with one line of credentials (e.g. *“Lead Designer – ex-Ubisoft, worked on Assassin’s Creed series”*). This demonstrates you have the talent to execute. If you have notable advisors or partnerships, include them here.
  - **Traction/Milestones:** Any accomplishments to date (prototype ready, number of playtesters, press reviews, etc.). If you have a gameplay trailer or demo, mention *“Playable demo available”* and be ready to share it. Visuals like graphs (for user growth or engagement metrics) can go here if applicable.
  - **Monetization & Business Model:** Explain briefly how you will make money. For instance, *“Free-to-play with in-game cosmetics. Target ARPU $10/month, similar to top quartile games in genre.”* Or *“Premium PC game, $20 retail with planned DLC packs post-launch.”* Investors need to see you have a revenue strategy.
  - **Financials & Ask:** Provide a summary of your funding ask (e.g. *“Raising $1M seed”*) and a **use-of-funds breakdown**【36†L278-L286】【36†L312-L320】. A pie chart or bar chart can be effective (e.g. 60% development, 20% marketing, 20% operations). Also project what that funding achieves (milestones). If you have high-level financial projections, you can include a chart of revenue projections over 3–5 years (optional in the main deck, but have details ready in an appendix or data room).
  - **Vision/Closing:** End with a strong statement of your long-term vision or what success looks like (e.g. *“Our goal is to build not just one game, but a lasting RPG franchise with a loyal community – a studio that can deliver hit after hit in the genre.”*). This leaves investors with a sense of the big picture. Also include contact info.

  *Design tips:* Keep the deck **visually clean and concise** – use large images, limited text per slide, and a consistent style【22†L139-L147】. Use game art/screenshots as much as possible to excite the viewer. Ensure it’s readable (no tiny fonts). Since you might send this deck as a PDF, every slide should be self-explanatory or have brief speaker notes. Also, **avoid information overload**: the goal is to spark interest, not to answer every possible question in the deck. You can always provide more detail in follow-ups, but a cluttered deck can overwhelm investors. Aim for the deck to be digestible in **~5 minutes** of reading【6†L39-L47】.

- **Game Prototype / Demo:** For game studios, the **playable demo** (or video footage) is as important as the pitch deck. Investors will almost always ask for a demo or at least a gameplay video. Prepare a build that you are comfortable sharing:
  - If the game is playable, consider also making a **gameplay video** (2-3 minutes) that shows the best moments. Some investors prefer not to struggle with installing a demo, and a video ensures they see the highlights quickly. *Pro tip:* Edit the video such that the most exciting or unique gameplay elements appear in the first 30 seconds – you need to hook their interest quickly【22†L95-L103】【22†L125-L133】.
  - **Distribution:** Host the demo build in an easy-access way (no complex sign-ups or broken links). For PC games, a direct download link or a Steam key (if on Steam) is good. For mobile, a TestFlight or APK. Ensure instructions are clear. If the demo requires special hardware or accounts (e.g. VR equipment, online server login), mention that upfront.
  - **Quality:** While the demo doesn’t need to be fully polished, it should represent the intended experience. Include a small readme about controls, and what content is or isn’t final. It’s better to provide a *vertical slice* that’s short but impressive, rather than a long buggy pre-alpha. Also, **limit required playtime** – busy investors might not play more than a few minutes. If possible, configure the demo so that the fun or innovative aspect can be experienced quickly (or provide a save file at a certain level).
  - **Alternatives:** If you truly cannot share a build (e.g. early concept stage), at minimum prepare a **concept video or trailer** – some visual proof of concept. Also, have additional **screenshots and art** available (perhaps in an appendix or separate “press kit”) to give a sense of the game’s style and quality.

- **Business Plan / Executive Summary:** This is a text document (1-3 pages or a Notion doc) that outlines the business in slightly more detail than the deck. Not all investors will ask for a full business plan, but having a concise **Executive Summary** ready is useful. It should cover: the game concept, target market, competitive advantage, team, funding needs, and basic financial outlook. Essentially, it’s your pitch deck in prose form. Since you’re an Office 365 shop, you can draft this in **Word**. This document can be shared with investors who request more info or used as a script for your verbal pitch. Make sure it’s well-formatted and free of typos (attention to detail counts).

- **Financial Model & Budget:** Prepare an **Excel spreadsheet** detailing your projected finances. This typically includes:
  - A **development budget** breakdown (expenses) for at least the next 18-24 months (or however long the funding round is meant to last). List roles, salaries, software/hardware costs, marketing budget, office or online services, etc. This shows you know how you’ll spend the money.
  - **Revenue projections** for the next few years, if applicable (for pre-revenue companies this will be very speculative, but it’s about demonstrating thought process). Include assumptions (market size, conversion rates, pricing, growth rate of users, etc.) on a separate sheet. Be prepared to explain these assumptions.
  - **CapEx** (if any large capital expenditures like game engine licenses or server infrastructure) and **OpEx**.
  - The **Cap Table impact** – if raising equity, how does this round dilute existing shareholders? (More on cap table below.)
  - Financial metrics like burn rate and runway: e.g. *“With $X monthly burn, this funding will last Y months.”*
  
  The model doesn’t need to be extremely complex for early stage, but it should be logically sound. Expect that some experienced investors (or their analysts) will examine it. Also, having a model enables you to try scenarios (e.g. what if launch is delayed 3 months? What if we only raise $800K instead of $1M?) and be ready with answers. This file will likely be part of your data room (see below), rather than sent cold with the first email. But some sophisticated VCs might request a **detailed financial model** early, so it’s best to have it prepared【43†L1-L4】.

- **Capitalization Table (Cap Table):** A **cap table** is a table showing the ownership of the company – founders, existing investors, option pool, etc., and how much equity each holds. For a new studio with no prior investors, this might be simple (founders owning 100%). If you’ve taken any prior funding (or grants, convertible notes, etc.), document the current equity structure. Investors will want to see this to understand how their investment fits in (and that there are no messy ownership issues). Use Excel or a cap table management tool to create a clear table【43†L1-L4】. Include the pre-money valuation you’re targeting for the round, and show how the new investment would dilute percentages. A clean cap table (no strange situations like 50% owned by an inactive co-founder, for example) makes you **“investable”**【43†L1-L4】. If there are existing convertible notes or SAFE notes, list those and their terms because they will convert in the round – transparency here is key. In summary, have a one-page cap table ready to share on request.

- **Data Room / Due Diligence Documents:** As discussions progress, serious investors will want to dive deeper. A **data room** is a folder (often a secure cloud folder or a service) with all the documents an investor might want to review【43†L11-L18】【43†L19-L27】. You can set this up on OneDrive/SharePoint (since you use O365) or use a specialized platform. For a game studio, your data room should include:
  - The pitch deck (most updated version).
  - Executive summary or full business plan.
  - Financial model Excel.
  - Cap table Excel.
  - Detailed **game design document or overview** (if they want to understand the game deeply – could be optional).
  - **Team resumes/CVs** (or LinkedIn profiles).
  - Any proof of traction: e.g. user metrics, playtest reports, testimonials, press articles.
  - **Incorporation documents** of your company, any existing shareholder agreements or IP ownership documents (for due diligence on legal aspects).
  - If you have **market research reports** or supporting data, include those.
  - Essentially, anything that backs up claims made in the deck and answers deeper questions. The data room is typically accessed after initial interest (e.g. post-pitch, when they’re considering a term sheet). Ensure it’s well-organized with folders and descriptive filenames【43†L15-L18】. 
  - Make sure to exclude anything not requested or unnecessary – investors appreciate a *concise* data room where they can find what they need easily (e.g., a folder for “Financial”, one for “Legal”, one for “Product” etc.). 

  *Tip:* The a16z guide and other VC sources note that a strong data room includes pitch deck, cap table, financials, and key docs ready to go【43†L11-L18】. Preparing this in advance shows professionalism and can speed up the due diligence process once you have an interested investor. 

- **Supporting Media and Online Presence:** In addition to formal documents, be mindful of your studio’s public presence. Investors will Google you and look at your game’s **website, social media, or trailers**. Ensure your website (if any) is up to date and presents the studio in a good light. If you have a **LinkedIn page** for the studio or personal LinkedIn profiles, update them to reflect your current project – investors often look up founders’ backgrounds there. If you have a presence on Steam (Coming Soon page) or others, those will be scrutinized too (especially any community feedback). Having a solid **press kit** on your website (with game info, screenshots, logos, team bios) can be helpful if an investor wants to share info with partners. It’s similar to what you’d give a publisher or press, and it shows you treat your project professionally.

- **Pitch Script and Q&A Prep (Internal artifact):** This is not something you send to investors, but internally you should craft a **pitch script** (especially for live presentations) and prepare a **Q&A cheat sheet**. The script ensures you hit all important points succinctly if you have a meeting or call (practice to not just read it, but to know the flow). The Q&A prep is basically the investor question list from section 2 with your polished answers – a quick reference so you can confidently address tough questions. This level of preparation often impresses investors during meetings, because you’ll have crisp, well-thought answers at your fingertips.

In summary, the key artifacts for a fundraising process include a pitch deck, a playable demo/video, an executive summary, a detailed financial model and cap table, and a well-organized set of due diligence documents (data room). Each of these should be created with the specifics of a game studio in mind – heavy on visuals and product experience for the deck/demo, solid on metrics and budget for the financials, and clear on team and ownership for the company information. **Investors have a checklist** of what they expect to see, and by providing all these in a professional manner, you make their job easier (which reflects well on you as founders). *For example, a startup fundraising checklist often includes: “Comprehensive pitch deck – check; Detailed financial model – check; Updated cap table – check; Data room ready – check”【43†L1-L4】.* If you can preempt that, you’re signaling that you are organized and serious.

Finally, ensure consistency across all materials. The numbers you mention in the deck should match those in the financial model; the narrative you tell in the summary should align with the demo experience, etc. Inconsistencies or errors in these docs can create doubt. So do a final review (maybe have a colleague proofread everything).

## 4. Process Tools and Ongoing Workflow

Landing an investment is not a one-off task but an **ongoing process** that can span weeks or months for each client. Building a reusable workflow will save time and improve outcomes. Here we outline how to manage the process and suggest specific tools (including those in the O365 ecosystem you use, and specialized tools) to streamline efforts:

**a. Workflow Overview:** For each new client (studio), you can follow these steps:
1. **Profile the Studio/Game:** Gather initial info about the studio’s game, team, and status. (Many of the questions in section 2 can serve as an intake form.) Determine if they are early-stage (pre-revenue, perhaps just a prototype) or growth-stage (having launched or generating revenue). This will dictate which investors to target and which materials to emphasize (e.g. early-stage deck focuses on vision and team, growth-stage deck focuses on metrics and scaling).
2. **Investor Research and Target List:** Using the criteria (stage, genre focus, region) generate a list of potential investors. Leverage databases and networks – for example, use **Crunchbase or PitchBook** for researching investors who have funded similar companies, or free resources like the **OpenVC database** (which allows filtering investors by check size, geography, sector)【17†L33-L40】. Also, read recent news (GameWorldObserver, VentureBeat, etc.) to see which funds are actively doing gaming deals, as the landscape evolves. Aim to categorize the list by priority: e.g. Tier-1 VCs, strategic corporates, seed funds, etc. Include a mix of global players and relevant regional investors (as identified in section 1). **Document this in a spreadsheet** (Excel) or a simple CRM. For each investor entry, note key details: fund name, typical check (size/stage)【15†L101-L107】【38†L95-L100】, relevant portfolio (did they invest in similar genre or platform?), contact person (partner name), and any connection you might have (LinkedIn 2nd-degree, etc.). This becomes your outreach list.
3. **Studio Readiness Assessment:** Conduct the question-based assessment (section 2) with the client. This could be done as a workshop or Q&A session, capturing answers to all key areas. Identify gaps and create an **action plan** to address them. For example, if the assessment finds that *“no clear monetization strategy”* – allocate a task for the game designer or a business mentor to help formulate one. If *“prototype unpolished”* – decide whether to polish it or at least record a guided demo video to showcase the vision despite rough edges. Essentially, use the grading to **iterate on the studio’s strategy and story**. This step might take some time (even a few weeks of preparation/improvement) before you move to creating final artifacts. It’s an ongoing advisory role: you might give the client homework (e.g., *“research 3 competing games and gather data on their sales – we’ll use that in the pitch”* or *“refine your core gameplay loop based on tester feedback to strengthen traction evidence”*). Once the studio can confidently answer the tough questions, proceed.
4. **Create/Refine Artifacts:** Now build the pitch deck, financial model, etc., as listed in section 3. Since you have O365: use **PowerPoint for the deck**, **Excel for the model and cap table**, **Word for any written plan**. Maintain version control (OneDrive or SharePoint can help here by storing files and having edit history). Solicit feedback on the deck – perhaps share with a friendly industry contact or use an advisor to critique it. Ensure the **demo is tested** (nothing more embarrassing than an investor encountering a game-breaking bug in the demo – if the build is unstable, lean towards the video). As you iterate, keep an eye on consistency. This artifact creation is a repeatable template – you might even develop a **pitch deck template specific for game startups** to reuse (with proper sections, just filling in new content each time) and a **financial model template for game studios** (with common expense categories and a basic revenue model that can be customized). This templating greatly speeds up work for each new client, while still allowing customization for their unique story.
5. **Outreach & Networking:** With materials ready, begin reaching out to the target investors list. Use a combination of **email and LinkedIn** for cold outreach, and leverage any warm intros where possible. Since you’re interested in tools:
   - **Email**: Use Outlook to send personalized emails (avoid generic blasts). Reference any connection or reason you chose them: e.g., *“We saw you invested in [Similar Game] – our studio is doing something in the same space and thought it’d be a fit.”* Keep it short and attach (or better, link to) your pitch deck. *Tip:* Instead of attaching a PDF, consider using a **trackable link** (see tools below) so you know if they opened it. Modern founders use such tools to gain insight: e.g., getting **notifications when an investor opens the deck, which slides they viewed, etc.【6†L47-L55】**. This helps in timing follow-ups and gauging interest.
   - **LinkedIn**: Connect with investors or their associates. Many VCs mention in their profiles if they’re interested in gaming investments. A polite brief message can work if no email is available. Also, follow their posts – sometimes they share what they are looking for.
   - **Events & Communities**: Encourage clients to attend virtual or physical game pitch events (many regions have pitch contests or incubator demo days). Online communities like **Achievers Hub** or **GameFounders alumni** can offer intros to investors looking for game deals. Since the user prompt mentioned LinkedIn posts: keep an eye on LinkedIn for posts by investors giving advice (like the Joakim Achren post with 20 questions, etc.) – engaging with those can build soft connections and also inform your approach (they often hint at their thesis).
   - **CRM tracking**: As you contact investors, track the status (Contacted, Deck Sent, Meeting Scheduled, etc.). You can do this in Excel or use a simple CRM tool. Even **Microsoft Excel/Lists** can function as a CRM for a lightweight process. Include columns for date of contact, response, next step, etc.
6. **Meetings and Pitches:** As interest comes, schedule meetings (use **Outlook/Teams** or whatever video call platform the investor prefers – being an O365 house, Teams is available, but many investors use Zoom, so be flexible). Have the client practice a live pitch (with the deck) beforehand. In the meeting, the client should present for maybe 15-20 minutes and leave ample time for questions. Your role can be to support (e.g., a team member can handle business questions if the founder is more product-focused, etc.). After each meeting, do a retrospective: note what questions were asked (were there any you didn’t anticipate?), and update your knowledge base for next time. Also, **always follow up** with any additional info requested within a day or two【22†L103-L109】. 
7. **Investor Follow-ups and Due Diligence:** If an investor shows serious interest, they will dig deeper. Use your prepared data room to send over materials swiftly. This is where having everything organized pays off. They might have further specific queries – respond promptly and honestly. If something isn’t ready (say, they want to see a detailed retention analysis but you haven’t run a long test yet), explain what your plan is to get that data. Keep communication professional and timely – this stage is as much about *trust* as it is about data.
8. **Negotiation & Closing:** When term sheets or offers come, you’ll negotiate valuation, terms, etc. It’s beyond the scope here, but ensure you involve legal counsel and understand terms (especially important in game deals can be things like IP ownership, etc., if any strategic investor is involved). Since this is a framework for connecting and landing investment, the key is to **remain responsive and maintain momentum** through the closing phase.

**b. Early-Stage vs Growth-Stage Differences:** You mentioned interest in both early and growth – ensure the process flexes for each:
- **Early Stage (Pre-seed/Seed):** Fewer metrics, more emphasis on team and vision. So the pitch deck and conversations will be storytelling-heavy. Investors here are often looking at the *founders’ potential and the game’s concept*. The due diligence is lighter (maybe no extensive data room beyond basic docs). The timeline from pitch to check can be quicker (weeks). Tools like incubators/accelerators are relevant (the studio might even go through one to build credibility). The check sizes are smaller, meaning you might target a larger number of investors (some will only put in $100k, so you may need 5-6 of them). The workflow might involve crafting a compelling prototype and narrative to inspire confidence despite lack of numbers.
- **Growth Stage (Series A/B):** Here, you’re dealing with VCs who require seeing consistent **traction metrics and financial performance**. The pitch will include more charts of KPI trends (DAUs, revenue, retention). The questions will be deeper on unit economics and scaling plans. Due diligence will be heavy: expect requests for cohort analyses, detailed financials, perhaps technical due diligence on your game’s infrastructure, and customer references (player community feedback). The artifacts need to be very polished (you may need an *info memorandum* or longer form deck). Check sizes are large, and often a **lead investor** will spearhead with others co-investing. The timeline can be longer (months of relationship building). Make sure the studio’s **analytics dashboards are in order** to feed data to interested parties. The overall process in growth stage is more about *selling the business’s growth story* (maybe the studio has multiple games or one game with steady revenue) and less about just selling the concept. So adapt the deck to include e.g. year-over-year growth, comparisons to industry benchmarks, etc. If early stage was about promise, growth stage is about proof and acceleration. 

You can reuse much of the framework for both, just dial up or down the detail accordingly. For example, your investor list for growth stage will tilt toward larger VCs (and perhaps exclude seed funds except for follow-ons). Your readiness assessment for growth will include auditing the *metrics quality* (do they track the right KPIs, is the accounting in order, etc.) in addition to the original questions.
