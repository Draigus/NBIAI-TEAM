### A. Formats that solve the “uneven team size” problem
1) **Fixed-roster matches (recommended)**
   - Every department fields the same number each match (example: 4 players).
   - Bigger departments have a bench and rotate people across rounds.
   - Scoring is by match wins, not total bodies.

2) **Per-capita scoring (works well for individual or party games)**
   - Everyone plays, but department score is **average score per person**, not total.
   - Variant: **Top-N average** where N equals the smallest department’s participant count. This is very fair.

3) **Relay format**
   - Each department nominates 1 player at a time; rotate through a queue.
   - Total department score is the sum of a fixed number of “legs,” not all participants.

If you only adopt one rule: use **fixed-roster matches** or **top-N average**. Those remove the headcount advantage cleanly.

### B. Games that stay fair with fixed rosters or per-capita scoring

#### 1) Low-skill, high-inclusion party games (best for mixed comfort levels)
- **Jackbox Party Pack games** (Quiplash, Fibbage, Drawful, Trivia Murder Party, Tee K.O.)
  - Fairness: score is per player; you can use per-capita or top-N.
  - Scales well, very low barrier to entry (phones as controllers).
- **Gartic Phone**
  - Fairness: per-capita scoring via voting, or judge-based points.
  - Art teams usually love it. It is playful, not mechanically sweaty.
- **Codenames (digital or physical)**
  - Fairness: do equal team sizes per match (5v5, 4v4).
  - Great for communication, inference, and shared context.
- **Wavelength**
  - Fairness: equal team sizes per match.
  - Strong for alignment and “how do you think” discussion.

#### 2) Team coordination games (excellent for “team building” in the literal sense)
- **Keep Talking and Nobody Explodes**
  - Format: 2–4 players per department per run.
  - Scoring: fastest clear time, fewest strikes.
  - Observers can rotate in; strong comms focus.
- **Overcooked! 2**
  - Format: 2–4 per department per run.
  - Scoring: highest score on the same level seed.
  - Very effective at revealing and improving coordination patterns.
- **PlateUp!**
  - Similar benefits to Overcooked, often a bit more systems-driven.

#### 3) Competitive but still accessible (good if your team enjoys “sport” energy)
- **Rocket League**
  - Use equal roster sizes (2v2 or 3v3) and private matches.
  - If skill disparity is a concern: do a quick seeding round, then bracket by tier.
- **Golf With Your Friends**
  - Very accessible; per-capita or top-N average works perfectly.
- **Tetris Effect: Connected (or another 1v1 puzzle battler)**
  - Run department “duels” as a ladder: each match is 1v1, departments rotate players.

### C. A ready-to-run structure (simple, fair, and scalable)
**Option 1: Department Cup (fixed roster)**
- Pick roster size: **4 players per match** (or 3).
- Each department plays a round-robin or bracket.
- Each match consists of:
  - Game 1: Jackbox (individual points, top-N average)
  - Game 2: Keep Talking (timed run)
  - Game 3: Overcooked or PlateUp (same map, same time limit)
- Department points per match:  
  - Win = 3, Draw = 1, Loss = 0  
  - Tie-breakers: aggregate time/score margins.

**Option 2: Skills-neutral “Top-N average” league**
- Everyone participates across 2–3 rounds.
- Department score = average of best N scores (N = smallest department size that participates).
- Use games like Jackbox, Golf With Your Friends, Tetris, or a timed challenge.

### D. Practical tips to keep it fun and not sweaty
- Publish the fairness rule up front (fixed roster or top-N average).
- Rotate roles deliberately in comms-heavy games (speaker, navigator, runner).
- If you have very different skill levels, split into two divisions (A and B) and award both.

A straight PvP deathmatch with no structure is usually not team building, it is just competition.

## What makes a video game genuinely team building
A game is team building when it has at least 3 of these:
- Shared objective that cannot be brute-forced by one carry
- Tight information flow (someone sees X, someone else must act on X)
- Role specialization (pilot vs repair vs scouting, etc.)
- Time pressure that punishes chaos and rewards planning
- Clear “what went wrong” moments that can be improved next round

## How to run it so it stays fair with uneven department sizes
- **Fixed roster pods:** each department fields a 4-person squad per round, bench rotates every round.
- **Scoring:** wins per round, plus a simple secondary metric (time, score, objectives completed).
- **No advantage from more people:** larger departments just rotate more people through the same number of “seats.”

## Minimal structure that turns it into team building (not just gaming)
- 3 minute pre-brief: assign roles and one comms rule (example: “call, confirm, execute”).
- 2 rounds back-to-back.
- 5 minute debrief with 3 questions:
  1) What broke first, comms, roles, or plan?
  2) What single change would improve the next round?
  3) Did we follow the comms rule, yes or no?
