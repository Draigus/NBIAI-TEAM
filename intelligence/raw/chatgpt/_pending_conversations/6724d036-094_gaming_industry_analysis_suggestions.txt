**Why Is the Gaming Industry Facing Shutdowns and Layoffs Amidst Rising Revenues?**

I get asked all the time: Why is the gaming industry experiencing shutdowns and layoffs when revenue and compounded annual growth rate (CAGR) are soaring upwards? It's a perplexing situation. Despite the industry's financial growth, studios are closing, and investors are more risk-averse than ever. In this article, I'll explore this paradox by reflecting on the evolution of the gaming industry, market saturation, challenges with live service models, the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, and the current investment climate.

---

**A Walk Down Memory Lane**

I remember when **King's Quest** by Sierra came out in 1984. There were around 32 floppy disks stacked beside the computer. You would venture forth in the game for about 15 minutes and then have to switch disks. In today's world, this would be laughable. Not to mention it was a side-scrolling, bit-style game with a MIDI rendition of "Greensleeves" playing in the background. Yet, to me, it was a wonder. I had played *Pong*, *Tank Buster*, and even *Pitfall*, but this was a new world I had never seen before—I was hooked.

Fast forward to today: my kids are all grown, the last now in university. I've worked in the games industry for 20 years. It's an interesting thing to say, yes, my kids love games, and they've played and loved some of the games I've worked on (I can't tell you how happy that made me). To them, though, that moment when they played their first game was wondrous, yes, but not the same as what I felt. What was different? First off, **choice**—lots of it.

---

**The Era of Overabundance**

In 1984, if you didn't want to play one of the few games available at that time, well... you read a book or rode your bike to a friend's house—or maybe to the video arcade when mom would give me some quarters. My kids, on the other hand, just clicked on another game, one of many, in every genre. They watched cool trailers, then found YouTube breakdowns on the pros and cons, critics' reviews, and walkthroughs of the game they were considering playing. Then, if it was free-to-play, they jumped in; if not, they bought it or asked us to buy it for them.

---

**Too Much of a Good Thing?**

The market is now more saturated and commoditized than it has ever been. This distributes the same user base across more titles of the same nature and forces multiple studios to vie for the same segments of the player base. This means fewer players for an individual game, even though there are more players overall.

If three arena-based shooters (you know who you are) are all vying for the same type of player, each hopes that the setting, mechanics, pacing, and, yes, marketing will get them the lion's share of users. Then they struggle to keep them because live service skills aren't quite there in maturity (more on that later).

As long as the number of players is larger than the ratio needed to keep all games afloat and in revenue, it becomes a healthy market, and each game will grow, right? Not necessarily. There's a critical recipe we haven't talked about: **Are they fun? Is there a reason to play 200 hours? Does it fit the target player's lifestyle? Is the monetization sound? Do their friends play it?** If these can be checked off as good, then yes, it can stay healthy.

---

**The Challenge of Differentiation**

So what happens when there are 20 clones of the same game without differentiation, or a poor live service, or mistargeted features? Simply put, the games industry is an entertainment industry. If the game isn't entertaining—or more entertaining than a similar game—players will leave for greener shores because there are a lot of shores to go to. Supply me something entertaining, or I'll demand it from somewhere else.

This was a growing situation before the current state but simply put, **too many of the same type of games**. It's like picking an orange at the fruit stand; the least little defect becomes a differentiator that defines purchase.

---

**The Live Service Model: A Double-Edged Sword**

I've worked on a fair number of live games—from MMOs to mobile games and subscriptions to in-app purchases (IAP) to battle passes. Studio leadership often loves the idea and sustainability of a live game model. It levels out revenue over the long haul and makes keeping a studio building games more likely. There's been just one problem: going from concept to fully developed pipelines with new roles and new cadences is, well, really hard. Sometimes worse when you're an existing studio.

From designs to code architecture to marketing beats, nothing is tooled or structured for live services. It's a fundamental shift that affects almost every area of a studio. Then it gets hard! I remember sitting in a large conference room with a number of very powerful and intelligent game industry leaders when one turned to me—who led one of the most well-known franchises in the world at the time—and said, "We sell boxes. We won't ever use player retention as a success factor for [Game Title]."

Simply put, many studios have implemented live services but not in the best way. This brought a wave of pay-gating, paywalls, low-quality content by often exhausted dev staff. Free-to-play competed with traditional boxed games. Many thought this was the end for the "buy a game and play it" model. Of course, that's not true—at least not yet.

---

**Investor Dynamics and the Surge of 'Cheap Money'**

During the early 2010s to 2020s, game studios were showing investors forecasts that were comparative to the best games in the genre that were in the market. "We are the next [insert billion-dollar game here]," and some investors, as savvy as they usually are, agreed and jumped in.

Different investors have different goals, but they all agree on one thing: **successful products**. By 2021-22, the games industry was referring to investment as "cheap money," meaning investors were very eager to throw a lot of cash at studios—and I mean a lot of cash. Projects were appearing every day.

---

**The Pandemic's Impact on the Gaming Boom**

**Let's have a pandemic!**

In 2020, the world was introduced to a new reality. It was the largest disruption of Maslow's concepts of physical health and safety to hit us, and it was global. It impacted every part of life for most people. Some industries were simply devastated, while others surged in unbelievable ways. Zoom video conferences went up 300%, and Amazon grew by 38% in 2020.

The games industry was one of these beneficiaries. Users surged, sales surged, play hours surged. According to Newzoo, the global games market grew by 23% in 2020, reaching $177.8 billion in revenue. Life was a bittersweet combination of incredible success working in games combined with the stress of a global pandemic while pajamas became the proper work attire.

---

**Too Good to Be True? The Bubble Bursts**

So, COVID made the games industry a lot of money; that had to drive a lot of investment, right? Yes! Remember those savvy investors we talked about earlier? By 2021-22, the games industry was referring to investment as "cheap money." Even as warning signs such as "The Great Resignation," where people began quitting their jobs due to COVID exhaustion, isolation, and rebalancing work-life, impacted the games industry.

By 2023, the pullback of investing was dramatic—a third less than the previous year. Wait, Microsoft was spending $75 billion on Activision Blizzard in 2023. Yes, this was happening, but overall investments in the industry were starting to fall precipitously, and layoffs were becoming rampant as confidence and estimates of the future market faltered. In part because of purchases like that of Activision and Bethesda, which caused a lot of redundant staff and where purchasers wanted to streamline big bets that were made by the ones they bought so it fit in the now larger plans.

By 2024, the broad industry investments had fallen to almost half in number. At the same time, studios and publishers were laying off at a rate never before seen. According to market analyses, global game industry investments dropped by approximately 50%, and over 6,000 game industry employees were laid off globally in the first half of 2024 alone.

---

**Understanding the Downturn**

Even now, we don't fully understand the downstream implications of COVID on economies, industries, social structures, and, of course, our own minds. What is clear is that the huge swell of COVID isolation caused an overindulgence in projects spun up and sold to investors based on the idea that player spend and volumes were the new normal. Driving as much of that "cheap" money as possible into investment, which in turn inflated the consumer spend potential and expectations on the performance of the products and industry in a way that was unsustainable.

The glut of gaming hours spent turned into people wanting to feel the sun on their faces. People were going outside again. Game purchases and spend fell—not just in games but in PCs and consoles as well. According to Gartner, global PC shipments declined by 9.5% in 2022. So when the inevitable decline occurred, it shook the investment community, which turned "cheap" money into something far more difficult to attain. Studios began to falter, the number of projects decreased, and the industry faced a correction.

---

**Moving Forward: Navigating the New Normal**

So, why is the gaming industry where it is now? The combination of market saturation, challenges with live service models, overestimation of growth during the pandemic, and a sudden decline in player engagement post-pandemic has led to shutdowns and layoffs. Investors have become more cautious, and studios are struggling to adapt to the new landscape.

---

**Recommendations for the Industry**

To navigate these challenges, the industry needs to:

- **Focus on Innovation**: Differentiate products through unique gameplay, narratives, and experiences rather than cloning existing successful titles.

- **Improve Live Service Models**: Invest in developing mature live service skills, including infrastructure, content pipelines, and community engagement strategies.

- **Adjust Expectations**: Align investor and studio expectations with realistic market growth projections, understanding that the pandemic surge was an anomaly.

- **Diversify Portfolios**: Explore new genres, platforms, and business models to reach different segments of the market.

- **Prioritize Quality Over Quantity**: Emphasize delivering high-quality content that keeps players engaged long-term.

---

**Conclusion**

The gaming industry is at a crossroads. Despite rising revenues, the challenges of market saturation, misaligned live service implementations, and the aftermath of the pandemic have created a complex environment. By understanding these factors and adapting accordingly, studios can navigate this period of correction and continue to thrive in the ever-evolving landscape of interactive entertainment.
