## 7. Cross-cutting red team points

1. **Density vs usability**  
   - The guide is rich, but borderline dense for a Seed team. A “light path” through the guide for first-timers would help: if you only do ten things, do these.  

2. **Handling weak or messy data**  
   - Investors see a lot of decks where metrics are mediocre but founders are honest and have a plan. The guide should explicitly teach “how to present ugly reality plus corrective plan” instead of letting founders infer it.  

3. **Over-templating risk**  
   - There is a risk that many decks become formulaic. Investors will still read them, but you might want one short section on “breaking the template safely” for teams with unusual businesses or strong brands.  

4. **Regulatory and platform risk**  
   - The document mentions compliance and “grey area” monetisation at a high level, but app store policy, regional regulation, and data privacy enforcement are real investment risks now. A bit more explicit treatment would be valuable. fileciteturn2file10turn2file3turn2file2  

5. **Portfolio studios and multi-game cases**  
   - The main flow assumes one lead game. A short sidebar for studios with more than one title would make the guide more broadly applicable.

### 1. Weak or messy metrics doctrine

**Heading suggestion:**  
**Weak or messy metrics: how to show them without killing your round**

Founders often ask whether they should hide weak or messy metrics. The short answer is no. Investors know that early cohorts are noisy and that most games look rough before they look good. What kills trust is not weak numbers. What kills trust is hiding, cherry picking, or pretending that noise is signal.

When your metrics are ugly or incomplete, use a simple three step pattern.

1. **Show the full picture, not only the flattering slice.**  
   - Always show D1, D7 and D30 for each meaningful cohort you have run, even if they are below your target bar.  
   - Show the full UA journey you have actually taken so far. If you only tested one or two networks or geos, say so.  
   - If you have changed builds, funnels or economies between cohorts, label those changes clearly on the charts.

2. **Explain what you learned and what you changed.**  
   - For each weak cohort, write down the key takeaways in plain language.  
   - Link those takeaways directly to changes in design, onboarding, core loop, economy, or UA targeting.  
   - Make clear which problems you believe you have addressed and which ones are still open.

3. **Define clear gates and decision points.**  
   - Explain which retention and monetisation thresholds would make you double down, pivot, or kill the game.  
   - Do not present every plan as a guaranteed fix. Spell out what you will do if the next round of experiments also fails.  
   - Align these gates with your model and runway, so investors can see how learning, cash and decision points connect.

A good “weak metrics” slide does not try to talk investors into ignoring reality. It shows that you see reality clearly, that you experiment deliberately, and that you are willing to walk away when the data tells you to. That mindset builds more confidence than any short term hack to make a chart look pretty.

Whenever you build traction, monetisation, UA or live ops slides, assume that at least one of your charts will look disappointing. Design the story so that you can talk through those charts with calm, specific and honest commentary. That is what experienced investors are listening for.

- A fully populated data room. You can add documents as investors progress from first meeting to deeper diligence.

Use this fast lane as a starting point, not a ceiling. As you gain traction and investor interest, you can grow into the full depth of the guide. The goal is to keep you moving without pretending that “just a pretty pitch” is enough.

### 3. Stage labels for data room and due diligence

**Heading suggestion:**  
**Using stage labels in your data room and due diligence checklist**

The data room structure and due diligence checklist in this guide are written to cover both Seed and Series A. If you treat every item as mandatory from day one, you will either stall for months or build a bureaucracy before you have a game.

To avoid that, each checklist item is tagged with a stage label:

- **Seed – must have**  
  Items you should have in place before you send your Seed deck to serious investors.  

- **Seed – nice to have**  
  Items that strengthen your Seed story, but can be filled in over the course of the round.  

- **Series A – must have**  
  Items that investors will expect to see before they sign a Series A term sheet, even if they are flexible at Seed.

Treat these labels as a roadmap, not a pass or fail test. When you prepare for a Seed raise, focus on completing the Seed – must have items, use Seed – nice to have as stretch goals, and read the Series A – must have items as a preview of where you need to be in twelve to twenty four months.  

Investors know that an early Seed data room will be thinner. They care far more about whether you are honest about the gaps, whether the material you do have is clean and consistent, and whether there is evidence that you can grow into the level of discipline that Series A requires.

### 4. Genre and product type lens

**Heading suggestion:**  
**Genre and product type lens: calibrating expectations**

Not all mobile games are judged by the same bar. A live ops driven 4X strategy title and an ad heavy idle game can both be good businesses, but they will have very different retention curves, ARPDAU profiles and UA realities.

When you design your deck, model and data room, you should anchor your narrative in the expectations for your specific genre and product type. As a simple reference, use the lens below as a starting point.

| Product type            | Typical monetisation mix         | Retention expectations (indicative)                  | UA difficulty and data needs                           |
|-------------------------|----------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| Ad heavy idle or hyper  | Mostly ads, light IAP           | D1 needs to be strong, long tail often shallow       | CPI can be low but fickle, creative fatigue is brutal  |
| Casual puzzle           | Mixed, IAP plus rewarded ads    | D1 and D7 moderate, D30 depends on content cadence   | Very crowded auctions, high creative iteration demand  |
| Midcore tactics or RPG  | Mostly IAP with some rewarded   | D1 and D7 moderate to high, D30+ matters a lot       | UA is expensive, LTV and cohort analysis are central   |
| 4X and strategy         | High value IAP, whales important| D1 often modest, D30 and beyond are critical         | Long payback windows, UA narrative must be very clear  |
| Lifestyle or sandbox    | Mixed, often sub plus IAP or ads| Retention driven by community and UGC over time      | UA often linked to creator partnerships and content    |

These bands are not rigid rules. They are a way to check whether your story about retention, monetisation and UA is internally consistent. When you present your numbers:

- Use peer games in your specific product type as reference points.  
- Explain where you expect to sit within or above those bands, and why.  
- Make clear if you are intentionally pursuing an outlier strategy, such as very low ARPDAU but enormous reach, or very high ARPPU in a narrow niche.

Investors are more comfortable when a founder knows which game they are playing, and which scoreboard they are using to judge success.

### 5. Multi title studios at Series A

**Heading suggestion:**  
**Multi title studios at Series A**

By the time you raise Series A, you may have more than one meaningful title. For example:

- One lead game in soft launch or early global launch.  
- One or two supporting titles in development or prototype.  
- Legacy or contract projects that still generate some revenue.

From an investor perspective, this is normal, but it changes how we read your deck. We want to see both focus and leverage.

When you have multiple titles, use a simple pattern.

1. **Declare a clear lead title.**  
   - Name one game as the primary vehicle for the Series A plan.  
   - Make that explicit on the cover and in your story slides.  
   - Most of your metrics, model and hiring plan should anchor on this lead title.

2. **Summarise the portfolio without diluting the story.**  
   - Include one slide that shows the full portfolio: live, soft launch, in development.  
   - For each supporting title, give only one or two key facts: stage, role in the strategy, and expected contribution.  
   - Make clear whether these titles are real options, experiments, or distractions that you intend to sunset.

3. **Explain resource allocation and risk.**  
   - Show how people, budget and focus are split between titles.  
   - Explain how you will avoid spreading critical roles too thin across projects.  
   - If you rely on one title to fund or cross promote others, make that assumption explicit in the model.

A multi title studio can be more valuable than a single title studio, but only if the founder has a clear hierarchy of focus and does not hide weak projects behind the success of a single hit. Your Series A deck should make that hierarchy very obvious.

### 6. Clean up from Seed to Series A

**Heading suggestion:**  
**Cleaning up your deck from Seed to Series A**

A good Series A deck is not a bloated Seed deck with extra charts. It is a sharper document that assumes the investor already understands the basic fantasy and focuses on whether the machine works.

When you upgrade your deck, use this checklist to remove noise and tighten the story.

- **Cut early concept art that no longer reflects the live game.**  
  Keep only a small number of screens or clips that show the current reality of the product.

- **Shorten the origin story.**  
  At Seed it was useful to explain how you came to the idea. At Series A you can compress that into one or two short sentences and move on.

- **Replace vision slides with evidence slides.**  
  Wherever you had “we plan to” statements at Seed, your Series A deck should now show “we did this, and here are the results”.

- **Avoid duplicating the same information in multiple places.**  
  If a chart appears in the data section, there is no need to repeat it on a product slide with slightly different formatting. Pick one location and keep the rest narrative.

- **Remove generic market hype.**  
  By Series A, investors already believe that mobile games can be big businesses. They do not need another global TAM slide. They need your actual position in your segment.

If you are in doubt about a slide, ask a simple question: “Does this slide help an investor decide whether to trust our machine with twenty to thirty million of capital” If the answer is no, cut or merge it.

### 7. Post privacy UA and attribution reality

**Heading suggestion:**  
**UA and attribution in a post privacy world**

User acquisition for mobile games no longer operates in a world of perfect device level tracking. Between platform privacy changes, SKAN style attribution and regional regulation, every studio has to deal with noisy, incomplete data.

Investors know this. They are not expecting you to magically reconstruct the past. They are expecting you to have a realistic, disciplined approach.

When you describe your UA and attribution strategy:

1. **Be explicit about what you can and cannot see.**  
   - Name the platforms, networks and measurement tools you use.  
   - Explain which signals you get at the campaign or creative level, and which ones you only see in blended form.  
   - Acknowledge any gaps in cohort level attribution, instead of glossing over them.

2. **Use blended and triangulated metrics.**  
   - Show how you look at revenue per install, ROAS and LTV to CPI at a blended or channel level over time.  
   - Explain how you cross check network dashboards against your own data warehouse.  
   - If you use proxy or directional metrics, such as early payer rates or funnel completion, state that clearly.

3. **Adapt your testing philosophy to your scale.**  
   - If you are too small for classical multi cell tests, say so.  
   - Describe lighter weight approaches that still inform decisions, for example rotating creative packages or testing in fewer geos.  
   - Focus on showing that you learn reliably, not that your statistics are perfect.

4. **Explain how uncertainty flows into your model and plan.**  
   - Show how you handle ranges rather than single point numbers in your UA assumptions.  
   - Be clear about what you would do if CPI inflation or tracking degradation pushes your effective payback out.

In a post privacy environment, there is no such thing as a perfectly measured UA strategy. What matters is whether you have a sane mental model, whether you watch the right indicators, and whether you adjust quickly when they move against you.

### 8. Reconciling deck, model and data exports

**Heading suggestion:**  
**One truth across deck, model and data room**

Investors will check that your story is numerically consistent. If they find that the same KPI has three different values in the deck, the model and the exports, they will not waste time trying to guess which one is true. They will assume either sloppiness or dishonesty.

To avoid this, apply three simple rules:

1. **Appoint a single owner for numeric consistency.**  
   - One person in the team should be responsible for making sure that any number which appears in more than one place is identical or clearly explained.  
   - That person should be involved in finalising the deck, updating the model, and populating the data room.

2. **Define a small set of canonical KPIs.**  
   - For example: DAU, MAU, D1 / D7 / D30 retention, ARPDAU, payer rate, CPI, ROAS, LTV, revenue, burn, runway.  
   - Decide which sheet, dashboard or export is the source of truth for each of these metrics.  
   - Build your deck charts directly from those sources wherever possible.

3. **Run a reconciliation pass before you share anything.**  
   - Before you send a deck or a data room link, pick ten to fifteen key numbers and trace them through deck, model and exports.  
   - Fix any mismatches, or add clear footnotes if the difference is intentional, for example due to currency or time window.

A clean, consistent numbers story does not guarantee that investors will like the numbers. It does show that you run a tight operation. That is often the difference between “interesting conversation” and “investable company”.

### 9. Bad, average and good slide patterns

**Heading suggestion:**  
**Bad, average and good patterns for key slides**

Rubrics and checklists are more powerful when founders can see what they mean in practice. For the most abused slides in mobile decks, use the following patterns as reference.

You can implement them as small visual examples. The text below captures the intent.

**Market and audience (S06)**  

- Bad: A huge global TAM number with no segmentation, a buzzword cloud, and a claim that you will “capture one percent”.  
- Average: A segmented market chart that shows your subgenre, but no link to actual reference games or acquisiton realities.  
- Good: A clear definition of your subgenre and target audience, three to five reference games with real performance data, and a concrete statement of where you intend to sit among them.

**Competitive landscape (S07)**  

- Bad: A generic two by two with every competitor conveniently placed in a weak quadrant.  
- Average: A feature checklist that lists ten features and ticks all of them for your game.  
- Good: A concise map that acknowledges real strengths of key competitors, shows where you are deliberately similar, and highlights a small number of genuine differences that matter for retention or monetisation.

**Traction and validation (S08)**  

- Bad: Total downloads, revenue and rating, with no cohorts or retention curves.  
- Average: Retention curves that mix builds and geos without labels, with only the best cohort shown in the deck.  
- Good: A small set of clearly labelled cohorts, including weaker ones, with commentary on what changed between them and what you learned.

**Monetisation model (S09)**  

- Bad: A single ARPDAU number and a vague statement that “we will add a battle pass later”.  
- Average: A shop screenshot and a list of planned features, but no link between the economy, your target segments and projected ARPU bands.

**UA and go to market (S11)**  

- Bad: “We will use influencers, TikTok, and virality” with no budget, no channels, and no sense of payback.  
- Average: A channel list and some CPI benchmarks, but no link to your own early tests or to your LTV assumptions.  
- Good: A staged UA plan with budgets, channels and geos, grounded in whatever experiments you have run so far, and consistent with the LTV and payback windows in your model.

You do not need perfect slides. You do need slides that show you are not lying to yourself about where you really sit on this spectrum.

### 10. Regulation and platform risk appendix

**Heading suggestion:**  
**Appendix – Regulation and platform risk**

Mobile free to play games operate inside a moving web of platform policies and regional regulations. For most studios, these risks are manageable. For investors, they are not optional. A strong pitch does not ignore them. It shows that the team has a basic grip on the problem.

When you think about regulation and platform risk, work through four lenses.

1. **Children and minors.**  
   - Are you explicitly targeting players under 18, or likely to attract them in large numbers  
   - How do you handle age gates, parental consent, and data collection for younger users  
   - Are there any mechanics in your game, such as gacha or loot boxes, that could be interpreted as gambling for minors in key markets

2. **Loot boxes, chance based items and payments.**  
   - Do you sell chance based items or bundles with random contents  
   - In which countries do you expect to operate, and what is the current stance on such mechanics in those regions  
   - How will you adapt your design if a major jurisdiction tightens enforcement during the life of the game

3. **Privacy and data use.**  
   - What personal data do you collect, and how is it stored and processed  
   - How do you comply with major frameworks such as GDPR and CCPA, including rights to access and deletion  
   - How do you communicate your data practices to players in clear language

4. **Platform policies and enforcement history.**  
   - Are there any aspects of your game that are close to the edges of store policy on gambling, adult content, misleading ads or harmful behaviour  
   - Have you reviewed recent examples of suspension or removal for games in your subgenre  
   - What is your plan if a platform forces a material change to your monetisation or content

In your deck you do not need a long legal essay. One clear slide that summarises your position on these four areas is enough. In your data room, provide the supporting material: policy reviews, legal opinions if you have them, and any internal guidelines you use for designers and marketers.

The goal is not to eliminate all risk. It is to show that you see the risk clearly, that you are not relying on tricks that will obviously be shut down, and that you are prepared to adapt when the environment moves.
