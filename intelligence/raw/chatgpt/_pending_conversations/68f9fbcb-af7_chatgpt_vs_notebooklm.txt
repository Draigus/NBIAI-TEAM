# Why the earlier attempts failed

1) Discovery-by-capitalisation  
Relying on simple capitalisation or word shapes promoted junk like Commander His, Tarros Do, Guild Collegiate, and That as “entities.” The rules did not insist on contextual signals such as titles, prepositions of place, or section anchors.

2) No structural parsing  
Your corpus already contains strong anchors in headings, lists, and section blocks, for example h2 or h3 entries such as Garren Orys, Breakwater Keep, Pelduin’s School of Magery, and dozens more in the Hule_Book HTML parts. Those were not harvested first, which forced the system to guess in free prose.

3) Mixed entity types and weak typing  
People, places, buildings, factions, and artefacts were not typed with explicit features. That turned tavern names, district names, and even rulebook terms into “people.”

4) No allow-list or stop-list tuned to Hule  
Hularen linguistic terms and clan words were not handled as vocabulary classes with definitions. That let common English words and function words leak in as false positives, and it also caused real Hularen terms to be misclassified as names.

5) Missing normalisation and alias control  
Variants such as Lehn’s Port vs Lehnsport vs Lehnport were not canonicalised. Hyphenation, quotes, and diacritics were not normalised before matching, which split a single place into multiple rows.

6) No provenance or confidence  
Rows arrived without file locations, heading trails, or confidence scores. That blocked triage, re-runs, and reproducibility.

7) Partial corpus scans  
Some passes only skimmed a subset of files, which made counts look artificially small and left duplicates untouched.

## C. Candidate generation with strict gating

Generate candidates in three passes, in this order:

1) Structural pass  
Every heading and list item that matches an entity pattern becomes a candidate. Examples of patterns that should promote immediately:  
- Person: `Proper Noun` sequences with either a title token or a place preposition, such as of, from, Lord, Elder, Archbishop, Knight, Marshal, Priest, Merchant.  
- Place or location: tokens that end in Hall, Inn, Keep, Port, Hill, Vale, Bay, District, Bridge, Tower, School, Temple, Ruins, Point, March, Home, Wood, Isles.  
- Organisations and facilities: School of X, Temple of X, Knights of X, House of X.

2) Patterned prose pass  
Scan prose for the same signals, but require at least one of: title terms, of <Place>, or proximity to a known section. Examples: Lokander “Lok” Farlind of Iron Hill, Lord Commander qualifies. The word Commander by itself does not.

3) LLM rescue pass  
Only for edge cases the rules cannot decide. Provide the sentence and the top N nearest structured anchors. Ask for a binary keep or drop, with a type guess and a one-line why.

Hard filters that stop the junk you saw earlier:  
- Reject any two-token candidate where the second token is a pronoun or a determiner, for example His, That, This, Do, Yes.  
- Reject single-token capitalised words unless they occur as a heading or in a known list section.  
- Reject tokens with leading numerals or Dream N markers.  
- Reject rulebook terminology such as Exhaustion and Concentration unless a matching section explicitly defines it as a game term for your rules appendix.

## D. Typing and feature scoring

For each candidate, compute a type with features and a score:

- Person features  
  Title present, of <Place> present, appears in a People of Interest section, has a personal nickname in quotes, has kinship markers, appears with verbs such as said, met, wrote, arrived.

- Place features  
  Ends with a place suffix, appears in a Districts or Locations section, appears in travel or arrival contexts, or is the object of go to, arrive at, return to.

- Organisation or building features  
  Contains School of, Temple of, House of, Inn, Keep, Ruins, Guild, Company, Hall, Embassy, Palace, Port Authority.

Keep the type with the highest score and store contributing features for audit.
