Here’s a list of **video game studio lingo and terminology** commonly used in the industry, categorized for easy reference:

### **Game Development Roles & Teams**
- **Dev (Developer)** – A game creator, often referring to programmers or the entire development team.  
- **Designer** – The person responsible for gameplay mechanics, levels, and overall player experience.  
- **Producer** – Oversees the project, schedules, and ensures the game is on track.  
- **QA (Quality Assurance)** – Testers who find and report bugs.  
- **Artist** – Responsible for visual elements, including concept art, character models, and environments.  
- **Animator** – Brings characters and objects to life through movement.  
- **Tech Artist** – Bridges the gap between artists and programmers, optimizing assets for performance.  
- **UI/UX Designer** – Designs menus, interfaces, and overall user experience.  
- **Sound Designer** – Creates sound effects (SFX) and works with composers on music.  
- **Narrative Designer** – Writes game stories, dialogues, and lore.  
- **Localization** – Adapting a game for different languages and regions.  
- **Community Manager** – Engages with the game’s community and manages social media.  

---

### **Game Production & Pipeline Terms**
- **Sprint** – A short development cycle, often used in Agile development.  
- **Vertical Slice** – A polished, playable section of a game that showcases core mechanics.  
- **MVP (Minimum Viable Product)** – A version of the game with just enough features to be functional.  
- **Graybox/Whitebox** – A rough, untextured version of a level used for early playtesting.  
- **Alpha** – An early, unfinished build of the game, usually missing assets or polish.  
- **Beta** – A near-complete version of the game, often released for testing.  
- **Gold Master** – The final version of the game ready for release.  
- **Day One Patch** – An update released immediately at launch to fix issues.  
- **Crunch** – Intense overtime work to meet deadlines, often controversial.  
- **Pipeline** – The process and workflow for developing a game.  
- **Vertical Slice** – A fully functional small segment of a game used to pitch or test features.  
- **Bug** – An error or glitch in the game’s code.  
- **Patch** – A post-release update that fixes bugs or adds content.  

---

### **Game Design & Mechanics**
- **Gameplay Loop** – The core set of actions that players repeat throughout the game.  
- **Mechanic** – A specific gameplay feature, such as jumping, shooting, or crafting.  
- **Hitbox** – The invisible area that detects collisions with other objects.  
- **Tweakables** – Variables that can be adjusted to balance gameplay.  
- **AI (Artificial Intelligence)** – The behavior of non-player characters (NPCs).  
- **NavMesh** – A navigation map that AI characters use to move through the game world.  
- **LOD (Level of Detail)** – A technique where objects decrease in detail based on distance to improve performance.  
- **Gating** – Restricting player progress until certain conditions are met.  
- **Metagame (Meta)** – Strategies or mechanics that go beyond the core gameplay, often related to competitive balancing.  
- **Juice** – The extra polish that makes actions feel satisfying, such as screen shakes, particles, or sound effects.  
- **Procedural Generation** – Using algorithms to generate game content dynamically.  
- **Fog of War** – A mechanic that hides unexplored areas in strategy games.  
- **Bullet Sponge** – An enemy that takes an excessive number of hits to defeat.  
- **Invisible Wall** – An unseen barrier preventing players from leaving a game area.  

---

### **Level Design Terms**
- **Choke Point** – A narrow area in a level designed to control player movement or create tension.  
- **Golden Path** – The intended route through a level.  
- **Set Piece** – A scripted, cinematic event in a game.  
- **Easter Egg** – A hidden reference, joke, or secret in a game.  
- **Line of Sight (LoS)** – How much a character can see based on obstacles in the environment.  
- **Zone** – A distinct area in an open-world game.  
- **Dungeon** – A self-contained level or area, often with unique challenges.  
- **Sandbox** – A game or level with open-ended player freedom.  
- **Hub World** – A central location connecting multiple levels or zones.  

---

### **Game Art & Animation**
- **Rigging** – Adding a skeleton to a 3D model for animation.  
- **Vertex Count** – The number of points that make up a 3D model, affecting performance.  
- **Texture Map** – A 2D image applied to a 3D model for visual detail.  
- **Normal Map** – A type of texture that creates the illusion of depth.  
- **Shader** – A program that controls how objects appear based on lighting and materials.  
- **Particles** – Small visual effects like smoke, fire, or rain.  
- **Keyframe** – A primary frame in animation that marks major motion changes.  
- **Tweening** – Automatically generating frames between two keyframes.  

---

### **Programming & Tech**
- **Engine** – The software framework used to build a game (e.g., Unity, Unreal Engine).  
- **FPS (Frames Per Second)** – The number of images rendered per second; higher is smoother.  
- **Tick Rate** – How often the game updates per second, critical in multiplayer games.  
- **Latency/Ping** – The delay between a player's action and the game server’s response.  
- **Physics Engine** – A system that simulates real-world physics in a game.  
- **Networking** – The system managing online multiplayer interactions.  
- **Optimization** – Improving a game’s performance by reducing resource usage.  
- **Garbage Collection** – Automatic memory management to prevent crashes.  
- **Netcode** – The programming behind online play and server-client interactions.  

---

### **Marketing & Monetization**
- **Live Service** – A game designed for continuous updates and long-term engagement.  
- **DLC (Downloadable Content)** – Extra content released post-launch.  
- **Microtransaction (MTX)** – Small in-game purchases, often cosmetic.  
- **Battle Pass** – A seasonal content system that rewards players for completing challenges.  
- **Gacha** – A random loot mechanic inspired by capsule toy machines.  
- **Pay-to-Win (P2W)** – A controversial system where players can buy advantages.  
- **Freemium** – A game that is free but includes monetization options.  
- **Soft Launch** – A limited early release to test a game’s market performance.  

---

### **Studio Culture & Slang**
- **Feature Creep** – When new ideas keep getting added, delaying release.  
- **Gold Plating** – Over-polishing a feature instead of shipping it.  
- **Spaghetti Code** – Messy, unorganized programming that’s hard to fix.  
- **Refactor** – Rewriting or optimizing code for better performance.  
- **Smoke Test** – A quick test to see if a feature works.  
- **Dogfooding** – The team playing their own game to find issues.  
- **Pixel F***ing** – Overly obsessing over minor visual details.  
- **Jank** – Clunky, unpolished gameplay that feels awkward.  
- **Brain Dump** – Rapidly writing down all ideas to sort later.  
- **Devsplaining** – A developer over-explaining mechanics to non-devs.  
- **Launch Hype** – The marketing buzz around a game’s release.

### **Game Development Slang & Studio Culture**  

- **Bug Factory** – A term for a developer known for introducing a lot of bugs in their code.  
- **Crunch Mode / Death March** – A brutal period of overtime to finish a project before a deadline.  
- **Smoke & Mirrors** – Tricks used to make the game look better than it actually is (e.g., clever lighting, camera tricks).  
- **Happy Path** – The ideal way a player is expected to play through a game without breaking anything.  
- **Sad Path** – When a player does something unexpected and causes the game to break.  
- **Rubberbanding** – When a game or network connection tries to correct lag, causing sudden position shifts.  
- **Scope Creep** – The project getting bigger and more complex as more features keep getting added.  
- **Yak Shaving** – Wasting time fixing minor issues that don’t impact the bigger picture.  
- **Gremlins** – Mysterious, unexplainable bugs that appear and disappear at random.  
- **Wonky** – When a game mechanic feels off or unpolished.  
- **Uncanny Valley** – When characters or animations look almost realistic but feel creepy instead.  
- **Brain Drain** – When too many developers leave a studio, causing knowledge loss.  
- **Cutting Room Floor** – Features or assets that were removed from the final game.  
- **Sacred Cow** – A feature or idea that no one is allowed to remove, even if it’s bad.  
- **Glass Cannon** – A high-damage but fragile character, enemy, or feature.  
- **Eldritch Code** – Old, cryptic, or overly complex code that no one understands anymore.  
- **Black Hole Task** – A development task that seems small but keeps sucking up time endlessly.  
- **GDC Special** – A rushed prototype or vertical slice made just in time for a demo at an event like GDC (Game Developers Conference).  
- **Banana For Scale** – A placeholder asset or reference used in level design for size comparison.  
- **Sea of Doors** – A level design with a ton of doors, but most don’t open.  
- **NPC Energy** – When someone acts robotic or predictable, like a bad AI character.  
- **Rubber Duck Debugging** – Explaining a bug to an inanimate object (like a rubber duck) to help figure it out.  
- **Monday Morning Patch** – A last-minute fix that breaks more things than it solves.  
- **Zombie Feature** – A feature that was cut but somehow keeps coming back into development.  
- **Meat Grinder** – A section of a game or workflow that is frustrating or punishing.  
- **Bleeding Edge** – Super new or risky technology that hasn’t been tested much yet.  
- **Frankenbuild** – A barely functional game build made by stitching together various unfinished features.  
- **Murder Wall** – A whiteboard full of sticky notes, red lines, and complex design ideas that look like a detective’s crime investigation board.  

---

### **Programming & Technical Slang**  
- **Spaghetti Code** – Messy, unstructured code that’s hard to debug.  
- **Code Rot** – When old code starts causing issues because no one has maintained it.  
- **Magic Numbers** – Random numbers in the code with no clear explanation for why they exist.  
- **God Object** – A piece of code that controls way too many things, making it a nightmare to manage.  
- **Null Pointer Hell** – A flood of errors caused by referencing something that doesn’t exist.  
- **Heisenbug** – A bug that disappears when you try to debug it.  
- **Schrödinger’s Bug** – A bug that both exists and doesn’t exist depending on who's testing it.  
- **Rubber Band AI** – AI that adjusts difficulty based on the player's performance (common in racing games).  
- **Code Gremlin** – A mysterious bug that no one can figure out.  
- **Ghost in the Machine** – An unexplained issue that seems to fix itself.  
- **Refactorpocalypse** – When a major overhaul of the codebase causes widespread chaos.  
- **Y2K Moment** – When something in the game breaks because of an overlooked numerical or time-related limit.  
- **Bit Rot** – When things in the game slowly stop working for seemingly no reason over time.  

---

### **Game Design & Art Slang**  
- **Feel-Good Physics** – Tweaks to physics that make gameplay feel more satisfying (e.g., extra jump height, slight air control).  
- **Hit Scan vs. Projectile** – "Hit Scan" weapons instantly register damage (like a laser), while "Projectile" weapons have travel time (like an arrow).  
- **Juice / Game Feel** – The polish that makes actions in a game feel impactful (e.g., camera shake, sound effects).  
- **Nerf / Buff** – Weakening or strengthening a game mechanic or character.  
- **Clown Shoes Physics** – When movement or animations look exaggerated and goofy.  
- **Z-Fighting** – A graphical issue where two textures flicker because they’re too close together.  
- **Mocap Jank** – Awkward or unnatural motion capture animation.  
- **Paper Design** – A game feature or system that sounds great on paper but doesn’t actually work well in-game.  
- **Ketchup Mechanic** – A feature that helps losing players catch up.  
- **Hard Lock vs. Soft Lock** – A "hard lock" completely breaks the game, while a "soft lock" traps the player in an unwinnable situation.  
- **Playtesting Hell** – When a game is stuck in endless playtesting without meaningful fixes.  
- **Cheese / Cheesing** – Using an unintended or overpowered strategy to beat a challenge easily.  
- **Jank** – Unpolished, weird, or clunky game mechanics.  
- **Smoke Test** – A quick, basic test to see if a game build is functional.  
- **Fridge Horror** – When a game seems normal at first, but thinking about it more makes it unsettling.  

---

### **Marketing & Monetization Slang**  
- **Whale** – A player who spends a ton of money on microtransactions.  
- **Dolphin** – A mid-tier spender in free-to-play games.  
- **Kraken** – A step above a whale; someone who spends absurd amounts of money.  
- **F2P Bait** – A free game designed to get players to spend money on premium content.  
- **Gacha Hell** – A term used in games with loot-box mechanics where players spend too much trying to get a rare item.  
- **Skinner Box** – A game design that keeps players addicted through rewards and psychological tricks.  
- **Content Drought** – A period where no new updates or expansions are released.  
- **Live Service Fatigue** – Burnout from games that demand constant engagement.  
- **Day-One DLC** – Downloadable content available at launch, often seen as greedy.  
- **Battle Pass FOMO** – Fear of missing out on seasonal rewards, pushing players to grind or spend money.  
- **Streamer Bait** – A game designed to be entertaining for streamers and viral content.  
- **Launch Window Wars** – Studios scrambling to release their game before a competitor.  
- **Hype Train** – Intense excitement before a game’s release.

Here’s the **completed list with definitions** for the remaining terms:

---

### **Common Video Game Studio Slang Terms & Definitions**

- **Crunch** – A period of intense overtime to meet deadlines, often controversial.  
- **Feature Creep** – When new ideas keep getting added, making the game bloated and delaying release.  
- **GDD (Game Design Document)** – A document outlining the game's mechanics, systems, and vision, serving as a blueprint for development.  
- **TDD (Technical Design Document)** – A document detailing the technical specifications needed to implement a design in code.  
- **Ghost Bug** – An error or glitch in the game’s code that disappears and reappears unpredictably, making it difficult to fix.  
- **Hitbox** – The invisible area that detects collisions with other objects, used for damage, interaction, and physics.  
- **Juice or Secret Sauce** – The extra polish that makes actions feel satisfying (e.g., screen shake, dynamic effects, sound feedback).  
- **Nerf (bat) / Buff** – Weakening ("nerfing") or strengthening ("buffing") a character, weapon, or mechanic for balance in a game.  
- **Fragile/Spaghetti Code** – Messy, unstructured programming that is difficult to debug and prone to breaking.  
- **Rubberbanding** – Sudden position shifts due to network lag, often in multiplayer games, where a character snaps back to a previous position.  
- **LOD (Level of Detail)** – A graphical optimization technique where object detail is reduced at a distance to improve performance.  
- **Hard Lock vs. Soft Lock** – A "hard lock" crashes the game or prevents further progress, while a "soft lock" traps the player in an unwinnable situation without crashing.  
- **Vertical Slice** – A small, polished section of a game showcasing all core mechanics, often used for pitching or internal review.  
- **Graybox/Whitebox** – A rough, untextured version of a level used in early development for playtesting layouts and mechanics.  
- **Live Service** – A game designed for ongoing updates, events, and monetization beyond its initial release.  
- **DLC (Downloadable Content)** – Extra content released post-launch, such as expansions, characters, or missions.  
- **Gacha** – A loot-box-style system where players spend money or in-game currency for random rewards, often in mobile or free-to-play games.  
- **Metagame (Meta)** – The most effective strategy or set of mechanics currently dominating competitive play, often shaped by balance updates.  
- **Playtesting** – The process of testing a game to find bugs, improve balance, and refine mechanics through iterative feedback.  
- **Ghost in the Machine** – A mysterious bug that appears and disappears unpredictably, making it difficult to track down.  
- **Draws and Calls** – The amount of memory the game uses to load assets and execute mechanics on screen at any given moment.  
- **Pri0 (Priority Zero)** – A game-breaking bug or task that must be fixed immediately before anything else.  
- **Treadmill** – The ongoing process of maintaining and creating new content/events for a live-service game.  
- **On Rails** – A game or section where the player follows a predetermined path with little to no freedom of movement.  
- **Open World** – A game environment where players can explore freely without being restricted to a linear path or specific objectives.  
- **Netcode** – A derogatory term referring to poor network programming that causes lag, rubberbanding, and synchronization issues in multiplayer games.  
- **Bullet Sponge** – A poorly designed enemy that takes an excessive number of hits to kill without offering an engaging challenge.  
- **Mob** – Short for "mobile entity," referring to a non-player character (NPC) or enemy in a game.  
- **Tech Tree** – A structured progression system that allows players to unlock abilities, upgrades, or research new technologies.  
- **Core Loop** – The fundamental cycle of gameplay actions that players repeat (e.g., combat, collecting resources, upgrading gear).  
- **Golden Cohort** – A specific group of players considered ideal for playtesting or market analysis due to their engagement and spending habits.  
- **RNG (Random Number Generator)** – A system that introduces randomness into games, affecting things like loot drops, critical hits, and procedural generation.  
- **Min-Maxing** – Optimizing a character or playstyle by maximizing beneficial stats and minimizing weaknesses, often at the expense of balance or roleplay.  
- **Tanking** – A strategy where a character absorbs damage and protects teammates, common in RPGs and multiplayer games.  
- **Glass Cannon** – A character or build that deals high damage but has very low defense, making them fragile.  
- **Door Boss** – A frustrating obstacle (often an actual door or locked area) that prevents progress more than the real enemies do.  
- **One-Shot** – Killing an enemy or player in a single attack, often seen as overpowered or requiring a high level of optimization.  
- **Cheesing** – Exploiting a game mechanic, AI behavior, or level design to gain an unfair advantage with minimal effort.

### **Save Scumming** – The act of repeatedly saving and reloading a game to manipulate outcomes, such as getting a favorable result in RNG-based mechanics (e.g., critical hits, loot drops, dialogue choices). Often used in strategy games, RPGs, and roguelikes to avoid negative consequences or optimize success.
