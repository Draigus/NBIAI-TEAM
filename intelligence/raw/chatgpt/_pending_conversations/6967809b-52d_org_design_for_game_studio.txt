## 1) Are the reporting lines optimal?
No.

The main issues are structural, not cosmetic:

### A. Production appears mis-parented and fragmented
- The “Producer” role looks effectively parented through Finance and Ops (or at least routed that way). That is a delivery risk. Production should be accountable to the studio delivery leader (Executive Producer or equivalent), not Finance. Finance should be a strong partner, not the chain of command.
- “Executive Producer” looks disconnected from the core game build. If EP only owns QA and Writing, you get partial delivery control with no authority over the critical path.

What breaks: schedule ownership, tradeoff authority, dependency resolution.

### B. Platform is treated like an ops subteam, but it is product
- “Platform Team” under Finance and Ops is a red flag if the platform is central to the strategy. Platform needs product ownership and engineering leadership, with a clear GM-style accountability (scope, timeline, quality).

What breaks: platform roadmap clarity, partner commitments, tech priorities, and resourcing arguments that become finance debates instead of product decisions.

### C. Engineering span of control is too flat
- CTO directly over multiple engineering subteams (web dev, gameplay) with no explicit engineering managers or leads shown. If those are missing, CTO becomes a bottleneck and quality drops.

What breaks: code review quality, architecture coherence, hiring and mentoring, delivery predictability.

### D. Tech Art and VFX sit in a grey zone
- Technical Art and VFX are structurally between Engineering and Art. That is normal, but the chart must explicitly show the matrix: functional line for craft development, project line for delivery priorities.

What breaks: tool ownership, pipeline priorities, and “who says yes” on tech art constraints.

### E. Content disciplines are not cleanly grouped under game direction
- Sound design and writing are not clearly under Game Director ownership (even if they collaborate). Game Director should own the full player-facing content stack: design, narrative, audio, UX content, and moment-to-moment experience, with production enforcing delivery.

What breaks: creative cohesion and decision velocity.

## 2) Three design alternatives

### Alternative 1: Classic functional studio with a strong Executive Producer hub
Best when: you are early, team is under ~60, you need speed, and the product is still forming.

Structure:
- CEO/Creative Director
  - Executive Producer (single throat to choke for delivery across game + platform)
    - Producers (Game Producer, Tech Producer, possibly Platform Producer)
    - QA Lead (not just QA as individuals)
  - Game Director
    - Design (Level, Systems, Combat)
    - Narrative/Writing
    - Audio
  - CTO
    - Gameplay Engineering Lead
    - Platform/Services Engineering Lead (web, backend)
  - Art Director
    - Concept, Characters, Environment, Animation
    - Technical Art Lead (solid line to Art Director for people and craft, dotted to CTO for tooling priorities)
- Finance/Ops and Legal remain as staff functions.

Why it is better than current: clear delivery authority, clear creative authority, clearer engineering leadership, platform treated as a first-class product.

Key risk: functional silos. Producers must actively run cross-discipline dependency management.

### Alternative 2: Two-GM model: Platform GM and Game GM
Best when: the platform is a co-equal product and partner commitments are real, not aspirational.

Structure:
- CEO/Creative Director
  - GM Game (owns game outcomes)
    - Game Director
    - Game Producer
    - Gameplay Engineering Lead
    - Art Director
  - GM Platform (owns platform outcomes)
    - Platform Product Lead
    - Platform Producer
    - Platform Engineering Lead (web, backend, services)
    - Partner Ops or Integrations Lead (if partner work is material)
  - Shared Services: Finance/Ops, Legal, HR
  - CTO becomes a standards and architecture authority across both GMs, or sits inside platform depending on how technical the platform is.

Why it is better than current: eliminates platform being “owned by nobody,” avoids constant priority fights, makes partner delivery realistic.

Key risk: duplicated process and competing roadmaps unless CEO forces alignment through quarterly priorities.

### Alternative 3: Cross-functional “pods” (squads) with discipline homes
Best when: you are actively shipping features and need predictable throughput, especially for live ops, identity, and partner drops.

Structure:
- Discipline homes (for hiring, craft, performance):
  - Engineering, Art, Design, Production, QA
- Delivery pods (for shipping):
  - Core Gameplay Pod
  - Platform Identity Pod
  - Content and Rewards Pod (includes partner drops)
  - Tools and Pipeline Pod (Tech Art-heavy)
Each pod has:
- Pod Lead (often a senior discipline lead)
- Producer (delivery)
- Product Owner (scope and priority, could be GM or Director depending on size)

Why it is better than current: reduces handoffs, makes ownership obvious, tech art and tools stop being “everyone’s problem.”

Key risk: requires mature production and leadership. If you are too early, it can feel like process overhead.
