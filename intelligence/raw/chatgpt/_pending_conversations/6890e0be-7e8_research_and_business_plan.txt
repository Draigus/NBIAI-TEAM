Our business will thus generate revenue directly from the game’s player base. - **Technology and IP:** Develop our core technology (game client, server, Telegram integration) as a reusable platform for future titles or expansions. In the high scenario, the per-person cost is a bit higher (allowing for more senior hires and big marketing spend on top).

We will maintain **financial discipline** by monitoring the burn closely against our milestones and funding runway. Our aim is to give ourselves at least 18–24 months of runway post-fundraising. If we raise, for example, £3 million, a medium burn of ~£200k gives ~15 months runway – we’d then either augment with revenue (through early launch) or plan a bridge funding around month 12. We will also keep a **burn-down chart of cash** for investor reporting and internally trigger cost controls if burn deviates from plan. As a rule, we intend to **keep burn below 10% of cash reserves per month** in normal operation, which aligns with startup best practices (e.g. spending 5–10% of raised capital per month to ensure ~1–2 years runway【50†L359-L368】).

In summary, financially we are targeting a moderate burn path to reach launch, with options to tighten or accelerate depending on results. The studio will utilize scenario planning and remain flexible – if metrics post-launch are strong, we might deliberately move from medium to high burn to scale the game (spend more on user acquisition). Conversely, if we need more time, we have the low-burn plan as a fallback (e.g. delaying some hires, focusing on organic growth). This adaptive financial strategy, backed by data and industry benchmarks, will be communicated clearly to stakeholders to build confidence that we can **achieve sustainability or hit key growth metrics before needing additional funding**.

## 4. Production Framework

We will implement a **hybrid production methodology (“Agile-Fall” approach)** tailored for game development. This combines upfront planning discipline with iterative agile execution. In practice, that means we’ll use **stage-gated milestones** to track big picture progress (waterfall-style checkpoints at key project phases), while running **scrum sprint cycles** day-to-day to remain flexible on tasks and improvements.

**Phase Gates & Milestones:** At the macro level, we define clear development phases – *Pre-Production, Production, Alpha, Beta, Launch*. Each phase has specific exit criteria (feature completeness, performance benchmarks, etc.). We will hold milestone review meetings at the end of each phase (“**feature gates**”) to evaluate whether the game is ready to move to the next stage. For example, the end of Pre-Production must yield an approved Game Design Document and a playable prototype (“vertical slice”) that proves the core gameplay fun. Only if the gate review approves (meeting quality and fun targets) do we fully commit to Production. Similarly, we will have an **Alpha checkpoint** (internal feature-complete build), a **Beta gate** (external or soft-launch ready build), and a **Launch readiness** review with criteria like stability and KPIs from beta. This staged approach is classic in game development to control risk and ensure quality at launch.

**Agile Sprints:** Within each phase, we operate in **bi-weekly sprints (2 weeks per sprint)** following Scrum practices. Each sprint starts with a planning meeting where the team commits to a set of user stories or tasks from the backlog. We will maintain a prioritized **product backlog** of features and improvements, which the Product Owner (likely our Producer or Creative Director) continuously refines. Short sprints allow us to get frequent playable builds and adjust to feedback or new ideas rapidly【39†L63-L72】. In game dev, this agility is crucial – for instance, if playtesting reveals a feature isn’t fun, we can pivot in the next sprint rather than discovering it too late. We expect to release an **internal build at the end of every sprint** for testing and team review.

**Daily Stand-ups & Collaboration:** The team will hold **daily stand-up meetings** (15-minute scrum meetings) every morning, either in person or via video for remote members. Each person briefly states what they did, will do, and blockers. This keeps everyone synchronized and surfaces issues early. Given our hybrid model, we’ll have a conference call running so remote teammates join stand-up virtually alongside those in the Cambridge office. We’ll also utilize Scrum boards (in Jira or Shortcut) to visualize task progress (To Do / In Progress / Done), so everyone sees the sprint status at a glance.

To complement stand-ups, we foster a culture of open communication: developers and artists will frequently sync throughout the day, and we’ll use Slack for quick discussions. We intend to create **cross-disciplinary “feature teams”** for efficiency – e.g. for a sprint focused on a new combat feature, a small team of a designer, programmer, and artist will closely collaborate (often in a shared Slack channel or breakout meetings) to deliver that feature. This is similar to the “Delivery Team” model used in live game studios to keep teams agile even as they grow【38†L93-L102】.

**Agile Tools & Ceremonies:** In addition to stand-ups and sprint planning, we will do **sprint demos/reviews** and **retrospectives**. At sprint’s end, the team will demo the new features or content to the whole group. This provides a morale boost and ensures visibility of progress to stakeholders. We’ll gather feedback from the Creative Director and possibly invite a few friendly testers to these demos in later stages. After the demo, a **sprint retrospective** lets the team discuss what went well and what to improve (e.g. we might identify that our task estimates were off or communication could be better – and then adjust our process next sprint). Continuous improvement of our development process is a key agile principle【39†L69-L77】【39†L85-L94】.

**Documentation vs. Flexibility:** In pre-production, we will create high-level design docs and a project roadmap (milestone definitions, major features), effectively using a **waterfall approach to initial design**. This gives us clear guidelines and scope upfront【39†L135-L143】. However, once production sprints start, we expect changes and new ideas – agile welcomes that change through iterative development and feedback loops【39†L63-L72】. For example, we might add a new mini-game if testing shows players want more variety. Our methodology explicitly plans for and allows such changes. We’ll keep documents living (updated as we iterate) rather than carved in stone. 

**Feature Pipelining and QA Integration:** We plan to break features into small, testable increments that can be completed in a sprint or two. For each feature, we’ll define “definitions of done” including basic QA testing. Our QA testers will be embedded into the agile process – ideally, QA is involved in planning to advise on testability, and they will test new features as they land (rather than only at the end)【38†L75-L83】. This aligns with modern continuous testing practices and avoids the crunch of leaving all QA to the final months. We’ll likely maintain an automated build pipeline (CI/CD) to get nightly builds for QA to smoke-test regularly. Issues found are fed back immediately into the backlog. By having QA and possibly a Community representative involved throughout, we ensure a feedback loop not just at formal milestones but continuously.

**Implementation of Hybrid Agile-Fall:** Concretely, our production framework might look like this: We begin with a ~4-week **planning sprint** in pre-production to produce the roadmap and prototype goals (waterfall style planning). Once approved, we transition to **development sprints** of 2 weeks, continually delivering small increments. Major milestones (vertical slice, alpha, beta) occur at predefined calendar points (e.g. end of Month 3, Month 9, etc.) where we pause and evaluate via milestone reviews – akin to mini-waterfalls where scope is reassessed against goals. After each review, we may re-plan the backlog (e.g. cut or add features) which is then executed via further sprints. This **hybrid model provides structure with accountability (through milestones) while preserving flexibility (through agile sprints)**. It is a proven approach in game studios to handle the creative uncertainty of game development【37†L1-L9】【39†L135-L143】. By integrating both, we reduce the risk of chaos (pure agile with no long-term plan) and the risk of inflexibility (pure waterfall that can’t adapt if the design needs change).

**Standards & Checkpoints:** We will institute additional checkpoints like **feature-complete freeze** (no new features after Beta start, only polish/bugfix – this is a common practice to stabilize the game), and **content lock** a few weeks before launch (stop adding new assets, focus on optimization). Daily builds and weekly playtests will act as ongoing checkpoints too – we want the game playable at all times. If any sprint deliverable threatens to slip, the Producer will use agile techniques (re-prioritize scope or swap tasks between sprints) to keep milestones on track. 

In summary, our production framework is designed to be **agile at its core**, enabling continuous integration of feedback and rapid iteration (we will “release early, release often” internally【38†L71-L79】), with an overlay of **waterfall-like milestone governance** to satisfy the needs of scheduling, investor oversight, and go/no-go decisions at key junctures. This approach will guide the team through an 18-month development cycle efficiently, and position us to transition into live operations smoothly post-launch (where agile processes will continue for ongoing updates).

## 5. 18-Month Milestone Roadmap

The following is a proposed **18-month roadmap** from project inception (Month 0) through launch (Month 18) and into early live operations. This timeline is tailored for a Telegram-first game studio, factoring in hiring ramp-up, tooling setup, development phases, testing, and marketing:

- **Month 0–1: Studio Setup & Initial Planning** – Finalize company formation, secure seed funding (if not already done). Founders (CEO, Creative Director, CTO) are in place from day 0. In the first weeks, secure a small office in Cambridge (or coworking space) and set up basic infrastructure: development workstations, version control repository, project management software (e.g. Jira), and communication tools (Slack, Google Workspace). Begin the **Concept & Discovery phase**: refine the game concept, core mechanics, and technology stack (confirm using Unity engine and Telegram Bot APIs, choose cloud backend). **Milestone:** *Project Kickoff* – by end of Month 1, we have a clear high-level game concept, a one-page design vision, and the team’s development environment ready.

- **Month 2–3: Pre-Production** – Key early hires join: a lead developer, a game designer, and a 2D artist. The small team focuses on creating a **Playable Prototype (Vertical Slice)**. We implement a basic core loop of the game on Telegram (for example, a simple gameplay scenario in text/graphic form via a Telegram bot). Simultaneously, the art style is explored through concept art, and we design the preliminary game economy and progression on paper. We also establish **tooling and workflows**: set up continuous integration (automated build pipeline), define coding standards, and create a basic design wiki (Notion or Confluence) for documentation. By Month 3, we aim for an **Internal Prototype Demo** – a minimal version of the game running on Telegram that we can play in-house. We will conduct an internal review (feature gate) to validate that the core gameplay is fun and technically feasible on the platform. **Milestone (end of M3):** *Greenlight to Production* – assuming prototype feedback is positive, we approve moving to full production. (If not, we’d iterate quickly or pivot within pre-production.)

- **Month 4–6: Main Production Kickoff & Team Scaling** – Expand the team to ~15+ members. Additional engineers are hired (client and backend), more artists (UI artist, perhaps a second general artist), and a dedicated product/project manager comes on to run sprints. Development now proceeds in earnest on all core features of the game. In this period, we build out the **Core Gameplay Systems** (combat, resource management, etc., whatever the game’s pillars are) and the **Telegram integration** (bot commands, social features). We also start developing the backend services (setting up databases, servers on AWS). Meanwhile, we implement foundational elements like user account system (likely leveraging Telegram login) and basic UI/UX flows. **Tooling during this phase:** project management is fully in swing – we hold sprint plannings, use Jira to track tasks, and set up a bug-tracking database. On the hiring side, by Month 6 we bring in a QA Lead or at least a dedicated tester to prepare for upcoming testing needs. We also on-board a part-time marketing/community consultant to start shaping our community strategy. **Milestone (end of M6):** *First Playable (Alpha 0)* – the game is now playable end-to-end with placeholder art. We can run a full game session on Telegram among the team. We’ll have an internal team playtest and **feature freeze on any new major features** at this point (ensuring we focus on completing existing ones).

- **Month 7–9: Content Creation & Alpha Testing** – With core systems in place, the focus shifts to creating **content and improving quality**. Artists produce final assets (illustrations, icons, animations) to replace placeholders. Designers add content such as levels, story dialogue, or item designs as applicable. We implement the **meta-game features** (progression, leveling, in-app purchase store, etc.) now that core gameplay is solid. By Month 8, most of the team is producing or polishing content. In parallel, we begin **internal QA testing** in earnest: the QA testers run daily smoke tests on new builds and start logging bugs. We set up a closed **“friends and family” alpha test** group – perhaps 20-30 people (some from our personal networks or even friendly folks in Telegram gaming communities) who try the game in a controlled environment. This provides early feedback on balance and tech performance. We use this feedback to iterate – for example, if a tutorial is confusing, we improve it in the next sprint. During this time, the community manager (who should be hired by now) establishes our **Telegram presence**: creating an official Telegram channel/group for the game, starting to post concept art, dev updates, and engaging potential fans. This is the **pre-launch community seeding** phase – building excitement among early adopters. We might also quietly open **pre-registrations** or a waiting list bot to capture interested users. **Milestone (end of M9):** *Alpha Complete* – all major features are implemented and the game content is nearing launch volume. The game should be **content-complete** by now, moving forward we focus on polish, performance, and bug fixing.

- **Month 10–12: Beta, Soft Launch & Marketing Prep** – This is a critical period of **testing, iteration, and marketing ramp-up**. We plan a **Soft Launch (Closed Beta)** around Month 10: we’ll invite a larger group of external testers, possibly a few hundred players, to play the game via Telegram. Since Telegram games can be global, instead of region-locking (as one would on app stores), we might do this by invite codes distributed on our Telegram channel or via a sign-up form. The goal is to observe real user behavior, measure retention and monetization KPIs in a small sample, and catch any scale issues. During soft launch, engineering will be monitoring server performance, fixing any crashes, and ensuring the game can handle concurrency. We’ll likely iterate rapidly – deploying updates weekly to address feedback (e.g. if one feature is not used, perhaps it needs rework before global launch).

  Meanwhile, **marketing moves into high gear**. By Month 10, our Marketing Manager will execute the pre-launch marketing plan: releasing a teaser trailer or gameplay clips on social media, starting a developer blog series, and continuing community building. We will leverage Telegram-specific marketing – for instance, cross-promotions with large Telegram groups or channels that align with our target audience. If budget permits, we’ll run a small **user acquisition campaign** in this soft launch (maybe targeting a specific region or community) to test advertising channels and cost-per-install, though on Telegram the dynamic is a bit different than typical app installs. We also begin outreach to press and influencers: contacting gaming news sites for a possible feature story near launch, and lining up a few Twitch or YouTube creators to play the game at launch (if the game format suits streaming).

  By Month 12, we aim to have **beta KPIs** that meet our targets (e.g. Day-1 retention, average session length, etc.). The team fixes final bugs and integrates any last-minute improvements gleaned from the beta. **Milestone (end of M12):** *Beta/Soft Launch Review* – a go/no-go meeting to decide if the game is ready for launch. If metrics are good and no show-stopping issues, we greenlight the global launch. If not, we might extend the soft launch a bit (which our plan allows, as we have runway to Month 18) to address issues.

- **Month 13–15: Final Polishing & Pre-Launch Marketing Push** – In this window, we polish the game to a launch-quality state. This includes optimizing performance (both client and server) – e.g. ensuring the bot response times on Telegram are snappy, optimizing images/assets for faster load, and making sure the game runs smoothly on lower-end devices. We also implement any remaining minor features that were deferred (for example, additional social features or cosmetic content that wasn’t critical for beta). The UI/UX is refined based on tester feedback (maybe some tutorials were unclear – we fix that, etc.). QA does thorough regression testing on all devices. We might also undergo an external **security audit or penetration test** on the backend since we’ll be handling user data and payments.

  Marketing at this stage goes into its final phase: **countdown to launch**. We will likely announce the official launch date about 4–6 weeks in advance on our communication channels. Our social media will publish more frequently, revealing features or characters of the game to build hype. We engage influencers to preview the game (under embargo until launch). If budget allows, we prepare some launch advertisements (perhaps a short promo video) to run on Telegram’s advertising platform or other social media. We’ll also ensure our **App Store presence** (if needed for any app wrapper or if we distribute through Telegram’s Game Platform) is ready with descriptions, screenshots, etc. Essentially, this period is analogous to “press tour” time for a bigger game – but for us, it might be more digital and community-focused. By Month 15, the aim is that a decent-sized audience is aware of the game and eager for release, evidenced by growing Telegram channel members, email signups, or pre-registrations.

  Internally, the team is on “**code freeze**” by late Month 15 – meaning we stop adding new code except critical fixes. This allows a stable build to be finalized. The live ops manager prepares the first few live events or updates post-launch (to ensure we have fresh content in the pipeline). Customer support tooling (FAQ, support bot, etc.) is set up so we can handle player issues at launch.

- **Month 16–18: Launch and Live Operations Kickoff** – **Launch occurs around Month 16**, give or take, based on beta feedback readiness. On launch day, we will officially open the game to the public on Telegram – likely by publishing the bot for anyone to add, posting the link in relevant channels, and sending press releases to media. The team will be on deck monitoring systems. We anticipate a spike of new users, so our backend devops will watch servers for scaling (auto-scaling rules should be in place). The community manager will be very active welcoming new players on the official Telegram group, answering questions, and funneling any critical issues to the team (sort of a war-room scenario for the first 48 hours).

  Assuming a smooth launch, by Month 17 we transition into **live ops mode**. This means the development cadence shifts to frequent updates: perhaps minor patches weekly for fixes and balance, and a major content update every month or two. We’ll schedule the first in-game event (for example, a special limited-time challenge) a few weeks after launch to keep engagement high. We also continue marketing post-launch: sharing player testimonials, positive reviews, and possibly ramping up paid user acquisition if the early metrics show good LTV. By Month 18, we will analyze our first month of global data and prepare a plan for the next 6 months based on it (are we focusing on scaling user acquisition, or improving retention features, etc.).

  In terms of team operations, after launch we might do a bit of reorganization: some developers may start on new feature development for season 2 or expansion content, while others handle live bugfixes. We ensure our support and community team is adequately staffed to handle the player base (if growth is beyond expectation, we might quickly onboard another support rep or outsource overflow support). We’ll also have a **post-mortem meeting** after launch to document lessons learned during the development cycle – this helps improve future production processes.

  **Milestone (Month 18):** *Post-Launch Evaluation* – we review the project against the initial goals. Are we hitting the player numbers and revenue we forecast? We report these to investors and use them to strategize next steps (e.g. pursuing additional funding for scaling if we have a hit, or focusing on improvements if metrics need work). At this point the game should be in a steady state with regular content updates and a growing community. The studio’s goal becomes long-term **live operations success** – keeping players engaged, expanding the universe, and possibly starting R&D on a second title using the same platform (depending on team capacity and company vision).

Throughout this 18-month roadmap, we remain attentive to **modern game studio practices**. We actively foster community feedback loops (inviting Telegram community input even before launch – for example, polls for feature ideas during beta). We utilize analytics tools from early beta onward to drive data-informed decisions (e.g. if analytics show a certain feature isn’t used, we redesign it). Our timeline also accounts for **team well-being**: we plan work in a way to avoid sustained crunch. If a milestone is in danger, we’ll reassess scope rather than overwork the team, as maintaining velocity sustainably is a priority.

Finally, we integrate key **operational tools and considerations** at each stage:
- During development, we use **project management and collaboration tools** (Jira for tasks, Slack for communication, Notion for documentation) to keep our hybrid team aligned. This was set up early and continuously used.
- For quality, we employ a combination of manual QA and automated testing (unit tests, possibly integration tests for the game server) with each sprint.
- We prepare for live ops by implementing the ability to update the game content without app store redeployment (likely all logic is server-side or in the Telegram bot, which allows fast updates – an advantage of Telegram-first approach).
- We ensure **legal and compliance readiness** by launch: terms of service, age ratings (if needed), and all vendor contracts (e.g. any licensed music or third-party tech) in place.

This roadmap is aggressive but achievable, leveraging the lightweight nature of a Telegram game (scope is narrower than a console/PC game) and a highly focused team. By adhering to it, The Sarge Universe studio aims to deliver a successful game to market in 1.5 years while laying the foundation for ongoing operations and future growth.

## Additional Operational Considerations

*(Industry-standard practices for modern hybrid studios, legal setup, vendor management, and tooling have been interwoven in the sections above. For clarity, key points are summarized here.)*

- **Hybrid Team Workflow:** We will cultivate an inclusive culture for remote and on-site staff. This includes supporting flexible hours (especially to accommodate any remote staff in different time zones), scheduling regular all-hands meetings via video, and perhaps quarterly in-person meetups for the whole team to build camaraderie. Most studios post-2020 are embracing such hybrid practices – for instance, only ~12% of game developers now work on-site full-time【23†L133-L141】, so our approach keeps us competitive in hiring.

- **Communication & Collaboration Tools:** Our tool stack is chosen to optimize remote collaboration:
  - *Slack* for team communication (organized by channels for each feature or department). Slack is widely used in tech and game companies for its real-time chat and integration capabilities【43†L184-L193】.
  - *Notion or Confluence* as a knowledge base where we document design decisions, technical docs, and company policies – ensuring information is accessible to all, anywhere【43†L195-L204】.
  - *Jira (Atlassian)* or an equivalent (Shortcut/Clubhouse as referenced【43†L201-L209】) for issue and task tracking. This keeps everyone aware of work items and progress.
  - *Version Control:* Git repositories (likely on GitHub or GitLab) for code, and Perforce or Git LFS for large assets. Proper version control is mandatory for a distributed team to collaborate on code and art without conflict.
  - *Cloud Collaboration:* We’ll use Google Drive or SharePoint for sharing documents/spreadsheets securely among the team. Also, tools like Figma or Adobe XD for collaborative UI/UX design can be used so designers and artists can work concurrently with feedback.
  - *Project Management & Agile Boards:* The team will have an online Scrum/Kanban board. Whether in Jira or Trello, this visual board will track tasks/status. It aids remote transparency – each member can see what others are working on and the sprint goals.
  - *QA & Testing Tools:* A bug tracking database (Jira can serve this purpose with workflows). Additionally, we may use test case management software (like TestRail) to organize QA test suites, and crash reporting tools (if applicable for any mobile builds).
  - *Remote Collaboration & Review:* For reviewing builds and giving feedback asynchronously, we can employ screen recording tools or Evercast for remote playtesting sessions【42†L1-L4】. Art reviews might use a tool like ShotGrid or Frame.io for giving notes on assets【42†L1-L4】. Code is reviewed via pull requests on Git to maintain code quality among remote devs.

- **Security & IT for Remote Work:** We will implement VPN access to any internal servers and enforce 2FA on all critical accounts (code repos, admin dashboards) to mitigate risks with remote access. Employee laptops will have full-disk encryption and centrally managed antivirus/endpoint security. It’s industry standard to protect game assets and code, as breaches or leaks can be catastrophic. Our IT policy will cover acceptable use and data handling (especially since we might handle personal data of users, security is key).

- **Legal & Compliance:** As noted, we’ll leverage VGTR and ensure we meet its criteria (cultural test paperwork, etc.). We’ll also be mindful of PEGI ratings if needed (Telegram games might not require formal ratings, but we will design content for at least 13+ audience given Telegram’s user base). We will comply with **GDPR** for any EU user data – likely by having a clear privacy policy and giving users rights to their data. If the game has any virtual currency, we’ll ensure the terms of use cover virtual goods transactions properly (to avoid legal issues around refunds, etc.). 

- **Vendor Contracts:** All outsourcing engagements will have proper contracts in place specifying deliverables, timelines, ownership (work-for-hire so that we own any outsourced art/code), and confidentiality (NDAs). We will likely engage a lawyer to draw a standard contractor agreement template. We will also verify that any third-party software or SDKs (e.g. analytics SDKs, perhaps Unity Asset Store plugins) are properly licensed for commercial use to avoid infringement. Vendor management will be aided by tools as well – we might use Asana or simple spreadsheets to track vendor tasks and budgets alongside our internal work.

- **Operational Metrics & Monitoring:** Being a live online game, we will set up dashboards to monitor metrics like CCU (concurrent users), server CPU/memory, error rates, etc., using services like Datadog or CloudWatch. This is operationally important to catch issues early (for example, a spike in errors after a deploy, or unusual drop in DAU signaling a problem). The live ops team will also monitor player sentiment on Telegram groups and social media daily as an early warning system for issues.

- **Business Continuity:** We will have contingency plans such as regular backups of code and assets (with offsite cloud backup). We will cross-train team members to cover critical responsibilities (so if one person is unavailable, the project doesn’t stall). This is part of risk management for a small studio.

- **Investor Reporting and Governance:** Since this plan is also for investors/stakeholders, we will institute a practice of monthly updates post-launch (covering key metrics and milestones achieved) and quarterly board meetings. Internally, we’ll review financials vs. budget monthly to ensure burn rate matches our plan and adjust if necessary (e.g. if we’re trending over budget, take corrective action like pausing hires or reducing discretionary spend).

By grounding decisions in *data and best practices* from the UK games industry and beyond, we have confidence in our estimates and approach. We’ve double-checked key assumptions (office costs, salaries, burn rates) against trusted sources to ensure accuracy and realism. This plan is designed to convince investors that we have a **solid operational roadmap** to build a successful Telegram-first game studio in Cambridge, with manageable risk and clear milestones to measure our progress【17†L48-L56】【50†L351-L359】.
