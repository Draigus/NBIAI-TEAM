**How to use this grid**

* Start with the “Small” column when soft-launching.

For a 180-person studio shipping and running a live-service racing game, set aside roughly **30 % of total headcount (≈ 55 people)** as the always-on “live game” crew.  

| Function | Full-time live staff (target) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Live Ops leadership & release mgmt | 4 | Live Ops Director 1, Live Producer 2, Release Manager 1 |
| Backend engineering & SRE | 10 | Scalable lobbies, leaderboards, seasonal reset scripts, uptime targets |
| Data science & analytics | 5 | Telemetry pipeline, churn prediction, A/B test design |
| Product, economy & monetization | 5 | Battle-pass tuning, shop rotations, event rewards |
| Live content & design | 6 | New car variants, track props, limited-time modes |
| QA (live environment) | 8 | Hotfix regression, platform cert, automated smoke suites |
| Community & player support | 10 | Community mgr 2, Social 1, Support lead 1, CS agents 6 |
| CRM & user acquisition | 4 | CRM mgr 2, UA analyst 1, Influencer/creator mgr 1 |
| Security / anti-cheat | 2 | Detection + ban waves, exploit triage |
| **Total live-service staff** | **54** | ~30 % of the 180-person studio |

**Why 30 %?**

* Racing titles need continuous car and track drops, competitive seasons, and strict uptime for multiplayer lobbies.  
* A dedicated live pod lets core devs stay focussed on large expansions while live staff run events, monitor data, and push weekly fixes.  
* The split above keeps enough engineers for real-time features and enough community boots on the ground for esports and social channels.

Tune the exact counts to your cadence (weekly events vs monthly) and outsourcing strategy, but keeping live capacity around one-third of total headcount is the norm for modern racing live-ops.
