Short answer: yes. A website is still one of the most useful tools you have for spreading and shaping a consultancy brand, but only if you treat it as a *proof and clarity engine*, not just an online brochure.

Here is how it actually helps in practice:

1. **Credibility and “are you real?” check**  
   For most buyers, after hearing your name they do three things: Google you, check your site, then check LinkedIn.  
   - No site or a dated site quietly signals “small, unproven, or inactive.”  
   - A clear, modern site with specific offers and real proof signals “serious, investable, trustworthy.”

2. **Controlling your story**  
   Social platforms and directories are noisy and constrained. Your site is the one place you fully control:  
   - What you do and do *not* do  
   - Your positioning, niche, and “who we are for”  
   - The story you want prospects to carry into internal discussions  
   That story is the core of your brand. The website is where it lives in a stable, sharable form.

3. **Making referrals work harder**  
   In consulting, most good clients still come through referrals. A site multiplies the value of each one:  
   - “Here is their website, have a look at the case studies” is an easy share.  
   - When the internal stakeholder you never meet does their own due diligence, the site does the heavy lifting.

4. **Proof of value, not just list of services**  
   This is where many consultancy sites fail. What actually spreads your brand is not the logo and colours. It is:  
   - Clear outcomes: “We help studios do X, Y, Z.”  
   - Case studies: before and after, numbers if possible.  
   - Point of view: short articles, frameworks, or tools that show you think deeply about your niche.  

   That proof gives people something to talk about and remember, which is what “brand” really means.

5. **Search and discoverability (SEO, but realistic)**  
   You probably will not win on generic keywords like “consultant” or “strategy.” You *can* win on:  
   - Specific niches: “game analytics consulting”, “live ops KPI review”, “studio organisational health audit”  
   - Specific problems: “how to fix player churn in F2P game”  
   This brings in a trickle of highly relevant visitors over time and quietly reinforces your brand positioning.

6. **Sales enablement, not just marketing**  
   A good consultancy site is also a sales tool:  
   - It gives prospects something to circulate internally.  
   - It answers predictable objections in advance.  
   - It provides simple next steps: book a call, download a framework, request a diagnostic.  
   That shortens sales cycles and makes you easier to “sell” inside client organisations.

7. **Hiring and partnership signal**  
   Even if you are small, partners, associates, and senior clients will look at your site to judge whether you are serious enough to align with. A sharp site makes it easier to attract good collaborators who reinforce your brand.

8. **Where a website matters *less***  
   It is *less* critical if:  
   - Your work comes from a tiny, closed network of long term clients.  
   - You are fully booked and have zero interest in growing, raising fees, or diversifying risk.  
   Even then, a small, clean one page site is still useful as a sanity check and a place to point people.

9. **What is “good enough” for a consultancy**  
   You do not need a giant site. You do need:  
   - A tight home page that states who you serve, what problems you solve, and how.  
   - 3 to 5 services or “offers” written in outcome language, not jargon.  
   - 2 to 6 case studies or “client stories” with concrete results.  
   - A clear “work with us” path and contact options.  
   - Basic search hygiene so people can actually find you.

If you tell me a bit about your consultancy and what kind of clients you target, I can sketch a very lean site structure that actually spreads *your* brand rather than just existing for the sake of it.

These are changes that increase how much brand value the site broadcasts without blowing up the structure.

### A. Sharpen the hero into a games-first statement

Right now:

> “NBI is your partner for designing tailored strategies, scalable systems, and insight-driven solutions that help organizations move faster, decide smarter, and deliver with confidence.” citeturn0view0  

Suggested direction for the hero copy (illustrative, you can tune wording):

**Headline**

**Sub-line**

- Makes clear you are partners to studio and platform leadership, not a dev house.  
- Inserts the “we do not build games; we enable them” brand guardrail directly into the core story.

### B. Reorder and reframe the three service pillars

Consider rebalancing the three pillars on the home page so they tell a clearer story.

For example:

1. **Digital Entertainment**  
   “From concept to live service, NBI helps you understand your players, shape your roadmap, and make better bets across your portfolio. We do not design or build games; we make them perform.”

2. **Data Systems and Decision Intelligence**  
   “We design data models, pipelines and decision frameworks that turn raw telemetry into trusted insight for product, marketing and leadership.”

3. **Organisational Performance**  
   “We help studio and central teams work with less friction and more clarity, from leadership alignment to ways of working.”

You keep the same three buckets, but their copy becomes explicitly games-anchored and outcome-led.

### C. Turn the logo wall and testimonials into brand stories

You already have high-trust names. To spread brand inside those organisations and beyond, people need “stories they can retell.”

Consider:

- Under the logo wall, add a one-line framing such as:  
  “Selected partners in AAA, platforms and live service.”  
- Directly beneath each testimonial, add a short descriptor. For example:  
  - “Multi-year portfolio strategy and analytics partnership”  
  - “Live service performance diagnostics across key titles”

This gives visitors language they can borrow when they talk about you internally.

### D. Add two or three short case snapshots

You do not need full case studies on the home page. Three short “snapshot tiles” can do a lot:

- **Halo / Xbox platform**  
  Challenge, intervention, and measurable impact in 3 lines.  
- **Blizzard**  
  Focus on long-term partnership and how you adapted to their culture.  
- **A games-adjacent data or organisational engagement**  
  To show you can handle complex data or team challenges beyond pure game design.

Each snapshot should include:

- Client type (not necessarily named if confidential).  
- Situation and constraint.  
- What NBI did.  
- One outcome, even if qualitative.

### E. Make the FAQ / approach section feel more NBI-specific

Keep the headings you have, but tune them to carry more brand:

- “Execution-Focused” could become “From strategy deck to live change” and mention that you stay with teams through implementation, not just slideware.

- “Impact” could reference that you measure impact in player behaviour, team health and commercial performance, not just deliverables.

Where relevant, you can repeat the line that NBI does not design or build client games, only enables better decisions and systems around them. That repetition helps fix the brand position.
