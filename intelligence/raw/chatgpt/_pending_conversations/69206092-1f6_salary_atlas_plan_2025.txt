- **Midlands, Scotland, Ireland**:  
  - Midlands often sits close to the UK median for each role.  
  - Scotland and the North tend to lag London by around 10 to 15 percent for mid level IC roles, but some senior roles can be closer to London levels when attached to major hubs.  
  - Irish studios that pay in euros often sit between continental EU and UK levels; some Dublin roles align more with London than with the UK median, especially in engineering and senior production. citeturn0search10turn0search11  
- **Discipline differences**:

  - Producers, product managers and data specialists often sit just behind top engineering roles.  
  - Art and design are mid pack, with concept and character art often earning more than pure generalists.  
  - QA remains the lowest paid across UK games, with game QA testers typically in the low 20 thousands at junior levels, rising into the low to mid 30 thousands at senior levels. citeturn1search21turn1search16turn0search10

  - **Eastern and Central Time** (New York, Boston, Texas hubs) are close behind, sometimes matching Pacific for remote first roles.  
  - **Mountain Time** tends to be lower, used as a proxy for lower cost cities and some fully remote teams. citeturn0search19turn0search18  
- **Overall levels**:

### 3. Patterns by discipline and seniority

**3.1. Discipline ranking (typical median pattern for IC roles)**

Across UK, US and EU, the typical order from highest to lowest median base pay is:

1. **Engineering / Programming**  
   - Engine, rendering, network, online services and platform engineers at the top.  
   - Gameplay programmers slightly lower, but still ahead of most disciplines. citeturn0search18turn1search0turn1search9  

2. **Data, analytics and monetisation / Product management**  
   - Senior product managers, live ops and monetisation designers often match or approach senior programmers.

3. **Production and project management**  
   - Producers and development directors are well-paid, but often slightly behind engineering and PM, except in very production led studios.

4. **Design and art**  
   - Game designers in Europe saw noticeable yearly growth, with medians around 3,686 euro per month in 2024 in one cross country survey, which is above typical artists and narrative designers in the same report. citeturn1search12turn0search12  

5. **Marketing and publishing**  
   - Broad spread; senior UA and growth roles can earn at or above production, especially in mobile.

6. **Audio and QA**  
   - Audio tends to sit roughly with mid level art.  
   - QA testers, even seniors, remain at the bottom of the pay ladder, although unionised roles (for example at Microsoft’s studios) have begun to push pay upwards. citeturn1search21turn1search16turn1news50  

**3.2. Seniority uplifts**

A common pattern, validated across sources:

- **Junior to Mid**: 10 to 25 percent uplift.  
- **Mid to Senior**: 20 to 35 percent uplift.  
- **Senior to Lead**: 10 to 25 percent.  
- **Lead to Director**: 15 to 30 percent, often with wider variance and title noise.  
- **Director to VP / C level**: step change, but much more variable and heavily influenced by equity, bonuses and company scale, so base salary alone can mislead.

These patterns will be used when extrapolating from a well supported level to an adjacent one, with “estimate” clearly labelled when necessary.

### 4. Key caveats and usage guidance

1. **Atlas as a decision aid, not a contract**  
   Use this as a first-pass reference when budgeting, planning headcount and framing offers, not as a binding pay scale. Local negotiation, equity and benefits can move total compensation significantly.

2. **Role title inflation**  
   Titles such as “Senior,” “Lead” and “Director” are not used consistently across studios and regions. Wherever possible, the atlas aligns roles with years of experience and scope rather than titles alone, and notes where a level is “not commonly used.”

3. **Data quality varies by role and region**  
   Common roles in major hubs will have robust data and real quartiles. Rare roles in smaller markets will often be estimates based on peers.

4. **Refresh cadence**  
   Given the volatility of the games labour market and recent rounds of layoffs and restructuring, a **full refresh every 12 months** is advisable, with a light review every six months for high priority roles or key hiring markets. citeturn0search5turn0search11turn0news48  

5. **Local legal and market context**  
   This atlas does not model overtime practices, crunch, or union negotiated floors. For some studios, especially in the US, a role may have a wide range between “minimal benefits, high overtime” and “unionised, strong protections.”
