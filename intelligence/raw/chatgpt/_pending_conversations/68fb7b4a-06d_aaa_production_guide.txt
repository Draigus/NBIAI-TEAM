Beta

# AAA Production Quick-Start — Expanded Tutor Guide

## 1) What “Agilefall” really means in a studio
**Plain English**  
Use big milestones to control funding and risk at the top, and short Sprints underneath to actually build and test the game. Milestones are the safety rails. Sprints are the engine that moves you forward. The Scrum events that power Sprints are Sprint Planning, the Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, and Retrospective. citeturn0search0

**Why it works**  
AAA needs approvals and predictability for leadership, but teams need iteration to find fun and de-risk tech. PMI calls these blended or hybrid approaches and notes they are now common because they are fit-for-purpose. citeturn0search2turn0search7

**How to set it up**  
1) Pick your phase gates: Discovery, Pre-production, Production, Alpha, Beta, Gold.  
2) Fix Sprint length at 1 or 2 weeks. Keep it stable.  
3) Map which gate checks you will prove in which Sprints.  
4) Book quarterly or 8–12 week planning intervals so multiple teams align on the same objectives. SAFe calls this PI Planning. citeturn0search3turn0search8

**Example**  
Quarter 1 objective: “Reach a playable vertical slice for one track with final-quality targets.” That objective breaks into 4–5 Sprints with specific exit checks.

## 2) Backlog hierarchy explained with one picture in your head
**Plain English**  
Think of work like a tree. Epics are the thick branches, Features are smaller branches, Stories are the leaves a player can feel, and Tasks are the screws you tighten to keep a leaf on the branch.

**Do this**  
- Write Epics for major outcomes, like “Rival AI v1”.  
- Split Epics into Features, like “Overtakes” and “Blocking”.  
- Split Features into player-facing Stories that you can demo in a Sprint Review.  
- Break Stories into Tasks for code, art, audio, UI, test.

**Readiness and Doneness**  
Create a **Definition of Ready** so teams only pull items that are clear and sized. Create a **Definition of Done** so teams finish to the same standard. Atlassian’s guides give practical checklists you can copy. citeturn0search1turn0search11

**Ready example**  
- Clear problem and acceptance criteria.  
- Dependencies planned.  
- Assets or mocks attached.  
- Performance and memory targets noted.

**Done example**  
- Acceptance criteria met.  
- Code reviewed and merged.  
- Assets integrated and validated in game.  
- Tests pass and build is green.  
- Notes updated.

## 3) The weekly rhythm, explained like you are shadowing me

Two hours at the start of a Sprint. We agree a Sprint Goal and select only Ready items that fit capacity. The output is a visible plan everyone understands. citeturn0search0

**Daily stand-up**

**Retrospective**  
60 minutes. Pick one Start, one Stop, one Continue with owners and dates. That is how culture improves each Sprint. citeturn0search10

## 4) Phases and the “vertical slice” in plain terms
**Discovery**  
Prove the concept should exist. Exit with success criteria, first risks, and a rough cost envelope.

**Pre-production**  
Prove fun, pipeline, and cost. The big check is a **vertical slice**: a short, representative chunk at near-final quality that shows code, art, audio, UI, save, performance, and telemetry working together. Industry articles and talks consistently frame a vertical slice as a tiny but complete representation of the intended experience. citeturn2search1turn2search8turn2search6

**Production**  
Build to Alpha with predictable cadence. At **Alpha**, features are implemented and testable and performance is on target. At **Beta**, content is locked and you mostly fix bugs and polish.

## 5) Bug triage without jargon
**Plain English**  
Severity is how bad it is for players or the business. Priority is how soon you fix it. A crash on boot is high severity and top priority. A rare cosmetic glitch is low severity and low priority. Atlassian’s guides summarise this and the triage process well. citeturn1search1turn1search6

**Daily routine**  
- Confirm repro steps.  
- Set severity.  
- Assign an owner.  
- Decide the fix window.  
- Track intake vs burn and escalate if S1 stays open.

**Example scale**  
S1 crash or data loss. S2 major gameplay break or performance collapse. S3 visible defect that harms experience. S4 minor cosmetic.

**Incident readiness for live service**  
Have an on-call rotation and a severity playbook before launch. Atlassian’s incident severity overview is a good starting point. citeturn1search11

# Glossary for new producers

Acceptance criteria: test statements that prove a Story is done.  
Alpha: features implemented and testable with target performance and stability.  
Backlog: ordered list of Epics, Features, Stories, and Tasks.  
Beta: content locked with bug fix and polish focus.  
Burndown: chart of remaining work in the Sprint.  
CFD: cumulative flow diagram that shows items by status over time.  
Definition of Done: shared finish line conditions.  
Definition of Ready: checks that an item is clear enough to start.  
Discovery: early phase to decide if the concept deserves funding.  
Epic: major outcome that spans weeks to months.  
Feature: cohesive part of an Epic.  
Gate: formal approval to enter the next phase.  
INVEST: rule of thumb for good Stories.  
Launch: release readiness and certification complete.  
PI: program increment, an 8 to 12 week planning window for multiple teams.  
Retro: meeting to improve how the team works.  
Slice vertical: story that cuts across code, content, UI, and test so it can be demoed.  
Sprint: fixed timebox, usually one or two weeks.  
Sprint Goal: outcome to demonstrate at review.  
Story: player facing slice of value completed inside a Sprint.  
Triage: daily meeting to decide bug priority and owners.
