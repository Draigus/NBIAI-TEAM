can I use the make platform to migrate all of microsoft teams and sharepoint content from one 0365 account to another? or would this be a terrible decision

[ASSISTANT]
Short version: Using Make as the primary way to move *all* Teams and SharePoint content from one Microsoft 365 tenant to another would be a bad idea. It can help around the edges, but it should not be your main migration engine.

Here is why.

---

### 1. What Make is actually good at in this context

Make is a general-purpose automation/iPaaS platform. It has connectors for:

- **SharePoint Online**: lists, items, files, sites, pages, etc.citeturn1search1  
- **Microsoft Teams**: list/create/update/delete teams and channels, send messages, watch messages, etc.citeturn3search0turn3search1  

It is designed for workflows like:

- “When X happens, create a file/list item/message over there.”
- Light integrations between SharePoint / Teams and other apps.

It is *not* designed as a full-fidelity migration tool.

---

### 2. Why using Make for “all Teams and SharePoint content” is a bad idea

For a proper tenant-to-tenant migration, you usually need to move:

- SharePoint sites, libraries, folders, files, metadata, permissions, sharing links, and sometimes version history.  
- Teams structure: teams, channels, tabs, apps, and the underlying SharePoint sites.  
- Teams *data*: channel messages, 1:1 and group chats, meeting artefacts, OneNote, wikis, Planner, etc.citeturn2search14turn2search22  

Make can hit some of the APIs, but:

1. **No first-class migration semantics**

   Dedicated tools and Microsoft’s own migration features use the SharePoint Migration API and tailored processes that understand identities, permissions, and links, and are tuned for performance.citeturn2search12turn2search20  

   Make just calls generic APIs. You would have to hand-roll:

   - Identity mapping between tenants.  
   - Permission mapping and re-assignment.  
   - Re-creation of Teams structures with correct backing SharePoint sites.  
   - Handling of broken references, links, and embedded content.

2. **Coverage gaps**

   Out of the box, Make’s Teams connector manages objects and messages, but there is no “migrate team and all its history, files, and settings” operation. Things like:

   - Full chat history (especially 1:1 DMs).  
   - Meeting artefacts and call records.  
   - All Planner, OneNote, app configs, and nuanced channel settings.

   These are exactly what dedicated Teams migration tools and scripts are built to handle.citeturn2search16turn2search22  

3. **Scale, throttling, and cost**

   - Make prices by operations; large migrations mean huge operation counts and potentially ugly bills.citeturn1search12turn3search5  
   - Microsoft heavily throttles Graph and SharePoint APIs. The SharePoint Migration API and associated tools are specifically tuned to work with this at high throughput.citeturn2search12  
   - With Make, you would be fighting throttling yourself, adding sleeps, retries, and chunking logic in scenarios.

4. **Reliability and restartability**

   Migration tools give you:

   - Batches, delta passes, resumable jobs.  
   - Detailed logs per site/team/user.  
   - Cut-over planning and test runs.

   With Make, you would be building all of that: error handling, idempotency, partial retries, and verification.

5. **Compliance and supportability**

   Microsoft’s own cross-tenant migration features and recognised vendor tools are the things support, FastTrack, and most consultants understand and will back.  

   Make is great glue. It is not a standard answer if something goes wrong and you need Microsoft or a migration vendor to help.

---

### 3. What you *could* sensibly use Make for

Make is still useful around the migration:

- **Post-migration automation**  
  Once content is in the new tenant, use Make to wire workflows, notifications, data syncs, etc., across the new environment.

- **Niche, low-volume copy jobs**  
  For example, copying a small number of lists, or doing light transform logic for a specific library, where you are OK if it is “best effort” and you can manually verify.

- **Side integrations**  
  E.g. feeding legacy systems, CRMs, or external tools as part of a broader change programme.

But I would not point it at “everything from Teams and SharePoint” and expect a sane, maintainable migration.

---

### 4. What is a more sensible approach

For tenant-to-tenant migrations of Teams and SharePoint, your realistic options are:

1. **Microsoft’s own cross-tenant features**

   - **Cross-tenant SharePoint site migration** via SharePoint Online PowerShell and the migration API.citeturn2search0turn2search2turn2search11  
   - **Cross-tenant mailbox / OneDrive migration** via the Cross-Tenant User Data Migration SKU and FastTrack where eligible.citeturn2search5turn2search7turn0search25  

   These give you proper handling of identities, permissions, and links.

2. **Specialist migration tools**

   Typical names:

   - **ShareGate** for SharePoint and Teams tenant-to-tenant, including Teams structure, files, and some conversation data.citeturn2search1turn2search14turn2search15  
   - **CloudFuze, Cloudiway, BitTitan MigrationWiz**, and similar for heavier Teams migrations (public/private teams, channel history, chats, etc.).citeturn2search22turn2search13turn2search16  

   These exist specifically because doing this with raw APIs is painful.

3. **Power Automate for edge cases**

   If you really want a low-code option inside the Microsoft stack, Power Automate is more “native” to M365 and has governance and connectors tuned to Microsoft workloads. It still is not a full migration tool, but it is often better aligned than Make for M365-only jobs.citeturn3search5turn1search21  

---

### 5. Direct answer to your question

- **Can you?**  
  You can technically use Make to move some subset of SharePoint files and maybe recreate Teams and channels, if you are prepared to build and maintain a fairly complex set of scenarios and accept data / metadata loss.

- **Should you, for a full Teams + SharePoint tenant migration?**  
  For anything beyond a tiny, low-risk environment, it would be a **bad** decision compared with purpose-built options.
