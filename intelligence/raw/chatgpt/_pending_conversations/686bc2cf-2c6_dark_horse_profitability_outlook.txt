is dark horse publishing profitable

[ASSISTANT]
**Short answer**: Yes, Dark Horse has been profitable in recent years, but its margin is getting squeezed and the outlook is less certain than it was a few years ago.

**Key points**

1. **Profit at the time of the Embracer deal**  
   When Embracer Group agreed to buy Dark Horse in late 2021 it reported about SEK 900 million (c. US $95 million) in sales and roughly SEK 130 million in operating profit, about a 14 percent margin. Embracer projected EBIT of 140-180 million for FY 22/23 and 170-200 million for FY 23/24, so the business was clearly profitable then. citeturn9search1

2. **Recent pressure on value and segment performance**  
   In May 2024 Embracer wrote down Dark Horse goodwill by roughly SEK 63 million. A write-down speaks to lower future expectations rather than an immediate cash loss, but it shows that Dark Horse’s earnings outlook has cooled. In the same period Embracer’s Entertainment & Services segment (which houses Dark Horse) saw sales fall 54 percent year-on-year in the April-June 2024 quarter, contributing to a group-wide loss. citeturn7view0turn6view0

3. **Cost-cutting in 2025**  
   Dark Horse confirmed staff reductions in February 2025, saying the step was needed to offset higher overhead and changing market conditions. Lay-offs do not prove the company is losing money, but they indicate management is working to protect, or restore, its profit margin. citeturn8view0

**Bottom line**

- On the numbers we can see (through FY 23/24 guidance) Dark Horse operated in the black.  
- The goodwill write-down and segment slump show earnings momentum has weakened.  
- Without full stand-alone accounts Embracer is the only source, and it no longer breaks Dark Horse out separately.  
- Most observers therefore describe Dark Horse as *still* profitable, but with tighter margins and more volatility than it enjoyed before the wider Embracer restructuring.

**Yes, both platforms expose REST-style APIs, but only on paid plans. Below is what you can (and cannot) do, typical auth flows, and quick tips for smooth integration.**

### 1. Data.ai (formerly App Annie)

| Feature | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Products that ship an API** | *Connect* (your own store data) and *Intelligence* (market estimates).|
| **Auth** | Bearer token in the `Authorization` header (`Authorization: Bearer <API-Key>`). citeturn11search1 |
| **Key endpoints** | `/v1.2/apps/{store}/{app_id}/downloads`, `/v1.2/apps/{store}/{app_id}/revenue`, `/v1.2/publishers/...` etc. |
| **Bulk export** | A dedicated “Bulk API” lets you pre-request archive files for heavy ETL jobs so you do not hit daily call limits. citeturn8search0 |
| **Access model** | Connect Free covers only your own apps. Market-wide data, historical ranges, or usage panels require Intelligence or an enterprise contract. |

**Sample call**

```bash
curl -H "Authorization: Bearer $DATA_AI_KEY" \
  "https://api.data.ai/v1.2/apps/ios/389801252/downloads?countries=US&start_date=2025-06-01&end_date=2025-06-30"
```

---

### 2. Sensor Tower

| Feature | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Product name** | *Connect API* (plus optional scheduled feeds or Snowflake share). |
| **Auth** | Single secret token, usually sent as `auth_token` query param or in an `Authorization` header depending on contract. |
| **Docs highlight** | Marketing page promises “APIs designed to support anything from fetching small datasets to processing high-volume data operations”. citeturn4view0 |
| **Typical endpoints** | `/v1/ios/top_grossing`, `/v1/android/app/{package}/estimates`, `/v2/keywords/...`, `/v1/ad_top_creatives/...`. |
| **Rate limits** | Soft daily cap negotiated per client; burst limits of roughly 30 req/s are common. |

**Sample call**

```bash
curl "https://api.sensortower.com/v1/ios/top_grossing?country=GB&auth_token=$ST_KEY"
```

---

### 3. Practical integration tips

1. **Budget for enterprise spend** – neither vendor sells API access à la carte. Expect minimum contracts in the mid-five-figure USD range per year for market data.  
2. **Start with their sandbox** – both teams will provision a test project with a low-traffic key so you can validate your pipeline before signing.  
3. **Use incremental windows** – pull “yesterday” each morning and back-fill only once to avoid re-billing identical rows.  
4. **Cache and dedupe** – regulations forbid public redistribution, but local caching keeps you inside rate limits and lowers costs.  
5. **Consider scheduled feeds** – for GB-scale historical pulls Sensor Tower’s CSV/Parquet drops or Data.ai’s Bulk URLs are less brittle than thousands of tiny GETs.  
6. **Mind the TOS** – raw figures cannot be exposed publicly or fed into models that will be sold onward without written permission.

---

### 4. Bottom line

*You can absolutely pipe both Data.ai and Sensor Tower into your data stack provided you have an enterprise contract and follow their auth schemes. For quick tests, ask each account manager for a trial token; for production, prefer their bulk or Snowflake options to keep maintenance low.*
