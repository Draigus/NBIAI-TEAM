Short answer: none of those is “best” on its own. For investors, the clearest phrase is usually something like:

> “Large, underpenetrated market with low competition”

Then you prove it with numbers.

On your three terms:

- **Blue ocean**  
  Use this if you are truly creating a **new category** with very few or no direct competitors. It signals “no one else is playing here yet,” but some investors read it as “no one has validated demand yet,” so you must show strong evidence of latent demand, willingness to pay, and analog markets.

- **Greenfield**  
  Use this when you want to emphasise **no legacy constraints** and very little direct competition. It is common in tech/infra and expansion (“greenfield market entry”), and feels more grounded than “blue ocean.” It works well if you are entering a new platform, geography, or segment where incumbents are weak or absent.

- **Whitespace**  
  Use this when you are in a **known market**, but there are clear **gaps or neglected segments**. It implies “this space on the market map is not being served,” which investors often like because the category itself is already proven.

If you want a single investor-friendly line for a pitch slide, something like:

> “Greenfield opportunity: [£X] billion market, early in its lifecycle, fragmented competitors, no clear category leader.”

You can then add a subtitle in the notes or narrative that this is effectively a “blue ocean / whitespace” if that framing helps.
