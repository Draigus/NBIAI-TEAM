# How it stacks up against top-tier game pitches
What the best gaming decks do consistently:
1) Open with a visceral 10–15 second product proof that shows the loop and the “why now.”  
2) Put live traction or a hard validation proxy in lights.  
3) Translate product into unit economics quickly, with Base, Target, and Upside gates that govern spend.  
4) Show a precise GTM with creator mechanics, K-factors, and stop-or-scale thresholds.  
5) Anchor the ask to KPI gates and a 24-month cash runway.  

Your draft hits several of these, but the order and emphasis underplay your strongest asset: a Telegram-native frictionless loop with creator-hosted tournaments. The pre-launch nature of several slides clashes with a declared Series A ask. That disconnect will suppress conviction even if the content is solid.

# What works now
- **Clear product loop** threaded across slides 2, 5, and 6.  
- **Creator-led tournaments** as a differentiated viral engine.  
- **KPI-gated spend** intent is present on slides 11 and 12.  
- **Hiring plan** is practical for a Mini App strategy game.

# Where it lags vs best practice
- **Story beats arrive out of order.** Investors want to see the product and current signal in the first 120 seconds.  
- **Series A label with pre-launch framing.** That invites immediate pushback.  
- **Forecasts without anchors.** Several ranges lack the benchmark and source note investors expect.  
- **Tone is slightly internal.** Some lines read like a planning doc rather than a public raise.
