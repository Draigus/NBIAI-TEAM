# Competitive Analysis of Top RTS Base-Battler Games and Monetization Strategies

## Overview of Selected Games  
To inform our strategy, we compare several successful **mobile RTS base-battler** games (mid-core focused) that blend base-building with combat. These include: 

- **Clash of Clans (Supercell)** – A pioneering base-builder (launched 2012) with a global casual-to-midcore appeal, known for its clan system and long-term player retention【6†L129-L137】【6†L149-L157】.  
- **Rise of Kingdoms (Lilith Games)** – A mid-core 4X strategy (launched 2018) with deep alliance warfare, hero collection, and heavy monetization that grew it into a $2+ billion franchise【10†L85-L93】.  
- **State of Survival (FunPlus/KingsGroup)** – A 4X base-builder with a zombie theme (launched 2019), notable for strong revenue ($1B+ lifetime) but also aggressive monetization tactics that have drawn player criticism【32†L75-L83】【32†L91-L99】.  
- **Last War: Survival (First Fun)** – A recent hit (launched Aug 2023) mixing casual elements and mid-core strategy, achieving *top-10 grossing* status in both Western and Asian markets within a year【1†L26-L34】. Last War scaled extremely fast, grossing over **$100 million** in its first 30 days with ~6 million downloads【22†L52-L60】【22†L63-L70】, though it has garnered many 1-star reviews over monetization issues【22†L71-L79】.

These games target regions including US, Europe, and emerging markets like India/Brazil, demonstrating global appeal. Team sizes and budgets vary (Supercell’s small teams vs. Lilith/FunPlus’s large operations), but for context our new project assumes a ~50-person team and ~$10M+ budget – capable of delivering a polished mid-core strategy game with robust live ops.

## Retention Mechanics Comparison  
Long-term **player retention** is critical for sustained monetization. Each competitor employs multiple retention **mechanics** to keep players engaged from Day 0 onward:

- **Clash of Clans:** Emphasizes *social play* through clans and clan wars. Joining a clan creates a sense of accountability and community, motivating daily play【6†L135-L143】. Regular **content updates** (new troops, buildings, seasonal events) continually add fresh goals【6†L139-L147】. This approach yields exceptional retention – industry reports indicate **Day 30 retention over 20%** for CoC, far above typical averages【6†L149-L157】. Players log in to collect resources, participate in wars, and complete **daily challenges** (especially after the Gold Pass introduction, which added daily/seasonal tasks). These mechanics, combined with moderate session lengths, have cultivated a *dedicated global community*. CoC demonstrates that strong social bonds and new content can drive *D1 ~50%* and *D30 ~20%+* retention【6†L149-L157】 – making each player far more likely to stick around and eventually spend.

- **Rise of Kingdoms:** Retains players via a robust alliance system, frequent events, and layered progression. RoK strongly pushes players to join alliances early (even offering **300 gems for joining** as an incentive)【12†L518-L527】. Alliances unlock team events, tech donations, and large-scale **Kingdom vs Kingdom** wars, creating social pressure to log in daily. RoK also uses *live events* on a calendar (e.g. holidays, server competitions) to give players new objectives constantly【12†L508-L516】. Push notifications are leveraged heavily to pull players back when buildings finish or if they’re absent (“Governor, our people need your leadership”)【12†L487-L495】【12†L497-L504】. This multi-pronged approach yields solid retention: roughly **41% D1**, **20% D7**, and **9% D30**【12†L472-L480】 – placing RoK near the top of the genre. While its 30-day retention (~9%) is lower than Clash of Clans’, it’s still above average in the strategy category【12†L474-L480】. The drop-off partly reflects RoK’s hardcore depth; still, those who remain become highly engaged (often due to alliance commitments). Retention is further reinforced by **VIP login rewards**, daily quests, and free chest timers – all encouraging habitual play【12†L528-L536】.

- **State of Survival:** Uses similar 4X retention loops (alliances, timed events) but with a PvE twist (zombie narrative). Early on, SoS guides players through a story-driven tutorial and then opens up an alliance-centered world. **Daily missions**, alliance donations, and periodic server-wide events (e.g. “kill infected” weekends, “state vs. state” war events) ensure there’s always a reason to log in. The game also partners with known IPs (e.g. a *Walking Dead* collaboration added new heroes/events), which brought in 20M new users during the event【24†L122-L130】. However, SoS’s retention has been hampered at times by player frustration over monetization (discussed later). It likely sees *D1 and D7 retention in the 30–40% and ~15–20% range* (benchmark for mid-core strategy), tapering to high single digits by D30 – decent but not as enduring as CoC’s. To keep engagement high, SoS offers **free daily rewards** (login crates, etc.), **hero upgrade loops** (grinding fragments keeps players chasing progress), and **cross-server social features** (chat, alliances) for community stickiness.

- **Last War: Survival:** Innovates in early retention by blending a *hyper-casual onboarding* with mid-core depth. The first ~5–15 minutes present a simple arcade “run and shoot” mini-game (as seen in its ads)【1†L44-L53】【1†L61-L64】, which quickly hooks casual players. After this, the game transitions the player into building a base and clearing zombies in their town. Crucially, **early goals** like “save the hostage Monica” give a clear short-term retention hook (a mission achievable in a few days)【4†L225-L234】【4†L239-L247】. By the time that’s done, Last War has introduced longer-term hooks: the **world map and alliances** unlock, revealing a persistent social competitive layer【4†L245-L253】. Alliance participation is essentially mandatory to survive, and the game establishes a habit of very frequent logins – players often feel compelled to check in **multiple times per hour** to keep up【4†L267-L275】【4†L269-L277】. This intense schedule, combined with **scheduled alliance wars and events**, creates *extremely high engagement*, albeit at the risk of burnout. Last War’s retention numbers aren’t published, but its ability to sustain top-10 grossing suggests it retains a core of deeply engaged players (likely similar D1~40%, D30 high single digits for the average player). The **constant updates** (reportedly 106 updates in 2023, almost 2 per week) also refresh content continuously【28†L178-L187】, preventing stagnation and enticing lapsed players back with new modes. *However*, the downside is rising player fatigue – some players report the game eventually felt like a *“responsibility”* due to incessant obligations【4†L299-L307】. We should strive to adopt Last War’s clever onboarding and active social hooks, but avoid pushing players to unhealthy engagement that can lead to churn.

**Key Takeaway:** Across all these games, the strongest retention drivers are **social commitment (clans/alliances)**, **regular events/content**, and **meaningful progression systems** that give players mid- and long-term goals. A carefully crafted onboarding (easing casual players in, as Last War did) can widen the funnel without sacrificing depth. Our new game should incorporate these, fostering a community and steady content cadence to achieve high retention. High retention will in turn support monetization, since retained players drive higher LTV【6†L149-L157】【6†L153-L157】.

## Battle Passes and Seasonal Systems  
Most of these games have introduced **Battle Passes** (a.k.a. season passes) as a core engagement and monetization mechanic. Battle passes reward sustained play over a limited season, offering a free tier and a premium tier (or multiple tiers) for paying users. This model has proven effective at increasing playtime, improving retention, and converting more players into payers【14†L79-L88】【14†L114-L122】. Here’s how each game utilizes passes and seasons:

- **Clash of Clans – Gold Pass:** CoC added the **Gold Pass** in 2019, a monthly battle pass costing \$4.99. It provides extra loot rewards, exclusive hero skins, and various perks for completing daily and weekly challenges. The Gold Pass was transformative – it immediately boosted CoC’s revenue **72% month-over-month** upon introduction【16†L120-L128】, indicating strong uptake. It also likely improved retention: players now log in daily to complete pass challenges and unlock rewards (without the pass, many of those tasks wouldn’t exist). Supercell explicitly balanced the Gold Pass to be *very free-to-play friendly* – it’s cheap ($5) and delivers ~10x value in items【14†L99-L107】, making it a “no-brainer” purchase for regular players. It doesn’t confer insurmountable power, mostly speeding progression and giving cosmetics, which avoids angering non-payers. CoC runs *monthly seasons* with the pass resetting each month (and new hero skins/themes each time to keep it fresh). This cadence proved sustainable – even years later, Gold Pass purchases remain a significant revenue stream and help stabilize CoC’s player spending【15†L21-L28】【16†L125-L133】. For our game, a similar low-cost, high-value battle pass could drive engagement and steady income without skewing game balance.

- **Clash Royale – Season Pass (Pass Royale):** Another Supercell title, Clash Royale (though not a base-builder) pioneered the popularization of battle passes in mid-core games. Its *Pass Royale* also costs around \$5 and runs on a roughly monthly season, offering premium track rewards (exclusive emotes, cosmetics, bonus cards/gold) for playing matches and completing quests. Clash Royale demonstrated that even competitive PvP games can use battle passes to boost retention and revenue – passes now contribute anywhere from **1% up to 40% of revenue** in top games, depending on integration【14†L192-L199】. Notably, Supercell later experimented with **multi-tier passes** (adding a higher-priced tier with more perks), similar to how some strategy games offer multiple pass options. This can increase ARPU, though it must be handled carefully to avoid perceptions of greed. *(Our focus is base-battlers, but CR’s success with passes underscores their broad effectiveness.)*

- **Rise of Kingdoms – Lucerne Scrolls:** RoK introduced a battle pass system called **“Lucerne Scrolls”**, unlocked once a city reaches a certain level (City Hall 10)【18†L13-L21】【18†L23-L30】. It features **80 levels** of missions split into weekly and seasonal challenges【30†L456-L464】. There is one free tier and *two* premium tiers: one for \$4.99 and a higher-value one for \$19.99【30†L458-L462】. This dual-tier approach follows the principle that a pricier pass yields more exclusive rewards (for instance, the \$19.99 pass might give unique cosmetics or larger resource packs). By offering a low-price option and a high-price option, RoK segments the audience: moderate spenders go for the \$5 pass, while heavy spenders might opt for the \$20 pass for maximal rewards【18†L23-L30】【18†L25-L33】. The battle pass content incentivizes daily play (complete quests to progress) and likely improves stickiness (players who buy it want to return regularly to “earn” all the rewards they paid for)【14†L113-L121】【14†L173-L182】. RoK seasons are periodic but not necessarily monthly – they might align with major game update cycles or events. Beyond the pass, RoK also runs **seasonal kingdom-vs-kingdom wars** (multi-week cross-server wars) which create a “season” of competition. Those are more about gameplay reset and rewards, and they tie into monetization by prompting spending on troops and boosts during each war season. In summary, RoK’s battle pass is tightly integrated into its core loop (via the Trading Post building), encouraging both engagement and monetization in a way players perceive as *fairly optional*. This aligns with the trend: **battle passes boost both retention and revenue** when built into the core loop【14†L119-L127】【14†L192-L200】.

- **State of Survival – Multiple Passes:** SoS implemented a battle pass as well (sometimes called a **“Survival Pass”** or seasonal event pass). In fact, FunPlus’s producer noted they initially had a complex separate pass quest system and later **merged it with daily quests for simplicity**【14†L201-L209】. SoS’s pass typically offers hero upgrade materials, resources, etc., with free and premium tracks. One interesting twist in SoS (and some similar titles) is the use of **multi-tiered passes** or recurring mini-passes: for example, *State of Survival* introduced things like a weekly or monthly mini-pass in addition to its main seasonal pass【13†L9-L14】. Also, GameRefinery noted SoS offered *multi-tier battle passes* (possibly a standard and an “elite” version) similar to Clash Royale【13†L11-L14】. These passes are designed to appeal to different spender categories. By giving players more to do and goals to strive for, SoS’s passes help mitigate mid-game stagnation. However, if the rewards are too powerful (e.g. exclusive hero fragments), non-paying players might feel disadvantaged – a delicate balance. SoS seasons generally last a few weeks with narrative events (they’ve run special seasons like the *Walking Dead* collab with unique rewards to drive both engagement and spending). The key goal was to **increase the percentage of paying users per month and improve retention without hurting free players**【14†L79-L87】, which aligns with our philosophy. When done well, SoS’s battle passes likely contributed significantly to its monetization (passes in top games can account for a large share of revenue) and gave players a *“badge of honor”* feeling for completing them【14†L101-L110】.

- **Last War: Survival – Layered Season Passes:** Last War is particularly aggressive and creative with its seasonal structure. It runs on an **8-week season cycle** followed by a 4-week off-season【20†L27-L36】【20†L28-L36】. During each 8-week season, Last War offers **multiple concurrent battle passes** (“Hot Deals”). For example: 
  - A **Hero Exclusive Weapon Pass** appears in weeks 1, 3, 6 of the season, focusing on a specific hero’s weapon. It has a free track and a paid track; only the paid track gives enough shards to unlock the new exclusive weapon immediately【20†L108-L116】. (Free players can still get the weapon later via off-season events, but with a delay【20†L117-L125】.) 
  - A **Hero Growth Pass** might run on certain weeks, rewarding materials to upgrade a new hero. 
  - An **Overlord Training Pass** and **Custom Weekly Pass** are also in rotation【20†L61-L69】【20†L73-L77】. The Custom Weekly Pass suggests even *weekly* micro-passes that reset each week with small rewards for a few dollars. 
  - During off-season, some passes (like those tied to exclusive weapons) go away, but others like a Weekly Pass continue in a 4-week repeating cycle【20†L81-L90】【20†L93-L100】. 
  Essentially, Last War always has some kind of pass running at any time – a clever way to continuously extract revenue while giving players time-limited goals. The passes are often tied to new content (heroes/weapons introduced that season), which drives **Fear of Missing Out (FOMO)** – spend now or wait months to get this item free. This strategy has proven extremely lucrative (contributing to that \$100M monthly revenue) but can tread the line of causing player fatigue or resentment. Some players feel *“tempted to spend a bit”* every week due to these passes【19†L5-L13】, which can lead to negative sentiment if they feel nickel-and-dimed. Last War mitigates backlash somewhat by ensuring that even free players *eventually* can obtain the content (e.g. via off-season Black Market events)【20†L117-L125】. Still, the design clearly prioritizes revenue now, potentially at the cost of long-term goodwill.

**Key Takeaway:** **Battle passes/seasons are a must-have** in modern mid-core games. They **boost engagement and monetization simultaneously** by giving players structured progression and high-value rewards for a modest price【14†L114-L122】【14†L192-L200】. For our new RTS battler, we should implement a battle pass that:  
- Has a **free track** (so all players benefit) and a **premium track** that offers excellent value (at a low price point, e.g. \$5, to maximize conversion of non-payers to payers)【14†L99-L107】.  
- Runs on a reasonable **season length** (e.g. monthly or maybe 6-8 weeks if that fits our event cadence) to give development breathing room and prevent burnout【14†L131-L139】.  
- Contains **cosmetic or convenience rewards** (skins, faster progress) that don’t heavily imbalance competitive play, to avoid pay-to-win accusations.  
- Optionally offers **two tiers** of premium (standard and deluxe) if we want to capture extra revenue from whales – but we must be cautious to ensure the cheaper pass is still very attractive on its own【18†L23-L30】【30†L458-L462】.  
- Integrates with our core gameplay loop (e.g. challenges tied to RTS combat or base building tasks) so that purchasing the pass naturally encourages more play (in turn improving retention). 

Battle passes are popular with players when done fairly – they’re seen as *high value* and *merit-based* (you have to play to unlock rewards, so it’s not an instant win purchase)【14†L103-L111】. This aligns perfectly with our goal of monetizing **without driving negative sentiment**. We can look to Clash of Clans’ Gold Pass as a gold standard of a **well-received monetization** that *increased revenue without upsetting the community*【16†L120-L128】.

## First 30-Day Performance: Revenue, Installs & Early Monetization  
Analyzing how these games performed in their first month (Day 0–30) provides insights into successful launch strategies and early monetization tactics:

- **Clash of Clans (2012):** CoC had a relatively quiet launch but then viral growth through player word-of-mouth and Supercell’s effective iOS featuring. By **3 months after launch, it became the #1 grossing app in the US**【8†L241-L249】 – an astonishing rise. While exact Day 30 numbers aren’t public, reaching #1 by month 3 implies that within the first month or two CoC was already pulling in millions of dollars monthly. CoC’s early revenue came entirely from optional **in-app purchases of gems** (to speed up building timers) – a monetization model novel at the time for many casual players. In the first weeks, *conversion rates* were likely modest, but the game’s superb retention meant that more players stuck around long enough to eventually spend. Notably, CoC did *not* have a launch-day battle pass or subscription; the monetization ramped up as players progressed and became invested in their bases. Early install counts likely started in the low hundreds of thousands but rapidly accelerated; CoC went on to achieve **over 500 million downloads** lifetime【7†L1-L8】. For a new mid-core title today, the first 30 days are much more competitive – but CoC shows that with strong retention (its D1 and D30 were excellent even in 2012) and organic growth, a game can become a top grossing hit even if monetization starts gently. The lesson is to focus on hooking players in Day 0–7; monetization can escalate after players are invested.

- **Rise of Kingdoms (2018):** RoK’s launch was successful but *not an overnight phenomenon*. It steadily grew via aggressive user acquisition and reached peak downloads **years after launch** (peak monthly downloads in Jan 2022 ~6M, well after release)【10†L85-L93】. In its first months (late 2018), RoK likely garnered a few million downloads globally, with revenue building as the hardcore strategy audience discovered the game. By 2020–2021, it was making **$20+ million per month** consistently【10†L87-L95】, which indicates how the compounding effect of content updates and UA investment paid off. We do know RoK’s early retention was strong (41% D1, 9% D30)【12†L472-L480】, meaning a solid core stuck around past the first month. Those players monetized via small purchases like growth bundles, VIP points, etc., and some converted to high spenders over time. A 4X game like RoK often relies on a portion of players becoming **whales after a few weeks** of play once competitive PvP heats up. Early on, Lilith seeded the game with many **one-time offers**: e.g. a “30-Day Gem Supply” subscription offer pops up by day 5 (half-price)【29†L425-L433】, and cheap $0.99 starter bundles appear by the end of the tutorial【29†L438-L446】. These Day 1–7 offers are designed to convert first-time buyers (FTBs) at a low barrier – a strategy we should emulate (e.g. a $0.99 starter pack with high value can significantly bump Day 1-3 revenue without much backlash). RoK’s first 30 days might not have broken records, but its careful funnel – moving players from small spends to larger ones (e.g. later selling \$99 packs once players are committed) – created a **sustainable revenue ramp**. Over five years, RoK exceeded \$2B total【10†L89-L97】, showing that a strong LTV can beat fast launch revenue if you retain and monetize deeply over time.

- **State of Survival (2019):** SoS’s first month was quite robust, thanks to heavy marketing and the zombie theme’s broad appeal. While exact first-month figures aren’t public, anecdotally SoS got millions of downloads quickly and by some reports was earning on the order of tens of millions within a month or two of launch. (One source notes SoS reached \$1B total by 2022 and 150M downloads【32†L91-L99】, which implies an average of ~$0.~6 ARPU overall, but in reality a small % of players drive most of that revenue.) During Day 0-30, SoS monetized through typical 4X methods: *starter bundles*, VIP unlocks, and releasing new “hero” gacha events to tempt early adopters. It also likely had a **strong Day 7 event** (many 4X games have a “State launch event” where spending yields extra rewards in the first week). One standout is that SoS early on didn’t have a battle pass – it was added later – meaning early revenue leaned on big spenders buying packs. And indeed, SoS cultivated big spenders: events where alliances compete to kill zombies or other PvP would see some players spending hundreds even in the first month to rank top. As a result, SoS might have seen a higher early ARPU than most (with some whales emerging by Day 30). However, this also meant **new-player experience could feel pay-to-win early**, which is something to be cautious about. SoS’s churn might have been high among purely free players who saw others accelerate via spending. The game’s strategy to counteract that was to keep adding new servers (so new players start fresh) and cross-promote with events to bring in fresh blood continuously. For our project, SoS teaches that while strong early revenue is good, one must ensure new players don’t feel hopelessly behind if they don’t pay in the first month. Offering competitive play *tiered by progression or server age* can mitigate this.

- **Last War: Survival (2023):** Last War’s first 30 days were record-breaking in the strategy genre. It reportedly **exceeded \$100 million in revenue in 30 days** (approx \$55.7M Google Play + \$53.6M iOS)【22†L52-L60】 – an extraordinary figure. Downloads in that period were ~5.76 million【22†L63-L70】, indicating a global blitz of user acquisition. This yields an ARPU of around \$17 *in the first month* – extremely high, meaning the average downloader generated \$17 (some via ads, most via IAP). The keys to this success were: a **massive marketing campaign** (celebrity ads, ubiquitous creatives on social media) and a very well-tuned monetization design from Day 1. Last War aggressively converts players early: within minutes you see the first spend offer, and by 15 minutes the game shifts from casual to mid-core, showing timers and asking for purchases to accelerate progress【4†L233-L241】【4†L235-L242】. It uses **“bait-and-switch” ads (hyper-casual gameplay to attract downloads) and then quickly introduces core monetization** – a tactic that got people in the door and then converted some to pay. Furthermore, Last War’s alliance system encourages whales to spend *immediately* to dominate; players observed that alliance power and PvP outcomes depended largely on how much money teams spent **even within the first weeks**【4†L273-L276】. The game showers alliances with spenders in gifts (when any member buys a pack, all members get a small gift), which normalizes spending and entices peer pressure (seeing *hundreds of \$50 gift boxes* from allies each day)【4†L259-L268】. This environment likely pushed many mid-spenders to make purchases early to “keep up,” driving that huge Day 0-30 revenue. However, the **negative sentiment** was also apparent early: Last War’s Google Play/App Store ratings were surprisingly low (over half of reviews are 1-star) as players complained about being duped by the ads and the heavy pay-to-win push【22†L71-L79】. In essence, Last War “squeezed” a lot of money out quickly, possibly at the expense of longer-term retention (some churned out after a few months feeling exhausted or cheated【4†L299-L307】【4†L309-L317】). 

**Key Takeaway:** There is a *spectrum* of Day 0-30 strategies: from **Supercell’s slow-burn approach** (focus on retention first, monetization ramps up gradually) to **Last War’s blitzkrieg approach** (maximize revenue quickly via aggressive monetization). For a sustainable mid-core RTS, a middle ground is wise. We want strong early revenue *and* happy players: 

- Plan compelling **Day 1-7 offers** (e.g. a $0.99 starter pack, a discounted 30-day resource supply) to convert new players while they’re excited【29†L425-L433】【29†L438-L446】. This boosts revenue early without high churn.  
- Introduce the **battle pass** by the end of the new user experience or within the first week, as a gentle nudge to spend $5 for extra rewards – a conversion that also deepens engagement (since buyers will stick around to complete the pass)【14†L114-L122】.  
- Avoid *deceptive advertising* and *false “90% off” sales* that can lead to lawsuits or backlash (FunPlus faced a class-action suit for fake 99% discounts in SoS【32†L75-L83】). Instead, be transparent: offer real value deals (e.g. first-time purchase double bonus) without trickery. 
- Monitor early-game difficulty and PvP balance to ensure non-payers aren’t immediately discouraged. Perhaps segregate early spenders in matchmaking or implement protected brackets so whales fight whales, letting others enjoy a fair competition. This can prevent the scenario where Day 5 of a new server is dominated by a spender with 100x the power of free players (a common complaint in Last War/SoS).  

Our goal is a strong first month *and* a foundation for growth. Hitting, say, \$10M+ in the first month would be great for a new title, but not if it causes a mass exodus by Day 90. So we will pursue healthy early monetization that aligns with long-term retention.

## In-Game Store and Monetization Assets for Sale  
Each game’s in-game store offers a variety of **assets for sale** – from currencies to cosmetics – reflecting different monetization designs. A competitive analysis of store offerings and monetization features:

- **Clash of Clans Store:** CoC’s store is relatively simple and player-friendly. It primarily sells **gems** (the premium currency) in packages ranging from \$5 to \$100. Gems can be used for speeding up build timers, buying missing resources, or cosmetic name changes. Over the years, Supercell added **Seasonal Offers** (limited-time packs that might bundle resources, magic items, etc. for \$ value) and the **Gold Pass** purchase. Additionally, CoC now sells purely cosmetic items: Hero Skins (outside of Gold Pass, older skins are sold occasionally) and Sceneries for your village (\$6.99–\$9.99 each). These cosmetics do not affect gameplay, which helps monetization *without* upsetting game balance – players who don’t buy only miss out on visual flair. CoC notably has **no direct “buy power” packs** (you can’t directly buy a max-level defense or army); you must convert gems or magic items into progress, which slows the pay-to-win impact. There’s also **no VIP subscription** in CoC (avoiding a spend-fueled power gap). This more conservative store approach earned CoC a reputation as one of the fairer F2P games, albeit with slightly lower monetization intensity than some newer titles. Still, CoC has made over \$7 billion to date by focusing on broad appeal and letting whales spend on convenience rather than exclusives【7†L1-L8】. As a design choice, CoC’s model shows that **selling speed-ups and cosmetics** can yield huge revenue while keeping player sentiment positive.

- **Clash Royale Store:** CR sells **gems** for speeding up chests and buying extra cards/gold, similar to CoC. But CR also heavily sells **cosmetic items**: exclusive emotes (animated emojis) and **Tower skins** each season. Emotes in CR are a prized cosmetic; they are often only obtainable via special offers or passes, and many players collect them. The store also offers rotating **chest offers** and **“Stacked Offers”** (where for a limited time you can buy a bundle of gold/cards for a set price). CR’s monetization shifted over time to rely on the **Pass Royale** (as the main purchase for most players each month) plus enticing whales with **max-level card offers** (once a player reaches endgame, they might be offered a chest that guarantees a needed Champion card, etc.). CR demonstrates that even in competitive games, cosmetics (that show off in battle or chat) can be big sellers. One insight for our game: if we can incorporate visual personalization (base themes, unit skins, alliance banners), those could be monetized without harming balance. Also, small fun items like emotes/stickers could add a revenue stream and community enjoyment.

- **Rise of Kingdoms Store:** RoK’s store is extensive and tailored to progression. Key offerings: 
  - **Gem Packs:** Standard \$5 to \$100 packs of gems. Gems are the foundation of all spending (used to speed up training, roll extra chests, etc.). 
  - **Resource Packs:** You can buy bundles of food, wood, stone, gold – these are mostly for whales during war who burn resources fast. Usually packaged in “Super Value Bundles” that increase in price as you buy repeatedly (a spend-depth design). 
  - **Speed-up Packs:** Bundles of speed-up items (universals or specific to building/training) for when you need to accelerate progress. 
  - **Boosts and Teleports:** Items like peace shields, army attack boosts, or city teleports can be bought, important for war strategy. 
  - **VIP Shop:** RoK has a VIP system (players increase VIP level by logging in daily and/or purchasing VIP points with gems). Higher VIP levels unlock a special **VIP Shop with discounted items**【29†L396-L404】【29†L398-L401】 – essentially rewarding spenders with better prices on consumables. However, VIP shop purchases are limited by VIP level and sometimes weekly limits, so whales can’t instantly buy infinite power, but they do get efficiency advantages (e.g., a VIP 15 player can buy more legendary sculptures per week than a VIP 10 player). 
  - **One-Time Event Bundles:** RoK is known for context-sensitive selling. For example, each time you upgrade City Hall to a new tier, a one-time “Growth Fund” or level-up bundle pops up (e.g., \$4.99 for a bunch of speed-ups and resources). When limited events run (like Lantern Festival in the Udonis example), a cheap \$0.99 bundle might appear to celebrate it【29†L438-L446】. These targeted offers drive a lot of impulse purchases. 
  - **Lucerne Scrolls Pass:** As discussed, the battle pass itself is sold via the Trading Post building – \$5 or \$20 options【30†L458-L462】. 
  - **Loot Boxes:** There are loot-box style chests (e.g. Golden Keys for hero draws) – while you earn some free, you can effectively buy more by spending gems. This gacha element for unlocking legendary commanders is a major monetization pillar (some players spend heavily to unlock or max a new commander during special “Wheel of Fortune” events). 
  In summary, RoK sells *power in a layered way*: direct resources and speed-ups for immediate needs, and gacha for long-term power (commanders). This fuels a heavy pay-to-win environment at the top end. RoK has had to manage sentiment by ensuring free players can still contribute (e.g. through alliance teamwork and some free legendary commanders), but ultimately spenders have big advantages. For our game, we should decide how far down the RoK path to go. It produces high revenue, but with obvious *competitive imbalance*. If we want to avoid negative sentiment, we might limit sales of direct power and emphasize fair monetization (like cosmetics, passes, and *optional* convenience).

- **State of Survival Store:** SoS’s store is comparable to RoK’s in many ways (typical for 4X games). It sells **biocaps** (premium currency) packs, **resource bundles**, **speed-ups**, and importantly **Hero gear and fragments**. SoS has hero units that you collect/upgrade, so they monetize via selling fragments of new heroes or specialized gear in bundles. They also introduced a **“HQ Skin” system** where base skins give stat boosts – some skins were only obtainable via gacha or events, effectively selling power with cosmetics as a wrapper. This caused some grumbling but many top players still bought them. SoS also often presents big “99% off” style sales – e.g. a bundle that supposedly is \$1000 value for \$9.99 (this marketing tactic went *too far*, leading to the lawsuit for false advertising of discounts【32†L75-L83】). That is a clear lesson: *do not mislead users with fake valuations*. It’s better to let players feel a deal is good by actual utility, not by an inflated strike-through price. Another aspect of SoS monetization is the **monthly subscription cards**: they offer a \$4.99 “Daily Supplies” card that gives some currency every day for 30 days (a very common feature in mobile games). This is a low-pressure sale that many F2P players accept, and it boosts retention (players log in daily to collect). We should strongly consider a similar **monthly card** in our game alongside the main pass, as it’s a proven, low-negative-sentiment monetization (Supercell added one in Clash of Clans too called the Season Bank in Gold Pass). Finally, SoS’s store, like others, updates with **event-specific deals**. E.g., during a “Halloween event” you might see limited packs containing event currencies plus regular items. Overall, SoS’s monetization is effective but has earned a mixed reputation – some players feel it’s very *“pay-to-win and manipulative”*, hence the community anger and legal issues. This reinforces that **transparency and fairness** in store practices are vital for not driving churn.

- **Last War: Survival Store:** Last War’s store and sales tactics are arguably an evolution of everything in RoK/SoS turned up to 11. The **dual monetization** model was: 
  - **IAP-heavy:** Sell premium currency, **speed-ups, and hero unlocks** directly【28†L153-L160】. Players can pay to **“recruit powerful heroes”** and *“unlock exclusive content”* (like those hero weapons) – which appeals to competitive players willing to spend【28†L153-L160】. In practice, Last War constantly tempts you with pop-up offers for bundles relevant to whatever challenge you’re facing (stuck on an upgrade? here’s a pack with the needed resources; a new hero released? here’s a bundle to get them). These packs often have multiple tiers (buy one, unlock a pricier one, etc.), encouraging deep spend. 
  - **Gacha mechanics:** There is a random draw system for heroes (spend premium currency or tokens to roll for hero shards)【28†L157-L160】. This “loot box” approach taps into luck and can generate high spend from those chasing a specific drop. It’s a psychologically potent but contentious monetization form. We must be careful if including gacha – it can cause negative sentiment if odds are low or if exclusive power is locked behind luck. If we include it, consider *publishing odds and having pity timers* to maintain trust. 
  - **Ad rewards:** Interestingly, Last War also leverages ad monetization – players can watch ads for small bonuses【28†L159-L163】. This ensures even non-spenders contribute to revenue and can slightly help their progress. It’s a way to monetize the long tail of free players without hurting anyone (ads are optional). For a $10M budget title, integrating opt-in ad rewards (say, watch an ad to speed up a build by 15 minutes, or to get a free loot roll) can add meaningful revenue (especially in markets like India/Brazil where purchasing power is lower). It’s worth including as long as it’s not overly intrusive. 
  - **Extensive “Hot Deals” rotation:** as detailed earlier, there’s always some special event and corresponding store offer. E.g., every week has a unique **Weekly Pass** for a few dollars, rotating **spend events** where cumulative spending yields bonus rewards, etc.【20†L61-L69】【20†L93-L100】. The sheer frequency of deals in Last War creates a constant impulse to spend (“there’s always *something* valuable I might miss if I don’t buy now”). While great for revenue, this can exhaust players. Indeed, Last War’s own players have described it as *“predatory...engineered to squeeze as much time and money as possible”*【4†L309-L317】. We absolutely want to avoid crossing into that territory. A game that feels fun and fair will keep players (and their wallets) around much longer than one that burns them out in 3 months. 

**Key Takeaway:** For our new RTS base-battler, we need to design a store and monetization system that finds the sweet spot between **profitability and player goodwill**. Based on the above:

- **Primary revenue drivers:** A **Battle Pass** (seasonal, low-cost) and **currency packs** (gems/coins) should be the backbone. These are standard, expected, and generally accepted by players. We will include tiered currency packs (\$5 to \$100) for whales to spend, but ensure the game isn’t *only* about buying currency.  
- **Fair power progression:** It’s okay to sell **speed-ups and resources**, but consider limits or diminishing returns to prevent pure wallet wars. Perhaps implement *daily purchase caps* on certain power items (some games do this to stop one whale from instantly maxing out). Also, ensure anything buyable (like resources) can also be earned by active play (just slower) – this fairness reduces resentment.  
- **Cosmetics and Customization:** We should offer **cosmetic skins** for bases, units, or commanders. These could be in the battle pass or sold standalone. Cosmetics do not affect gameplay, so even high prices for them usually don’t anger players. They also act as status symbols (e.g., unique base skins for top alliance in a season) that can drive engagement.  
- **Hero/Gear system with caution:** If our game includes collectible heroes or special gear, monetizing them is lucrative (gacha or direct purchase). However, to avoid negative sentiment, implement a **pity system or earnable path**. For instance, a new hero could be available via gacha for spenders, but after the event, shards slowly become earnable for free or available in a later battle pass. This mirrors Last War’s approach to exclusive weapons – spenders get it early, others get it later【20†L117-L125】. It maintains competitiveness without completely paywalling content.  
- **Subscription/Membership:** A **monthly subscription** (e.g., \$4.99 for daily bonuses) is highly recommended. It provides stable revenue and encourages daily login (retention). This is usually seen as a good deal by players and not pay-to-win (since it’s generally affordable and gives steady resources, not instant power).  
- **Avoid shady practices:** Do **not** use false reference prices (SoS’s \$997 -> \$9.99 fake sale was disastrous【32†L75-L83】). If we show a discount, it must be a genuine one. Transparency builds trust. Also avoid overly aggressive pop-ups – e.g., don’t interrupt gameplay with an offer every few minutes as that frustrates users. Instead, use a storefront or a subtle notification (Supercell games rarely flash sales in your face; offers live in the store unless tied to a level-up moment).  
- **Community and Whales:** Embrace social monetization but carefully. Features like **alliance gift chests** (where spending rewards your whole guild) can actually incentivize group spending and lessen jealousy (“at least I got something when the whale spent”). Last War and many Chinese games use this effectively【4†L259-L268】. We can include a lighter version of this – e.g., small gifts to others when someone buys a high-tier pack. This encourages a culture of spending as helping the team. But we must also manage whale expectations: perhaps create VIP-only leaderboards or cosmetic titles for big spenders so they feel recognized without breaking the game for others. 

In designing our store, we’ll continuously solicit player feedback (via community or soft launch metrics) to ensure our monetization is strong but not pushing players past their comfort level.

## Monetization Strategy Recommendations for the New RTS Base-Battler  
Combining the above insights, here is a **detailed monetization and design plan** for our new RTS base-builder that aims for high revenue **while respecting player experience** (monetizing “only to the line” that avoids churn or negative sentiment):

**1. Implement a Generous yet Engaging Battle Pass:** Make a seasonal battle pass the centerpiece of monetization. Priced low (around \$4.99), it should provide tremendous value – **10x rewards for its cost** is a good rule【14†L99-L107】. This will drive a high uptake rate (as seen in CoC’s Gold Pass success【16†L120-L128】) and turn many non-spenders into mild spenders, boosting our payer percentage. Structure the pass with daily/weekly challenges that encourage playing all aspects of the game (base building, PvE battles, PvP raids, etc.), thereby increasing engagement. Ensure that free players also get some rewards from the pass (so they see its value and are tempted to upgrade). Each season, include one exclusive cosmetic (like a hero skin or base theme) in the premium track – this creates **FOMO** but since it’s cosmetic, it doesn’t unbalance gameplay. Over time, older exclusives can rotate into the store or events so latecomers can obtain them (signals that we respect all players, not just early spenders). We’ll also consider a second tier “Elite Pass” at ~$14.99 for bonus cosmetics or convenience (like auto-completing some challenges), but this must be tuned not to feel necessary – the \$5 pass remains the core. The goal is a battle pass that **players rave about as a great deal**, improving sentiment and retention, while it steadily monetizes a broad base of users【14†L79-L88】【14†L114-L122】.

**2. Fair Progression with Optional Speed-ups:** The game’s core loop (building/upgrading, training troops) will naturally involve timers and resource constraints. We will monetize this similar to Clash of Clans – by selling **premium currency** that can be used to speed up timers or purchase missing resources. The key is to tune base progression so that free players can make satisfying progress daily, but spenders can accelerate if they choose. We’ll include **wait-time reduction mechanics for free** (e.g., free speed-up for short timers, or help from clanmates to reduce timers) so that the game doesn’t feel like “pay or quit”. This keeps sentiment positive. For spenders, we’ll sell speed-up bundles and resource packs, but perhaps implement *diminishing returns* – e.g., the more you speed-up consecutively, the more it costs – to prevent a single whale from instantly maxing out and demoralizing everyone. Additionally, having **building level caps per HQ level** ensures even spenders must play somewhat. These strategies mean monetization boosts progress but doesn’t totally bypass gameplay, striking a balance.

**3. Avoid Pure Pay-to-Win in PvP:** Since this is an RTS battler, competitive balance is critical for long-term health. We should design monetization such that paying accelerates you *getting to power*, but does not grant unique *unattainable* power. For example, we might sell **gacha pulls for hero units** (common in games like RoK/Last War), but ensure that heroes have counter-play and that f2p players can eventually earn those heroes through grind or events. Introducing a **pity system** for gacha (guaranteed epic hero after X pulls) can prevent the frustration of unlucky free players. Moreover, to reduce whale domination, consider **bracketing PvP by power or time** – e.g., newer servers for new players, periodic merges only when populations drop. This way whales are often competing with each other (as seen in RoK’s KvK where big spenders face off, allowing smaller spenders to compete in lower-tier battlegrounds). Ensuring that skill and strategy (good base layouts, smart troop combos) can sometimes overcome pure spend will also mitigate churn among competitive players. Essentially, we monetize power *incrementally* and **never sell a one-hit win**. No \$100 “Ultimate Laser that destroys any base” – such things would kill the game’s credibility.

**4. Leverage Social Monetization Positively:** We will incorporate alliance/clan systems where members benefit from each other. For monetization, a **gift system** like in Last War could be added: when a player makes a purchase above a threshold, all clanmates get a small gift (e.g., a resource crate). This turns whale spending into a celebrated event rather than something resented – free players literally get freebies when someone spends, so they’re less likely to be bitter about big spenders. It also encourages social pressure in a mild way (people appreciate the gifting player). However, we’ll cap how much gifts can provide (to not inflate the economy too much). Additionally, we might include **alliance-level purchases**: e.g., a clan could collectively work towards purchasing a “Clan Battle Boost” for an upcoming war, funded by contributions (which could be bought with currency). This spreads the cost and makes monetization feel cooperative. The guiding principle: use the **social fabric to enhance monetization** in a feel-good manner, rather than making it a source of stress. (Recall how Last War’s alliance pressure went too far – some felt *obligated* to spend for their team【4†L253-L261】【4†L269-L277】. We want to avoid mandatory spend obligation. Everything should remain *optional*, and a truly skilled or active free player should still be valued in an alliance.)

**5. Embrace Ethical Design – Transparency and Earnable Alternatives:** To keep players on our side, our monetization should be transparent. Clearly display drop rates for any loot boxes. Communicate the value of passes and packs honestly (no fake 99% off tags). If we have VIP levels, ensure the benefits are mainly convenience (e.g., auto-complete daily tasks, minor stat boosts) and not game-breaking, and possibly allow non-spenders to slowly earn VIP points via daily logins (so VIP isn’t purely paid). Also, design events where *both spenders and non-spenders can participate*, with spenders maybe finishing faster but free players still completing eventually. A good example is how Last War lets free players obtain “exclusive” items in the off-season through grind【20†L117-L125】 – we should build similar mechanics so free players know that *with time and effort, they won’t be completely left behind*. This greatly reduces negative sentiment; players may actually feel more inclined to spend when they trust the game isn’t trying to extort them. 

**6. Content Updates and Events for Longevity:** Allocate part of the budget and team to a robust **live-ops schedule**. Frequent updates (though not as crazy as Last War’s every 4 days!) keep the game fresh and give new monetization opportunities. For example, every month introduce a new unit or hero (monetize it lightly at launch, then make it widely available later), or run seasonal events (with thematic cosmetics to buy). Live-ops should be data-driven: if we see a dip in engagement at day 15 for new players, maybe introduce a Day 15 mini-event or reward to re-engage them. By showing players that the game is constantly evolving with **free new features and content**, we justify the spending they do (they feel the game is a service that the developers are supporting, not a static cash-grab). This approach mirrors Supercell’s commitment to updates in CoC【6†L141-L147】 and Last War’s high-frequency content drops【28†L178-L187】, which both aid retention (though we will find a sustainable cadence that a 50-person team can handle). 

**7. Global Market Adaptation:** Since our target markets include US, UK, EU as well as India and Brazil, we must be mindful of economic differences. A one-size pricing may not work globally. We should consider **regional pricing adjustments** or tailored bundles. For instance, India/Brazil have huge player numbers but lower ARPU – maybe offer a special \$1.99 mini-pass or smaller denomination currency packs to encourage spending there. In contrast, Japan (though not a primary target for us, but a good example from Last War) has fewer players but far higher ARPMAU (Japanese players spent ~2.5x more per user than Americans in Last War)【1†L99-L107】. For Western markets, focusing on value (battle pass, cosmetics) and fair play will be key to avoid backlash. For emerging markets, keeping the cost of entry low (even ad-based monetization) will help. Our system of optional ads for bonus resources is especially useful in regions where players can’t afford IAP – it gives them a progression path and monetizes via ad revenue. 

**8. Community Engagement and Feedback Loops:** To ensure we never cross the line into churn-inducing monetization, establish channels to gather player feedback on our monetization. Whether through Discord, in-game surveys, or community managers, we will listen if players say, for example, “X event felt too pay-to-win” or “Y price is too high”. This allows us to adjust before sentiment sours publicly. Being proactive – maybe a monthly dev blog explaining upcoming changes or why we price things as we do – can build goodwill. Players are more forgiving when devs acknowledge issues and show a plan to address them. For instance, if we see battle pass completion rates are low (meaning it’s too grindy, hurting retention), we adjust it next season. If a particular sale caused backlash, we apologize and tweak it. This agile, player-centric approach will differentiate us in a market where many companies are seen as just profit-driven. Our stance should be: **we want to earn revenue by *delighting* players, not by exhausting or deceiving them**.

By following these guidelines, our RTS base-battler can adopt the *most effective monetization elements* from top competitors—such as battle passes, limited-time events, hero collection, and social spend features—while **avoiding the pitfalls** that lead to negative sentiment (predatory pricing, unbridled pay-to-win, neglect of free players). The end result should be a game that **monetizes deeply but sustainably**: players feel comfortable spending on it for years because they enjoy the experience and trust the product. This balances our revenue goals with player satisfaction, leading to healthy retention, a strong community, and long-term franchise potential. 

Ultimately, the strategy is to monetize **respectfully**: push the envelope in terms of creativity and value (so our \$10M budget is well-recouped and then some), but always stop short of the point where players feel exploited. If we execute well, we can achieve a reputation akin to Supercell’s (quality and fairness) with a revenue profile approaching the best of Lilith/FunPlus’s games – a combination that would make our new title a durable top performer in the genre. 

**Sources:** Competitive research and player reports were used to inform these recommendations, including data on retention and monetization from Clash of Clans【6†L149-L157】, Rise of Kingdoms【12†L472-L480】【30†L458-L462】, State of Survival【32†L75-L83】, and Last War: Survival【22†L52-L60】【22†L71-L79】, among others. These sources highlight the strategies and outcomes (both positive and negative) of each game’s monetization approach, guiding our balanced plan.
