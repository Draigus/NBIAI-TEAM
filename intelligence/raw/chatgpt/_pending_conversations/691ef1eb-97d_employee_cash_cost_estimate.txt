Assuming UK employees on typical salaries and a standard benefits package, a good working estimate is that 20 people will cost you roughly **£60k to £100k per month**, depending on salary level and how generous you are with pension and benefits.

To actually put a number on it I need to make some assumptions, so here is one concrete worked example plus a quick way for you to plug in your own numbers.

---

## 1. What drives the employer cash cost

For each employee, your annual cash cost is roughly:

> **Total employer cost  
> = Gross salary  
> + Employer National Insurance  
> + Employer pension contributions  
> + Employer paid benefits**

Key UK parameters right now:

- Employer NI on salaries is moving to **15%** of earnings above the secondary threshold from April 2025.citeturn1search4turn1search8turn1search10turn1search14  
- Minimum auto enrolment pension is an overall **8% of qualifying earnings**, of which the employer must pay at least **3%**, but many decent schemes pay **4–6%** from the employer side.citeturn1search1turn1search3turn1search9turn1search15  

For a planning model, most UK SMEs assume:

- Employer NI: about **14–15% of salary**  
- Employer pension: **3–6% of salary**  
- Other benefits (healthcare, life, perks): use a flat **3–7% of salary** as an estimate, unless you cost them line by line.

That gives a typical **“on top” load of roughly 20–30% of salary**.

---

## 2. Worked example: average salary £40k, 5% pension, 5% benefits

Assumptions for this example:

- Location: UK, tax year 2025–26  
- Average gross salary per person: **£40,000**  
- Employer NI: **15%** of earnings above £5,000  
- Employer pension: **5%** of full salary  
- Employer benefits: **5%** of full salary  

Step by step per employee, per year:

1. **Employer NI**

- Taxable earnings for NI = £40,000 − £5,000 = **£35,000**  
- Employer NI = 15% of £35,000  
  - 10% of 35,000 = 3,500  
  - 5% of 35,000 = 1,750  
  - Total = **£5,250**

2. **Employer pension (5%)**

- 5% of £40,000 = 0.05 × 40,000 = **£2,000**

3. **Employer benefits (5%)**

- 5% of £40,000 = **£2,000**

4. **Total employer cost per employee**

- Salary: 40,000  
- Employer NI: 5,250  
- Pension: 2,000  
- Benefits: 2,000  

Total per employee per year = 40,000 + 5,250 + 2,000 + 2,000  
= **£49,250**

Per employee per month = 49,250 ÷ 12 ≈ **£4,104**  

Now scale up to **20 people**:

- Annual cost = 49,250 × 20 = **£985,000**  
- Monthly cost = 985,000 ÷ 12 ≈ **£82,083 per month**

So under these assumptions, **20 staff at £40k each costs you roughly £82k per month in cash outlay**.

---

## 3. How it changes with salary level

Using the same assumptions (15% NI above £5k, 5% pension, 5% benefits):

### If average salary is £30k

Per employee, per year:

- Employer NI: 15% of (30,000 − 5,000) = 0.15 × 25,000 = 3,750  
- Pension (5%): 1,500  
- Benefits (5%): 1,500  
- Total per head = 30,000 + 3,750 + 1,500 + 1,500 = **£36,750**

For 20 people:

- Annual = 36,750 × 20 = **£735,000**  
- Monthly ≈ **£61,250**

### If average salary is £50k

Per employee, per year:

- Employer NI: 15% of (50,000 − 5,000) = 0.15 × 45,000 = 6,750  
- Pension (5%): 2,500  
- Benefits (5%): 2,500  
- Total per head = 50,000 + 6,750 + 2,500 + 2,500 = **£61,750**

For 20 people:

- Annual = 61,750 × 20 = **£1,235,000**  
- Monthly ≈ **£102,917**

---

## 4. Quick rule of thumb you can reuse

For UK planning, a simple planning shortcut is:

> **Employer monthly cost for N staff ≈  
> N × (Average salary ÷ 12) × (1 + overhead%)**

Where:

- Overhead% = Employer NI% on salary + pension% + benefits%  
- For many reasonable packages, overhead% lands in the **23–30%** range.

So, for example, 20 staff at £40k with a 25% overhead:

- Per head per month base salary = 40,000 ÷ 12 ≈ 3,333  
- Add 25% overhead: 3,333 × 1.25 ≈ 4,167  
- For 20 staff: 4,167 × 20 ≈ **£83,340 per month**  
  (which is very close to the fully worked £82k figure above, the difference is the NI threshold.)
