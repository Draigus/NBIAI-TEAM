### 1.4 How Seed, Series A, and due diligence differ in practice

From an investor’s perspective, Seed and Series A are different questions:

- Seed: “Is this the right team, with a compelling game and a credible plan, worth betting on now?”  
- Series A: “Is this a growing games business with evidence of product market fit and a scalable machine we can pour capital into?” citeturn0search29turn0search1  

In practice this means:

At Seed, investors mainly look for:

- A clear game vision and sharp understanding of the player fantasy  
- Evidence that you can build, test, and learn quickly, even if metrics are early  
- A founding team that understands F2P, live ops, and UA at least at a conceptual level  
- A realistic plan for using the money to reach strong proof points

At Series A, investors expect:

- Live game or soft launched product with real players  
- Retention curves that show a path to healthy D1, D7, and D30 for your genre (for many genres, investors look for roughly 30 percent D1 and 10 to 20 percent D7 as a starting point, with nuance by genre and market) citeturn0search15turn0search3turn0search11  
- Early monetisation: conversion to payer, ARPDAU, and emerging LTV curves  
- A clear UA story: channel mix, creatives that work, and growing cohorts where predicted LTV is on track to exceed CPI  
- A functioning data stack, dashboards, and cohort analysis, not screenshots from a single ad platform  
- Early organisation structure: who owns product, data, UA, live ops, and how you will scale headcount  

Due diligence and the data room are the “audit trail” behind the deck:

- The deck is the story.  
- The data room is the evidence.

For Seed, the data room can be relatively light but must still show clear thinking, simple but coherent financials, and honest documentation. For Series A, the data room becomes a structured library of:

- Product documentation  
- Metrics exports  
- Financial statements and projections  
- Legal and corporate documents  
- Hiring and organisational information citeturn0search2turn0search6turn0search30

## Seed Slide 1: Cover and one line summary

### Goal of the slide

To orient the investor instantly:

- Who you are  
- What you are building  
- At what stage  
- How much you are raising  

This slide should make it trivial to remember you later.

  

### What investors expect at Seed

A Seed investor expects to see, within two seconds:

- Company or studio name  
- Game or project name (if named)  
- One line that captures the essence of the game or studio  
- Round type (pre seed, Seed, Seed extension)  
- Amount you are raising now  

They do not expect dense text, metrics, or logos of every tool you use. They just want a clean, confident opening.

  

### What to gather before building it

Collect:

- Your agreed studio name, spelled consistently with your legal documents  
- The working or final title of your lead game  
- A one line summary, for example:  
  - “Tactical co op roguelite for busy adults on mobile”  
  - “Cosy merge farming game for 25 to 45 year old women who love decorating”  
- Round label: “Raising Seed round” or “Raising 1.5 million Seed round”  
- Current logo or wordmark for studio and game, even if it is temporary  
- A single strong key image: concept art, early in game screenshot, or a clean genre based visual  

  

### How to build the slide (layout and structure)

Recommended layout:

- Top left: studio logo or name  
- Top right: round label, for example “Seed round” or “Raising 1.5 million Seed”  
- Centre: bold game or studio title  
- Under title: your one line summary, in plain language  
- Background: one strong image or clean gradient, not a noisy collage  

Avoid overcrowding. This is essentially your front cover.

  

### What to write and what to chart, with examples

There are no charts on this slide.

Example text:

- Title: “Northshore Games”  
- Subtitle: “Project Tidefall”  
- One liner: “A co operative, session friendly tactics game for mobile players who love deep strategy but only have 10 minutes at a time.”  
- Round label: “Raising 2 million Seed round”

If you are a studio with multiple titles, you can choose either:

- Studio led: “Northshore Games” as the main title, with one line about the portfolio and a “Lead title: Tidefall” sub line; or  
- Game led: “Tidefall” as the main title, with “A Northshore Games title” under it.

Pick the framing that matches your narrative. If the studio vision is clear and long term, lead with the studio. If investors will mostly care about the flagship game, lead with the game.

  

### Pitfalls to avoid

- Overloading the slide with buzzwords like “AI driven”, “blockchain”, “metaverse”, unless truly central to the game and acceptable to your investors.  
- Putting metrics or detailed bullet points on the cover. Those belong later.  
- Using concept art that does not remotely match your current plan or style.  
- Inconsistent naming: using one studio or game name here and a different one later, or spelling it differently across slides.  
- Making the one liner incomprehensible without insider knowledge.

  

### How to check the quality of this slide

Ask:

- Can someone who sees only this slide remember what we do and at what stage?  
- Is our one line summary understandable to a non gamer investor?  
- Does the image broadly reflect the game we plan to build, not a fantasy we cannot deliver?  
- Is every name on this slide used consistently in the rest of the deck and data room?

If the answer to any question is “no”, rewrite before moving on.

### Goal of the slide

To give investors a concise, compelling description of:

- The game you are building  
- The players you serve  
- Why it matters now  

By the end of this slide, they should have a clear mental picture of the experience and the ambition.

  

### What investors expect at Seed

Investors expect:

- A short, sharp “elevator pitch” paragraph, two to four sentences  
- Plain language description of genre, platform, and core experience  
- A hint of why this opportunity is timely or differentiated

### What to gather before building it

Collect:

- A two to four sentence elevator pitch that covers:

  - Platform and session length  
  - One clear differentiator  

- A short statement of vision, for example:

  - “To build the leading tactical co op franchise on mobile for players who want depth without 30 minute sessions.”  

- Reference games you can name for comparison.

Recommended layout:

- Top: slide title “Vision and elevator pitch”  
- Left side: one text box with your elevator pitch paragraph  
- Right side: a simple “at a glance” list:

  - Genre  
  - Platforms  
  - Target regions  
  - Session length  
  - Solo / co op / competitive  

- Optional small strip at the bottom: “Reference titles: X, Y, Z”

### What to write and what to chart, with examples

No charts here, but precise wording matters.

Example elevator pitch:

“ Tidefall is a co operative, turn based tactics game for iOS and Android, designed for five to ten minute sessions. - Genre: Co operative tactics, squad based  
- Platforms: iOS, Android  
- Regions: Global English first, then Western Europe and North America

### Pitfalls to avoid

- Using only abstract marketing language: “redefining engagement”, “next generation experience” without saying what the player actually does.  
- Hiding the fact that it is F2P. Say “free to play” openly.

- Do we clearly state genre, platform, and rough session length?  
- Is our differentiator understandable and believable, not just “better game design”?

## Seed Slide 3: Player problem and fantasy

### Goal of the slide

To show:

- The player needs or frustrations you are addressing  
- The fantasy or emotional promise your game fulfils  

You are answering “why will anyone care?” in human terms, not just market size.

  

### What investors expect at Seed

Investors expect:

- A clear description of the player problem and emotional need  
- Simple, believable statements backed by light evidence: quotes, survey snippets, or behavioural observations  
- A link between the problem and your game’s core experience  

They do not expect an academic research paper, but they do expect more than “players are bored”.

  

### What to gather before building it

Collect:

- A short list of player problems, for example:

  - “Strategy fans feel forced into 30 minute sessions or energy timers that do not fit into their day.”

SEED SLIDE 5: CORE LOOP AND PROGRESSION  

Goal of the slide  

Explain what players actually do over and over, and how they grow in power, options, or status over time.  

By the end of this slide an investor should be able to describe:  

- The main actions players repeat.  
- How those actions connect to rewards.  
- How progression systems keep the game interesting over weeks and months.  

What investors expect at Seed  

They expect:  

- A clear, readable core loop diagram.  
- A short explanation of how that loop feels to play.  
- A simple view of progression: what grows, what unlocks, and why that is compelling.  

They do not expect full design documents, spreadsheets, or every edge case.  

What to gather before building it  

- A draft core loop diagram, even if sketched on paper first.  
- A list of three to six key player verbs, such as explore, fight, craft, merge, decorate, socialise, compete.  
- A short description of progression systems, for example:  
  - Hero levels, gear tiers, building upgrades, collection sets, league ranks.  
- A sense of your difficulty curve and content pacing over the first days and weeks.  

How to build the slide (layout and structure)  

Suggested layout:  

- Centre: circular or flow diagram of the core loop with four to six steps and arrows.  
- Left or below: three to five bullets describing how the loop feels in play.  
- Right: a small “progression map” listing your main progression systems and what they give the player.  

Keep text large and labels clear. The loop diagram should be legible when printed.  

What to write and what to chart, with examples  

Example core loop for a tactics game:  

Diagram steps:  

1. Choose mission.  
2. Select squad and loadout.  
3. Play mission (position, act, co operate).  
4. Earn rewards.  
5. Upgrade heroes and base.  
6. Unlock new missions and events.  

Supporting bullets (how it feels):  

- “Every session delivers at least one complete mission and noticeable progress.”  
- “Skillful play and co operation produce better rewards and unlock harder content.”  
- “Metagame upgrades unlock new tactical options, not just bigger numbers.”  

Progression map example:  

- “Heroes: level, skills, gear tiers, passive traits.”  
- “Base: buildings that unlock new missions, gear crafting, and social features.”  
- “Account: league or rank that unlocks new matchmaking tiers and cosmetics.”  

Pitfalls to avoid  

- Overly complex diagrams that need a magnifying glass.  
- Loops that are purely mechanical and do not connect back to the fantasy you described earlier.  
- Progression systems that are just linear power ramps without new choices.  
- Diagrams that do not match what is actually in your prototype or GDD.  

How to check quality  

Ask:  

- Can a non designer investor explain what players do in a five or ten minute session after viewing this slide?  
- Is there a clear link between the loop and the fantasy from slide 3?  
- Do progression systems add new options and depth over time, not only higher stats?  
- Does this slide align with your monetisation and live ops plans later in the deck?  

If you cannot answer yes to these questions, simplify and tighten the loop until you can.

They expect:  

- A clear target audience description, not “everyone on mobile”.

- A reason why this audience and segment is attractive and reachable for a small studio.

What to gather before building it  

- A specific audience definition, for example:  
  - “Women 25 to 45 who enjoy decorating and cosy games on mobile.”  
  - “Men and women 25 to 40 who grew up on PC tactics games and now play on mobile.”

  - Market and genre reports for mobile games.

  - Target regions.

How to build the slide (layout and structure)  

Suggested layout:  

- Left: “Target audience” block with a short profile and two or three personas if useful.  
- Right top: simple market size visual, such as a funnel or segmented bar showing the broad mobile market, your genre segment, and your reachable slice.  
- Right bottom: one or two bullets summarising why this segment is attractive and why you can win in it.  

What to write and what to chart, with examples  

Target audience example:  

“Primary: Adults 25 to 40 who enjoy deep strategy but have limited continuous time to play. They own mid to high end Android or iOS devices, play several times a week, and have a history with tactics titles such as X and Y.

Reasons this segment is attractive example:

- Huge, vague market claims that are clearly unrealistic for your team.

- Inconsistent numbers across slides and your financial model.  

How to check quality  

Ask:

- Are our market numbers grounded in sources we can point to in the data room?

SEED SLIDE 7: COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE AND DIFFERENTIATION  

Goal of the slide

What investors expect at Seed  

They expect:  

- A clear list of reference and competitor titles for your genre and fantasy.  
- A simple visual comparison, such as a 2 by 2, table, or spider chart.  
- A believable differentiation story, not just “better game”.  

They do not expect you to claim that you have no competition.  

What to gather before building it  

- A shortlist of three to six competitor and reference titles in your space.  
- For each:  
  - Genre and high level pitch.  
  - Download and revenue level if available.  
  - Strengths and weaknesses relative to your game.  
- Two to three axes that matter for your players, for example:  
  - “Session length” and “depth of tactics”.  
  - “Co op focus” and “aggressiveness of monetisation”.  
  - “Cosy feel” and “pressure to grind”.  

How to build the slide (layout and structure)  

Suggested layout:  

Option A: 2 by 2 map  

- Place axes that matter, such as “session friendly” versus “session heavy” on one axis, and “tactical depth” versus “shallow” on the other.  
- Place competitors and your game on the map.  

Option B: feature table  

- Columns for each title, including yours.  
- Rows for core aspects: co op design, monetisation style, art tone, depth, content cadence.  
- Ticks or simple notes in cells.  

Either way, keep labels readable and avoid clutter.  

What to write and what to chart, with examples  

Example 2 by 2 axes for tactics game:  

- Horizontal: “Session length” from “short and snackable” to “long and demanding”.  
- Vertical: “Tactical depth” from “light” to “deep”.  

You might place:  

- Popular idle battler: short sessions, light depth.  
- Hardcore PVP strategy: long sessions, deep depth.  
- Your game: short sessions, deep depth.  

Differentiation bullets example:  

- “Designed from day one for short sessions with deep tactical decisions.”  
- “True co op campaign rather than only PVP or friend list boosts.”  
- “Monetisation focused on cosmetic and convenience purchases, not pay to win.”  

Pitfalls to avoid  

- Axes that are clearly chosen just to make you look good and put everyone else in a bad corner.  
- Claiming features that you cannot realistically build with your team and budget.  
- Ignoring very close substitutes because they feel uncomfortable to admit.  
- Overcrowded tables with tiny text that nobody can read.  

How to check quality  

Ask:  

- Would players of these games agree with where we place them?  
- Are we honest about where we are weaker or narrower than some competitors?  
- Does our differentiation matter for our target audience, not just for us as designers?  
- Does this slide line up with our feature roadmap, monetisation, and UA messages?  

  

SEED SLIDE 8: TRACTION AND VALIDATION SO FAR  

Goal of the slide  

Show what you have already done to de risk the opportunity: prototypes, tests, metrics, and learning, even if sample sizes are small.  

What investors expect at Seed  

They expect:  

- Honest, early proof that players like what you are building.  
- Clear explanation of what you tested, how, and what you learned.  
- Early metrics if you have any external tests or soft launch numbers.  

They do not expect polished KPIs at full scale.  

What to gather before building it  

- A list of all meaningful validation activities so far, such as:  
  - Internal playtests and structured feedback.  
  - External playtests, closed alphas, or test flights.  
  - Small UA tests with landing pages or simple builds.  
  - Surveys, interviews, or diary studies.  
- Any available metrics, for example:  
  - D1, D3, D7 retention from early tests.  
  - Session length and frequency.  
  - Basic funnel numbers (tutorial completion, core action completion).  
- Key learnings and changes made as a result of these tests.  

How to build the slide (layout and structure)  

Suggested layout:  

- Left: “What we have tested” block with short bullets.  
- Right top: simple chart or table with one or two key early metrics, clearly labelled as early and small sample.  
- Right bottom: “What we learned and changed” bullets.  

What to write and what to chart, with examples  

Example “What we have tested”:  

- “Three internal playtest rounds with 30 players total across four weeks.”  
- “One closed alpha on Android with 400 installs driven by small UA spend.”  
- “Two rounds of concept testing on art style and messaging with 120 survey responses.”  

Example chart:  

- Simple bar chart for D1, D3, D7 retention across two alpha builds, with labels like “Build A (n=180)” and “Build B (n=220)”.  
- When sample sizes are small, include the n values and describe them in text.  

Example “What we learned and changed”:  

- “Players loved the tactical gameplay but felt missions were too long; we cut average session length from 18 minutes to 9 minutes.”  
- “Our initial monetisation design felt pay to win; we removed direct stat items and moved value into cosmetics and battle pass track.”  
- “Art direction B clearly outperformed A in surveys; we switched to a slightly stylised but grounded look.”  

Pitfalls to avoid  

- Hiding weak numbers. Better to be transparent and show what you changed in response.  
- Mixing store wide metrics (for example retention from unrelated tests) with your game metrics.  
- Presenting UA tests that are too small as definitive proof.  
- Using blended averages that hide strong cohort differences.  

How to check quality  

Ask:  

- Are we honest and explicit about sample size, region, and channel for every metric?  
- Do we clearly show cause and effect between learnings and design changes?  
- Can we back raw numbers with exports in the data room?  
- Does the level of traction match the claims we make in our narrative?  

  

SEED SLIDE 9: MONETISATION MODEL AND ECONOMY PHILOSOPHY  

Goal of the slide  

Explain how the game will make money, and how that sits with your values about fairness, fun, and long term player relationships.  

What investors expect at Seed  

They expect:  

- A clear description of your revenue model: in app purchases, optional ads, and any other streams such as cosmetics, battle passes, or expansions.  
- A simple breakdown of where you expect revenue to come from in the long run.  
- An explicit philosophy on what you will and will not do in monetisation.  

They do not expect a fully tuned economy model at this stage, but they do expect you to understand basic F2P economics.  

What to gather before building it  

- List of your main revenue components, for example:  
  - Cosmetic skins, cosmetics bundles, and personalisation.  
  - Battle pass or season track.  
  - Convenience items such as time savers, but with clear limits.  
  - Optional rewarded video or interstitial ads, with frequency caps.  
- High level economy and pricing structure:  
  - Target price points for starter packs and bundles.  
  - Where payers gain value and where non payers still feel respected.  
- Examples of reference economies from comparable titles.  
- Any early revenue or conversion data, if you already ran a soft launch.  

How to build the slide (layout and structure)  

Suggested layout:  

- Left: “Monetisation model” bullets describing the main revenue streams.  
- Right top: simple revenue mix diagram or table showing, for example, “cosmetics 40 percent, battle pass 30 percent, convenience items 20 percent, ads 10 percent” as long term intent, clearly marked as illustrative.  
- Right bottom: “Economy philosophy” bullets that state your red lines and principles.  

What to write and what to chart, with examples  

Example “Monetisation model”:  

- “Primary: cosmetic skins for heroes and bases, cosmetic bundles, and event cosmetics.”  
- “Secondary: season pass with free and paid tracks, offering cosmetics and mild progression boosts.”  
- “Tertiary: optional rewarded video slots in low intensity parts of the loop, with hard frequency caps.”  

Example revenue mix (clearly labelled as target, not actual):  

- “Long term target revenue mix (illustrative):  
  - 40 percent cosmetics.  
  - 30 percent season pass.  
  - 20 percent convenience.  
  - 10 percent ads.”  

Example “Economy philosophy”:  

- “No pay to win: spenders gain convenience and customisation, not unbeatable power.”  
- “Non payers can access all core gameplay content with reasonable effort.”  
- “Ads are optional and never interrupt high intensity combat or critical decisions.”  
- “Limited use of time savers with clear caps to avoid pressure to pay simply to play.”  

Pitfalls to avoid  

- Vague statements such as “we will monetise like top games in the genre” with no detail.  
- Heavy reliance on mechanics that you have explicitly told investors you will not use, such as loot boxes or gambling like wheels.  
- Revenue mixes that are unrealistic for your genre.  
- Avoiding the topic of payer share and conversion entirely.  

How to check quality  

Ask:  

- Can we explain how a typical payer will spend in our game, month by month?  
- Does our philosophy here match the experience we describe earlier and later?  
- Can we point to reference titles that use similar models successfully?  
- When we produce a detailed economy and revenue model, will it make sense against this slide?

1. INTRODUCTION AND HOW TO USE THIS GUIDE  

1.1 What this guide is  

This guide is a practical manual for founders of free to play mobile game studios who want to raise Seed and Series A investment. It is designed to be used as a working tool, not just something to read once.

It takes you from a blank file to three connected outcomes.  

You will build:  

- A Seed pitch deck for your studio and lead game.  
- A Series A ready version of the same story, upgraded with live data and deeper expectations.  
- A due diligence pack and data room that back up every material claim in the deck.  

The guide assumes you are prepared to do real work. It will tell you what to gather, how to structure each slide, and how to check whether your work is good enough. It will also show you what investors actually care about, and where they are likely to push back.

For the Seed section, every individual slide in the deck has a matching teaching page with the same pattern:  

- Goal of the slide.  
- What investors expect at Seed.

- What to write and what to chart, with examples.  
- Pitfalls to avoid.  
- How to check the quality of the slide, including a simple 1 to 5 score.  

The Series A section shows how expectations change and how to upgrade each cluster of Seed slides when your game is live.  

The due diligence section gives you a recommended data room structure, and shows how each key slide maps to related documents, exports and model tabs.  

The appendices expand on the parts founders usually find hardest: data and observability, UX research, financial models, creative testing, talking to different investor types, running the live pitch, and choosing charts.  

The glossary and final checklist give you a way to finish. They help you decide whether the deck, model and data room are ready to send.  


1.2 What this guide is not  

This is not:  

- A generic startup pitch guide rewritten with a few games examples.  
- A substitute for thinking deeply about your players, product and finances.  
- A magic template you can fill in without gathering real data.  

It is also not a guide for:  

- Crypto, NFTs or token based fundraising.  
- On chain tokenomics and similar financial engineering.  
- Grey area monetisation such as heavy loot boxes and disguised gambling.  
- Hyper aggressive dark patterns that would fail platform policies or serious investor ethics.  

The working assumption is a clean, long term, free to play mobile studio. Monetisation is based on in app purchases, with optional respectful ads that do not mislead players or break the fantasy.

The guide does not try to teach basic game design from first principles. It assumes you can design and build a game. It focuses instead on how to communicate that game and business to investors.

1.4 How Seed, Series A and due diligence differ in practice  

Investors look at Seed and Series A very differently.

In simple terms:  

- Seed is a belief decision.  
- Series A is a scaling decision.  

Seed  

At Seed, investors mostly ask:  

- Is this the right team to bet on for this space and this product?  
- Is the game vision sharp and compelling enough to be worth the risk?  
- Have they done enough early validation to prove that players care?  
- Do they understand the basics of F2P: retention, monetisation, live ops and UA?  
- Do they have a realistic, staged plan for the next 18 to 24 months?  

Seed investors are tolerant of incomplete data, rough edges and prototypes. They are not tolerant of sloppiness, fantasy and dishonesty.

Series A  

At Series A, investors expect a real, operating business in embryo form. They ask:  

- Does this game have a credible path to product market fit for its audience and genre?  
- Do we believe in the retention curves, and are they moving the right way cohort by cohort?  
- Do we see evidence of monetisation: conversion to payer, ARPDAU, ARPPU, early payer distribution?  
- Is there a coherent LTV versus CPI story with a view on payback time, channels and creative?  
- Does the team have the people, structure and processes to run a live game at scale?  
- Does the financial model connect sensibly to game and UA metrics?  

Series A investors are still tolerant of uncertainty. They are not tolerant of invented numbers, blended metrics that hide issues, or decks that talk about data but never show it properly.

Due diligence  

Due diligence is the process where investors and their advisors check whether the story in your deck matches reality.

Three rules:  

- The deck is the pitch.  
- The model is the map.  
- The data room is the evidence.  

At Seed, due diligence is often lighter. It typically focuses on:  

- Corporate and legal basics: company structure, ownership, options, IP assignment.  
- Basic financials: historic accounts if any, plus a simple forward model.  
- Product and UX documentation.  
- Any metrics, playtest results or research you already have.  

At Series A, due diligence becomes more formal and heavier. It will usually include:  

- Legal and corporate: full set of corporate documents, shareholder agreements, option plans and IP assignments.  
- Financial: historic accounts, management accounts, forecasts, revenue recognition policies.  
- Product: detailed design documentation, roadmaps, live ops calendars, backlogs.  
- Data: raw exports of retention, revenue, funnels, cohorts, experiments, and UA performance.  
- People: organisation charts, key hire plans, contracts for senior staff.  

This guide treats due diligence as something you design for from day one. Every important chart or claim in the deck should have a matching file or export in the data room.  


1.5 How to work through the guide and map it to slides  

The guide is written to be used step by step. It is also designed so that you can drop into a specific section when you need it.

A suggested workflow for a Seed stage founder is:  

Step 1: Skim the landscape  

Read:  

- Section 1 (this introduction).  
- Section 2.1 (Seed slide list).  
- Section 4 overview (Series A upgrades) once you reach that part.  

Your goal is to understand what investors will expect over the life of the company, not only at the next round.  

Step 2: Build the Seed deck slide by slide  

For each Seed slide S01 to S14:  

- Read the teaching page.  
- Decide which of the variations apply to you. For some slides there are options for pre prototype, prototype and soft launch stages.  
- Gather the inputs the page tells you to collect.  
- Build the slide in your deck tool.  
- Use the pitfall and quality checklist to score the slide from 1 to 5.  
- Write down any obvious gaps that you cannot yet fill.

Step 3: Prepare the model and data room in parallel

- Start a simple three sheet model using the structure in Appendix C.

- As you finish a slide, put the underlying spreadsheets, docs or exports into the matching data room folders.

Step 4: Run a first full self review  

Once all Seed slides exist:  

- Walk through the whole deck as if you are an investor seeing it for the first time.

Step 5: Use the Series A section as a roadmap

1.6 Scoring your own deck and using this as a coaching tool  

This guide is designed to support three different views:  

- A founder working alone.  
- A founder working with an internal leadership team.  
- A founder working with an external advisor or NBI style consulting partner.  

Each teaching page includes a simple 1 to 5 scoring grid. The same pattern is used across slides. You can write this on paper or make your own tracking sheet.

Suggested 1 to 5 rubric per slide:  

1: Poor  
- The slide is missing, misleading, or clearly not ready.  
- If an investor saw this now, they would downgrade the whole deck.  

2: Weak  
- The basics are present but important pieces are missing.  
- Clarity is low or design is messy.  
- You would not be comfortable showing this slide without apologising.  

3: Adequate  
- The slide covers the basics correctly.  
- It avoids most major pitfalls.  
- It would not win any prizes, but it would not damage you.  

4: Strong  
- The slide is clear, well structured and backed by real data or specific thinking.  
- It anticipates the first obvious investor questions.  
- An investor could lift it and use it as an example in a workshop.  

5: Outstanding  
- The slide is crisp, elegant and well supported.  
- It shows insight, not just information.  
- It would survive being picked apart by a harsh panel without needing excuses.  

How to use this rubric:  

- After you draft each slide, give it an honest 1 to 5.  
- Once you have the full deck, compute an average and distribution.  
- As a rough guide:  
  - Average below 3: do not send the deck to investors yet.  
  - Average between 3 and 4 with a few 2s: suitable for friends and advisors, not yet for funds.  
  - Average above 4 with no 1s or 2s: this is approaching investor ready.  

If you work with a consultant, you can each fill out the rubric independently and compare. The gaps between scores are where conversation and improvement will be most useful.

3. SEED SLIDE TEACHING PAGES (S01 TO S07)  

3.1 S01 – Cover and one line summary  

Purpose of this slide  

Orient the investor instantly. In a single glance they should know:  

- Who you are.  
- What you are building.  
- What stage you are at.  
- How much you are raising.  

This is your first impression and your anchor when they talk about you later.  

What investors look for at Seed  

- Studio name and game name, clearly stated and consistently used.  
- A plain language one line summary that actually explains the product.  
- Round label and target raise amount.  
- Founder contact details.  
- Visual tone that matches the kind of game you are building.  

They do not want:  

- Buzzword soup.  
- A wall of logos.  
- A background that makes the text unreadable.  

What to gather before building it  

- Final or working studio name and logo or wordmark.  
- Working title of the lead game.  
- One sentence summary in plain language, for example:  
  - “Co operative tactics for busy adults on mobile.”  
  - “Cosy merge farming and decorating for women 25 to 45.”  
- Round label and amount: “Raising 1.8 million Seed round for 18 to 24 months of runway.”  
- Best current image that reflects the actual art direction: early in engine screenshot, polished mockup or concept that is realistic.  
- Founder name and a professional contact address.  

How to build the slide  

Layout suggestion:  

- Top left: studio wordmark or logo.  
- Top right: round label and amount, for example “Seed round – 1.8 million”.  
- Centre: game title or, if you are very studio led, studio title with “Lead title: X”.  
- Below title: one line summary in a large readable font.  
- Bottom left or right: founder name and email.  
- Background: clean image or simple gradient, not a collage.  

Keep it simple. Think of this as a book cover, not a summary slide.  

Content, examples and stage variants  

Example wording  

- Title: “Northshore Games”.  
- Subtitle: “Tidefall”.  
- One line: “Co operative turn based tactics on mobile, designed for five to ten minute sessions.”  
- Round label: “Raising 2.0 million Seed round”.  
- Contact: “alex@northshoregames.com”.  

Stage variants  

Pre prototype  

- You may only have concept art and a working title. Use what you have, but keep the art close to what you can actually achieve in scope and style.  
- Do not pretend you are already in soft launch. The round label might be “Pre seed” or “Seed to build prototype”.  

Prototype  

- Use in engine screenshots, even if rough, rather than concept art that will never ship.  
- You can mention “prototype live with internal testers” on a very small line if you want, but the main function of this slide does not change.  

Soft launch  

- You can optionally add a small line such as “Soft launched in Canada and the UK” if this is central to your story. Do not clutter the slide trying to add metrics.  

Pitfalls and red flags  

- Overly clever taglines that hide the actual genre.  
- Round amount missing or inconsistent with what you are asking for in conversations.  
- Art that looks like a completely different game.  
- Company or game names that change spelling later in the deck.  
- No contact details.  

Quality check and scoring  

Link to data room:  

- A1: One page overview of studio and game.  

Score your slide from 1 to 5:  

- 1: A stranger could not tell what you do, or key details are missing.  
- 2: Basics are there, but visual clutter or vague wording make it hard to parse.  
- 3: Clear, acceptable cover that does not harm you, but not memorable.  
- 4: Clean, sharp and aligned with your game. Easy to remember and share.  
- 5: Instantly communicates who you are and what you do, with visuals and text working together.  

If you are below 4, rewrite the one liner and simplify the layout.  


3.2 S02 – Vision and elevator pitch  

Purpose of this slide  

Explain, in a short, concrete way:  

- What you are building.  
- Who it is for.  
- Why now is the right time.  

After this slide an investor should be able to tell a colleague what your game is without opening the rest of the deck.  

What investors look for at Seed  

- A clear two to four sentence elevator pitch.  
- Plain language description of genre, platform, session pattern and key fantasy.  
- A hint of differentiation: what you are doing that is genuinely different or better.  

They are not looking for lore, history or full feature lists.  

What to gather before building it  

- A two to four sentence elevator pitch that includes:  
  - Genre and key verbs (fight, build, merge, decorate, explore, socialise, compete).  
  - Player fantasy (“feel like a brilliant commander”, “create and care for your own cosy space”).  
  - Platforms (iOS, Android).  
  - Typical session length and frequency.  
  - One or two real differentiators.  
- Two or three reference titles that investors are likely to know.  

How to build the slide  

Layout suggestion:  

- Left side: elevator pitch text (your best short paragraph).  
- Right side: “At a glance” box with short bullets:  

  - Genre.  
  - Platforms.  
  - Session pattern (for example “5 to 10 minute sessions, 1 to 3 times per day”).  
  - Modes (solo, co op, competitive, guilds).  
  - Initial target regions.  

- Bottom of the slide: “Reference titles” as a simple text line.  

Content, examples and stage variants  

Example elevator pitch  

“Tidefall is a co operative, turn based tactics game for iOS and Android designed for five to ten minute sessions. Players command small squads of heroes through tightly designed missions that reward smart positioning and team play, not reflexes. The game brings the depth of classic tactics to mobile with modern F2P progression, live seasons and respectful monetisation.”  

“At a glance” example  

- Genre: Co operative turn based tactics (midcore).  
- Platforms: iOS and Android.  
- Sessions: 5 to 10 minutes, 2 to 4 times per day.  
- Modes: Solo, co op, weekly events.  
- Initial regions: UK, Germany, US and Canada.  

Reference titles:  

- “For example: X, Y and Z.”  

Stage variants  

Pre prototype  

- Focus the pitch more on player fantasy and market gap, less on specific systems you have not validated.  
- Be explicit that the vision will evolve with playtests.  

Prototype  

- Keep the pitch but ensure it matches what people actually experience in the prototype.  
- If you have learned something that changed your vision, update the slide.  

Soft launch  

- You can include one short sentence acknowledging that you are in market, such as “Currently soft launched in Canada and the UK.”  
- Do not add metrics; there will be separate slides for data.  

Pitfalls and red flags  

- Taglines that could fit any game, such as “redefining engagement” or “next generation metagame”.  
- Hiding the fact that your title is free to play.  
- Overloading the slide with secondary features.  
- Claiming to be “AAA” without the team and budget to back that up.  

Quality check and scoring  

Link to data room:  

- A1: One page overview.  
- A3: Concept and pitch docs, including early versions if helpful.  

Score from 1 to 5:  

- 1: Vague, jargon heavy, or misleading. An outsider could not explain your game.  
- 2: Partially clear but missing genre, platform or session details.  
- 3: Clear enough, but generic; could describe several other titles.  
- 4: Concrete, specific, clearly linked to a real audience and fantasy.  
- 5: Sharp, memorable, and consistent with the rest of the deck and product.  

If you are not at least at 4, refine until a non gamer friend can repeat your pitch back to you accurately.  


3.3 S03 – Player problem and fantasy  

Purpose of this slide  

Show that you understand:  

- The frustrations, gaps or unmet needs your players feel today.  
- The emotional promise your game is making to those players.  

This is where you answer “why will anyone care?” in player language.  

What investors look for at Seed  

- Clear, human statements of player problems and desires.  
- Evidence that these statements come from real players, not wishful thinking.  
- A clean link from problems to the fantasy your game delivers.  

They are fine with small samples if you are honest about them. They are not fine with inflated claims about research you did not do.  

What to gather before building it  

- Three to five short “player problem” statements, for example:  
  - “Strategy fans now have families and jobs and cannot commit to 30 minute sessions.”  
  - “Cosy game players love decorating but feel pushed into aggressive monetisation that breaks immersion.”  
  - “People who enjoy co operative games often end up in PVP titles where team play is punishing rather than rewarding.”  
- Evidence sources, such as:  
  - Notes from interviews or playtests.  
  - Survey summaries.  
  - Store review excerpts from comparable games (properly labelled).  
- One or two fantasy statements, for example:  
  - “Feel like a brilliant commander in ten minutes, on your phone.”  
  - “Return to a safe, cosy space between stressful real world tasks.”  

How to build the slide  

Layout suggestion:  

- Left block: “Player problems” with three to five concise bullets.  
- Right block: “Our fantasy” with a short paragraph or two to three bullets.  
- Bottom strip: one or two real quotes, attributed and labelled (“Playtest quote”, “Store review for reference title”).  

Content, examples and stage variants  

Example “player problems”  

- “Strategy fans who grew up with PC tactics games now have limited continuous time.”  
- “Mobile tactics games often demand 20 to 30 minute sessions or heavy grinding.”  
- “Existing co op titles lean on pay to win gear and gacha, which feels unfair to many players.”  

Example “our fantasy”  

“Tidefall lets players feel like brilliant commanders in the spaces between real life. In a five to ten minute break they can plan a mission, outwit enemies with a friend and see meaningful progress on their heroes and base. The core fantasy is about smart decisions and co operation, not reaction time or grinding.”  

Example quote  

“I only have ten minutes before my next call. I want something deeper than an idle game but I cannot commit to a long match.”  
Playtest, age 36, UK  

Stage variants  

Pre prototype  

- You may rely more heavily on reference game reviews and early concept testing.  
- Make it clear in the data room that this is pre build research.  

Prototype  

- Push to include at least one or two quotes and observations from people who played your own build.  
- Start to map problems to actual behaviours you see in sessions, not just opinions.  

Soft launch  

- You can now back up these statements with behaviour data: session length, frequency, funnel drop offs.  
- You may choose to keep this slide mostly qualitative and push the numbers to S08, but the narrative should be aligned.  

Pitfalls and red flags  

- Writing problems from your own viewpoint rather than the player’s.  
- Exaggerating the scope of research, for example claiming “hundreds of players” if you interviewed ten.  
- Overstating how unique your fantasy is. Players can want similar things from several games.  
- Contradictions between this slide and the monetisation slide later, for example claiming “no pressure to pay” while also planning heavy progression gating.  

Quality check and scoring  

Link to data room:  

- A2: Player research summary and raw notes.  

Score from 1 to 5:  

- 1: Problems are vague or self centred, and there is no evidence.  
- 2: Some understanding of players, but statements are generic and weakly supported.  
- 3: Clear statement of problems and fantasy, with at least some real quotes or data.  
- 4: Strong, grounded understanding; investor can see you have listened and responded to players.  
- 5: Deep insight into players and a tight link between their needs and your design; feels like a team that will keep learning from players as they scale.  


3.4 S04 – Game overview: genre, platform, status  

Purpose of this slide  

Summarise the game as it stands today and where you aim to get to by the end of the Seed runway. Answer:  

- What is it?  
- Where does it run?  
- How far along is it?  

What investors look for at Seed  

- Clear description of genre and core pillars.  
- Platforms and technology choices.  
- Honest statement of development status.  
- Realistic target status by end of Seed.  

They are not looking for a complete GDD. This is the top level snapshot.  

What to gather before building it  

- One sentence “at a glance” description of the game.  
- Three to five “pillars” that define the experience, for example:  
  - “Short, replayable missions.”  
  - “Co operative tactics.”  
  - “Hero collection and progression.”  
  - “Seasonal events.”  
- Platform and tech information: engine, backend approach, online requirements.  
- Current status in clear language: concept, prototype, vertical slice, internal alpha, external alpha, soft launch.  
- Target status at the end of this round: closed beta, soft launch, global launch readiness, etc.  

How to build the slide  

Layout suggestion:  

- Top line: “At a glance” sentence.  
- Left block: “Core pillars” list.  
- Right upper block: “Platforms and tech”.  
- Right lower block: “Status today”.  
- Bottom line or box: “Target status at end of Seed” with one to three bullets.  
- Optionally: one labelled image (prototype screenshot, concept) if space permits.  

Content, examples and stage variants  

Example  

“At a glance: Turn based co operative tactics game for iOS and Android, built in Unity.”  

Core pillars  

- “Short, replayable missions designed for five to ten minute sessions.”  
- “Synchronous co op with a friend or match made ally.”  
- “Hero collection and build variety.”  
- “Persistent base that unlocks new missions and options.”  

Platforms and tech  

- “iOS and Android.”  
- “Unity client with server authoritative combat.”  
- “Managed backend and analytics stack, initially using X and Y.”  

Status today  

- “Playable prototype with three replayable missions, basic hero levelling and local co op simulation.”  
- “Internal tests with 30 players over four weeks.”  

Target status at end of Seed  

- “Closed beta in one region with 500 to 2,000 players.”  
- “Soft launch in at least one additional region with early monetisation live.”  
- “Basic live ops cadence in place with weekly events and monthly updates.”  

Stage variants  

Pre prototype  

- Be honest: “Concept with early paper and grey box prototypes, no external players yet.”  
- Focus “target status” on reaching a strong prototype and limited external testing.  

Prototype  

- Describe what is actually playable: number of levels, heroes, systems.  
- If you have scheduling risk, acknowledge it in the roadmap rather than pretending you are further along.  

Soft launch  

- You can say “Soft launch live in X and Y regions” in the status block.  
- Make sure your target status lines up with Series A expectations and the financial model.  

Pitfalls and red flags  

- Calling something “soft launch” when it is really a disguised prototype test with friends and colleagues.  
- Using “AAA quality” language without any reference points.  
- Omitting platforms or failing to mention that your game requires a constant network connection.  
- Inconsistencies with later slides that talk about runway, milestones and metrics.  

Quality check and scoring  

Link to data room:  

- B1: Product documentation and GDD.  
- B2: Prototype builds, videos and captures.  

Score from 1 to 5:  

- 1: Confusing or misleading about status. Key information missing.  
- 2: Basic information present but vague or poorly structured.  
- 3: Clear enough snapshot of what exists and what is planned.  
- 4: Strong, honest overview that gives investors confidence in your execution plan.  
- 5: Crisp, realistic articulation of status and trajectory that matches roadmap, model and data room files.  


3.5 S05 – Core loop and progression  

Purpose of this slide  

Show what players actually do, repeatedly, and how their experience deepens over time. Answer:  

- What are the main actions in a typical session?  
- How do those actions connect to rewards and progression?  
- How does progression avoid becoming a shallow numbers treadmill?  

What investors look for at Seed  

- A clear core loop diagram that they can understand at a glance.  
- A short description of how it feels to play that loop.  
- A simple view of progression: what grows and why players care.  

They do not want a dense system diagram that only your lead designer understands.  

What to gather before building it  

- A draft core loop diagram with four to six steps.  
- A list of three to six main player verbs: explore, build, fight, merge, decorate, negotiate, socialise, etc.  
- Overview of progression systems: heroes, buildings, collections, ranks, cosmetic collections.  
- A rough view of early game pacing: first hour, first day, first week.  

How to build the slide  

Layout suggestion:  

- Centre: core loop diagram in a circle or flow with labelled steps.  
- Left or above: “How it feels” bullets.  
- Right: “Progression systems” bullets or a simple ladder diagram.  

Keep labels short and fonts large.  

Content, examples and stage variants  

Example core loop  

Diagram steps  

1. Choose mission or activity.  
2. Prepare squad, deck, or layout.  
3. Play mission or level (make tactical or creative choices).  
4. Receive rewards and feedback.  
5. Upgrade heroes, base or collection.  
6. Unlock new missions, challenges or content.  

“How it feels” bullets  

- “Every five to ten minute session contains one complete mission or meaningful activity.”  
- “Success depends on smart choices and teamwork, not only power level.”  
- “Progression unlocks new options, not just higher stats.”  

“Progression systems” bullets  

- “Heroes: levels, skills, gear, passive traits.”  
- “Account: leagues or ranks that unlock modes and cosmetic rewards.”  
- “Base: buildings that unlock new content, crafting and social features.”  

Stage variants  

Pre prototype  

- Use paper diagrams and reference loops from comparable titles as inspiration.  
- Make clear where you are still deciding between design options.  

Prototype  

- Show the loop that actually exists in the build, even if simpler than the final vision.  
- Make note in your product docs about what you will add to deepen the loop.  

Soft launch  

- The loop should now be grounded in real player behaviour and metrics.  
- You should understand where players drop out or stall, and this should feed changes to the loop and progression.  

Pitfalls and red flags  

- Core loop diagrams that are too complex, with tiny labels and ten steps.  
- Progression that is purely linear power accumulation, with no new decisions or variety.  
- Loops that do not match your monetisation or live ops story.  
- Claims of “short sessions” when the loop clearly demands long ones.  

Quality check and scoring  

Link to data room:  

- B1: Core loop documentation and design notes.  
- D1: Playtest reports for early versions of the loop.  

Score from 1 to 5:  

- 1: Diagram is confusing or missing; investor still cannot describe what players do.  
- 2: Basic loop shown but either too shallow or too busy to be useful.  
- 3: Reasonably clear loop and progression, but with gaps in pacing or depth.  
- 4: Strong articulation of loop and progression that ties clearly to your fantasy and monetisation.  
- 5: Elegant, readable loop that demonstrates insight into long term engagement and variety.  


3.6 S06 – Market and audience  

Purpose of this slide  

Show that you understand the market you are entering and the specific audience you are aiming to serve. Answer:  

- Who is this really for?  
- How large and attractive is that segment?  
- Why is it a sensible place for your studio to compete?  

What investors look for at Seed  

- A specific audience definition, not “everyone on mobile”.  
- Realistic market sizing, segmented down to your relevant niche.  
- A clear statement of why this slice of the market is attractive and reachable.  

They are not impressed by “the global games market is X billion” without a clear path to your share.  

What to gather before building it  

- A clear primary audience description in human terms: age, life situation, play habits, favourite games.  
- Optional secondary audience description.  
- Market data for:  
  - Total mobile gamers in your target regions.  
  - Players of your genre or similar games.  
  - Genre benchmarks: installs, revenue, ARPDAU or ARPU ranges where available.  
- Any segmentation you have done: personas, cluster analysis, or survey results.

- Milestones that are vague (for example “improve metrics”) or unreachable with proposed budget and team size.  
- Use of funds figures that differ from those implied by your model.  

Quality check and scoring  

Link to data room:  

- E3: Financial model with assumptions, P and L and cashflow.  
- B3: Roadmap that should match milestones.  

Score from 1 to 5:  

- 1: Raise amount, runway and milestones are incoherent or unrealistic.  
- 2: Some structure but weak connection between spend and milestones.  
- 3: Coherent plan that would not worry investors but lacks depth.  
- 4: Strong, realistic plan tightly connected to roadmap and model.  
- 5: Very clear path from spend to proof points, with thoughtful buffers and scenario awareness.  


3.14 S14 – Closing and contact  

Purpose of this slide  

Provide a clean, confident closing that reinforces the core of the story and makes it easy to follow up. Answer:  

- What is the essence of your pitch in one or two sentences?  
- How can investors contact you and access deeper materials?  

What investors look for at Seed  

- A short, grounded closing statement.  
- Clear contact details.  
- Optional reminder of raise amount and stage.  

They do not want new information or surprises here.  

What to gather before building it  

- One or two sentence closing statement that reflects your whole story.  
- Primary contact details (name, role, email).  
- Optional mention of data room or model availability.  

How to build the slide  

Layout suggestion:  

- Centre: closing statement in a large font.  
- Bottom or side: small text block with raise reminder and contact details.  
- Background: clean; you may echo the cover image or use a simple variant.  

Content, examples and stage variants  

Example closing statement  

“We are building the tactics game we always wanted on mobile: deep, fair and built for real life schedules. Seed funding lets us take it through soft launch and reach the proof points needed for a strong Series A.”  

Example contact block  

- “Raising: 2.0 million Seed.”  
- “Contact: Alex Smith, CEO – alex@northshoregames.com.”  
- “Financial model and data room available on request under NDA.”  

Stage variants  

Pre prototype  

- Acknowledge the stage in the statement, for example “Seed funding lets us build and validate a strong prototype and first external tests.”  

Soft launch  

- You may emphasise that you are already in market, but keep it short and consistent with earlier slides.  

Pitfalls and red flags  

- Introducing new claims or partnerships here that are not explained earlier.  
- Overly emotional or vague statements that do not match your measured tone elsewhere.  
- Missing or inaccurate contact information.  

Quality check and scoring  

Link to data room:  

- E3: Financial model and projections.  
- Root folder readme: data room index and short instructions for investors.  

Score from 1 to 5:  

- 1: Weak or missing closing, no clear way to follow up.  
- 2: Statement is generic or inconsistent with earlier content.  
- 3: Adequate closing with working contact details.  
- 4: Strong, aligned closing that reinforces the pitch.  
- 5: Memorable, concise ending that leaves investors clear on who you are, what you want and how to engage.

4. SERIES A UPGRADES – FROM BELIEF TO SCALING  

4.1 What Series A is really for  

By the time you pitch a Series A, investors are no longer deciding mainly on belief and potential. They are deciding whether to scale something that already works, at least in embryo, or that has a credible line of sight to working soon.

At Series A, the questions become:  

- Is there clear evidence that the game can reach and keep the right players at acceptable unit economics.  
- Is there a repeatable path from spend on UA to revenue and margin.  
- Can this team operate and improve a live game while building new content and features.  
- Does the financial model capture how this business behaves in different scenarios.  

Your Series A deck is usually an evolution of your Seed deck, not a brand new story. The structure can stay very similar. The upgrades are in:  

- Depth and honesty of metrics.  
- Cohort based evidence instead of snapshots.  
- Clarity on UA strategy, channel mix and creative learnings.  
- Organisation, hiring and process maturity.  
- Financial scenarios, sensitivities and capital efficiency.  

You are no longer selling a dream. You are selling a machine that turns ideas, art and code into a measurable, growing business.  


4.2 How the Series A deck differs in practice  

You can usually keep the same 14 slide skeleton. What changes is the level of evidence and the nature of the conversations around each slide.

Typical differences:  

- Story slides (S01 to S03) become shorter and sharper. Investors already know you or can look you up, so these slides set context rather than carry the whole pitch.  
- Product and experience slides (S04, S05, S10) now need to show how the real game has evolved, not just the intended design.  
- Market and competition slides (S06, S07) should be grounded in your live player base and category performance, not just desk research.  
- Data slides (S08, S09, S11) are now the core of the Series A conversation. They must present cohorts, trends, tests and learnings, not cherry picked numbers.  
- Team and plan slides (S12, S13, S14) must show a live organisation with roles, responsibilities and a well considered scaling plan.  

You will also often add one or two Series A specific slides, for example:  

- Detailed metrics overview: retention, monetisation and UA in one place.  
- Scenario and sensitivity overview: how your business behaves under different assumptions.  

This section focuses on upgrading each cluster of slides from Seed to Series A expectations.  


4.3 Cluster A – Story and fantasy upgrades (S01 to S03)  

S01 – Cover and one line summary at Series A  

Purpose stays the same: orient and anchor. Differences:  

- You can now include “Live in” or “Soft launched in” with regions and launch month.  
- It is acceptable to add one short line that hints at scale, for example “1 million plus installs” or “Top 100 grossing in [market] for [period]”, as long as you can back it up.  
- Keep the one line summary consistent with what the data shows. If players use different language to describe your game than you did at Seed, update the line.  

Upgrade checks  

- Does the one liner still match how players talk about the game.  
- Does the visual tone match the current live art and brand, not an old concept.  
- Are any headline claims backed by metrics in S08 and the data room.  

S02 – Vision and elevator pitch at Series A  

At Series A your elevator pitch should:  

- Still be understandable in two to four sentences.  
- Reflect the reality of the live game and roadmap.  
- Acknowledge the size of what you are building. For example “a multi year F2P franchise”, not just a single game if that is your intention.  

Upgrade steps  

- Review what has actually resonated with players and press. You may refine the fantasy or positioning based on this.  
- If you have clear proof points, you can add one short sentence such as “Already reaching [X] monthly players with [Y] minutes per day on average.”  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Keeping an old vision statement that no longer matches the actual experience.  
- Over expanding the vision to justify a larger round, without real foundations.  

S03 – Player problem and fantasy at Series A  

Now you have real players and real behaviour. This slide should evolve from hypothesis to grounded insight.  

Upgrade actions  

- Replace or supplement early interview quotes with real player feedback from reviews, community channels and structured research.  
- Explicitly tie behavioural data to the problem and fantasy. For example:  
  - “Players say they want short sessions, and we see median session length at 8.2 minutes on weekdays and 10.5 minutes on weekends.”  
  - “Players talk about fairness and skill; our monetisation and matchmaking support that.”  

- Highlight how your understanding of player needs has changed since Seed, and what you did about it.  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Ignoring disconfirming feedback because it is uncomfortable.  
- Painting too rosy a picture of player sentiment and retention.  
- Letting the problem and fantasy slide drift from what your retention curves suggest players actually do and value.  

Upgrade quality test  

- Can you truthfully say “we know who our best players are, what they care about and how our game fits into their lives” and back it up with both quotes and numbers.  


4.4 Cluster B – Product and experience upgrades (S04, S05, S10)  

S04 – Game overview at Series A  

This slide now needs to show:  

- What is live today (modes, regions, platforms).  
- What has changed since Seed.  
- Which major product risks are now de risked and which remain.  

Upgrade elements  

- Clearly state live status:  
  - “Soft launched in [countries] since [month, year].”  
  - “Global launch in [regions] since [month, year].”  

- Highlight major product changes made based on data:  
  - “Removed X mode due to weak engagement, doubled down on Y.”  
  - “Rebuilt onboarding to improve early retention from [percentage] to [percentage].”  

- If you have multiple products (for example second title or tools) only include them if they are strategically important for the Series A story.  

S05 – Core loop and progression at Series A  

At Seed you described how the loop should work. At Series A you must show how it does work, what you changed and how it connects to metrics.  

Upgrade steps  

- Keep the core loop diagram, but annotate it with one or two data backed insights. For example:  
  - “We saw large drop off between steps 2 and 3; redesign reduced funnel loss by [percentage].”  
  - “Progression bottleneck at level [X] led to frustration; change improved D7 retention by [Y points].”  

- Link progression systems to long term engagement:  
  - Show distribution of player levels, progression milestones reached by day 30, and how these relate to payer behaviour.  

- Include one short example of how a specific progression change affected cohort curves.  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Keeping the original loop diagram when the live game has drifted substantially.  
- Failing to connect design changes to observed outcomes.  
- Ignoring late game and elder game loops; Series A investors want to see beyond day 7.  

S10 – Live ops thinking and roadmap at Series A  

By Series A you should have:  

- Run multiple events or seasons.  
- Learned about what drives engagement and revenue.  
- Established basic tools and processes for live operations.  

Upgrade content  

- Summarise what you actually did:  
  - “Three seasons completed, each eight weeks.”  
  - “Weekly events with [average participation rate] and [impact on DAU or revenue].”  

- Highlight key learnings:  
  - “Players respond best to events that remix existing content with new rules, not pure grind.”  
  - “Our first three battle passes underperformed due to poor mission design; redesign improved completion and revenue.”  

- Show the next 12 to 18 month live ops roadmap at a higher scale, including:  
  - Season cadence.  
  - Event templates.  
  - Planned collaborations or major tentpoles.  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Presenting an ambitious live ops calendar without acknowledging team capacity and tooling.  
- Ignoring the operational load of running events and frequent updates.  
- Failing to connect live ops plans to retention and monetisation projections in the model.  

Upgrade quality test  

- Can you show a specific example where live ops changes improved metrics over one or more cohorts.  
- Do your planned live ops beats align with your Series A revenue scenarios.  


4.5 Cluster C – Market and positioning upgrades (S06, S07)  

S06 – Market and audience at Series A  

At Series A, your market and audience slide should evolve from theory to mixed theory and evidence.  

Upgrade pieces  

- Keep the segmented market view, but now layer in your actual penetration:  
  - “We currently reach approximately [X] percent of our target segment in [regions], based on [metric].”  

- Show how your audience understanding has changed with data:  
  - “Initial target was [segment], but our highest value cohorts are [segment], which led us to adjust creatives and product.”  

- Include any meaningful regional learnings:  
  - For example differences in retention, monetisation or engagement between countries that changed your roadmap.  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Continuing to pitch the original target audience when live data shows your main paying audience is different.  
- Over extrapolating from small samples.  
- Claiming more penetration or category impact than your data supports.  

S07 – Competitive landscape and differentiation at Series A  

Here you are no longer guessing how you sit in the landscape. You are competing directly.  

Upgrade actions  

- Update competitor set to include any titles that launched since your Seed round that are now relevant.  
- Use live data to back up positioning where appropriate, such as:  
  - “Our median session length and retention are closer to [Title A] than [Title B].”  
  - “We see overlap in our players’ installed apps: [percentage] also play [competitor title].”  

- Refine your differentiation story to reflect how players actually use your game and what they say they value.  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Ignoring new competitors that occupy similar space.  
- Drawing a favourable picture that contradicts store rankings or public data.  
- Failing to acknowledge where competitors still beat you and how you plan to respond.  

Upgrade quality test  

- Could a specialist games investor accept your positioning even if they disagree on some details.  
- Do your UA and creative strategies align with this competitive view.  


4.6 Cluster D – Data, monetisation, UA and measurement upgrades (S08, S09, S11 and Series A metrics)  

This cluster is the heart of a Series A. You may add one or two dedicated Series A metrics slides, but the teaching below describes what you need to show and understand.

Core expectations  

By Series A you should:  

- Use cohorts, not just point in time averages.  
- Understand retention curves by cohort and segment.  
- Have an emerging LTV model that connects cohorts, monetisation and UA.  
- Have at least basic payback calculations by channel and creative.  
- Understand measurement constraints and attribution strategy.  

S08 – Traction and validation at Series A  

Upgrade scope  

- Move from “we ran these tests” to “we have run and learned from a continual stream of tests and releases.”  
- Show traction at three levels: usage, engagement and monetisation.  

Key elements  

- High level KPIs for the last several months:  
  - MAU, DAU, DAU/MAU ratio.  
  - Average sessions per DAU.  
  - Average time per DAU.  

- Retention curves by cohort:  
  - Show D1 to D30, and if possible up to D90, for relevant cohorts.  
  - Label cohorts clearly (for example by country, release wave, channel).  

- Cohort commentary:  
  - Explain what changes you made between cohorts and how they affected curves.  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Showing aggregated “lifetime” retention curves that average across very different product versions.  
- Hiding weak cohorts.  
- Over claiming product market fit based on a single strong cohort that you cannot yet repeat.  

Series A metrics slide (suggested additional slide)  

You may split metrics into a dedicated slide or two, covering:  

- Retention: key cohorts and targets.  
- Monetisation: ARPDAU, payer share, ARPPU, early LTV estimates.  
- UA and growth: CPI ranges, early payback times.  

This slide will be closely tied to the data room and model.  

S09 – Monetisation and economy at Series A  

Upgrade components  

- Present actual early monetisation behaviour:  
  - Payer conversion and payer distribution.  
  - ARPDAU by country and platform.  
  - Revenue mix by source: cosmetics, battle pass, convenience, ads.  

- Show at least simple LTV estimates:  
  - Either LTV over a fixed window (for example 90 days) or inferred from ARPDAU curves and retention.  
  - Present as a range, not a single precise number.  

- Show how design changes affected monetisation:  
  - For example “Battle pass redesign in month [X] improved pass attach rate from [percentage] to [percentage].”  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Presenting LTV as overly precise when driven by small or short cohorts.  
- Ignoring differences across regions and channels.  
- Letting monetisation drift into pay to win while still claiming a fair philosophy.  

Upgrade quality test  

- Can you talk through monetisation by cohort and segment without referring only to global averages.  
- Does your monetisation story connect clearly to your financial model and UA assumptions.  

S11 – UA, channel mix, creative learnings and attribution at Series A  

At Series A investors expect a deeper understanding of UA economics and measurement.

Upgrade elements  

- Channel mix overview:  
  - Share of spend and installs by channel (Meta, TikTok, Google, networks, influencers, cross promo).  
  - Rough CPI ranges and quality indicators per channel.  

- Creative learnings:  
  - Which creative concepts perform best on CPI.  
  - How different creatives affect downstream metrics such as D7 retention and early revenue.  
  - Examples of creative iteration cycles and uplift achieved.  

- Attribution and measurement approach:  
  - How you track cohorts under ATT and other privacy constraints.  
  - Any use of probabilistic modelling, geo testing or incrementality experiments.  
  - How you handle organics and mixed traffic.  

- Payback and scaling view:  
  - Simple payback curves by channel and creative, even if still early.  
  - Discussion of how you will test higher budgets and new channels.  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Treating UA success as “CPI is low” without acknowledging cohort quality.  
- Over relying on any single attribution source without cross checks.  
- Assuming that early CPI will hold when you scale spend.  
- Presenting payback as certain when the underlying LTV model is still immature.  

Upgrade quality test  

- Can you clearly explain, for each major UA channel, what kind of players it brings, how they perform and how you plan to scale or limit that channel.  
- Are your UA assumptions in the financial model visibly linked to the payback and cohort evidence you show in the deck.  


4.7 Cluster E – Organisation, team, financials and scenarios (S12, S13 and model)  

At Series A, investors are also funding your organisation and your ability to manage risk.  

S12 – Team and organisation at Series A  

Upgrade content  

- Move from “who we are” to “how we are organised and how we work.”  
- Provide:  
  - Updated team list, including key mid level leaders.  
  - Simple organisation snapshot: who reports to whom, functional groupings.  
  - Evidence of effective collaboration and process.  

- Highlight:  
  - Experience running live games.  
  - Experience hiring and managing larger teams.  
  - Any key gaps you still need to fill with this Series A.  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Rapid growth with unclear structure or too many people reporting directly to founders.  
- Lack of clear ownership for critical areas (product, live ops, data, UA).  
- No plan for building out studio functions such as HR, finance and recruiting if the headcount will grow significantly.  

S13 – Plan, use of funds and milestones at Series A  

The scale and purpose of funding is different at Series A.  

Upgrade elements  

- Use of funds now tilts more towards:  
  - Team growth across disciplines.  
  - UA to scale a working funnel.  
  - Tools and infrastructure to support higher DAU and more frequent updates.  

- Milestones are now more about:  
  - Reaching certain revenue and margin thresholds.  
  - Hitting target retention and monetisation metrics for stable cohorts.  
  - Expanding to new markets or platforms.  
  - Building out second title concepts if relevant.  

- Introduce basic scenario thinking:  
  - Base, upside and downside cases for revenue and growth.  
  - How spend scales in each scenario.  
  - What you will do if metrics underperform (for example resizing team or UA budgets).  

Upgrade pitfalls  

- Treating Series A as “more of the same Seed plan” instead of a step change.  
- Assuming linear or exponential growth without considering constraints.  
- No contingency plans for under performance.  

Financial model expectations at Series A  

Your model must now be coherent and connected to reality. Investors will expect:  

- A clear assumptions sheet that explains:  
  - Cohort behaviour assumptions: retention, ARPDAU, payer conversion.  
  - UA assumptions: CPI, channel mix, budgets over time.  
  - Cost assumptions: headcount by role and timing, overheads, platform fees and tax.  

- P and L and KPI sheet:  
  - Revenue broken down by in app purchases and ads, and by platform where relevant.  
  - Costs separated into COGS, UA and overhead.  
  - KPIs like DAU, MAU, payers, ARPDAU, LTV, CPI and payback per month.  

- Cashflow and runway sheet:  
  - Net cash flow by month.  
  - Minimum cash balance and runway under each scenario.  

Scenario and sensitivity guidance  

- Base case should be grounded in current cohorts and realistic improvements.  
- Upside case should reflect successful optimisation, not miracles.  
- Downside case should show what happens if metrics improve slowly or UA costs rise.  
- Sensitivity checks might include:  
  - What if CPI is 20 percent higher than expected.  
  - What if LTV is 20 percent lower.  
  - What if launch in a key region is delayed by six months.  

Model pitfalls  

- Hard coding key numbers rather than driving them from assumptions.  
- Inconsistent use of cohorts (for example mixing soft launch and global cohorts).  
- Ignoring platform fees, VAT and local taxes where relevant.  
- Forecasting smooth exponential growth without reflecting likely plateaus and step changes.  

Series A quality test for organisation and model  

- Can you explain how changes in retention or CPI affect runway and hiring plans.  
- Does your hiring plan match projected content, live ops and tech demands.  
- Do the Series A use of funds and milestones in S13 align with the model outputs.  


4.8 Investor questions your Series A deck must be able to handle  

A useful way to stress test your upgraded deck is to imagine the harshest version of common investor questions. You do not need separate slides for each, but your answers must be rooted in the material in the deck, model and data room.

Examples:  

Product and players  

- “How do you know you have product market fit, or are close to it. Show us in the curves.”  
- “What is the typical journey of a high value player over 30, 90 and 180 days.”  
- “What are the biggest product risks remaining, and how are you addressing them.”  

Metrics and cohorts  

- “Show us your three best and three worst cohorts. What did you learn from each.”  
- “How much of your retention is driven by live ops versus core loop.”  
- “What metrics are you optimising for this quarter, and why.”  

UA and growth  

- “Which channel is your workhorse, and how does cohort quality compare across channels.”  
- “How do you think about creative fatigue and replacement.”  
- “What is your plan if your primary channel becomes significantly more expensive.”  

Economy and monetisation  

- “Who pays in your game, when and for what. How does that compare to benchmarks.”  
- “What changes have you made that increased payer conversion or ARPDAU.”  
- “How will you protect against pay to win complaints as you scale.”  

Organisation and financials  

- “Where are you short on talent or experience.”  
- “Which hires are most critical in the first 6 to 12 months after Series A.”  
- “Show us how you would adjust budgets if LTV or CPI are off plan by 20 percent.”  

If your Series A deck and accompanying materials can confidently answer these questions, you are close to investor ready.

5. DUE DILIGENCE AND THE DATA ROOM  

5.1 What due diligence is really for  

Due diligence is the stage where investors and their advisors check whether your story is true, coherent and complete. It is not supposed to be a trap. It is meant to answer three questions.  

- Are the claims in the deck accurate.  
- Are the numbers in the model grounded in reality.  
- Are there any hidden risks that would change the investment decision.  

You can think of it in three layers.  

- Deck: the curated story and headline metrics.  
- Model: the structured view of how the business behaves over time.  
- Data room: the evidence that supports both.  

A well organised data room makes you look disciplined and trustworthy. A messy or incomplete one makes investors suspicious, even if your product is strong.  

In this section you will:  

- Set up a recommended data room structure for a mobile F2P studio.  
- Map key slides to specific folders and files.  
- Build a simple due diligence checklist you can use before you invite investors in.  


5.2 Principles for a good data room  

Before we list folders, two principles matter more than structure.  

Principle 1: No surprises  

Everything important in the deck should be backed by something in the data room. There should be no key metrics or claims that exist only in slides.  

Examples.  

- If you show retention curves, the underlying cohort exports and dashboard screenshots must be present.  
- If you claim specific revenue per user, the supporting reports must be present.  
- If you claim a partnership or collaboration, there should be at least a letter of intent or email confirmation, not just a logo.  

Principle 2: Sufficiency, not overload  

You do not earn extra points for uploading every file your studio has ever produced. More is not always better.  

Each folder should contain:  

- One clearly labelled primary document or sheet for each important topic.  
- Supporting exports or raw files as needed.  
- A short readme where necessary to explain context.  

Investors should be able to find what they need without digging through dozens of near duplicates.

Red lines and pitfalls in due diligence  

There are specific behaviours that cause immediate concern for investors. Avoid them.  

- Cherry picking metrics: showing only the best cohorts or periods without context.  
- Blending numbers that hide problems: for example mixing an early strong cohort with later weak ones and presenting an average.  
- Overclaiming AI, live ops or data sophistication that is not visible in your tools or outputs.  
- Presenting pay to win monetisation while claiming a fair, player first philosophy.  
- Hand waving around attribution and LTV, relying only on ad network dashboards without cross checks.  

If you are caught in any of these, trust erodes quickly. The safest path is honesty: acknowledge weaknesses, show what you have already tried and explain what you will try next.  


APPENDIX A – OBSERVABILITY AND DATA TOOLING  

A.1 Why observability matters for your pitch  

For a mobile F2P studio, data is not just an internal tool. It is part of the pitch. Investors want to see that you can:  

- Instrument your game sensibly.  
- Answer key questions about player behaviour.  
- Run tests and learn from them.  

You do not need an enterprise data stack at Seed. You do need enough observability to produce the charts in your deck and to convince investors that you can improve your product rationally.  


A.2 Stage appropriate analytics setups  

Prototype stage  

Goal: understand whether the core loop feels good and where players drop out.  

Minimum stack.  

- Client SDK from an analytics provider (for example Unity Analytics, Firebase, GameAnalytics or equivalent).  
- Simple event tracking for:  
  - Session start and end.  
  - Tutorial steps.  
  - Core loop actions (levels started and completed, failures, retries).  
  - Key economy interactions (currency earn and spend).  

- Basic dashboards for:  
  - D1 and D7 retention (even if small samples).  
  - Number of sessions per user.  
  - Tutorial funnel completion.  

Soft launch stage  

Goal: understand product market fit signals and monetisation.  

Stack upgrades.  

- Event tracking extended to:  
  - Account creation and linking.  
  - Payer events (purchase start, purchase complete, product ID, price).  
  - Live ops participation (event and season participation events).  
  - UA cohort attributes (channel, campaign, creative ID where possible).  

- Data warehouse or database, for example BigQuery, Snowflake or a managed warehouse.  
- ETL or ingestion from analytics, store and UA platforms into the warehouse.  
- Dashboards for:  
  - Retention curves by cohort.  
  - Revenue over time by cohort, country and platform.  
  - Funnels for onboarding, key features and monetisation entry points.  

Early scale and Series A  

Goal: improve unit economics and operate a live game.  

Stack upgrades.  

- More automated pipelines, with daily or more frequent updates.  
- Basic experimentation framework, even if manual, to run A/B tests.  
- Deeper views of:  
  - LTV by cohort and segment.  
  - UA performance and payback by channel and creative.  
  - Live ops event performance across cohorts.  

You may still not need a full custom stack. Using a mix of commercial tools and a clear data warehouse can be enough if your event design is thoughtful and your dashboards answer the right questions.  


A.3 Event schema principles and examples  

A poorly designed event schema leads to messy dashboards and weak insights. A good schema is:  

- Player centric: events are tied to players and sessions.  
- Consistent: names and properties follow clear conventions.  
- Minimal but sufficient: you track what you need, not everything you can imagine.  

Example event categories  

Core events  

- session_start, session_end.  
- tutorial_step_start, tutorial_step_complete.  
- mission_start, mission_complete, mission_fail.  
- level_up, resource_earn, resource_spend.  

Economy events  

- store_open.  
- offer_view, offer_click, offer_purchase.  
- currency_balance_change (with type and source).  

Live ops events  

- event_join, event_complete.  
- season_start, season_pass_purchase, season_pass_tier_reached.  

UA and attribution context  

- For each player, store: install_timestamp, country, platform, campaign_id, channel, creative_id where available.  

Event properties  

Each event should include consistent fields. Examples.  

- player_id.  
- session_id.  
- timestamp (UTC).  
- country, platform.  
- mission_id, difficulty, outcome for mission events.  
- currency_type, amount for economy events.  
- offer_id, price, currency for offer events.  

Design rules  

- Avoid overloading generic events; better to have mission_complete than a generic “action” event with many meanings.  
- Use lower case with underscores for event and property names.  
- Document everything in a tracking plan that engineering, design and data can share.  

The tracking plan itself should be in your data room (folder 05.01).  


A.4 Dashboards that directly feed deck charts  

Every chart in your deck should either be:  

- Exported from a dashboard you use regularly.  
- Derived from a repeatable analysis that lives in your warehouse or sheets.  

Key dashboards for Seed and Series A.  

Retention dashboard  

- D1, D3, D7, D14, D30 curves by cohort.  
- Cohort definitions by install week, country, platform and channel.  
- Ability to filter by build version or major game changes.  

Onboarding and funnel dashboard  

- Tutorial funnel: step completion rates and time to complete.  
- First session funnel: how many players reach key early milestones.  
- Drop off heatmap that shows where new players leave.  

Engagement dashboard  

- DAU, MAU, DAU/MAU ratio.  
- Sessions per DAU by day of week.  
- Average session length and time of day patterns.  

Monetisation dashboard  

- ARPDAU by cohort, country and platform.  
- Payer conversion over time.  
- Revenue mix by product type and source.  
- Distribution of spend per payer.  

UA and cohort dashboard  

- Installs, CPI and basic UA metrics by channel and campaign.  
- Linking UA cohorts to in game outcomes (D7 retention, ARPDAU, early LTV).  

For each chart you include in S08, S09, S11 or Series A metrics slides, note which dashboard it comes from and store a PDF or image export in folder 05.05 or 06.03 or 07.02. This makes it easy to prove that the slide is a faithful representation of your data.  


APPENDIX B – PRESENTING QUALITATIVE FEEDBACK AND UX RESEARCH  

B.1 Why qualitative work matters  

Numbers tell you what players do. Qualitative work tells you why.  

Investors are reassured when they see that you:  

- Talk to players regularly.  
- Observe real behaviour, not just survey answers.  
- Turn feedback into specific product decisions.  

This appendix shows how to run lightweight but robust qualitative work, how to turn it into themes and how to present it in slides and the data room.  


B.2 How to run playtests and surveys  

Basic formats  

- In person moderated sessions.  
- Remote moderated sessions over video.  
- Unmoderated tests with tasks and follow up surveys.  
- In game surveys and feedback prompts.  

For Seed stage, you usually rely on a mix of small moderated sessions and simple surveys.  

Playtest structure  

- Define objectives:  
  - “Observe first time onboarding.”  
  - “See whether players understand the core loop.”  
  - “Test reaction to early monetisation concepts.”  

- Prepare tasks:  
  - “Install the build and play until you reach level 3.”  
  - “Try to upgrade your main hero.”  
  - “Attempt to purchase a cosmetic and cancel.”  

- Recruit participants:  
  - From your target audience if possible, not only friends and colleagues.  
  - Aim for diversity in age, platform and experience.  

- Run sessions:  
  - Ask players to think aloud as they play.  
  - Observe silently, only prompting when necessary.  
  - Record sessions if participants consent.  

- Capture feedback:  
  - Immediate notes during sessions.  
  - Short post session surveys for ratings and comments.  

Survey structure  

- Keep surveys short and focused.  
- Use a mix of scale questions and open questions.  
- Examples:  
  - “On a scale of 1 to 7, how likely are you to keep playing this game next week. Why.”  
  - “What did you enjoy most.”  
  - “What confused or frustrated you.”  
  - “How would you describe this game to a friend.”  

Store playtest plans, instruments and summaries in folder 10.01 and 10.02.  


B.3 Turning raw feedback into themes, quotes and actions  

After running tests, you must process the feedback.  

Step 1: Collect and clean  

- Gather notes, transcripts and survey responses.  
- Remove personally identifiable information where necessary.  

Step 2: Code and theme  

- Read through feedback and mark recurring topics.  
- Group these into themes, such as:  
  - Onboarding clarity.  
  - Mission difficulty.  
  - Controls and input.  
  - Visual clarity.  
  - Monetisation reactions.  

- For each theme, record:  
  - How many participants mentioned it.  
  - Whether it is positive, negative or mixed.  

Step 3: Extract representative quotes  

- Choose short quotes that express common views in players own words.  
- Attribute them generically (for example “Playtest, UK, age 35”).  

Step 4: Translate into actions  

- For each strong theme, decide:  
  - What change you will make, if any.  
  - How you will measure the impact.  

Example theme summary  

Theme: Onboarding clarity  

- “Seven out of ten participants struggled to understand the purpose of the base screen in the first session.”  
- “Multiple players did not notice the tutorial prompt for the second mission.”  

Representative quotes  

- “I am not sure what this base does yet, I am just clicking the arrows.”  
- “I missed the flashing arrow and got lost on the map.”  

Actions  

- “Simplify base screen for first two sessions, unlocking features later.”  
- “Increase contrast and size of navigation cues in early missions.”  
- “Add an in game tip after first failure explaining how to re attempt missions.”  

You can summarise themes and actions in a short report for each round of research and upload it to 10.01.  


B.4 Presenting UX findings in slides and the data room  

In the deck you usually do not have space for full research reports. Instead you should:  

- Show that you have done structured research.  
- Highlight one or two key themes that materially changed your design.  
- Use real player quotes sparingly and clearly labelled.  

Example UX slide structure  

Title: “What players told us and how we responded”  

Layout.  

- Left: three to four bullet points, each summarising a theme and action.  
- Right: two short quotes with attribution.  
- Bottom: small note pointing investors to folder 10.01 in the data room for full reports.  

Example bullets  

- “Onboarding was too long and complex, causing drop off before level 3. We simplified tutorials and saw D1 retention rise from 32 percent to 39 percent on the next cohort.”  
- “Players wanted more clarity on fair play and pay to win. We redesigned the shop and battle pass to emphasise cosmetics and skill based progression.”  

In the data room, provide:  

- Playtest and survey plans.  
- Raw anonymised data where appropriate.  
- Thematic summaries for each round of testing.  
- Post mortems after major changes.  

This shows investors that you have a repeatable, disciplined approach to qualitative learning, not just one off anecdotes.  


APPENDIX C – FINANCIAL MODEL TEACHING APPENDIX  

C.1 What the model is and is not  

Your financial model is a structured way of expressing your beliefs about how the business will behave under different conditions. It is not a prediction that will come true exactly.  

It must be:  

- Cohort driven: built from player behaviour up.  
- Transparent: assumptions are explicit and easy to change.  
- Consistent: deck, model and data room all tell the same story.  

The simple three sheet model recommended here has:  

- Sheet 1: Assumptions and drivers.  
- Sheet 2: P and L and KPIs.  
- Sheet 3: Cashflow and runway.  

You can add more tabs later for specific analyses, but if these three sheets are clear you will already be above average.  


C.2 Sheet 1 – Assumptions and drivers  

Organise this sheet into blocks with clear labels.  

Cohort and user assumptions  

- New users per month by source (organic, paid channels).  
- Retention assumptions by day or month for key cohorts.  
- Average sessions per user per day.  

Monetisation assumptions  

- Payer conversion rate by cohort.  
- ARPDAU over time for each cohort or an average curve.  
- Revenue mix assumptions (for example percentage from cosmetics, passes, ads).  

UA and marketing assumptions  

- CPI by channel and region.  
- Channel mix over time.  
- Planned monthly UA spend by channel.  

Cost assumptions  

- Headcount by role and timing, with fully loaded cost per person.  
- Overheads such as rent, software, legal and accounting.  
- Backend and infrastructure costs (often driven by DAU).  
- Platform fees and taxes (for example 30 percent store fees, VAT).  

Scenario flags  

- Cells or dropdowns to choose scenario (base, upside, downside).  
- Adjustments tied to scenario choice (for example plus or minus 20 percent on CPI or LTV).  

Best practices  

- Keep all hard coded numbers in the assumptions sheet. Other sheets should reference them, not repeat them.  
- Use clear units (for example “per day”, “per month”, “per user”).  
- Comment on any assumption derived from data with the source and date.  

C.3 Sheet 2 – P and L and KPIs  

This sheet shows how cohorts and assumptions turn into revenue, costs and key metrics over time.  

Structure  

- Columns: months (for example Month 1 to Month 60) or quarters if you prefer.  
- Rows grouped into sections:  

Users and cohorts  

- New installs per month by source.  
- Active users (DAU and MAU) derived from retention curves.  
- Payers and active payers per month.  

Revenue  

- In app purchase revenue by month.  
- Ad revenue by month.  
- Total net revenue after platform fees.  

Costs  

- Cost of goods sold (store fees, payment processing).  
- UA spend by channel.  
- Staff costs by function.  
- Other operating expenses.  

KPIs  

- ARPDAU and ARPPU.  
- Payer conversion.  
- Revenue per install over specific windows (for example 90 days).  
- CPI and LTV by cohort where appropriate.  
- Marketing efficiency metrics (for example LTV to CPI ratio).  

Build from users and cohorts up  

At a simple level:  

- For each month and cohort, calculate retained users using your retention assumptions.  
- Multiply retained users by ARPDAU and days in the month to obtain revenue.  
- Sum across cohorts to get total monthly revenue.  

This level of detail may seem heavy at first, but it is the only way to connect player behaviour meaningfully to cash flows.  

C.4 Sheet 3 – Cashflow and runway  

This sheet focuses on cash in and cash out.  

Structure  

- Start with opening cash balance.  
- Add: net cash from operations (revenue minus costs).  
- Subtract: capital expenditure or exceptional costs.  
- Add: funds raised at Seed or Series A and timing.  
- Track closing cash balance per month.  

Key outputs  

- Minimum cash balance over time.  
- Month when cash runs out under each scenario.  
- Ability to test different raise sizes and timings quickly.  

This sheet should be easy to read for non financial people. Highlight:  

- The path from current cash to the requested raise and beyond.  
- When key milestones occur relative to cash levels.  


C.5 Scenarios and sensitivities  

Scenarios  

At minimum you should have:  

- Base case: grounded in current cohorts and realistic improvements.  
- Upside case: stronger retention or monetisation from successful optimisation.  
- Downside case: slower improvement or higher acquisition costs.  

You can implement these by:  

- Having separate assumption blocks for each scenario and switching between them.  
- Or using adjustment multipliers controlled by a scenario flag.  

Sensitivities  

Common stress tests include:  

- CPI 20 percent higher than planned.  
- LTV 20 percent lower than planned.  
- Launch delayed in a key market by six months.  
- Hiring delayed or accelerated.  

You can either:  

- Build a separate sensitivity tab that references the main sheets.  
- Or add switchable cases to key assumption rows.  

Presenting scenarios to investors  

In the deck, you will usually show:  

- Base case headline numbers (revenue, DAU, EBITDA, runway).  
- Upside and downside ranges, labelled clearly as scenarios, not promises.  

In the data room, your model should let investors inspect the mechanics.  

C.6 Consistency checks before sharing the model  

Before you include the model in folder 08.02 and mention it in the deck, run these checks.  

- Deck revenue and user numbers match the base case in the model.  
- Cash runway on S13 matches the cashflow sheet.  
- Use of funds percentages match the cost structure implied by the model.  
- Key ratios such as LTV to CPI make sense for your genre and are consistent with early cohort data.  

If you find inconsistencies, fix the model and slides until everything aligns. A small amount of extra work here saves a lot of confusion later.

- Basic data protection and privacy posture, including treatment of user data.  

If you treat this checklist as a design target rather than a hurdle, your deck and conversations will feel more solid and professional.  


6. APPENDICES – DATA, UX AND FINANCIALS  

6.1 Appendix A – Observability and data tooling  

A1. Why observability matters  

For a free to play mobile game, data is how you see reality. You need observability that is:  

- Stage appropriate – not overbuilt early, not underbuilt later.  
- Reliable – events fire when you think they do and are not duplicated or missing.  
- Interpretable – you can connect dashboards directly to the charts in your deck.  

Founders often either try to build too much too early or defer instrumentation until late. Both approaches slow learning and weaken investor confidence.  

A2. Stage appropriate analytics and observability  

Prototype stage  

Goals  

- Understand basic engagement with the core loop.  
- See where players drop out in onboarding and early progression.  
- Capture enough data to run simple retention and funnel analyses.  

Recommended setup  

- Use a robust analytics SDK integrated into your engine (for example a specialist games analytics provider or a general events system plus a lightweight data warehouse).  
- Start with a small, clean event set:  
  - Session start and end.  
  - Tutorial steps.  
  - Key actions in the core loop.  
  - Progression milestones.  

- For storage and analysis:  
  - A simple warehouse or database.  
  - A basic dashboarding tool that can plot retention curves and funnels.  

Soft launch  

Goals  

- Track cohorts by country, device and UA source.  
- Understand D1 to D30 retention drivers.  
- See conversion to payer, first purchase timing and early revenue patterns.  

Recommended setup  

- Extend event schema to include:  
  - User acquisition source identifiers (campaign, creative, channel where possible).  
  - Economy events such as currency earned and spent, store opens, item purchases.  
  - Live ops events: event participation, season progress, battle pass progress.  

- Add tools or processes to:  
  - Compute retention by cohort and segment.  
  - Calculate ARPDAU, ARPPU and payer share per cohort.  
  - Track funnels for onboarding, key features and monetisation.  

Early scale  

Goals  

- Support ongoing live ops and feature development.  
- Run A/B tests and structured experiments.  
- Feed a robust LTV model.  

Recommended setup  

- Formalise your data pipeline, typically:  
  - Client events to collection service.  
  - Stream and batch processing into warehouse.  
  - Semantic layer or data models for standard metrics.  

- Add tools for:  
  - Experiment configuration and analysis.  
  - Segmentation and cohort explorer.  
  - Automated alerting on critical metrics.  

A3. Event schema principles and examples  

Principles  

- Fewer, well defined events are better than many loosely defined ones.  
- Each event should have a clear name, description and list of properties.  
- Use consistent identifiers for users, sessions, devices and campaigns.  
- Version your schema when you make breaking changes.  

Example events (simplified)  

- session_start  
  - Properties: user_id, device_type, country, app_version, session_id, acquisition_source.  

- tutorial_step_completed  
  - Properties: user_id, step_id, step_order, session_id, timestamp.  

- mission_completed  
  - Properties: user_id, mission_id, difficulty, success_flag, time_taken_seconds, session_id, rewards_earned.  

- currency_changed  
  - Properties: user_id, currency_type, amount_change, source (mission, shop, reward), new_balance.  

- iap_purchase  
  - Properties: user_id, product_id, price_local, price_usd, currency, is_promo, store_platform, order_id.  

- ad_watched  
  - Properties: user_id, ad_type (rewarded, interstitial), placement_id, reward_type, reward_amount.  

Document your event schema in B1 or D1, and keep it up to date. Investors will not read every line, but they will appreciate evidence that you treat data as a product.  

A4. Dashboards that directly feed deck charts  

Think of your dashboards as the source of truth for deck visuals. For every chart you plan to include in Seed or Series A decks, there should be a corresponding dashboard view.  

Core dashboards  

- Retention dashboard  
  - D1, D3, D7, D14, D30 retention by cohort, with filters for country, platform, acquisition source, app version.  

- Funnel dashboard  
  - Onboarding funnel: installs to first session, tutorial completion, first mission completed.  
  - Feature funnels: first event participation, first purchase, first season pass purchase.  

- Monetisation dashboard  
  - Daily and monthly revenue.  
  - ARPDAU and ARPPU by cohort and segment.  
  - Payer share and revenue distribution.  
  - Revenue by source (IAP categories, ads).  

- UA and cohort dashboard  
  - Installs, spend, CPI by channel and creative.  
  - Cohort level retention and monetisation for major campaigns.  
  - Simple payback views based on current LTV estimates.  

Design dashboards so that:  

- Time windows and cohort definitions are explicitly shown.  
- Definitions are consistent with those in your model.  
- You can export the underlying data for audit and deeper analysis.  


6.2 Appendix B – Presenting qualitative feedback and UX research  

B1. Why qualitative work matters  

Quantitative data shows what players do. Qualitative work helps you understand why. A professional pitch uses both.

Investors want to see that you:  

- Talk to players regularly.  
- Treat their feedback seriously but not literally.  
- Connect feedback to design changes and experiments.  

B2. How to run playtests and surveys  

Playtests  

- Define a clear objective, for example:  
  - “Test understanding and enjoyment of the new tutorial.”  
  - “Assess perceived fairness of the monetisation changes.”  

- Decide format:  
  - In person moderated.  
  - Remote moderated (video call).  
  - Remote unmoderated with tasks and follow up questions.  

- Recruit:  
  - Aim for participants who match your target audience.  
  - Start with small numbers (6 to 12 per round) and run several rounds over time.  

- Prepare materials:  
  - Build tasks or scenarios for players to complete.  
  - Prepare a short interview guide with open questions.  

- Run and capture:  
  - Observe players, note where they struggle or disengage.  
  - Record sessions where possible.  
  - Take structured notes.  

Surveys  

- Use surveys when you need broader patterns, for example:  
  - “How many players understand this feature.”  
  - “How do different groups rate overall satisfaction.”  

- Design principles:  
  - Keep surveys short.  
  - Use simple scales and avoid leading questions.  
  - Include one or two open questions for richer insight.  

- Sampling:  
  - Decide where surveys appear, for example in game, by email to beta testers, or on community channels.  
  - Be clear about sample size and any biases.  

B3. Turning raw feedback into themes, quotes and actions  

A simple process to make qualitative data useful.  

Step 1: Gather  

- Collect notes, transcripts, recordings and survey responses.  

Step 2: Code and group  

- Read through and mark comments by topic: onboarding, difficulty, art, controls, monetisation, social, etc.  
- Within each topic, tag sentiment (positive, negative, mixed) and any patterns.  

Step 3: Identify themes  

- For each topic, summarise recurring issues or delights. For example:  
  - “Many players fail to notice the mission timer in early levels.”  
  - “Players appreciate the art style but find the UI cluttered.”  

Step 4: Extract representative quotes  

- Select short quotes that clearly illustrate a theme.  
- Attribute them properly: “Playtest, UK, age 32” or “Survey respondent, US, age band 25 to 34.”  

Step 5: Define actions  

- For each theme, decide initial actions or experiments, for example:  
  - “Simplify HUD in early missions, test with next cohort.”  
  - “Add an onboarding step that explains energy system and test impact on confusion markers.”  

Document these steps in a short UX report and store it in A2 and D1.  

B4. Presenting UX findings in a slide  

Investors do not need every detail of your research. They need proof that you have a process and that it feeds decisions.

Seed stage UX slide pattern  

- Top: “What we tested” – bullet list of tests and sample sizes.  
- Middle left: “Key findings” – three to five themes.  
- Middle right: “Representative quotes” – two to four short quotes.  
- Bottom: “What we changed” – specific actions and links to product changes.  

Example  

What we tested  

- “Two rounds of tutorial playtests with 16 players each (UK and Germany, midcore mobile players).”  
- “In game survey with 240 responses from prototype users.”  

Key findings  

- “Players enjoyed the core combat but did not understand the energy system.”  
- “Many skipped the tutorial text and relied on trial and error.”  
- “Co op feature interest was high but unclear how to invite friends.”  

Representative quotes  

- “I like the fights but I have no idea why I suddenly cannot start another mission.”  
- “I ignored most of the text because I just wanted to play.”  

What we changed  

- “Simplified energy display and added a short visual explanation.”  
- “Reduced tutorial text, added more contextual hints.”  
- “Added clearer invite button and first time co op mission flow.”  

B5. Documenting UX work in the data room  

Place UX work primarily in:  

- A2: Player research and UX reports – readable summaries with methodology, findings and changes.  
- D1: Raw data – anonymised transcripts, surveys, observation tables.  

Make it clear:  

- What each study was for.  
- When it ran.  
- Who participated.  
- Which build or feature version it related to.  

This allows investors or their advisors to spot check a study without needing to digest everything.  


6.3 Appendix C – Financial model teaching appendix  

C1. Overview of the three sheet model  

You need a simple but coherent model that you can understand and maintain. The structure below is a practical starting point.  

Sheet 1: Assumptions and drivers  

- All core inputs: cohorts, retention, monetisation, UA, costs.  

Sheet 2: P and L and KPIs  

- Monthly revenue, costs and key performance indicators.  

Sheet 3: Cashflow and runway  

- Monthly cash movements and closing balance.  

You can add more sheets for detail as you grow, but these three should remain the core.  

C2. Sheet 1 – Assumptions and drivers  

Structure this sheet so that you can see and change assumptions without digging into formulas.

Key sections  

- Time frame  
  - Start month and end month.  
  - Round closing month.  

- Cohort and retention assumptions  
  - New users per month by major source (UA, organics, cross promotion).  
  - Retention curves or tables, for example percentage of cohort active each month for 12 to 24 months.  

- Monetisation assumptions  
  - ARPDAU by month since install or by cohort.  
  - Payer conversion rates and ARPPU where useful.  
  - Revenue split assumptions between IAP and ads.  

- UA assumptions  
  - Monthly UA spend by channel.  
  - Expected CPI per channel.  
  - Allocation of spend across regions where relevant.  

- Cost assumptions  
  - Headcount numbers by role over time, with fully loaded cost estimates.  
  - Server, tools and platform fees as percentage of revenue or fixed amounts.  
  - Office and other overheads if applicable.  

Good practice  

- Keep all raw assumptions in one place rather than scattered across sheets.  
- Use named ranges or clear cell names so formulas elsewhere are readable.  
- Include annotation cells that explain where key assumptions came from (for example “based on soft launch cohort X, adjusted down 20 percent for conservatism”).  

C3. Sheet 2 – P and L and KPIs  

This sheet combines assumptions into monthly outputs.

Revenue build up  

- For each month, calculate:  
  - Active users by cohort (using retention assumptions).  
  - DAU approximation, for example active users times average days played per month.  
  - ARPDAU to monthly revenue: DAU times ARPDAU times days per month.  
  - Ads revenue if modelled separately.  

Costs  

- Staff costs by function, derived from headcount and cost per head.  
- UA spend, driven by assumptions in sheet 1.  
- Server and tool costs.  
- Platform fees (typically a percentage of gross revenue).  
- Other operating costs.  

KPIs  

- DAU, MAU and DAU/MAU ratio.  
- Payers and payer share if computed directly.  
- ARPDAU overall and by platform if needed.  
- LTV estimates if you choose to compute them here.  
- UA metrics: CPI and payback indicators for key channels.  

Output format  

- Monthly columns, row groups for revenue, costs and KPIs.  
- Subtotals for revenue and each cost category.  
- A simple EBITDA or operating profit line if helpful.  

C4. Sheet 3 – Cashflow and runway  

This sheet tracks cash movement over time and helps you reason about runway and funding needs.

Inputs  

- Opening cash balance.  
- Cash inflows:  
  - Equity funding draw downs.  
  - Revenue receipts, possibly lagged if platforms pay on delay.  

- Cash outflows:  
  - Total monthly costs from the P and L.  
  - Tax payments if relevant.  

Outputs  

- Net cash flow per month.  
- Closing cash balance.  
- Minimum cash balance over the period.  
- Months of runway at any point (for example closing balance divided by trailing three month average burn).  

Use this sheet to check:  

- How long your Seed or Series A round will last under each scenario.  
- When you need to start preparing the next round.  
- Where major spend increases might stress cash.  

C5. Building from users and cohorts up  

Cohort based modelling connects behaviour to revenue.

Basic approach  

- For each month, define new users acquired by source.  
- For each user cohort, track retention over future months.  
- For each active user in each future month, apply ARPDAU or similar revenue assumption.  

You do not need a complex matrix to start. A simple implementation can be:  

- Assumptions sheet contains retention and ARPDAU tables by month since install (for example month 1, month 2, month 3).  
- For each calendar month, sum:  
  - Cohort size from current and prior months multiplied by the relevant retention percentage and ARPDAU for their age.  

For more advanced models, you can:  

- Split cohorts by country or region.  
- Split cohorts by UA source.  
- Use different ARPDAU curves by segment.  

C6. Structuring base, upside and downside scenarios  

Investors expect scenarios, not a single line forecast.

Scenario definitions  

- Base case  
  - Reasonable expectation if you execute your current plan competently and metrics improve along the trajectory you are already seeing.  

- Upside case  
  - Outcomes if key optimisations work well: improved retention, better monetisation tuning, stronger UA performance.  

- Downside case  
  - Outcomes if improvements are slower than expected or if UA costs rise.  

Implementing scenarios  

- Create a small control area in the assumptions sheet with:  
  - Scenario selector cell.  
  - Multipliers for key metrics per scenario, for example:  
    - Retention multiplier.  
    - ARPDAU multiplier.  
    - CPI multiplier.  

- Use these multipliers in your formulas so you can switch between scenarios easily.  

Using scenarios  

- In the deck, show:  
  - Base case summary with revenue and EBITDA or cash position over time.  
  - Short explanation of upside and downside and what changes in each.  

- In conversation, be ready to explain:  
  - What you would do differently if you are tracking towards downside.  
  - How you would allocate extra capital if upside begins to materialise.  

C7. Common financial model pitfalls  

Avoid the following patterns.  

- Hard coded revenue numbers that do not link back to cohorts and ARPDAU.  
- Ignoring platform fees, local taxes or revenue shares.  
- Having staff costs that do not match your hiring plans or headcount slide.  
- Assuming UA budgets that are too small to generate the installs implied by your revenue forecasts.  
- No link between metrics shown in the deck and the numbers in the model.  

A solid, simple model that you understand deeply is better than a complex model that you cannot explain.

6.4 Appendix D – Creative testing and attribution  

D1. Why creative testing matters  

For mobile F2P, creative is often the biggest lever you control in UA. The same product can have very different CPI and cohort quality depending on how you frame it in ads. Serious investors know this and will probe whether you have a structured creative process.

Good creative testing helps you:  

- Discover which aspects of your game actually attract the right players.  
- Reduce CPI without destroying cohort quality.  
- Build a repeatable process for generating and refreshing ads.  

D2. Planning creative tests with clear hypotheses  

Treat creative tests like product experiments. Each test should answer a specific question.

Step 1: Define the question  

Examples:  

- “Do players respond better to the fantasy of being a commander or to the fantasy of co op fun with friends.”  
- “Does showing real gameplay outperform stylised cinematic footage.”  
- “Does focusing on challenge or relaxation attract the cohorts we want.”  

Step 2: Define the variants  

- Create two to four creative variants that differ clearly along one dimension.  
- Keep other factors constant where possible: format, length, CTA, channel, targeting.  

Step 3: Define success metrics  

- Primary: CPI and install rate for the ad group.  
- Secondary: post install metrics for the cohort, such as D1 and D7 retention, tutorial completion, early monetisation.  

Step 4: Define minimum sample size and budget  

- Decide in advance how many impressions or installs you need before you interpret results.  
- Avoid calling winners on tiny samples.  

Step 5: Run and document  

- Run the test on one or two channels initially (for example Meta and TikTok).  
- Document: set up, creatives used, targeting, timeframe, budgets, results, next steps.  
- Store the documentation in D4 with links to the raw data.  

D3. Common creative testing mistakes and how to avoid them  

Mistake 1: Judging only on CPI  

- Risk: A creative can bring cheap installs that churn immediately.  
- Fix: Always compare CPI together with early retention and monetisation by cohort.  

Mistake 2: Testing too many variables at once  

- Risk: You cannot tell what caused differences in performance.  
- Fix: Change one main concept per test round. Once you have a winner, iterate around it.  

Mistake 3: Under powering tests  

- Risk: You make decisions on noisy data.  
- Fix: Set minimum thresholds for impressions or installs and stick to them before declaring a winner.  

Mistake 4: Ignoring creative fatigue  

- Risk: A creative that works well at low spend may decline as you scale.  
- Fix: Track performance over time and plan for creative refresh cycles.  

Mistake 5: Creative that misrepresents the game  

- Risk: You attract the wrong players and damage trust.  
- Fix: Ensure that your best performing creatives are honest about core gameplay and experience. Investors will expect this.  

D4. Attribution and measurement under privacy constraints  

Modern UA operates under privacy rules that limit user level tracking. You need a measurement strategy that recognises these constraints.

Key principles  

- You cannot rely solely on deterministic user level attribution.  
- You should combine several views: network dashboards, internal cohorts, and aggregated models.  
- You should be transparent about uncertainties.  

Practical components  

1. Channel and campaign tagging  

- Use campaign and creative identifiers consistently so you can group cohorts by acquisition source.  
- Ensure that your analytics events capture acquisition_source or similar fields where permitted.  

2. Internal cohort tracking  

- Create internal cohorts by first seen campaign, creative or channel, even if attribution is probabilistic.  
- Track retention and monetisation per cohort over time.  

3. Incrementality testing  

- Where spend is sufficient, use geo or time based tests to estimate incremental impact of campaigns.  
- For example, increase spend in one region while keeping others stable and compare blended performance.  

4. Blended and portfolio metrics  

- Track high level blended metrics (overall CPI, overall payback) but use them together with cohort views, not instead of them.  

5. Honest limitations  

- Document where your data is noisy or incomplete.  
- Be clear in the deck and data room about what is measured precisely and what is estimated.  

D5. Presenting UA and creative results in Seed and Series A decks  

Seed  

- Focus on process and early signals:  
  - Channels tested and why.  
  - Creative concepts tried and initial performance.  
  - Link from creative concepts to in game behaviour.  

- Use simple charts:  
  - Bar charts comparing CPI and D7 retention by creative concept (numbers illustrative).  
  - Short bullets describing learnings and next steps.  

Series A  

- Present more structure and depth:  
  - Channel mix with spend, installs, CPI and cohort quality.  
  - Creative evolution story: how you iterated from first to current winners.  
  - Payback curves by channel and creative, even if early.  

- Be explicit about:  
  - How you deal with attribution gaps.  
  - Where you see room to scale spend and where you are constrained.  

Always label charts clearly with:  

- Timeframe.  
- Countries.  
- Channels.  
- Whether numbers are illustrative or real.  


6.5 Appendix E – Speaking to different investor types  

E1. Overview  

Different investors often look at the same deck through different lenses. You should not change your facts, but you can adjust emphasis and the order of your story.

The main types you will likely encounter:  

- Angels.  
- Generalist funds.  
- Specialist game funds.  
- Strategic investors and publishers.  

E2. Angels  

Angels often care more about the team and the personal story than about detailed models, especially at Seed.

What to emphasise  

- Founders and team slide.  
- Vision, fantasy and motivation.  
- Early product and player validation.  

How to speak to them  

- Spend more time on why you personally care about this game and audience.  
- Explain your background and why you are the right people to build this.  
- Use simpler language around metrics and models. Offer follow up detail if they want it.  

Example adjustment  

- When walking the deck, spend slightly more time on S02, S03 and S12, and slightly less on deep UA mechanics.  

E3. Generalist funds  

Generalist funds invest across sectors. Many will have limited games expertise.

What to emphasise  

- Market size and position.  
- Business model and unit economics.  
- Team quality and track record.  
- Clear milestones and funding path.  

How to speak to them  

- Translate games metrics into more general SaaS or consumer internet language where possible. For example, draw parallels between retention curves and subscription churn.  
- Clarify where games are different, especially around UA, LTV and content risk.  
- Avoid jargon, or define it clearly.  

Example adjustment  

- Spend more time on market segmentation (S06), competitive landscape (S07), monetisation (S09) and financials (S13 and model appendix).  
- Be ready with simple explanations of ARPDAU, LTV, CPI and payback.  

E4. Specialist game funds  

Specialist funds know the space well. They will often dig deeper into product, metrics and team experience.

What to emphasise  

- Product depth and differentiation.  
- Cohort and monetisation data.  
- Live ops strategy and track record.  
- Specific team experience shipping and scaling games.  

How to speak to them  

- Be more direct and technical. They will know the benchmarks and reference titles.  
- Do not skip over weaknesses; explain what you tried and what happened.  
- Be ready for detailed questions about economy, live ops and UA experiments.  

Example adjustment  

- Highlight your strongest cohorts and be candid about weaker ones.  
- Use examples from comparable games they likely know and respect.  

E5. Strategic investors and publishers  

Strategic investors may be platforms, larger studios or non financial partners. They often care about fit with their portfolio and long term collaboration.

What to emphasise  

- Strategic fit: genre, audience, technology, platform.  
- Synergy opportunities: cross promotion, IP, tools, talent.  
- Governance and collaboration expectations.  

How to speak to them  

- Recognise their existing products and strengths. Show that you understand how you would fit beside them.  
- Be clear about what you want to protect: creative control, IP ownership, studio culture.  
- Be upfront about any non financial value you expect and what you can offer in return.  

Example adjustment  

- Include a short slide or extra bullets describing how your studio complements their portfolio.  
- Cover operational alignment, for example tooling, pipelines or geographic presence.  

E6. Adjusting emphasis without changing facts  

You should never:  

- Change numbers or hide material issues depending on who you are speaking to.  
- Present different versions of the same core metrics to different investor types.  

You can legitimately:  

- Reorder sections to match interests. For example, start with product for specialist funds, or with market for generalist funds.  
- Spend more detail on certain slides in conversation.  
- Prepare tailored Q and A notes for each investor type.  

A useful exercise is to write one or two sentences for each investor type that explain:  

- Why this is interesting for them.  
- Which parts of the deck matter most to them.  

Use these as a mental checklist before each meeting.  


6.6 Appendix F – How to run a live pitch using this deck  

F1. Suggested meeting structure  

For a typical 60 minute meeting:  

- 5 minutes: introductions and context.  
- 20 to 25 minutes: walk through the deck.  
- 25 to 30 minutes: questions and deeper discussion.  
- 5 minutes: wrap up and next steps.  

Adjust timings for shorter or longer slots, but aim to spend at least half the time on discussion.  

F2. How to walk through the deck without reading bullets  

Investors can read faster than you can talk. Use the slides as anchors, not scripts.

Practical techniques  

- For each slide, know the one or two sentences you want to say out loud. The rest is backup if they want more detail.  
- Talk about the story behind the bullet points, not the exact wording.  
- Use examples and anecdotes, especially from player research and live ops.  

Example  

Instead of reading each bullet on the player problem slide, say:  

“We have spent a lot of time talking to midcore tactics players who are now in their thirties and forties. They love depth, but their lives are busy. They hate games that demand 30 minute uninterrupted sessions. What they want is to feel clever in ten minutes, not to grind. That is the gap we are aiming at.”  

F3. Handling questions and pushback  

Questions are a good sign. They show engagement. Your job is to respond clearly, honestly and without becoming defensive.

Guidelines  

- Listen fully, then paraphrase the question to confirm you understood it.  
- If you know the answer, give it concisely and refer to supporting slides or data if helpful.  
- If you do not know, say so. Offer to follow up with data or a short note.  
- If a question exposes a weakness, acknowledge it and describe what you are doing about it.  

Examples  

Question: “Your D30 retention looks low compared to title X. Why will this improve.”  

Response pattern:  
- Acknowledge: “You are right that our current D30 retention is weaker than we want.”  
- Context: “We think two issues drive this: late game content thinness and an unbalanced progression spike at level 25.”  
- Action: “We have two experiments running now: a new mission type for elder players and re tuned progression around that spike. Early cohorts suggest we can improve D30 by 2 to 3 points.”  

F4. Using appendices and the data room in conversation  

You rarely need to present appendices in full. Instead:  

- Use them when a question calls for more depth. For example, jump to the UX appendix when asked about player research.  
- Refer to the data room and model for detailed checks. For example, “We can share the full cohort analysis behind this chart in the data room so your team can review.”  

Make sure you know where everything is so that navigation feels smooth.  

F5. Follow up best practices  

Immediately after the meeting:  

- Send a short thank you email.  
- Include:  
  - The deck used.  
  - Any requested files or links to the data room, with clear access instructions.  
  - Answers to any questions you promised to follow up on.  

Over the next days and weeks:  

- Keep investors updated with meaningful progress, not noise.  
- If metrics or plans change in ways that affect the pitch, be transparent.  
- Manage your own time; avoid sending frequent tiny updates just to stay visible.  

A disciplined, transparent follow up pattern builds trust even before a term sheet exists.  


6.7 Appendix G – Chart and graph recipes  

Every metric chart in your deck should be clear, honest and easy to read. The examples below describe structures and number patterns. All numbers should be replaced with your own data.

G1. Retention curves  

Purpose  

- Show how often players come back over time, for one or more cohorts.  

Chart structure  

- Line chart.  
- X axis: days since install (D1, D3, D7, D14, D30, optionally D60, D90).  
- Y axis: percentage of the cohort active on that day.  
- One line per cohort, with clear legend (for example “Canada soft launch, January”, “UK soft launch, February”).  

Example pattern (illustrative only)  

- D1: 40 percent.  
- D7: 15 percent.  
- D30: 7 percent.  

Notes  

- Label sample sizes for each cohort in the legend or caption.  
- Avoid averaging across many different builds or regions.  

G2. Revenue mix  

Purpose  

- Show how revenue is split between sources, such as IAP categories and ads.  

Chart structure  

- Stacked bar or simple bar chart.  
- X axis: time periods (for example months).  
- Y axis: total revenue for that period.  
- Each bar segmented by source: cosmetics, battle pass, convenience items, ads.  

Example pattern (illustrative)  

- Month 6:  
  - Cosmetics: 35 percent.  
  - Battle pass: 30 percent.  
  - Convenience: 25 percent.  
  - Ads: 10 percent.  

Notes  

- Make sure categories match those in your economy docs and model.  

G3. LTV versus CPI and payback  

Purpose  

- Show whether UA is economically viable at current or target performance.  

Chart structure option A – Payback curve  

- Line chart.  
- X axis: days or months since acquisition.  
- Y axis: cumulative revenue per user (LTV) and cumulative UA spend per user (CPI equivalent).  
- One line for LTV, one horizontal line for CPI, or two lines if modelling dynamic spend.  

Chart structure option B – Scatter  

- X axis: CPI by channel or campaign.  
- Y axis: projected LTV over a fixed window (for example 180 days).  
- Each point is a channel or cohort.  

Example pattern (illustrative)  

- Channel A: CPI 1.20, LTV at 180 days 1.80.  
- Channel B: CPI 0.80, LTV at 180 days 1.10.  
- Channel C: CPI 1.50, LTV at 180 days 2.10.  

Notes  

- Label clearly whether LTV is observed or modelled, and what window you used.  
- Make clear any assumptions in a caption or in the data room.  

G4. Onboarding funnels  

Purpose  

- Show where new players drop out of early experience steps.  

Chart structure  

- Funnel chart or bar chart.  
- X axis: onboarding steps (for example “Install”, “First session”, “Tutorial complete”, “First mission complete”, “Account created”).  
- Y axis: percentage or absolute count of players reaching each step.  

Example pattern (illustrative)  

- Install: 100 percent.  
- First session: 80 percent.  
- Tutorial complete: 60 percent.  
- First mission complete: 50 percent.  
- Account created: 35 percent.  

Notes  

- This chart is especially useful for showing before and after improvements in onboarding.  

G5. Chart hygiene and honesty  

General rules  

- Label axes and units clearly.  
- Do not truncate the Y axis in ways that exaggerate small changes unless you clearly explain why.  
- State time windows and sample sizes.  
- Mark whether numbers are illustrative or real.  
- Never smooth or manipulate curves to hide volatility unless you also show the raw view elsewhere.  

If you treat your charts as tools to think with, not decoration, they will hold up well under investor scrutiny.  


7. GLOSSARY  

This glossary provides short, plain language definitions for terms used in the guide. It is not exhaustive, but covers the main concepts a first time founder will encounter.

Ad monetisation  

- Earning revenue from advertisements shown in your game, such as rewarded videos or interstitials.  

ARPDAU (Average Revenue Per Daily Active User)  

- Total revenue in a period divided by the number of unique daily active users in that period. Commonly expressed in currency per DAU per day.  

ARPPU (Average Revenue Per Paying User)  

- Total revenue from payers in a period divided by the number of unique payers in that period.  

Battle pass or season pass  

- A time limited progression track where players unlock rewards by playing. Often has a free track and a paid track.  

Blended metrics  

- Metrics that combine data from multiple cohorts, channels, regions or time periods, which can hide important differences and problems.  

Cohort  

- A group of users who share a characteristic, such as install date, country, channel or device type, and who are tracked over time as a group.  

CPI (Cost Per Install)  

- UA spend divided by installs attributed to that spend, usually per campaign or channel.  

DAU (Daily Active Users)  

- Number of unique users who opened and used the game in a given day.  

Downside case  

- A scenario in your financial model that assumes weaker performance, such as lower retention, lower monetisation or higher CPI, used to understand risk.  

Due diligence  

- The process by which investors and their advisors check the accuracy and completeness of your story, numbers and legal position before investing.  

F2P (Free to play)  

- Games that are free to download and play, with revenue from in app purchases and sometimes ads.  

Hyper casual  

- A category of very simple, short session games, typically with ads as the main revenue source and minimal progression. Not the focus of this guide.  

IAP (In app purchase)  

- Purchases made by players inside the game, such as bundles, currency packs or cosmetic items.  

KPI (Key Performance Indicator)  

- A metric that is used to track progress on important aspects of the business, such as retention, ARPDAU or CPI.  

LTV (Lifetime Value)  

- The total revenue expected from a user over their life with the game. Often estimated over a fixed window, such as 90 or 180 days, rather than true lifetime.  

MAU (Monthly Active Users)  

- Number of unique users who opened and used the game at least once in a given month.  

Payback period  

- The time it takes for the revenue from a cohort of users to cover the cost of acquiring them.  

Payer conversion  

- Percentage of active users who have made at least one purchase in a given period.  

Retention  

- The percentage of a cohort that returns to play after a given period, such as day 1, day 7 or day 30 after install.  

Runway  

- How many months a company can operate before running out of cash, given current or projected burn.  

Seed round  

- An early funding round aimed at building and validating product and team, usually before large scale revenues exist.  

Series A  

- A later funding round aimed at scaling a product and business that shows signs of working, typically with live metrics.  

Soft launch  

- A limited launch in selected regions to test and improve product, monetisation and UA before a wider or global release.  

UA (User Acquisition)  

- The process of acquiring new players, usually through paid advertising campaigns, influencer deals and other marketing activities.  

Upside case  

- A scenario in your financial model that represents better than base performance, used to explore potential growth and capital needs.  

UX (User Experience)  

- The overall experience and usability of the game from the player’s perspective, including interfaces, flow and feedback.

Use this checklist as a last pass before you send your deck, model and data room to external investors. Be strict with yourself.

Story and structure

- Each Seed slide (S01 to S14) maps cleanly to one teaching page pattern.  
- For Series A, each major area has upgraded content reflecting live data and operations.

- All metrics in the deck are consistent with those in your dashboards and exports.  
- Time windows, cohorts and sample sizes are labelled.

- Monetisation, UX and marketing stories are aligned and do not contradict each other.  

Seed slide quality  

For each slide S01 to S14:

- No slide is a 1.  
- Any slide that is a 2 has a clear plan to improve.

Series A readiness (if relevant)

- You have a credible UA approach with early channel and creative learnings.

- The financial model has three main sheets: assumptions, P and L/KPIs, cashflow.  
- All revenue lines are driven by cohorts, retention and ARPDAU, not hard coded.  
- UA spend, CPI and resulting installs are internally consistent.  
- Staff costs and hiring timeline in the model match the team slide and plan.  
- Base, upside and downside scenarios are implemented and understood.  

Data room and due diligence

Presentation readiness

- You have prepared short, clear answers to likely questions about product, metrics, UA and finances.  
- You can explain your biggest risks and what you are doing about them.  
- You know which parts of the story to emphasise for different investor types, without changing facts.  

Personal readiness  

- You and your co founders are aligned on what you want from investors beyond money.  
- You have thought about valuation ranges, ownership and how much dilution you are willing to accept.  
- You have a realistic sense of your own constraints and where you will need help.  

If you can tick most of these boxes honestly, your deck and data room are likely ready for serious conversations. If not, treat the gaps as a work plan. This guide is intended to help you fill them step by step and arrive at a robust, investor ready package that reflects the real strength and potential of your studio.

Most first time founders in mobile games are asked to do three difficult things at once:

1.2 Who this guide is for  

This guide is written for:

- Teams with limited or no previous fundraising experience.  
- Studios who intend to build fair, long term games, not short term extraction machines.

- You understand the basics of free to play game design and production.

- Have raised money before.  
- Are already confident with investor language.  

If you are more experienced, you can still use the guide as a checklist and teaching tool inside your team.  


1.3 What this guide is and is not  

What this guide is  

- A teaching manual. It explains the purpose of each slide, what to prepare, what to show and what to avoid.  
- A set of patterns and recipes. It gives you slide structures, chart patterns and data room layouts that match current practice in mobile free to play.  
- A lens from both founder and investor viewpoints. It repeatedly asks what serious investors look for and why.  
- A reference for internal coaching. You can use it to train junior team members or to align co founders on what “good” looks like.  

What this guide is not

- Legal, tax or accounting advice. It describes what investors tend to expect, but you should always consult professional advisors for specific jurisdictions.  


1.4 What investors actually buy at Seed and Series A  

Investors do not buy slides. They buy a combination of four things.  

- Team. Can this group of people build and operate the game and business they describe.  
- Product. Is this game capable of delighting a specific audience strongly enough that they stick.  
- Machine. Is there a credible, measurable way to turn marketing spend and time into players, revenue and margin.  
- Fit. Does the opportunity fit the investor’s fund, thesis and portfolio.  

The same deck can land very differently with different investors. You cannot control the last item fully. You can greatly improve the first three.  

This guide is structured so that every slide you build contributes to one of those three areas, and so that your data room and model make it easy for investors to verify what you say.  


1.5 How Seed, Series A and due diligence differ in practice  

Seed  

At Seed, investors mostly decide based on:  

- Team and founding story.  
- Concept, vision and early gameplay.  
- Early signs that players care, even if samples are small.  
- A realistic plan for the next 18 to 24 months.  

Metrics are helpful but often noisy and incomplete. Investors lean more on:  

- Whether you understand the genre and audience.  
- Whether your plans feel grounded in mobile free to play reality.  
- Whether you seem like people they can work with through the messy early stages.  

Series A  

At Series A, investors mostly decide based on:  

- Live metrics and cohorts.  
- Evidence that the game can be scaled with reasonable economics.  
- Whether the organisation can handle a larger team and more complex operations.  
- The quality and robustness of your financial model.  

Narrative still matters, but the centre of gravity shifts from belief to evidence.  

Due diligence  

Due diligence runs alongside and after these rounds. It is:  

- A structured check that your story matches the facts.  
- A deeper look at legal, financial and technical risks.  
- A way for investors to assess working style and transparency.  

The Seed and Series A decks should both be designed with due diligence in mind. Every material claim should have a clear source in the data room or model, even at early stage.

Some approaches to free to play monetisation and live operations may create short term revenue but are unlikely to survive platform rules, regulatory scrutiny or serious investor ethics tests. This guide deliberately avoids and warns against:

2.5 Common failure modes this guide is designed to avoid  

Several patterns repeatedly cause problems for founders during fundraising. This guide aims to prevent them.  

Pattern 1: Beautiful but shallow decks  

- Slides look polished but contain vague claims, no numbers and little evidence.  
- Investors cannot see how you think about cohorts, payback or live ops.  

Pattern 2: Deck and model that do not match  

- Slide numbers do not reconcile with the financial model.  
- Retention, ARPDAU or CPI in the deck differ from those implied by the model.  

Pattern 3: Overfitting to one investor type

Pattern 5: Overcomplicated or untrusted model  

- Large, opaque spreadsheet that even the founders do not fully understand.  
- Many hard coded numbers and few links to real cohorts.  

The structure and patterns in this guide are deliberately simple enough to maintain, but detailed enough to satisfy serious investors.
