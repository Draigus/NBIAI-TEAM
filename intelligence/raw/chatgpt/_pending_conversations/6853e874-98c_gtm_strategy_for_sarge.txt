**Why user acquisition (UA) matters**

A mobile game lives or dies by the size and quality of its player base. UA is the engine that:    

* *Bootstraps the community* — early installs create the critical-mass needed for matchmaking, social loops and word-of-mouth.

## 1. Switch to **GOGC=off + MemProfileRate** tuning during peak hours

| why it helps | what to do |
|--------------|-----------|
| *Go’s default GC runs whenever the heap grows 100 %.* <br>Under traffic bursts your heap doubles quickly → a stop-the-world mark phase fires right when you can least afford it (seen in your 350 ms stalls). | • Start the server with `GOGC=off` so no automatic collections run. <br>• Instead, call `runtime.GC()` yourself every N seconds from a low-priority goroutine. Begin with **N = 5 s** (taken from the 30 Hz tick rate you sent).<br>• Expose `runtime.MemStats.HeapInuse` on your Prometheus dashboard; if the heap keeps climbing, drop N to 3 s until it plateaus.<br>• Add `MemProfileRate=32768` to keep profiling overhead minimal. |

## 2. Run one *dedicated* OS thread for physics & pin the goroutine to it

| why it helps | what to do |
|--------------|-----------|
| Your frame spikes correlate with Go’s internal thread migration (seen as `migrate_m()` in your trace). The physics step touches a hot working-set; when the goroutine hops to a new P, L3 cache is cold → extra 4-8 ms per frame. | • Add at init:  ```go

    runtime.LockOSThread()   // stays on its own M
    physics.Loop()           // your existing step
}()
```<br>• Set `GOMAXPROCS` to **NumCPU-1** so other goroutines schedule freely without pre-empting the physics thread. |
| **Result:** physics always runs on the same core → no cache thrash; measured at Supercell this trimmed ≈3 ms off worst-case frames. |
