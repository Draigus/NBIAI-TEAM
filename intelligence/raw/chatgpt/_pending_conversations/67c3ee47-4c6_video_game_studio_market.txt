# Consulting Services for Video Game Studios – Viability Analysis

**Demand for Consulting:** There is *genuine demand* for consulting in game development, driven by the complexity of making successful games today. Studios large and small face challenges in areas like monetization design, live-ops strategy, user acquisition, analytics, production pipeline optimization, and new technology adoption. These are domains where outside experts can add value. In fact, many game companies already leverage external help – for example, nearly **70% of game developers use outsourcing services** to support development【50†L1-L4】. This reliance on external partners indicates an openness to third-party expertise, which extends to advisory roles. **Indie studios** often need guidance on **marketing, fundraising, or game design improvements**; they may lack senior expertise in-house and seek mentors or consultants to review their games. (It’s common to see indie teams requesting feedback on design, usability, monetization strategy, etc., even on forums – effectively looking for consulting【29†L269-L276】.) **Mobile game studios** frequently consult specialists for **monetization and user acquisition** strategy, given how data-driven and competitive the mobile market is. **AAA studios** and publishers, on the other hand, might engage consultants for high-level strategy (market expansion, studio reorganization, M&A integration) or technical expertise (e.g. adopting a new game engine or optimizing development processes at scale). The demand is particularly strong in areas that directly impact a studio’s success: a consultant who can help boost a game’s retention or revenue can justify their cost. As games have become a high-stakes business (a single failed project can sink a studio), many studios are willing to pay for **experienced advice to “de-risk” development and improve odds of success**.

**Competitive Landscape:** The market for advising game studios has a mix of **big consulting firms and specialized boutique agencies**. This means more seasoned professionals are available for hire on a contract/consulting basis, **intensifying competition** for providing advice and services to studios. Any new entrant must carve out a clear niche or value proposition to stand out in this landscape.

**Challenges:** While opportunities exist, consulting for game studios comes with **significant challenges**:

- **Budget Constraints:** Unlike enterprise clients in other industries, many game studios (especially indie and small mobile studios) operate on *very tight budgets*. Over 80% of studios are small indies【53†L272-L280】, and these teams typically prioritize funding development and marketing over external consulting. In Canada, for example, most studios are “smaller independent shops” with <25 employees【26†L1-L9】 – such teams may simply *not afford* typical consulting fees. Even when they recognize the value of advice, paying hundreds of dollars per hour for consulting might be infeasible unless they have strong funding or revenue. This limits the addressable client pool primarily to **mid-sized and larger studios, or well-funded startups**. Converting indie developers into paying consulting clients is tough; services often need to be packaged affordably (or provided in group workshops, accelerators, etc.) to attract them.

- **Industry Specificity:** Game development is a very *specialized, creative process* – generic business consulting tactics don’t always translate well. Studios might be skeptical of consultants who lack game industry experience. Successful consulting in this field usually requires **domain expertise** (e.g. understanding how game production cycles work, gamer community trends, game-specific KPIs like retention, ARPU, etc.). This creates a *barrier to entry* for generalist consultancies and means NBI Consulting must have bona fide gaming experts on the team. Even with expertise, influencing a studio can be hard; game teams may be protective of their design vision or tech choices. A consultant must integrate with the studio’s culture and not come off as simply an outsider “suit.” In short, the **credibility hurdle** is higher in gaming – it’s a tight-knit industry where reputation matters.

- **Value Demonstration:** Consulting services need to demonstrate clear **ROI in the game context**. If a studio spends $50k on consulting, they’ll expect, say, that their game’s performance improves meaningfully (better sales, successful launch, etc.). But linking advice to outcomes can be tricky. A marketing consultant’s guidance might be sound, but the game could still underperform due to genre trends or quality issues out of the consultant’s control. This uncertainty can make studios hesitant. Moreover, **measurement** is an issue: it’s easier to quantify an outsourced task (e.g. “this art asset was created”) than to quantify advice. Consulting engagements might therefore be a harder sell unless the offering is tied to tangible deliverables (market research reports, financial projections, etc.) or reputation (e.g. a known designer consulting on improving game design).

- **Market Fragmentation:** The needs of game studios vary widely. AAA publishers might want high-level strategy or restructuring guidance, while an indie team might need a hands-on gameplay critique or help debugging their build process. It’s challenging for one consulting firm to service the entire range. NBI Consulting would need to either specialize (and thus narrow its market) or maintain a broad roster of expertise (which can be costly). Additionally, economic cycles affect viability – the past year saw many studios **downsizing** and some closures amid a post-pandemic market correction【54†L55-L63】【54†L79-L87】. When studios are cutting costs or headcount, discretionary spending on consultants could be trimmed first, making the business cyclical.

**Opportunities:** Despite challenges, there are compelling reasons this strategy *can* be viable, given certain industry trends:

- **Need for Specialized Knowledge:** As games become more complex (technically and commercially), even experienced studios encounter areas outside their core expertise. Similarly, areas like **data analytics, live operations, user research, community management** are now crucial to success and often warrant external help. Studios know that *getting these things wrong can doom a game*, so they’re willing to bring in consultants to guide them rather than purely trial-and-error. NBI Consulting could tap into these specialized needs (e.g. offering a “Monetization Health Check” service or “Live Ops Strategy Workshop” for studios).

- **Mid-size Studio Growth:** The rise of **AA studios and indie publishers** provides a sweet spot of clients who have budgets and ambition. These companies (say 50–200 staff) are large enough to afford consulting and small enough to *not* have all functions in-house. Many are at a growth stage – perhaps they just secured investment or had a hit game – and now need to formalize their business strategy, production pipelines, or expand globally. Consulting can help with exactly those scaling challenges. If NBI targets these growing studios (e.g. a 50-person studio that wants to become 150-person multi-project studio in 2 years), the value proposition is strong. There’s evidence of this segment’s expansion: e.g., more indie studios are releasing second and third titles successfully, indicating maturation【44†L57-L63】. These maturing studios will seek guidance on **best practices, process improvement, and strategic planning** as they professionalize.

- **New Studio Formation and Innovation:** The continuous flow of **new studios** each year means new potential clients entering the market who may lack business experience. In regions like the UK, despite many closures, **678 companies still grew and ~166 new game companies were founded in a 12-month period**【54†L79-L87】【8†L19-L27】. Globally, the allure of the gaming market is drawing entrepreneurs (often first-time studio founders). These founders are typically tech or creative folks (engineers, designers) who might *not* have background in running a business or launching a product worldwide. This creates an opportunity for consulting services in **mentorship, go-to-market strategy, and operations setup** for startups. Programs like accelerators and government game incubators (common in some countries) could be channels for NBI to offer consulting or workshops. Essentially, as long as people keep starting new game studios, there will be demand for guidance to avoid common pitfalls.

- **Adjacent Service Integration:** A consulting strategy could be made more viable by **bundling services** or adopting a hybrid model. Many consulting firms in gaming also provide some execution support. For example, consultants might not only advise on a marketing plan but also offer to manage a UA campaign, or they might deliver market research data via partnerships with analytics firms like Newzoo. NBI could differentiate by combining pure consulting with practical services (or partnering with development agencies). This one-stop-shop approach can attract studios who prefer to outsource a problem entirely rather than just get advice. Given that outsourcing development is already common, consulting that segues into outsourcing (or vice versa) can blur lines and create more opportunities.

**Conclusion – Is It Viable?** Targeting video game studios with consulting services **can be a viable strategy** if approached correctly, but it requires focus. The **viability** hinges on targeting the right **segment of studios** and offering them **clear value**. Larger and mid-tier studios are the most likely to pay for consulting – and they are indeed spending on advisors for strategy, monetization, and tech (we see both big firms and boutique consultants already serving this need)【27†L1-L8】【28†L68-L75】. The *demand* is there and growing, as games industry competition intensifies and mistakes become costlier (an expert’s guidance to fix a design flaw or optimize revenue can make or save millions). However, success in this niche means **overcoming challenges**: many potential clients are small and cash-strapped, and the field already has seasoned specialists. NBI Consulting would need to establish **strong game industry credentials**, perhaps by hiring veteran game producers or designers, to build trust. It could focus on high-impact consulting offerings (e.g. helping launch games in new markets, improving monetization for live games, training teams in new technology) where studios see a direct benefit. In terms of competition, while there are other players, the consulting market in gaming is not saturated – it’s still an emerging service area relative to the size of the industry. By leveraging recent **industry trends** (like the surge in indie revenue share, mobile growth, and increased outsourcing) and addressing both the **business and creative** facets of game development, a consulting firm can carve a meaningful niche. In summary, consulting for game studios is **viable** if the firm targets a segment willing to invest in external expertise, differentiates itself with deep industry know-how, and remains adaptable to the fast-changing gaming landscape. It’s not a strategy without risks (the client base can be fickle and budget-limited), but with gaming’s continued growth and complexity, **expert consultants are in demand** – positioning NBI to capitalize on a **growing TAM** of studios that seek to level up their business side in line with their games.
