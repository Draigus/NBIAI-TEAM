what does a business need to do and pay to support skilled worker visas in the uk

[ASSISTANT]
In the UK, businesses that wish to sponsor skilled workers under the Skilled Worker visa route need to take the following steps and pay the associated costs:

### 1. **Obtain a Sponsor Licence**
   - **Application Process**: The business must apply for a sponsor licence from the Home Office. This involves proving that the business is genuine, capable of fulfilling sponsorship duties, and needs to hire skilled workers.
   - **Fees**: 
     - **Small or charitable sponsors**: £536
     - **Medium or large sponsors**: £1,476

### 2. **Sponsorship Management**
   - Once the licence is granted, the business must assign a Certificate of Sponsorship (CoS) to each worker they want to hire.
   - **Costs**: Each Certificate of Sponsorship costs **£199**.

### 3. **Immigration Skills Charge**
   - Employers are required to pay the Immigration Skills Charge (ISC) for each sponsored worker. The charge is due when assigning the Certificate of Sponsorship.
   - **Costs**: 
     - **Small or charitable sponsors**: £364 for the first 12 months, and £182 for each additional 6 months.
     - **Medium or large sponsors**: £1,000 for the first 12 months, and £500 for each additional 6 months.

### 4. **Sponsor Duties**
   - The business must comply with a number of ongoing duties, such as reporting changes in the worker’s employment status, ensuring that the worker meets visa requirements, and keeping appropriate records.

### 5. **Optional Costs**
   - Some companies offer to cover the visa application fees and the Immigration Health Surcharge for the sponsored worker, but these costs are not mandatory for the employer.

### Overview of Key Costs:
   - **Sponsor Licence**: £536 or £1,476
   - **Certificate of Sponsorship**: £199 per worker
   - **Immigration Skills Charge**: £364 or £1,000 (depending on size of business) for the first 12 months

In total, the exact cost varies based on the size of the business and the number of employees being sponsored.

As a Skilled Worker visa holder in the UK, you are generally not allowed to start a business as your main form of employment. The Skilled Worker visa is tied to the job you are being sponsored for by your employer, and your primary obligation is to work in that sponsored role. However, you can engage in some limited activities related to starting or running a business under specific conditions:

### Key Conditions for Starting a Business:
1. **Main Employment**: Your primary job must remain the one for which you are being sponsored. You cannot leave this job and rely solely on a business you start.
   
2. **Secondary Employment (Business)**: You can start a business or work for a business outside of your primary employment under these conditions:
   - The secondary job or business activity must be in a role that is **at the same skill level** as your sponsored role (RQF level 3 or above).
   - You can work for **up to 20 hours per week** in the business or any other secondary employment.

3. **No Full Control**: The visa does not allow you to start and fully control a business as a sole trader or a full-time entrepreneur. If your main role shifts to running your own business full-time, you would need to switch to a different visa, such as the **Innovator Founder Visa** or **Start-up Visa**, which are designed for entrepreneurs.

### Alternative Options:
- **Innovator Founder Visa**: If you intend to start a business full-time, you may apply for this visa. It requires a viable business plan with potential for growth and innovation. You would need to be endorsed by an approved body.
  
- **Switching to a New Visa**: If you decide to focus entirely on starting and running a business, you can explore switching from the Skilled Worker visa to a visa category that supports full business ownership, such as the Innovator Founder Visa.

### Summary:
- You **can** start a business while holding a Skilled Worker visa, but it must be secondary to your main job, and you cannot run the business full-time.
- If your goal is to focus entirely on entrepreneurship, you would need to switch to a visa that allows for full business ownership.

how much do you have to pay a skilled worker

[ASSISTANT]
The salary you need to pay a Skilled Worker under the UK Skilled Worker visa route depends on several factors, including the type of job, the experience of the worker, and whether they are classified as a "new entrant" or an "experienced worker." However, there are general minimum salary thresholds that employers must meet.

### 1. **General Salary Thresholds:**

#### a. **Experienced Workers**
   - The general rule is that the salary must be at least **£26,200 per year** or the "going rate" for the specific occupation, **whichever is higher**.
   
#### b. **New Entrants (e.g., recent graduates or workers under 26 years old)**
   - The minimum salary for new entrants is **£20,960 per year** or 70% of the "going rate" for the specific occupation, **whichever is higher**.

### 2. **"Going Rate" for Occupations:**
   Each job type has its own "going rate," which is set by the UK government. The salary you pay must either meet or exceed this rate. The going rate varies by occupation and is usually calculated as an annual salary for a 39-hour workweek. For example:
   - A software developer might have a going rate of **£29,000 per year**.
   - A civil engineer might have a going rate of **£30,600 per year**.

You must pay whichever is **higher**—the general minimum threshold or the job's going rate.

### 3. **Pro-Rated Salary**
   If the skilled worker works less than 39 hours a week, the salary must be pro-rated based on the going rate. For example, if they work 20 hours a week, their salary must still be in line with what the pro-rated going rate would be for that amount of work.

### 4. **Exemptions or Adjustments**
   Some exemptions allow for lower salary requirements, such as roles on the **Shortage Occupation List** or jobs under **PhD-level occupations**, which may have lower minimum salary thresholds.

### 5. **Important Considerations**
   - The salary must be paid in actual earnings (not including perks or bonuses) and must be reflected in the worker’s employment contract.
   - Part-time workers or jobs with variable pay (such as commission-based roles) must also meet the minimum salary thresholds on an annualized basis.

### Summary:
- **Experienced workers**: Minimum salary of **£26,200** or the "going rate" for the job, whichever is higher.
- **New entrants**: Minimum salary of **£20,960** or 70% of the "going rate" for the job, whichever is higher.
- Specific job roles have their own going rates, which may be higher than the general thresholds.

### 1. **Relationship Requirements**
   - You must be married to or in a civil partnership with a UK citizen, someone with settled status, or a person with refugee or humanitarian protection status.
   - If unmarried, you must demonstrate that you have been in a relationship akin to marriage for at least **2 years** (for unmarried partners).
   - You must be able to provide evidence that your relationship is genuine and subsisting (e.g., joint bills, photos, travel records, etc.).

### 2. **Financial Requirements**
   - The sponsor (the UK citizen or settled person) must meet a **minimum income threshold** to show they can financially support the applicant without relying on public funds. The minimum gross income requirements are:
     - **£18,600 per year** if applying for just the partner.
     - **£22,400 per year** if applying with **1 dependent child**.
     - An additional **£2,400 per year** for each additional dependent child.
   - Income can come from employment, self-employment, savings (over £16,000), pension, or other permitted sources.
   - If the sponsor does not meet the income threshold, they can rely on **savings**, with the requirement being at least **£62,500** in savings held for at least 6 months.

### 3. **Accommodation Requirement**
   - You must demonstrate that you have adequate accommodation in the UK, meaning there is suitable living space for you, your partner, and any dependents without overcrowding.

### 4. **English Language Requirement**
   - The applicant must demonstrate proficiency in English by either:
     - Passing an **approved English language test** at A1 level or higher, or
     - Showing they are from an English-speaking country or have an academic degree taught in English.

### 5. **Genuine Relationship**
   - You must provide evidence that your relationship is genuine. This can include:
     - Marriage or civil partnership certificate.
     - Proof of cohabitation (for unmarried partners) for at least 2 years.
     - Communication records (emails, messages, etc.), photos together, and joint financial commitments (e.g., joint bank accounts).

### 6. **Immigration Status of the Sponsor**
   - The sponsor must be:
     - A **British citizen**, or
     - Holding **Indefinite Leave to Remain (ILR)**, or
     - Holding **settled status** under the EU Settlement Scheme, or
     - Holding **refugee** or **humanitarian protection status**.

### 7. **Application Process and Fees**
   - **Application Fee**:
     - Applying from outside the UK: **£1,538**.
     - Applying from inside the UK: **£1,048**.
   - **Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS)**: £624 per year of the visa (usually £1,560 for a 2.5-year visa).
   - The initial visa is granted for **30 months** (2.5 years), after which you can apply for an extension.
   - After **5 years**, you may be eligible to apply for **Indefinite Leave to Remain (ILR)**, followed by UK citizenship.

### 8. **Other Requirements**
   - You must not have a criminal record in the UK or abroad.
   - You may need to pass a **tuberculosis (TB) test** if applying from a country where TB is prevalent.

### **Summary of Key Requirements**:
1. **Relationship**: Married, in a civil partnership, or in a durable relationship for at least 2 years.
2. **Financial**: The sponsor must earn at least £18,600 (more with children).
3. **Accommodation**: Adequate living arrangements.
