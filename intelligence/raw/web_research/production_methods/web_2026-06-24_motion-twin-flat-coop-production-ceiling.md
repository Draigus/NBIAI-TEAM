# Motion Twin -- Flat Co-op Production Model: What Scales and What Does Not (Dead Cells, 11 people)

## Key Content (max 200 words)

Motion Twin (Bordeaux) is a SCOP (workers' co-operative) of 11 people deliberately kept below 15. All members receive equal base salary regardless of role; performance bonuses apply when projects succeed. Decision-making is tiered: minor decisions via chat, strategic decisions by full-team consensus, deadlocks by vote. The studio uses strict time-tracking -- late one day means leave early another.

Dead Cells was built over 18 months of early access (planned 12, extended on community feedback). The biggest explicit production mistake: shipping console versions simultaneously with PC. Bénard: "deciding to make the console versions all at the same time was ridiculous." Console certification requires working backwards at least 6 months from the target ship date.

When Motion Twin spun off Evil Empire (now 70+ people, traditional CEO/lead hierarchy), the flat model was explicitly rejected at scale. Motion Twin sold roughly 1M copies of Dead Cells before handoff; Evil Empire sold approximately 9M copies with the structured organisation. The flat model's specific failure mode at growth stage: approved decisions can be re-litigated at any point ("permanent battles"), creating a velocity ceiling proportional to team size.

## Decisions / Insights

- The flat co-op model has a documented upper ceiling of approximately 10-15 people; beyond that, consensus-based decision-making degrades production velocity faster than the cultural benefits compensate
- Consensus is reversible by default in flat structures -- any member can reopen a previously-approved decision, which is a specific and predictable production risk, not a culture problem
- Console certification lead time must be planned backwards from ship date at a minimum of 6 months; simultaneous multi-platform launch is not appropriate for studios without prior console experience
- Early access planned duration should be set at the maximum expected, not the minimum -- committing short and extending publicly damages trust more than committing long
- The Evil Empire split provides a natural experiment: same IP, flat vs structured hierarchy, documented commercial outcomes. Structure generated 9x more commercial volume once team exceeded the flat model's ceiling

Primary sources: Sébastien Bénard (designer, Motion Twin) interviewed in PC Games Insider; Steve Filby (formerly head of marketing, Motion Twin; later CEO of Evil Empire) in Kotaku and Vice.
