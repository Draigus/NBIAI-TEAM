# The Game Outcomes Project Part 3 -- Empirical Correlates of Production Success

## Key Content (max 200 words)

Multi-studio survey, several hundred developers, 120 questions, correlated against four outcome variables (schedule adherence, ROI, critical reception, internal goal achievement). Central finding: specific production methodology (Scrum, waterfall, agile, ad-hoc) showed no statistically significant difference in outcomes.

Highest-correlating success factors: design risk management (0.57 -- strongest single factor), team focus (0.50), crunch avoidance (0.44), team stability and communication (0.36-0.39). Production methodology as a category scored 0.29 -- meaningful but not dominant.

79% of studios surveyed did NOT use agile or Scrum explicitly, confirming most small-studio production is ad-hoc. Only 21% planned projects around team capability -- all of those completed under 2 years. Crunch "did not actually save projects from delays" -- all five crunch-related questions showed negative correlations. Project reorganisations showed "strong and unequivocal correlations with inferior outcomes." Design document specificity had very low correlation (<0.2), suggesting documents drive schedule adherence only, not quality or commercial outcomes.

The survey instrument (38 self-assessment questions) is publicly available for teams to use as a diagnostic.

## Decisions / Insights

- Design risk management is the strongest success predictor: studios that proactively identify and reduce design unknowns outperform those that do not, regardless of which methodology they use
- Crunch is empirically counterproductive at project level -- it does not reduce delays and correlates negatively with ROI and critical reception
- Planning around actual team capability (realistic scoping) is the strongest predictor of on-time delivery; the overwhelming majority of studios do not do this
- Methodology selection is a weak lever: investing in changing methodology without addressing design risk and team stability first is misallocated effort
- Team stability is near-causal: reorganisations during production correlate unequivocally with inferior outcomes

## Context

Paul Tozour and colleagues, published gamedeveloper.com as part of a multi-part research series. Data collected 2014. The methodology is dated but the outcome correlates are structural and corroborated by the independent Shirinian postmortem statistical analysis from a different dataset (see web_2026-06-24_dissecting-postmortem-scope-statistics).

## Applicability

Directly applicable as evidence in any NBI client engagement where a studio is debating methodology adoption. The key NBI advisory position: "choose your methodology for cultural fit, not because you believe it determines outcomes -- what determines outcomes is design risk management, realistic scoping, and crunch avoidance." This gives NBI a data-backed position that differentiates from generic Scrum certification advice. Particularly useful for countering the assumption that switching to agile will fix production problems that are actually rooted in scope discipline failures.
