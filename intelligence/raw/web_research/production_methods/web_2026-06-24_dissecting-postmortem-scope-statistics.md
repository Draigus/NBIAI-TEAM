# Dissecting the Postmortem -- Statistical Analysis of 24 Games' Production Failures

## Key Content (max 200 words)

Ara Shirinian analysed 24 consecutive postmortems from Game Developer magazine (Feb 2008 to Jan 2010). 240 data points across successes and failures. Team size clustering: studios naturally clustered at 5-10, 30-40, 68-90, and 100-plus rather than distributing uniformly. 29% cited "small team" as a positive while averaging 30-40 members -- "small" is relative to developer expectations, not an absolute figure.

71% reported scope problems -- the dominant production failure category. 50% reported late-stage feature additions or changes. Production management failures dominated "what went wrong": 68 of 120 failure data points were production-category, with scope and feature creep the largest sub-category. Only 21% used agile or Scrum explicitly; 75% valued rapid iteration in principle. 46% used contractors; outsourcing success rate was 43% (3 of 7 reporting positive experiences). Only 21% planned projects around team capability; all of those projects completed under 2 years. Conclusion: production management quality was the most impactful factor -- capable of compensating for creative weaknesses and "unravelling even the best creative work" when absent.

## Decisions / Insights

- 71% scope problem rate means scope failure is the default production outcome, not an exception -- any production advisory engagement should treat scope management as the primary intervention target
- Realistic scoping (planning around team capability) correlated with on-time delivery in the full dataset -- studios that did this consistently shipped within their planned window
- Studios consistently underestimated: 38% required time extensions ranging from weeks to 17 months; the upward tail is extreme
- Outsourcing success rate of 43% confirms that contractor integration is high-risk by default and should be treated as a managed dependency, not a simple capacity addition
- Self-reported "small team" averaging 30-40 members means NBI should not accept client team-size characterisations at face value -- probe the actual headcount before applying any framework

## Context

Ara Shirinian, gamedeveloper.com, analysis of 2008-2010 postmortem data. Dataset is approximately 15 years old; industry scale has changed but structural failure modes (scope, leadership, communication) are stable across eras, corroborated by the 2014 Game Outcomes Project data from a different dataset (see web_2026-06-24_game-outcomes-project-production-correlates). The 71% scope problem rate and team-size clustering findings are independently consistent with the Game Outcomes Project's scope findings.

## Applicability

NBI can use the 71% scope-problem figure and the realistic-scoping/on-time correlation as client-facing anchor statistics. These provide concrete numerical backing for the advisory position that methodology choice matters less than scope discipline. The clustering data (teams group at 5-10, 30-40, 68-90) is useful for benchmarking client team sizes against industry norms and for calibrating which production framework applies. The 43% contractor success rate is a specific risk figure for client studios using extensive outsourcing.
