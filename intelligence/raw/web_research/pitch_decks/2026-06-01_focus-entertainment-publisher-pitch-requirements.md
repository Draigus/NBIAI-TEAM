## Focus Entertainment pitch volume

CCO Yves Le Yaouanq disclosed that Focus receives 3,000-4,000 pitches per year. "The majority? Completely off the mark." Focus has never signed a game based solely on a cold email -- most deals originate at events (GDC, Gamescom, smaller local conferences).

## What Focus evaluates (from Le Yaouanq)

Beyond the game itself, Focus invests in teams:
1. **Human connection** -- can they see themselves working with this studio for years?
2. **Unique angle** -- gameplay, tone, or visual style that stands out
3. **Professionalism and structure** -- evidence the team knows how to finish a game
4. "Sometimes a good-but-not-great game will get signed simply because the team is that strong"

### Instant rejection triggers
- Pitching genres Focus doesn't publish (mobile, NFT, pixel-art)
- Bad financials (suggesting 100% sales retention, ignoring platform fees/VAT)
- Unrealistic comparables (comparing indie game to Elden Ring)

### What Focus wants to see in comparables
Use three benchmarks:
1. One successful comparable
2. One average comparable
3. One failure -- with reasons why your game won't fail the same way

## Jason Della Rocca (GDC Pitch host, 30 years in industry)

From GameDeveloper.com interview:

"A lot of developers think that making a pitch deck is the goal, but there's a whole bunch of stuff that happens before that." Required preparation BEFORE the deck:
- Game timeline
- Budget details
- Team information
- Market analysis and sales projections
- Playable build (proof of concept, prototype, or vertical slice)

**The pitch itself should be 5-6 minutes** of a 30-minute meeting. Rest is dialogue.

On traction: "If you walk in and you're like 'here's the build, we're already at 50,000 wishlists, and we're running a playtest', then all of a sudden the people with money perk up."

### GDC Pitch 2026 panel judges
Innersloth, Griffin Gaming Partners, Hooded Horse, 1Up Ventures, Finji, Behold Ventures.

## The "paper pitch" is dead (2025-2026 consensus)

Multiple sources converge on this: publishers no longer sign from decks alone. The requirement stack in 2025-2026:
1. Polished vertical slice or 10-15 minutes of bug-free gameplay
2. Clear market positioning with realistic comparables
3. Measurable traction (wishlists, community, playtest data)
4. Realistic production plan and budget
5. Post-launch strategy (first 3 months: QA, patches, content updates)
6. Team credibility

## Industry-wide pitch funnel data

- Focus: 3,000-4,000 pitches/year, signs ~10-15 (0.3-0.5% acceptance)
- Outersloth: ~1.4% signing rate
- Cold outreach response rate: 3-5% is considered normal
- Average studio contacts 50+ publishers before securing interest
- 15 of 50 publishers won't respond at all (30% ghost rate)

## Deal terms benchmarks (from Voyer Law / GameDiscoverCo)

- Average advance: $318K (all deals) / $460K (advance-bearing deals only)
- Range: $100K to $2M
- Standard split: 60% developer / 40% publisher
- No-advance deals: 71% developer / 29% publisher
- With-advance deals: 55% developer / 45% publisher
- 42% require full recoup before developer revenue; 58% split recoup
