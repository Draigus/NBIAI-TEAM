# Xbox Announces Business "Reset" Under New CEO Asha Sharma — 1,000+ Layoffs Expected July 2026

**Date:** 2026-06-11
**Domain:** industry_current
**Category:** Industry Structure
**Relevance:** 9 | **Novelty:** 8 | **Actionability:** 8

## Summary

On 10–11 June 2026, Xbox CEO Asha Sharma (appointed February 2026, succeeding Phil Spencer) announced a sweeping "reset" of the gaming business, coinciding with Bloomberg reports of impending major layoffs. Cuts are expected shortly after Microsoft's fiscal year ends 30 June 2026, with unconfirmed reports suggesting approximately 1,000 roles at risk and a possible studio closure. Strategic shifts include dropping the Game Pass price, reconsidering day-one Call of Duty inclusion in Game Pass, and rethinking exclusivity strategies for Gears of War: E-Day and Clockwork Revolution. Xbox's underlying problem: $20B invested in content, platform, and hardware over five years while annual revenue declined by approximately $500M.

## Key Facts

- CEO Asha Sharma issued a 100-day plan memo calling for a business "reset" (10 June 2026)
- Layoffs expected July 2026, post fiscal year end (30 June); approximately 1,000 roles cited by Bloomberg but unconfirmed by Microsoft
- Studio closure or "lineup changes" possible, per The Verge sources
- Previously closed studios this generation: Arkane Austin, Alpha Dog Games, The Initiative
- $20B invested in content/platform/hardware over 5 years (excluding the $68.7B Activision Blizzard merger)
- Annual revenue declined approximately $500M over the same period despite that spend
- Console storage component costs are now 5x higher than two years prior; overall component costs have doubled since Sharma's February arrival
- Game Pass price reduction under consideration; Call of Duty day-one inclusion in Game Pass being reviewed
- Gears of War: E-Day and Clockwork Revolution exclusivity strategies under reconsideration
- New strategic priorities: hardware, PC, mobile, streaming — traditional console exclusives deprioritised

## Strategic Significance

1. A second CEO-level Xbox reset in three years (the first under Phil Spencer's post-COVID adjustment) signals the $68.7B Activision Blizzard acquisition has not produced the expected returns within the expected timeframe. The business is being rebuilt around hardware, PC, mobile, and streaming rather than traditional console exclusive IP.

2. Dropping day-one AAA titles from Game Pass (if confirmed) would be a fundamental shift in Xbox's market identity. Day-one Game Pass access has been the platform's primary differentiator versus PlayStation since 2020; removing it narrows the reason for a developer to prefer Xbox as a primary platform partner.

3. The pattern of closing first-party studios (Arkane Austin, Alpha Dog, The Initiative) despite paying premium acquisition prices compounds trust damage. Independent studios and publishers evaluating Xbox as an acquirer or publishing partner now face a demonstrable track record of post-acquisition closures.

## NBI Advisory Relevance

- For clients considering Xbox publishing or exclusivity deals: the current instability raises genuine counterparty risk. IP reversion clauses, contractual obligations on first-party support, and studio continuity protections deserve scrutiny before signing
- The potential unwinding of day-one Game Pass is important context for distribution strategy advice — if Xbox walks back this differentiator, platform value proposition shrinks and multi-platform strategies become more defensible
- Useful in any NBI pitch covering publisher risk diversification: the Xbox restructuring illustrates exactly why multi-platform IP ownership matters
