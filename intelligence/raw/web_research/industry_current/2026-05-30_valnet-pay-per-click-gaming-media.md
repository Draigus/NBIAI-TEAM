# Valnet Introduces Pay-Per-Click Model at TheGamer — Gaming Media Crisis Deepens

**Date:** 2026-05-21 (surfaced in Patch Notes #54, 2026-05-29)
**Source:** kotaku.com, gamedeveloper.com, aftermath.site
**Domain:** industry_current
**Category:** Media / Industry Structure
**Relevance:** 6 | **Novelty:** 7 | **Actionability:** 5

## What happened

Valnet, the parent company of TheGamer (and previous acquirer of Polygon), introduced new contract terms for writers at TheGamer. Under the "Pay Per Session" model:
- Writers receive $3-$8 per 1,000 sessions depending on role
- Editors: $3 per 1,000 sessions
- Written articles: $5 per 1,000 sessions
- Self-published articles: $8 per 1,000 sessions
- Articles that fail to meet a minimum view count result in **zero compensation**

Half of TheGamer's writers are reportedly in "open revolt" against the new terms. The move was widely characterised as a de facto soft layoff — writers who cannot consistently produce viral content will earn nothing and self-select out.

## Why it matters

1. **Gaming media business model collapse.** Valnet acquired Polygon (from Vox Media) and TheGamer and is now systematically extracting cost. The pay-per-click model eliminates editorial risk for the publisher and transfers it entirely to writers. This is the logical endpoint of the ad-revenue-driven gaming media model: content quality becomes irrelevant, only traffic matters.

2. **Information ecosystem degradation.** Gaming media outlets are the primary channel through which industry news, reviews, and analysis reach developers, publishers, and consumers. As outlets shift to traffic-driven compensation, coverage will skew toward engagement bait over substantive reporting. This affects the quality of information available to the entire industry.

3. **Labour precedent.** If the pay-per-session model proves financially successful for Valnet, other media companies (gaming and otherwise) will adopt it. This would fundamentally restructure freelance journalism economics and accelerate the hollowing out of specialist gaming press.

4. **Contrast with independent outlets.** Game Developer (gamedeveloper.com), Aftermath, and a handful of others maintain traditional editorial models. The gap in coverage quality between these outlets and traffic-farm operations is widening — which in turn affects which sources NBI can rely on for intelligence gathering.

## NBI relevance

Indirect but real. NBI's intelligence pipeline relies on gaming media as a source layer. The degradation of gaming journalism quality affects the reliability of web research cycles. This extract documents a structural shift in the information ecosystem that NBI operates within.
