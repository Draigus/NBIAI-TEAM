# Fortnite Returns to App Store Worldwide -- Epic Declares "Final Battle" with Apple

## Key Content

On 19 May 2026, Epic Games returned Fortnite to the Apple App Store in every country except Australia. This follows the US-only reinstatement in May 2025, which was forced after District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers threatened to require the Apple official overseeing app decisions to appear in court.

Australia is excluded because Epic won its court case there and the court found Apple's developer terms unlawful -- Epic refuses to return "under an illegal payment arrangement" and awaits a court order compelling Apple's compliance.

Tim Sweeney framed the move as strategic provocation, writing on X that it marks "the beginning of the end of the Apple Tax worldwide." Epic's trigger was Apple's own words to the US Supreme Court, where Apple acknowledged that "regulators around the world are watching this case to determine what commission rate Apple may charge on covered purchases in huge markets outside the United States."

## Decisions / Insights

Combined with the Google Play commission drop to 20%, mobile platform economics are being restructured in real time. Apple's 30% commission is increasingly isolated -- Google at 20%, Epic's own store at 12%, and regulatory pressure mounting in EU, UK, Australia, Japan, and Korea. The question is not whether Apple's rate drops, but when and by how much.

For mobile game studios, the prudent move is to model three scenarios: status quo (30%), negotiated reduction (25%), and regulatory-forced reduction (20%). Studios with sufficient scale should explore Epic Games Store distribution on iOS as a hedge.

## Context

The Epic v Apple saga began in August 2020. Six years later, the battle is reaching endgame. The global App Store return is a deliberately provocative move designed to force Apple into defending 30% commissions across every jurisdiction simultaneously.

## Applicability

Relevant to all mobile gaming advisory. Studios should be aware that platform fee reductions are coming and should factor them into 3-5 year financial models. The Australian legal precedent is particularly notable -- courts there have already ruled Apple's terms unlawful, creating a template for other jurisdictions.
