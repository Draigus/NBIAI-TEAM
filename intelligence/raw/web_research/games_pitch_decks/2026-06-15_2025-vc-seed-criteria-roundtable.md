# 2025 VC Seed Criteria Roundtable — LVP, Hiro Capital, 1Up Ventures, Kowloon Nights

## Key Content

The Game Business published a 2025 multi-investor roundtable with named investor quotes on what they are looking for in seed-stage game studio pitches. Participants: Gus Curley (London Venture Partners), Spike Laurie (Hiro Capital), Kelly Wallick (1Up Ventures), Alexis Garavaryan (Kepler/Kowloon Nights), Nick Button-Brown.

**LVP five-pillar evaluation framework (Gus Curley):**
1. LVP Fit — does this align with our thesis (scope, geography, stage)?
2. Market Opportunity — is there a real, sized addressable market?
3. Team — can this team execute at this scope?
4. Product — is there evidence of quality (prototype, demo, previous title)?
5. Financial Opportunity — does the unit economics case work?

**Investor criteria in their own words:**

Gus Curley (LVP): "We're looking for teams that are able to build something, get it out to market and get feedback." Explicit prototype requirement. LVP made 3 studio investments in early 2025. "Scrappy founder profile" preferred over AAA executive backgrounds.

Spike Laurie (Hiro Capital): "If a team comes to me looking for £20-30 million to get to a vertical slice for a AAA game, I'm not the fund for you." Ceiling of $5-15M for initial investments. Lean teams preferred. "Budget-to-scope alignment is the first filter."

Kelly Wallick (1Up Ventures): "There's a little less doom, maybe a little more gloom." Cautious optimism characterisation for 2025. 1Up focuses on underrepresented founders and under-served audience segments.

Alexis Garavaryan (Kowloon Nights): "The most successful teams tend to go for something more modest on their first game." Scope discipline signals execution maturity. First-game scope is interpreted as a signal of the team's self-awareness about what they can ship.

Nick Button-Brown: Distribution plan required alongside creative vision. Audience-building strategy must be in the pitch, not a post-funding afterthought.

## Decisions / Insights

The dominant 2025 consensus is the inversion of the 2021-era model: where studios once raised on decks alone, the current bar across all five investors requires either a working prototype or exceptional pedigree (and even pedigree cases like Midsummer Studios are now closures, not success stories).

The prototype requirement from LVP is particularly notable because LVP is one of the oldest and most respected specialist gaming VCs in Europe. If the benchmark-setter requires a prototype before seed, the market has effectively closed the "vision only" funding window.

The five-pillar framework is the most structured public evaluation rubric available for game studio seed pitches. It maps directly to pitch deck sections:
- Pillar 1 (LVP Fit) → thesis alignment slide
- Pillar 2 (Market Opportunity) → market sizing slide
- Pillar 3 (Team) → team slide
- Pillar 4 (Product) → demo / prototype evidence
- Pillar 5 (Financial Opportunity) → unit economics / ROI model

The gap between this framework and Midsummer's successful 2024 raise without a prototype is the most important signal in this research cycle: the 2024 market allowed exceptional-pedigree exceptions; the 2025 market appears to have closed that exception for all but the most credentialed teams.

## Context

The roundtable directly addresses the question NBI clients most frequently ask: "can we raise before we have a prototype?" The answer from 2025 is: only if your team credentials are at Firaxis/XCOM level AND you are pitching to investors who already know and trust those specific credits. For the overwhelming majority of indie-to-mid-tier studios, a prototype is now a prerequisite, not a nice-to-have.
