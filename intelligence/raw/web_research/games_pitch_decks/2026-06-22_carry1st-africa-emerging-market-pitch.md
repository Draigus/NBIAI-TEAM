# Carry1st — $60M+ for Africa Mobile Publishing: Emerging Market Analogy Pitch + Fintech Layer as TAM Expansion

## Key Content

Carry1st is a South Africa-based mobile games publisher focused on Sub-Saharan Africa. Total raised: ~$60M+ across seed ($4M, 2018-2020), Series A ($6M, 2021), Series A extension ($20M, 2022 led by a16z), pre-Series B ($27M, January 2023 led by BITKRAFT Ventures), and strategic investment from Sony Innovation Fund: Africa (January 2024, undisclosed). Investors include a16z, BITKRAFT, Google, Riot Games, Konvoy Ventures, Nas (rapper), TTV Capital, Alumni Ventures, Lateral Capital, Sky Mavis.

**The core investment thesis in one sentence:** Africa is mobile gaming's pre-Reliance Jio India — massive demographic tailwinds meeting nascent infrastructure, with a first-mover platform positioned to own the market before international publishers realise it exists.

**Pitch narrative structure (from Naavik analysis):**
1. Demographic tailwinds — 1.4B people, youngest median age globally, 186M mobile gamers, 82% of African web traffic via mobile, 9.4% CAGR through 2024
2. Market readiness — 4G adoption accelerating, payments infrastructure improving but still nascent (5-7% electronic transactions vs 50%+ in Turkey)
3. Comparison framing — Africa = India pre-2015 (before Reliance Jio's broadband revolution triggered 2.6B market expansion). Founder Cordel Robbin-Coker: "Africa is home to the largest population of young people in the world, and this upcoming generation will grow up digitally native."
4. Proof of early traction — SpongeBob Krusty Cookoff scaled from 127K to 165K downloads/month post-partnership; CoD Mobile South Africa downloads measurably increased after Carry1st server launch
5. Pay1st: the fintech layer — embedded payment infrastructure for Africa's cash-dominated economy, repositioning the TAM beyond gaming to broader digital commerce (Sea Limited evolution parallel)
6. Platform thesis — three pillars: hyper-local distribution and marketing, Pay1st monetisation-as-a-service, in-house content development

**Investor class curation (deliberate, not accidental):**
- Gaming-focused VCs (Konvoy, BITKRAFT): validated the gaming thesis
- Pan-African funds (Africa Investment Fund, Avenir, Lateral Capital): endorsed the regional opportunity
- Tech giants (Google, Riot Games): signalled distribution and infrastructure value
- Strategic (Sony Innovation Fund: Africa): added platform credibility
- Celebrity angle (Nas): created media reach and community signal

David Haber, a16z General Partner: "We see immense opportunity for the company to mirror outstanding successes we've seen in markets like India, China, and Southeast Asia."

## Decisions / Insights

The Carry1st pitch is structurally different from every other mobile pitch in this bank: it is a **market-creation play**, not a market-capture play. The deck was not arguing "we will take share from Supercell in Africa." It was arguing "this market does not yet exist at scale, we are building the infrastructure to create it, and the returns will mirror Sea Limited's trajectory in SEA."

The **Sea Limited parallel** is the most powerful element: Sea Ltd started as Garena (games/e-commerce in Southeast Asia), grew into Shopee (the dominant SEA e-commerce platform), then expanded into SeaMoney (digital payments). Carry1st's trajectory — publisher → Pay1st → potential commerce platform — maps directly onto that sequence. This expanded the VC-addressable TAM from $250-750M (Africa gaming) to something far larger.

The **two-sided investor validation approach** is notable: game VCs (Konvoy, BITKRAFT) validated gaming competence; Pan-African funds validated regional expertise. Neither set of investors needed to take both risks simultaneously. Carry1st had already secured validation from each specialist before asking the other to participate.

**The 34% payer conversion rate cited in pitch materials drew scepticism in the Naavik analysis** — this figure is implausibly high for African markets where electronic payment penetration is 5-7%. The lesson: when pitching emerging markets with limited comparable data, aggressive metric claims invite more scrutiny than moderated claims with clear methodology.

## Context

Africa's total gaming market was ~$1.8B in 2024 (per Carry1st's own reporting). The pitch was made well before this number was public; the Series A extension ($20M, 2022) was based on a $250-750M estimated total market. The market-creation argument was both the strength and the risk: no comparable exit existed to anchor VC return models.

The Sony Investment Fund: Africa participation in January 2024 added a platform credibility layer that previous rounds lacked — Sony's involvement validated that a major publisher believed Africa's market was worth investing in beyond Carry1st's own claims.

## Applicability

**For NBI:** The Carry1st structure is the most transferable framework for any NBI client pitching an underserved regional opportunity (MENA, SEA, LATAM). Key lessons:
1. The emerging market analogy is the most powerful framing device — find a prior success story that maps the same trajectory (Garena/SEA, Reliance Jio/India) and position your market as that story at an earlier stage
2. Fintech or infrastructure layers dramatically expand the VC-addressable TAM beyond gaming alone — if a studio builds payments, localisation, or platform infrastructure for its market, the pitch becomes a market-creation play rather than a genre competition
3. Investor class curation is a deliberate design choice — Carry1st assembled investors who each validated one dimension (gaming, regional, distribution) rather than asking a single VC to accept all risks
4. Avoid overreach on early metrics — the 34% payer conversion figure created credibility risk in a data-thin market
