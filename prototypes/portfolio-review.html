Product Management Review
Strategic value & decision support
6/10
CRITICAL
The page answers "what's the status?" but not "what should I do?"
The current layout is descriptive but not prescriptive. A portfolio page for an MD running 6 client engagements needs to answer three questions in order: (1) Is anything on fire? (2) Which client needs me right now? (3) Am I on track overall? Currently, answering question 2 requires scanning the sidebar cards and then cross-referencing with the Needs Attention panel at the bottom. Two separate zones for one question.
CRITICAL
No sortable portfolio table
Every best-in-class tool (Monday.com, Asana, Smartsheet) centres on a table with one row per client, sortable by health, progress, or risk. WorkSage uses client cards in a sidebar. This means you cannot rank clients by severity, cannot scan a column of health indicators, and cannot see team allocation per client without clicking into each one.
MAJOR
Milestones disconnected from client context
Milestones appear in a separate panel. To answer "what's due for Lighthouse Games?" you look at the sidebar card, then scan the milestones panel to find matching entries. Monday.com and Productive.io show milestones inline per client row, answering the question without leaving the row.
MAJOR
No "key risk" indicator per client
The sidebar cards show counts (active, overdue, blocked) but there is no single-line risk summary. Productive.io's "budgets with lowest profit" widget pattern works because it answers "what's the one thing I should know about this client?" in a glance. The current page requires reading multiple stats and mentally computing risk.
STRENGTH
KPI strip with week-over-week deltas exists
The 6-card KPI strip at the top with comparison to last week's snapshot is genuinely useful and ahead of several competitors. This should be kept and refined with sparkline trends.

UX Research Assessment
Scan patterns, cognitive load & readability
4/10
CRITICAL
7 competing zones break the F-pattern
Research shows users scan dashboards in an F-pattern: left-to-right across the top (KPI strip), then down the left column (client names), then selectively right (status columns). The current page's left column is a sidebar of cards which works for the downward scan, but the content to the right is a 2×2 panel grid. The eye cannot do the selective rightward scan that the F-pattern depends on. Result: users bounce between zones rather than flowing through the data.
CRITICAL
RAG signal is buried
Every best-in-class tool puts a coloured health indicator in a vertical column so you can scan client health in under 2 seconds. The current page uses coloured left-borders on sidebar cards (4px wide, easy to miss) and requires reading text labels ("overdue", "at risk", "blocked") to understand the status. That's reading, not scanning. The eye processes colour 200ms before it reads text — the current design wastes that advantage.
CRITICAL
Progress Status donut wastes 25% of viewport
The Progress Status panel uses a full 2×2 grid quadrant for a hand-rolled SVG with a backlog bar and a donut chart. It displays 5 numbers (backlog, completed, in progress, planning, waiting). Those 5 numbers could be a single KPI card or an inline legend, freeing 25% of the main content area for actionable information. Information density: approximately 5 data points per 400×400px. That is extremely low.
MAJOR
KPI cards lack trend context
The current strip shows numbers with week-over-week deltas (e.g., "+2 vs last week"), which is good. But there is no sparkline or micro-chart showing the trajectory. A number going up from 1 to 3 feels different from a number that has been oscillating between 2–4 for weeks. Nielsen Norman Group research confirms that numbers without visual trend context force users to rely on memory, which degrades decision quality.
MAJOR
Sidebar locks 240px permanently
The client sidebar takes 240px of horizontal space on every screen size. This forces the content panels into a narrow area and creates a two-axis reading pattern: scan down the sidebar, then scan across the panels. Every competitor uses a table or card grid instead, which gives full-width rows and a single-axis scan pattern.
STRENGTH
Project Timeline (mini-Gantt) is well-built
The timeline panel with collapsible client groups, health-coloured bars, progress fills, and a TODAY marker is a strong implementation. The client collapse/expand with summary dots in the header is a good progressive disclosure pattern. This should be preserved and given more visual prominence.

Application Design Review
Visual quality, consistency & craft
5/10
CRITICAL
No visual hierarchy between panels
All panels share the same background colour, border treatment, and padding. The Progress Status donut, the Milestones list, the Timeline, and the Needs Attention panel all look equally important. When everything has equal visual weight, nothing is the hero. The most critical information (blocked items, client health) gets the same visual treatment as supporting context (work types, completing soon).
MAJOR
Chart variety is low
The page uses a donut chart, horizontal bars, and a Gantt timeline. All three reference dashboards that score well visually use a mix of ring gauges, area charts, bar charts, and at least one unique/distinctive visualisation. The current donut is functional but generic. There is no visual element that makes this dashboard feel premium or distinctive.
MAJOR
Text sizing too small across the board
Panel titles at 0.82rem (13px), stat labels at 0.65rem (10px), attention badges at 0.52rem (8px). For a dashboard that needs to be read in 30 seconds by someone scanning across a desk, minimum readable size should be 13px with body content at 15–16px. Several elements fall below the threshold for comfortable reading at arm's length.
MAJOR
No depth or elevation system
Cards are flat rectangles with 1px borders on a flat background. There is no shadow, no layering, no sense that panels are physically present on the surface. Best-in-class dashboards (reference: Neumorphism UI pattern, Shutterstock dashboard concepts) use elevation through shadows and semi-transparency to create a tactile, premium feel.
STRENGTH
Colour system is correct
Green/yellow/red/purple for health states, blue accent for interactive elements, consistent across all components. The CSS variable system supports 5 themes and the colour semantics are universally understood. This is a solid foundation to build visual polish on top of.
Competitive Benchmark
Monday.com
Portfolio table as the hero. Owner, Health, Progress columns locked (cannot remove). AI-powered Risk Insights. 50+ widget types.
KEY PATTERN: LOCKED COLUMNS
Asana
Status dots in a scannable column. Written narrative updates per project. Timeline view alongside list view. Universal Reporting for cross-portfolio.
KEY PATTERN: NARRATIVE STATUS
Smartsheet
Executive vs operational dashboard templates. Widget-based freeform layout. Portfolio rollups auto-include new projects. Quick links for drill-down.
KEY PATTERN: AUDIENCE-SPECIFIC VIEWS
Productive.io
"Budgets with lowest profit" widget answers "where am I losing money?" instantly. Forecasting charts. Margin by client as a first-class view.
KEY PATTERN: WORST-FIRST RANKING
Teamwork.com
Retainer view for agencies: monthly scope, hour budgets, deliverables. Profitability integrates with time tracking. "Scan and act" design.
KEY PATTERN: SCAN AND ACT
Harvest Forecast
Single horizontal timeline with colour blocks per person. Gaps = availability. Over-allocation shown as overlap. One thing done exceptionally well.
KEY PATTERN: VISUAL SIMPLICITY

Design Principles for the Rebuild
These principles guided the v5 prototype and should carry forward into production implementation.
01
Neutral by default, colour only for exceptions
The page should feel calm. Green means no colour needed — it is the absence of a problem. Only amber and red carry colour. When everything is coloured, nothing stands out. The accent cyan is for interactive elements and data, not status.
02
Table is the hero, charts are supporting cast
The portfolio table answers "which client, what status, what risk" in one scannable structure. Charts provide trend context and visual interest but do not compete with the table for primacy. Progressive disclosure: click a client row to expand detail.
03
Physical presence through neumorphic depth
Cards are raised objects on a surface, not flat rectangles. Dual shadows (dark bottom-right, subtle light top-left) create tactile depth. Inset wells for inputs and tracks. Press animation on click. The dashboard should feel like something you could touch.
04
30-second comprehension or it has failed
The entire portfolio state must be graspable in 30 seconds of scanning. 5 seconds for the KPIs. 15 seconds for the client table. 10 seconds for the attention items. If it takes longer, the hierarchy is wrong. Text minimum 13px, KPI values at 22px+.
05
Worst-first, always
The client table is sorted by health (red first, then amber, then green). The Needs Attention panel ranks blocked above overdue, overdue above at-risk. The dashboard exists to surface problems, not celebrate the status quo. Inactive/complete clients are greyed out at the bottom.
06
Every interaction has feedback
Hover lifts cards with shadow intensification. Click compresses with inset shadow. Progress bars animate on load. Ring gauges fill on load. The hero chart draws its line on load. Hover on data points reveals values. The dashboard feels alive, not static.
