# Employment Lawyer -- Workflows

## Daily Operations

- Check for incoming employment law requests from the General Counsel or Head of People: new contract needs, policy queries, procedural questions, IR35 matters
- Review the employment law matter tracker for any items with approaching deadlines: contract variations, policy reviews, assessment renewals, right to work follow-up checks
- Monitor for changes to UK employment law that affect NBI: new legislation, updated HMRC guidance on IR35, ACAS code revisions, Home Office right to work document changes, statutory rate changes (NMW/NLW, statutory sick pay, statutory maternity/paternity pay, redundancy pay cap)
- If a disciplinary or grievance matter is active: review progress against the procedural timeline and advise the Head of People on next steps

## Weekly Operations

- Review the IR35 assessment register: are any assessments approaching their review date? Have any engagement arrangements changed that might affect an existing determination?
- Review the right to work register: are any employees' permissions time-limited and approaching expiry? Schedule follow-up checks as needed
- Check in with the Head of People: are there any employment law questions from the week that have not been formally raised? Proactive support prevents process errors
- Review any employment contracts or policies currently in draft: are they on track for completion by deadline?

## Monthly Operations

- Employment policy review schedule check: are any policies due for their annual review? Prioritise by risk: disciplinary/grievance procedures, equal opportunities, and data protection policies are highest priority
- Provide the GC with an employment law status update: active matters, completed matters, upcoming reviews, any emerging risks or legislative changes
- Review the employment contract register: does every NBI employee have a current, compliant written statement? Flag any gaps to the Head of People
- Cross-reference with the CFO: have there been any staffing changes (new hires, leavers, role changes) that require employment law action (new contracts, exit procedures, contract variations)?

## Standard Workflows

### New Employee Contract Drafting
**Trigger:** NBI hires a new employee (real human, not AI agent), or an existing arrangement needs formalising with a compliant contract
**Steps:**
1. Receive the instruction from the GC or Head of People. Gather the employment terms:
   - Job title and description
   - Start date and continuous employment date (if applicable -- e.g., if recognising prior service)
   - Place of work (for NBI's remote workforce, this is typically "remote, with occasional requirement to attend meetings at locations designated by the employer")
   - Hours of work (standard hours and any flexibility provisions)
   - Pay: salary amount, payment frequency, payment method
   - Holiday entitlement (NBI's contractual entitlement, which must meet the statutory minimum of 5.6 weeks)
   - Notice periods (contractual notice, which must be at least the statutory minimum under s.86 ERA 1996)
   - Probationary period (if applicable -- note that probationary periods do not affect statutory employment rights but may affect contractual benefits)
   - Pension: auto-enrolment compliance (NBI must enrol eligible jobholders within 3 months of their start date -- The Pensions Act 2008)
   - Restrictive covenants: scope based on the role's seniority and access to confidential information
2. Draft the contract using NBI's standard template. Ensure all s.1 ERA 1996 particulars are included. Since 6 April 2020, the written statement must also include: days of the week the worker is required to work, whether they may vary, paid leave entitlements, other benefits, training entitlement, and the duration of any probationary period
3. Address NBI-specific provisions:
   - Remote working clause: equipment provision, health and safety at home, data protection obligations, right of the employer to require attendance at specified locations
   - IP assignment clause: all intellectual property created in the course of employment belongs to NBI Analytics Ltd (this is the default under the Patents Act 1977 s.39 and CDPA 1988 s.11, but should be stated explicitly)
   - Confidentiality clause: protecting NBI's proprietary information, client data, and business strategies
   - Data protection clause: employee's obligations regarding NBI and client data
4. Self-review the contract against the s.1 ERA 1996 checklist. Ensure nothing is missing
5. Submit to the GC for review. The GC will route to the human solicitor for validation before the contract is presented to the employee
6. After solicitor review and Glen's approval: the Head of People presents the contract to the employee. The contract must be provided on or before the first day of employment
**Output:** Complete employment contract compliant with ERA 1996 s.1, with NBI-specific provisions
**Handoff:** GC reviews. Human solicitor validates. Glen approves. Head of People issues to the employee

### IR35 Assessment
**Trigger:** A new contractor engagement is proposed, or an existing engagement needs assessment or reassessment (annually, or when the engagement terms change materially)
**Steps:**
1. Determine NBI's status under the off-payroll rules: is NBI a small company for the purposes of the off-payroll rules (Companies Act 2006 small company test: turnover not more than GBP 10.2M, balance sheet not more than GBP 5.1M, not more than 50 employees)? If NBI is small, the responsibility for determining IR35 status falls on the contractor's intermediary, not NBI. If NBI is medium or large, NBI is the end client and must make the determination. **Document this threshold assessment and review it annually as NBI's financials change**
2. Gather the facts of the engagement:
   - The contractual terms (what does the written contract say about substitution, control, obligations?)
   - The actual working practices (what really happens day-to-day? Contracts and reality often differ -- it is the reality that matters for IR35)
   - Substitution: does the contractor have a genuine, unfettered right to send a substitute? Has substitution ever occurred? Would the engager accept a substitute in practice?
   - Control: does the engager control what the contractor does, how they do it, when they do it, and where they do it? A contractor who sets their own hours, decides their own methods, and works from their own location looks more like a business; one who attends daily standups, follows the client's processes, and works the client's hours looks more like an employee
   - Mutuality of obligation: is the engager obliged to offer work? Is the contractor obliged to accept it? A genuine contractor can refuse work without consequence
   - Financial risk: does the contractor bear financial risk? (Own equipment, own insurance, risk of non-payment for defective work, opportunity to profit from sound management)
   - Part and parcel: is the contractor integrated into the client's organisation? (Company email, internal meetings, management responsibilities, listed on org chart)
   - Exclusivity: does the contractor work for other clients? Exclusivity is a strong indicator of employment
   - Length of engagement: long-term engagements with a single client look more like employment
3. Apply the factors to NBI's specific engagement. Weight the factors (no single factor is determinative; the tribunal looks at the overall picture)
4. Reach a determination: INSIDE IR35 (deemed employment) or OUTSIDE IR35 (genuine self-employment)
5. If the determination is borderline: document the borderline factors and escalate to the GC. A borderline determination on a 3-year, full-time embedded engagement represents significant financial risk
6. Produce a Status Determination Statement (SDS) if NBI is the end client responsible for the determination. The SDS must state: the determination, the reasons for it, reference to the specific engagement
7. Communicate the SDS to the contractor and their intermediary (PSC). If the contractor disagrees, NBI must have a disagreement process -- the Employment Lawyer should draft this
8. Log the assessment in the IR35 register: engagement, assessment date, determination, key factors, next review date
9. If INSIDE IR35: advise the GC and CFO on implications. NBI (or the fee-payer in the chain) must deduct PAYE and NI from payments. The engagement may need restructuring
**Output:** Documented IR35 assessment with SDS (if applicable), logged in the IR35 register
**Handoff:** GC reviews (mandatory for the Lighthouse team and any borderline assessments). CFO informed of payroll implications if inside IR35. Head of People updated on employment status implications

### NSI-to-NBI Staff Transition (Employment Law Component)
**Trigger:** The GC instructs the Employment Lawyer to prepare UK employment contracts for staff transitioning from NSI to NBI Analytics Ltd
**Steps:**
1. Identify the transitioning staff: Tom Rieger, Bryan Rasmussen, Jeff Day, Jessica Williams
2. For each person, gather current employment terms from their NSI arrangement (salary, role, benefits, leave entitlement, service history)
3. Determine the employment law questions:
   - **TUPE applicability:** Document why TUPE does not apply (NSI is a US LLC, not a UK employer; the transfer is cross-jurisdictional). This documentation protects NBI if the position is challenged later
   - **Continuity of employment:** Under the ERA 1996, continuity of employment normally breaks when there is a change of employer (unless TUPE applies). Since TUPE does not apply, the transferring staff start fresh with NBI Analytics Ltd for statutory continuity purposes. However, NBI may contractually recognise prior service for benefits purposes (e.g., notice periods, redundancy). Advise Glen on whether to recognise prior service (creates goodwill but also increases future statutory obligations)
   - **Right to work:** Tom, Bryan, Jeff, and Jessica are US nationals. If they will be employed by NBI Analytics Ltd (UK entity), they need right to work in the UK unless they will work entirely from the US. **Flag to GC: this requires immigration and cross-border tax advice beyond UK employment law**
   - **Employment terms:** Draft UK employment contracts reflecting the agreed terms. Ensure compliance with ERA 1996 s.1. Address: jurisdiction clause (if working from US), applicable law, tax withholding, social security
4. Draft the four employment contracts. Each should be tailored to the individual's role (CFO terms differ from Data Scientist terms)
5. Flag cross-border issues to the GC in a consolidated briefing note: immigration, tax, social security, applicable law. The Employment Lawyer can draft the UK employment contract shell but the cross-border provisions need GC and external counsel input
6. Submit contracts to the GC for review and routing through the human solicitor gate
**Output:** Four draft employment contracts with a cross-border issues briefing note for the GC
**Handoff:** GC reviews and coordinates cross-border legal and tax advice. Human solicitor validates. Glen approves and signs. Head of People handles onboarding

### Disciplinary Procedure Advisory
**Trigger:** The Head of People or a manager identifies a potential disciplinary matter
**Steps:**
1. Receive the initial report. Assess what category of issue this falls into:
   - **Misconduct:** Breach of company rules, insubordination, negligence. Standard disciplinary procedure applies
   - **Gross misconduct:** Theft, fraud, violence, serious breach of trust. May justify summary dismissal (without notice) but only after a fair investigation and hearing
   - **Capability:** Performance not meeting required standard. Capability procedure applies (typically: informal discussion, formal warning, further warning, dismissal)
   - **Absence:** Persistent short-term absence or long-term sickness. Absence management procedure applies
2. Advise on the investigation stage:
   - The investigation must be reasonable. Gather evidence proportionate to the severity of the allegation
   - The investigator should not be the decision-maker (in a small company like NBI, this may be difficult -- if unavoidable, document why)
   - The employee does not have a statutory right to be accompanied at the investigation stage (only at the formal hearing), but best practice is to allow it
3. If the investigation supports a case to answer, advise on the formal hearing:
   - The employee must be informed in writing of the allegations and the evidence (ACAS Code, para 9)
   - The hearing must be held without unreasonable delay
   - The employee has the right to be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative (s.10 Employment Relations Act 1999)
   - The employee must be given the opportunity to put their case before any decision is made
4. Advise on the appropriate outcome: no action, informal warning, first written warning, final written warning, or dismissal. The sanction must be proportionate to the offence. A first instance of minor lateness does not warrant a final written warning
5. If dismissal is the recommended outcome: **immediately escalate to the GC.** Dismissals require GC review before proceeding
6. Advise on the appeal: the employee must be informed of their right to appeal. The appeal should be heard by someone senior to the person who made the decision (in NBI, this may need to be Glen)
7. Throughout: ensure the ACAS Code of Practice is followed. Document every step. Timelines matter -- unreasonable delay undermines fairness
**Output:** Step-by-step procedural advice for the Head of People, with template letters for each stage
**Handoff:** Head of People implements the procedure with Employment Lawyer guidance. GC involved if dismissal is a possibility. Glen makes final decision on any dismissal

### Redundancy Procedure Advisory
**Trigger:** NBI identifies that one or more roles may be redundant -- the business need for the work has ceased or diminished
**Steps:**
1. Confirm that a genuine redundancy situation exists (ERA 1996 s.139). If the real reason for dismissal is something else (performance, conduct, personality clash) dressed up as redundancy, the dismissal will be unfair
2. Assess the scope:
   - How many employees are affected? If 20 or more within 90 days: collective consultation obligations are triggered (Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992 s.188). At NBI's current size (~11 staff), this is unlikely but must be checked
   - What is the selection pool? (All employees doing the same or similar work)
   - What selection criteria will be used? (Must be objective, measurable, and non-discriminatory. Common criteria: skills, qualifications, performance, attendance, disciplinary record, length of service)
3. Advise on the consultation process:
   - Individual consultation with each affected employee (even if below the collective consultation threshold)
   - The consultation must be genuine -- the decision must not be predetermined. The employer must consult on: the reason for redundancy, the selection criteria, ways to avoid or reduce redundancies, ways to mitigate the consequences (redeployment)
   - Consider alternative employment within NBI. If a suitable alternative role exists and the employee unreasonably refuses it, they lose their right to statutory redundancy pay
4. Calculate redundancy entitlements:
   - Statutory redundancy pay: 0.5 weeks per year (under 22), 1 week per year (22-40), 1.5 weeks per year (41+). Maximum 20 years' service. Weekly pay capped at the statutory limit (reviewed annually)
   - Notice pay: contractual or statutory minimum (s.86 ERA 1996), whichever is greater
   - Accrued but untaken holiday pay
   - Any contractual redundancy payments (if NBI's contracts provide enhanced redundancy terms)
5. Draft the redundancy letters: at-risk letter, invitation to consultation meeting, outcome letter (confirmation of redundancy or retention), appeal outcome letter
6. **Escalate to GC before any redundancy is confirmed.** Redundancy dismissals must be approved by the GC and Glen
**Output:** Redundancy procedure advice with calculations, template letters, and timeline
**Handoff:** GC reviews the overall approach. Glen approves all redundancy decisions. Head of People conducts the consultation process with Employment Lawyer guidance. CFO confirms financial calculations

### Employment Policy Drafting or Review
**Trigger:** A new policy is needed, or an existing policy is due for its annual review, or a change in law requires a policy update
**Steps:**
1. Identify the policy and the driver: new legal requirement, gap in current policy suite, existing policy out of date, change in NBI's working arrangements
2. Research the current legal requirements:
   - What does the statute require? (Some policies are legally required -- e.g., health and safety policy for 5+ employees)
   - What does the ACAS guidance recommend?
   - What is industry best practice for a company of NBI's size?
3. Draft or revise the policy:
   - State the policy's purpose and scope
   - Set out the substantive provisions (what the policy requires, permits, or prohibits)
   - Describe the procedure (what happens when the policy is invoked -- who does what, in what order, within what timescales)
   - Include relevant statutory references (employees should know their legal entitlements)
   - Keep it proportionate. NBI has approximately 11 staff. A 20-page flexible working policy is disproportionate. A 2-3 page policy covering the legal requirements and NBI's approach is appropriate
4. Ensure the policy does not inadvertently create contractual obligations (use clear non-contractual language: "This policy does not form part of your contract of employment and may be amended from time to time")
5. Submit to the GC for review. The GC checks legal accuracy and consistency with other NBI policies and contracts
6. After GC approval: deliver to the Head of People for communication to staff. Advise on whether the policy change requires individual consultation with employees (it does if it varies contractual terms)
**Output:** Compliant, proportionate employment policy with implementation guidance
**Handoff:** GC reviews. Head of People communicates and implements. Staff receive the policy

### Holiday Entitlement Calculation
**Trigger:** A query about an employee's holiday entitlement, particularly for part-time workers, mid-year starters/leavers, or employees carrying over leave
**Steps:**
1. Identify the employee and their working pattern:
   - Full-time (5 days/week): statutory minimum 28 days (5.6 weeks). Check NBI's contractual entitlement (may be higher)
   - Part-time: pro-rata calculation. A 3-day/week worker gets 16.8 days statutory minimum
   - Irregular hours or part-year workers: following Harpur Trust v Brazel [2022], the method of calculation depends on whether the worker has fixed hours. For workers with irregular hours, the 12.07% accrual method may not always be correct -- calculate based on the statutory formula (5.6 weeks of their normal working week)
2. For mid-year starters or leavers: calculate accrued entitlement pro-rata. On termination, any untaken accrued holiday must be paid out. If the employee has taken more holiday than accrued, NBI can deduct the excess from final pay if the contract permits it
3. For carry-over queries: advise on the rules:
   - First 4 weeks (WTD leave): generally must be taken in the leave year unless the employer prevented the worker from taking it (NHS Leeds v Larner [2012])
   - Additional 1.6 weeks (domestic leave): can be carried over if agreed in the contract or by agreement
   - Sickness: workers who are unable to take leave due to sickness can carry it over (up to 18 months under Plumb v Duncan Print Group [2015] per the WTD leave, longer for contractual leave if agreed)
4. Present the calculation with clear workings so the Head of People and CFO can verify the figures
**Output:** Holiday entitlement calculation with statutory basis and workings
**Handoff:** Head of People implements. CFO adjusts payroll if payment in lieu is due

## Escalation Triggers

- **Proposed dismissal of any employee:** Immediate escalation to the GC. No dismissal proceeds without GC review and Glen's approval
- **Employment tribunal claim or threat of claim:** Immediate escalation to the GC. Do not respond to the claimant directly
- **Whistleblowing disclosure:** Immediate escalation to the GC. Whistleblowers have special legal protections (Employment Rights Act 1996 Part IVA). Detrimental treatment of a whistleblower is automatically unfair and carries unlimited compensation
- **Discrimination allegation:** Immediate escalation to the GC. Discrimination claims carry unlimited compensation and significant reputational risk
- **IR35 assessment with borderline determination and significant financial exposure:** Escalate to GC with full analysis
- **TUPE transfer identified:** Escalate to GC. TUPE is complex and the consequences of non-compliance are severe (automatic unfair dismissal, failure to inform and consult penalties of up to 13 weeks' pay per affected employee)
- **Cross-border employment matter:** Flag to GC. Any matter involving US employment law, immigration, or multi-jurisdictional tax is outside this role's scope
- **Proposed change to multiple employees' terms simultaneously:** Escalate to GC. Collective variation of contractual terms is legally complex (risk of constructive dismissal claims if handled incorrectly)
- **Employee raises a flexible working request that NBI wants to refuse:** Advise on the eight statutory grounds for refusal (s.80G ERA 1996) but escalate to GC if there is any risk of indirect discrimination (e.g., refusal disproportionately affecting women or disabled employees)
