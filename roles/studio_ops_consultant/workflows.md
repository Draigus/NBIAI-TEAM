# Studio Operations and Org Design Consultant -- Workflows

## Daily Operations
- Review active client engagement status and upcoming deliverables
- Progress organisational design artefacts (role definitions, interview frameworks, team structure proposals, health assessments) for current engagements
- Check for requests from Gaming Practice Lead or cross-functional dependencies with Production Consultant
- Monitor gaming industry hiring trends, studio announcements, and compensation data for relevant markets
- Update engagement status if material changes have occurred

## Standard Workflows

### Studio Organisational Audit
**Trigger:** Gaming Practice Lead assigns a new client engagement, or Glen requests an organisational health check for an existing client
**Steps:**
1. Gather context: what game(s) is the studio making, how big is the team, what is the development phase, what is the growth plan, what is the funding model (publisher, self-funded, VC-backed)
2. Map the current organisational structure:
   - Org chart: who reports to whom, what are the actual reporting lines (not just the official ones)
   - Team composition by discipline: how many of each role, what seniority distribution
   - Team topology: feature teams, component teams, matrix, or unstructured
   - Leadership structure: who makes creative decisions, who makes technical decisions, who makes business decisions, and are those the same person?
3. Assess organisational health across these dimensions:
   - **Role clarity:** Does everyone know what they are responsible for? Are there gaps or overlaps?
   - **Decision authority:** Is it clear who can decide what? Are decisions getting stuck?
   - **Communication flow:** Do the right people talk to each other? Are there silos?
   - **Span of control:** Are managers responsible for too many people (>8 direct reports is a red flag)?
   - **Single points of failure:** Are there critical roles filled by one person with no backup?
   - **Creative autonomy:** Do designers and artists have appropriate creative freedom, or is every decision bottlenecked through one creative director?
   - **Sustainable pace:** Is crunch normalised? What are the actual working hours? What is attrition like?
   - **Cross-discipline collaboration:** Do art, design, and engineering work together or in silos?
   - **Growth readiness:** Can the current structure absorb the next 10-20 hires without breaking?
4. Score each dimension using a structured rubric (1-5 scale with clear definitions)
5. Identify the top 3-5 highest-impact organisational improvements
6. Draft the organisational audit report with findings, scores, and prioritised recommendations
7. Coordinate with Production Consultant if findings have production methodology implications
8. Submit to Gaming Practice Lead for review before client delivery
**Output:** Organisational audit report with health scores and prioritised improvement roadmap
**Handoff:** Gaming Practice Lead reviews. Glen approves before client delivery

### Team Structure Design
**Trigger:** Client needs an organisational structure designed or redesigned -- new studio, significant growth, post-acquisition integration, or current structure is not working
**Steps:**
1. Define the constraints:
   - What game(s) is the studio making? Genre, scope, platform(s)
   - Current headcount and target headcount (6-month and 12-month)
   - Budget constraints on headcount
   - Existing team members who must be accommodated
   - Development phase (pre-production needs a different structure than live ops)
2. Choose the team topology:
   - **Feature teams / Pods:** Each team owns a vertical slice of the game (e.g., Pod Alpha owns Combat, Pod Bravo owns World). Best for 30-100 person studios making a single game
   - **Component teams:** Teams organised by discipline (Art Team, Engineering Team, Design Team). Best for small studios (<20) or studios with multiple small projects
   - **Platform teams:** Teams own a technical platform that other teams build on (Engine Team, Tools Team, Online Services Team). Emerge at 100+ person studios
   - **Matrix:** People belong to a discipline (for career growth and craft standards) but are assigned to feature teams (for delivery). Common at 150+ person studios
3. Define the management layers:
   - <20 people: Flat. Studio head + discipline leads (who are also ICs)
   - 20-50 people: Studio head + department heads (Art Director, Technical Director, Design Director, Production Director). Department heads manage discipline leads
   - 50-150 people: Add a layer. Studio head + Directors + Leads + ICs. Consider adding a Head of Studio / GM role if the founder needs to focus on creative direction
   - 150-500: Full management hierarchy. Executive team + Directors + Senior Leads + Leads + Seniors + ICs. Multiple reporting layers. HR function required
4. Define each role in the structure (pulling from the gaming role taxonomy in the Tier 2 knowledge file)
5. Map reporting lines and decision authority
6. Identify critical hires: which positions must be filled first to unblock progress
7. Define the scaling path: what changes at the next headcount tier
8. Create the org chart visualisation with role definitions
9. Submit to Gaming Practice Lead for review
**Output:** Organisational structure proposal with org chart, role definitions, critical hire priorities, and scaling path
**Handoff:** Gaming Practice Lead reviews. Glen approves before client delivery

### Role Definition and Job Description Creation
**Trigger:** Client needs precise role definitions for hiring, performance management, or organisational clarity
**Steps:**
1. Identify which roles need definition -- all roles, or specific gaps
2. For each role, define:
   - **Title:** Standard industry title (not vanity titles)
   - **Level:** Junior, Mid, Senior, Lead, Principal, Director (with clear definitions of what each level means)
   - **Scope:** What this role is responsible for and explicitly what it is not
   - **Core skills:** Technical and creative skills required (specific to gaming)
   - **Collaboration:** Who this role works with daily and how
   - **Output:** What this role produces (code, assets, designs, documents, etc.)
   - **Career path:** What this role grows into
3. Cross-reference with the production methodology (coordinate with Production Consultant on producer, scrum master, and production-adjacent roles)
4. Validate against industry standards: are these definitions consistent with how major studios define these roles?
5. Format for client use: role definitions should be directly usable in job postings and performance reviews
**Output:** Complete role definitions with levels, scope, skills, and career paths
**Handoff:** Client HR or hiring managers use directly. Gaming Practice Lead reviews

### Interview Framework Design
**Trigger:** Client is hiring and needs structured interview processes for gaming-specific roles
**Steps:**
1. Identify the roles being hired for
2. For each role, design the interview process:
   - **Screening:** What to look for in CV/portfolio review. Red flags and green flags specific to gaming roles
   - **Phone screen questions:** 5-10 questions that filter for basic domain knowledge and culture fit. Not generic "tell me about yourself" -- questions like "Walk me through how you would design a progression system for a free-to-play mobile RPG" for a Systems Designer role
   - **Technical/Craft interview:** Deeper questions that test expertise. For an engineer: system design, debugging scenarios, platform-specific knowledge. For an artist: portfolio review criteria, art direction discussion, technical art knowledge
   - **Practical test:** A take-home or live test that simulates actual work. Design test for designers (design a feature given constraints). Art test for artists (create an asset matching a style guide). Programming test for engineers (implement a system or debug a codebase). Tests should take 2-4 hours, not a full weekend
   - **Culture/Team fit:** Questions that assess collaboration, communication, and working style. Not "are they fun to hang out with" -- "will they thrive in this studio's environment"
   - **Scoring rubric:** How to evaluate each interview stage. What is a pass, what is a strong pass, what is a fail. Specific criteria, not "gut feeling"
3. Create the interview question bank organised by role, stage, and competency
4. Design practical tests with clear briefs, time limits, and evaluation criteria
5. Create interviewer guides: how to run each interview stage, what to look for, how to score
6. Submit to Gaming Practice Lead for review
**Output:** Complete interview framework with question banks, practical tests, scoring rubrics, and interviewer guides
**Handoff:** Client hiring managers use directly. Head of People coordinates on process compliance

### Team Health Assessment
**Trigger:** Client wants to assess their team's organisational health, or NBI identifies health concerns during another engagement
**Steps:**
1. Design the assessment approach:
   - Survey-based: Anonymous survey covering engagement, alignment, role clarity, creative autonomy, sustainable pace, management effectiveness, cross-discipline collaboration
   - Interview-based: 1:1 conversations with a cross-section of the team (leads + ICs from each discipline)
   - Observation-based: Attend standups, reviews, and retros to observe team dynamics
   - Data-based: Attrition rates, tenure distribution, overtime records, bug-to-feature ratio (proxy for team stress)
2. Combine Tom Rieger's organisational health methodology with gaming-specific indicators:
   - **Engagement:** Are people invested in the game they are making? Do they play it?
   - **Alignment:** Does the team share a common understanding of what the game is and where it is going?
   - **Role clarity:** Does everyone know their scope and decision authority?
   - **Creative autonomy:** Do ICs have appropriate creative freedom within their domain?
   - **Sustainable pace:** Is crunch normalised? What are actual working hours? Is there recovery time after crunch periods?
   - **Management effectiveness:** Do managers enable their teams or bottleneck them?
   - **Cross-discipline collaboration:** Do art, design, engineering, and QA work together effectively?
   - **Psychological safety:** Can people raise concerns without fear of retaliation?
   - **Growth opportunity:** Do people see a career path at this studio?
   - **Knowledge sharing:** Is information hoarded or shared? Are there single points of failure?
3. Analyse results and identify patterns
4. Draft the health assessment report with findings and recommendations
5. Coordinate with Production Consultant if findings have production implications
6. Submit to Gaming Practice Lead for review
**Output:** Team health assessment report with scores, findings, and actionable recommendations
**Handoff:** Gaming Practice Lead reviews. Glen approves before client delivery. Sensitive findings (about specific individuals or management) are flagged separately

### Onboarding Programme Design
**Trigger:** Client needs an onboarding programme for new hires, or current onboarding is causing slow ramp-up times
**Steps:**
1. Assess current onboarding (if any): what do new hires experience today? Where do they get stuck?
2. Design the onboarding programme in layers:
   - **Day 1:** Equipment, accounts, source control access, build the game, meet the team. New hire should be able to run the game by end of day 1
   - **Week 1:** Studio orientation (team structure, who does what), tools training (JIRA, Perforce/Git, engine, communication tools), codebase/asset structure walkthrough, first small task assigned
   - **Month 1:** Deeper project context (GDD review, TDD review for their system area, art bible review), first meaningful contribution shipped, established working relationships with immediate team
   - **Quarter 1:** Full contributor. Understands the project holistically. Can work independently on standard tasks. Has a clear development plan
3. Create onboarding materials:
   - New hire checklist (who sets up what, account creation, equipment)
   - Studio guide (team structure, communication norms, meeting cadence, where to find things)
   - Technical onboarding (how to build and run the game, source control workflow, coding/asset standards)
   - Project onboarding (game overview, current development phase, milestone targets, their team's focus area)
4. Define the buddy/mentor system: pair each new hire with an experienced team member for the first month
5. Define onboarding success metrics: what does "onboarded" look like, and how quickly should it happen
6. Submit to Gaming Practice Lead for review
**Output:** Onboarding programme with materials, checklists, timelines, and success metrics
**Handoff:** Client HR and team leads implement. Follow up after 3 months to assess effectiveness

### Contractor and Outsource Strategy
**Trigger:** Client is considering outsourcing work or hiring contractors and needs strategic guidance
**Steps:**
1. Assess what work is being considered for outsourcing and why
2. Evaluate suitability for outsourcing:
   - **Good candidates:** Asset production to clear specifications (environment art, props, VFX), QA (with clear test plans), localisation, audio recording/mixing, porting, back-office tools
   - **Risky candidates:** Core gameplay engineering, game design, creative direction, anything requiring deep project context
   - **Bad candidates:** Technical direction, production management, anything requiring constant communication and iteration with the core team
3. Define the outsource framework:
   - Selection criteria: portfolio quality, relevant experience, communication capability, time zone compatibility, cultural fit
   - Quality framework: style guides, specification documents, review gates, revision limits
   - Integration plan: how outsourced work enters the asset pipeline, who reviews it, how feedback works
   - Contract structure: per-asset, milestone-based, or time-and-materials (and when each is appropriate)
4. For contractor (individual) hiring:
   - When to use contractors vs full-time: short-term capacity needs, specialist skills for a specific phase, uncertainty about long-term needs
   - IR35 considerations (UK): coordinate with Employment Lawyer via General Counsel
   - Integration: contractors need the same onboarding as full-time hires to be productive
5. Draft the outsource/contractor strategy recommendation
6. Submit to Gaming Practice Lead for review
**Output:** Outsource/contractor strategy with selection criteria, quality framework, integration plan, and risk assessment
**Handoff:** Gaming Practice Lead reviews. Client implements with NBI support

## Escalation Triggers
- Client's organisational problems are actually production methodology problems: escalate to Gaming Practice Lead and coordinate with Production Consultant
- Client pushes back on restructuring recommendations due to internal politics: escalate to Gaming Practice Lead
- Engagement scope is expanding beyond agreed terms: escalate to Gaming Practice Lead
- Team health assessment reveals serious issues (bullying, discrimination, burnout crisis): escalate immediately to Gaming Practice Lead and Glen
- Client wants compensation advice that could expose NBI to liability: escalate to Gaming Practice Lead and coordinate with General Counsel
- Restructuring recommendations could result in redundancies: escalate to Gaming Practice Lead and Glen before presenting to client
