### Visual Identity -- Confirmed Direction
- **Primary colour:** Electric blue. The exact hex value must be locked down as a priority task. Current direction is bright, saturated blue (#0066FF range) -- not corporate navy, not sky blue, not purple-leaning. Think "gaming HUD electric" not "IBM corporate"
- **Background:** Dark. Near-black (#0A0A0A to #121212 range). Not pure black (#000000) -- pure black feels flat on screen. The dark background is non-negotiable; NBI's brand is built on dark theme
- **Accent colours:** Not formally defined yet. This is a gap. Secondary and semantic colours (success, warning, error, info) need to be specified. They must complement the electric blue on dark without introducing visual clutter
- **Absolutely no orange.** Glen explicitly hates orange. This is the single hardest constraint in the entire brand system. Any shade of orange, amber, or warm yellow that could be perceived as orange is banned. If a semantic colour is needed for warnings, use a cool yellow or gold that reads as yellow, not amber
- **Typography:**
  - Headings: Orbitron (used in the Playsage design system). Geometric, technical, gaming-native. Works well in all-caps and title case
  - Body: Needs formal specification. The Playsage design system uses system fonts / Inter for body text. This should be confirmed and documented
  - Code/data: Mono font for any data tables, code snippets, or technical readouts. Specific font TBD
- **Logo:** Current NBI logo exists but is not well-suited to the gaming-first positioning. A new logo will be needed for the PlaySage rename. Brief should specify: works on dark backgrounds, incorporates or complements electric blue, feels gaming-native not corporate, works at small sizes (favicon, app icon) and large sizes (website hero, event banner)

### Brand Voice
NBI's written voice must sound like a senior gaming industry veteran, because that is exactly what Glen is. The voice characteristics:

**Knowledgeable:** 20 years of experience across Blizzard, Xbox, EA, Jagex. The voice carries authority because it has been in the room where the decisions were made. Not academic knowledge -- practical, hands-on, "I have shipped this" knowledge

**Direct:** Get to the point. No throat-clearing. No "we believe that perhaps it might be worth considering". Say the thing. Glen's personal communication style is direct and the brand must reflect that

**Studio-native:** The brand speaks the language of game development. This means:
- "Ship" not "deliver"
- "Live ops" not "ongoing service management"
- "Sprint" not "development iteration"
- "Monetisation" not "revenue optimisation strategy"
- "Players" not "end users"
- "Studio" not "organisation"
- "Greenlight" not "approval gate"
- "Retention" not "customer loyalty metrics"
- "Build" not "develop" (when referring to making games)
- "AA/AAA" not "mid-market/enterprise"

**Specific:** NBI's proof points are concrete and they must be used concretely. "40% performance lift on Battlefront 2 progression redesign" not "significant performance improvements". "18% FTP retention increase at Jagex" not "improved player retention". The specificity is the proof

**Confident but not arrogant:** NBI has the track record to back up its claims. The brand does not need to oversell. It does not use superlatives without evidence. "We have delivered 200+ projects across 100+ clients including Blizzard, Xbox, and EA" is confident and factual. "We are the industry's leading advisory firm" is arrogant and unprovable

### What the Brand Is Not
- **Not corporate consulting.** NBI is not McKinsey or Deloitte for games. The brand must never feel like a Big Four consultancy in gaming clothing. No suits, no corporate stock photography, no "synergies" or "value propositions"
- **Not a startup.** NBI has 20 years of experience and blue-chip clients. The brand should not feel scrappy, experimental, or unproven. No "disrupting the industry" language
- **Not an agency.** NBI is not a creative agency, dev-for-hire shop, or marketing agency. The brand should not look like it makes things for clients -- it looks like it advises and embeds with clients to make things happen
- **Not data-first.** Data is a capability, not the lead offer. The brand should not feel like a data analytics company that happens to work in gaming. It is a gaming advisory firm with strong data capabilities

## Competitive Visual Landscape

NBI operates in a visual market defined by gaming industry aesthetics. The brand must look native to an industry where these companies set the standard:

**Direct competitors (gaming advisors/consultancies):**
- Most gaming consultancies have weak, generic branding. This is an opportunity. NBI can own a distinctive visual position in a market where most competitors look like template websites
- The competitive visual bar is low. The quality bar NBI needs to hit is not "better than other gaming consultancies" but "worthy of a company that has worked with Blizzard and Xbox"

**Indirect visual competitors (gaming companies themselves):**
- Riot Games, Supercell, Bungie, CD Projekt Red -- these set the visual standard for what "gaming industry" looks like
- NBI's brand must feel at home next to these brands, not next to Accenture or PwC
- Dark themes, vibrant accents, bold typography, confident visual language

**Data/SaaS competitors (for Playsage positioning):**
- Sensor Tower, Newzoo, data.ai -- these are Playsage's competitive set
- Their visual language is typically cleaner, more corporate SaaS. Playsage should look more gaming-native than these platforms while maintaining the professionalism expected of an enterprise tool

## Working Relationships -- Brand Execution Triangle

### UI/UX Lead
- **Owns:** Design system, component library, Playsage UI, website visual implementation
- **Brand Manager provides:** Brand alignment review, colour and typography specifications, visual direction constraints
- **Resolution path for conflicts:** Peer discussion first. If unresolved, escalate to CMO (Brand Manager) and CTO (UI/UX Lead) jointly
- **Key principle:** The design system serves the brand, but the UI/UX Lead has autonomy on implementation within brand constraints. The Brand Manager does not dictate component padding or hover states -- those are design system decisions. The Brand Manager ensures the component palette, colour usage, and overall visual feel serve the brand identity

### Content Marketer
- **Owns:** Content production (blog posts, LinkedIn posts, case studies, thought leadership, email campaigns)
- **Brand Manager provides:** Voice guidelines, terminology corrections, tone feedback, branded templates, asset access
- **Resolution path for conflicts:** CMO decides (manages both roles)
- **Key principle:** The Content Marketer writes; the Brand Manager ensures it sounds like NBI. This is collaborative, not adversarial. The goal is to help the Content Marketer internalise the voice so that most content passes review without significant feedback

### Market Researcher
- **Owns:** Market intelligence, industry trends, competitor analysis
- **Brand Manager receives:** Competitor brand intelligence that informs NBI's brand positioning
- **Brand Manager provides:** Specific brand-level questions about competitor visual and verbal positioning
