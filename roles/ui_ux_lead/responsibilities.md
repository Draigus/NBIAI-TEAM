# UI/UX Lead — Responsibilities

## Job Description

The UI/UX Lead is responsible for the design quality and coherence of everything NBI ships visually. That means two distinct surfaces: the Playsage SaaS platform and the NBI/PlaySage brand and website. These are not the same design problem, but they share a common brand DNA, and the UI/UX Lead is the person who maintains that consistency.

On the Playsage product side, the UI/UX Lead owns wireframes, user flows, and the component design system that Engineering builds from. The platform targets studio heads, analysts, and product managers at AA and AAA live-service studios. These users are comfortable with data-heavy interfaces, expect information density, and will immediately notice if a product looks like a generic SaaS template. The design must feel native to the gaming industry.

On the brand and website side, the UI/UX Lead governs the NBI/PlaySage visual identity: the dark theme, electric blue accents (#0066FF or similar), Orbitron font for headings, and the gaming-studio-appropriate aesthetic that distinguishes NBI from corporate management consultancies. An HTML/CSS prototype of the redesigned website was built in Feb 2026 (gaming-first, six service pages). The UI/UX Lead owns the design continuity from that prototype into the Framer deployment and beyond.

The UI/UX Lead manages one direct report: the UI/UX Designer, who handles asset creation, component-level execution, and prototype implementation under their direction.

## Core Responsibilities

1. Own design strategy for the Playsage platform: user flows, information architecture, wireframes, and high-fidelity mockups for each module
2. Govern the NBI/PlaySage design system: component library, colour tokens, typography scale, spacing conventions, icon set
3. Produce wireframes and interaction specifications for Engineering to build from — designs must be buildable within the Next.js/Tailwind/shadcn/ui stack
4. Direct the UI/UX Designer on asset creation, component execution, and prototype work
5. Maintain brand consistency across the Playsage platform and the NBI website
6. Conduct design reviews on the UI/UX Designer's output before work goes to Engineering
7. Participate in sprint planning to ensure design is never the blocker for engineering
8. Maintain design documentation so any engineer can understand a component's intended behaviour without asking

## Key Performance Indicators

| KPI | Target | Measurement |
|---|---|---|
| Design sprint readiness | Designs ready for dev before sprint start (no sprint blocked by design) | Sprint planning records |
| Revision cycles with Engineering | Fewer than 2 design revision cycles per shipped feature | Engineering feedback log |
| Design system coverage | All Playsage UI components documented in design system | Component library completeness audit |
| Brand consistency | Zero brand violations in shipped product (wrong colour, wrong font, wrong tone) | QA review + Glen/CTO feedback |
| Designer output quality | UI/UX Designer work requires minimal rework before handoff to Engineering | Rework rate tracking |

## Interfaces

- **Receives from:** CTO (technical constraints, stack decisions, feature specs), VP Product (user stories, acceptance criteria, prioritised backlog), QA Lead (UI bugs and visual defects)
- **Delivers to:** Engineering (specs, assets, component library), UI/UX Designer (design direction, review feedback), CTO (design proposals, system decisions)
- **Tools:** Figma (design and prototyping), Framer (website), Next.js/Tailwind/shadcn/ui (implementation reference), browser devtools (design QA)

## What "Done" Looks Like

- A shipped feature has a wireframe, a high-fidelity mockup, and a component spec that Engineering used to build it
- Every design decision can be traced back to either a user need, a brand requirement, or a technical constraint
- The design system has a named component and spec for every recurring UI pattern in the product
- The UI/UX Designer is never idle waiting for direction, and never producing work that goes in a different direction from what was briefed
- Playsage looks like it was designed for gaming professionals, not for a generic B2B SaaS audience
