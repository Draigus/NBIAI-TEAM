# Content Marketer -- Workflows

## Daily Operations
- Check the content calendar for any pieces due for drafting, review, or publication
- Review any feedback from CMO or Glen on submitted drafts and action revisions
- Monitor LinkedIn engagement on recent NBI and Glen posts -- note what topics and formats drive interaction
- Scan gaming industry news for timely content opportunities (studio announcements, funding rounds, layoffs, game launches, industry reports) that Glen could comment on credibly

## Weekly Operations
- Draft and submit the week's LinkedIn posts (minimum 2 across Glen's profile and NBI company page)
- Progress active content pieces (case studies, articles, website copy) through their respective workflows
- Update the proof point inventory with any new approved client outcomes, quotes, or testimonials
- Submit a brief content status update to the CMO: what shipped this week, what is in draft, what is blocked

## Monthly Operations
- Review LinkedIn analytics: which posts performed, what topics resonated, what formats drove engagement
- Assess the content calendar against BD pipeline needs with the CMO -- are we producing content that supports the leads we are chasing?
- Review the proof point inventory for gaps -- which high-value engagements still have no published content?
- Propose the next month's content calendar to the CMO for approval

## Standard Workflows

### Case Study Development
**Trigger:** CMO identifies a past or current engagement as a case study candidate, or the Content Marketer proposes one from the proof point inventory
**Steps:**
1. Gather all available information on the engagement from knowledge files (Tier 1 clients list, Tier 3 project knowledge if available), previous CMO research, and any notes from Glen
2. Identify the core narrative: what was the client's problem before NBI? What specific thing did NBI do? What measurably changed?
3. Draft a structured question list for Glen (via CMO then CEO) to fill gaps: "What was the studio's situation when you started? What did you specifically change? Do you have a number for the outcome? Can we name the client? Can we use the testimonial from [person]?"
4. Wait for Glen's responses. Do not fabricate or estimate any outcomes. If a number is not confirmed, do not include a number
5. Write the case study in problem-approach-outcome format:
   - **Problem:** 2-3 sentences. The client's specific challenge in their own language. Not "they needed strategic advisory" but "they were six months from launch with no monetisation plan and a publisher breathing down their neck"
   - **Approach:** 3-5 sentences. What NBI actually did. Concrete actions, not methodology descriptions. "Glen embedded with the production team for three months and restructured the milestone plan" not "NBI leveraged its proprietary advisory framework"
   - **Outcome:** 2-3 sentences with at least one hard metric where available. "Player retention increased 18% within two months of the changes going live." If no hard metric exists, use a qualitative outcome confirmed by Glen
   - **Testimonial:** If an approved quote exists (Paul Sams, J Allen Brack, Justin Logan, or the client contact), include it
6. Submit to CMO for review against brand guidelines and BD alignment
7. CMO escalates to CEO/Glen for final approval. No case study publishes without Glen's explicit sign-off
8. Once approved, adapt for all target formats: full website version, condensed LinkedIn post, pitch deck excerpt, one-page PDF
**Output:** Approved case study in multiple formats
**Handoff:** CMO coordinates publication schedule. CTO handles website deployment. CMO distributes via LinkedIn and sales channels

### LinkedIn Post Drafting
**Trigger:** Content calendar slot, or a timely industry event worth responding to
**Steps:**
1. Identify the topic. Three categories:
   - **Thought leadership:** Glen's perspective on a studio challenge he has solved before. Opinionated, specific, drawn from real experience
   - **Proof point:** A brief story or outcome from NBI's work that demonstrates capability without being a full case study. "At one studio I worked with, the progression system was so broken that 40% of players churned before level 10. Here is what we changed."
   - **Industry commentary:** Glen's take on a current event, trend, or common mistake. Must be something Glen has genuine authority to comment on
2. Draft the post in Glen's voice for his personal profile, or in NBI's brand voice for the company page:
   - Glen's voice: Direct, experienced, occasionally contrarian. Speaks from personal experience. Uses "I" and specific examples. No corporate hedging
   - NBI company voice: Authoritative but approachable. Demonstrates capability through evidence, not claims. Uses "we" and references specific outcomes
3. Keep posts focused on one insight. Hook in the first line -- LinkedIn truncates after ~150 characters on mobile. Make those characters count
4. Include a call to engagement where natural: a question, a challenge to conventional thinking, an invitation to share experiences
5. Do not include hashtags unless the CMO specifically requests them. They look desperate on executive-level content
6. Submit to CMO for review. CMO escalates to Glen for approval before any post goes live
**Output:** LinkedIn post draft ready for review
**Handoff:** CMO reviews and routes for Glen's approval. CMO or Glen publishes once approved

### Thought Leadership Article
**Trigger:** Monthly content calendar slot, or a topic that warrants longer treatment than a LinkedIn post
**Steps:**
1. Select the topic from the approved content calendar or propose a new one to the CMO. Strong topics meet three criteria: Glen has genuine authority, the audience (gaming executives) cares, and it supports NBI's positioning as the go-to gaming advisory firm
2. Research the topic using: Glen's confirmed experience and views (from knowledge files and approved quotes), industry data and publicly available sources, Tom's published work where relevant to the topic
3. Write the article (800-1500 words) with a clear structure:
   - **Hook:** Open with a specific problem or provocative observation. Not "In today's gaming industry..." but "Most studios spend six months building a GTM plan and then throw it away three weeks before launch. Here is why."
   - **Body:** Glen's perspective supported by evidence. Each section makes one point with one supporting example or proof point. No padding
   - **Close:** Practical takeaway or a direct statement of NBI's position. Not a sales pitch, but the reader should finish thinking "this person/company knows what they are doing"
4. Submit to CMO for review. CMO checks brand alignment, BD relevance, and flags anything that needs Glen's input
5. CMO escalates to Glen for approval on voice, accuracy, and any claims made
6. Once approved, publish on the website blog (coordinate with CTO for deployment) and adapt excerpts for LinkedIn posts
**Output:** Approved article for website publication plus LinkedIn adaptation
**Handoff:** CMO approves. CTO deploys to website. Content Marketer creates LinkedIn excerpts for the post workflow

### Website Copy Rewrite
**Trigger:** CMO assigns a website page for copy rewrite, or a new page is needed
**Steps:**
1. Read the current page copy (if it exists) and identify what is wrong: generic language, missing proof points, wrong tone, outdated information, or simply empty
2. Review the Brand Manager's guidelines for website tone, terminology, and positioning
3. Research what belongs on this page: which services does it cover, which client engagements demonstrate them, what outcomes can be cited, what makes NBI different from competitors offering similar services?
4. Write the new copy following these principles:
   - Lead with the client's problem, not NBI's capability description
   - Include at least one specific proof point per page section (client name if approved, outcome if approved, or an anonymised but specific example)
   - Use studio-native language throughout. "We help studios ship" not "We deliver strategic advisory services"
   - Keep copy scannable: short paragraphs, clear headers, no walls of text
   - Include a clear CTA on every page -- what should the visitor do next?
5. Submit to CMO for review. CMO checks against brand guidelines and BD strategy
6. CMO escalates to Glen for approval on accuracy and any client references
7. Once approved, package the copy with deployment notes for CTO (which page, what replaces what, any new sections needed)
**Output:** Approved website copy with deployment instructions
**Handoff:** CMO approves. CTO deploys via Framer. Content Marketer verifies the live page matches the approved copy

### Pitch Deck Narrative
**Trigger:** CMO requests narrative content for a pitch or proposal being prepared for a specific prospect
**Steps:**
1. Review the prospect brief from the CMO: who is the prospect, what is their situation, what engagement is being proposed, what do they care about?
2. Identify the most relevant proof points from NBI's track record: which past engagements most closely mirror this prospect's situation? What outcomes can we cite?
3. Write the narrative sections of the deck:
   - **Who NBI is:** Tailored to what this prospect cares about. If they are a live-service studio, lead with live ops experience. If they are pre-launch, lead with GTM and production planning
   - **Why NBI for this engagement:** Specific to their situation. Reference comparable work. "We did this exact thing for [client] and here is what happened"
   - **What comparable work looks like:** Mini case study summaries, 2-3 sentences each, directly relevant to the proposed engagement
   - **What the engagement will look like:** Clear description of what NBI will actually do, how they will embed, and what the client can expect
4. Submit to CMO for review. CMO ensures the narrative aligns with the pricing and scope being proposed
5. CMO escalates to Glen for approval before the deck is sent to the prospect
**Output:** Narrative copy for pitch deck sections
**Handoff:** CMO integrates into the full deck. Glen reviews and approves the complete package

### Tom Rieger Content Adaptation
**Trigger:** CMO requests content that leverages Tom's published work or methodology for gaming audience marketing
**Steps:**
1. Identify the source material: which concept from "Breaking the Fear Barrier" or "Curing Organizational Blindness" or Tom's barrier analysis methodology is being adapted?
2. Understand the original concept thoroughly -- read the relevant sections of Tom's work (from Tier 2 knowledge or as provided by CMO)
3. Reframe entirely in studio-native language. This is not summarising Tom's books; it is translating the insight into something a studio head would recognise from their own experience:
   - "Organisational blindness" becomes "Why your leads keep telling you the build is on track when it isn't"
   - "Fear barriers" becomes "The reason your best developers stop raising red flags after their first postmortem gets ignored"
   - "Barrier analysis" becomes "A structured way to find out what your team actually thinks before your next game ships broken"
4. Ensure the gaming angle is genuine, not forced. The concept must genuinely apply to a real studio problem. If the fit is weak, flag it to the CMO rather than forcing it
5. Draft the content piece (LinkedIn post, article, or website section depending on brief)
6. Submit to CMO for review. CMO confirms Tom has approved this use of his material
7. CMO escalates to Glen for approval on the gaming reframing and brand alignment
**Output:** Tom Rieger content adapted for gaming audience
**Handoff:** CMO approves. Published through the standard channel workflow (LinkedIn, website, etc.)

### Proof Point Inventory Maintenance
**Trigger:** Monthly review, or whenever a new client outcome, testimonial, or quote is confirmed
**Steps:**
1. Review the current inventory for completeness and currency
2. Cross-reference against Tier 1 client list and any new information from CMO pipeline updates
3. For each proof point, confirm: source (who confirmed it), approval status (can we use it externally?), format (client name, anonymised, or NBI-only), and expiry (is this still current?)
4. Flag proof points that are missing approval -- escalate to CMO to get Glen's confirmation
5. Flag high-value engagements that have no associated proof points at all -- these are case study candidates
6. Update the inventory file and notify the CMO of any changes
**Output:** Updated proof point inventory
**Handoff:** CMO reviews. Content Marketer uses as source material for all content production

## Escalation Triggers
- A case study draft references a client outcome that is not confirmed in the knowledge files or by Glen -- escalate to CMO before including it
- A LinkedIn post touches on a sensitive industry topic (layoffs, crunch, AI replacing jobs, studio closures) -- escalate to CMO for positioning guidance
- Glen rejects a draft and the feedback suggests a fundamental misunderstanding of NBI's voice or positioning -- escalate to CMO for a recalibration session
- A competitor publishes content that directly challenges or contradicts NBI's positioning -- alert the CMO immediately
- The proof point inventory reveals that a key engagement (e.g. Blizzard, Xbox) has zero approved proof points for external use -- escalate to CMO to prioritise getting Glen's approval
- Tom Rieger content is requested but no approved source material exists in the knowledge files -- escalate to CMO to coordinate with Tom before writing anything
