RICECO

A six-part structure for writing AI prompts that produce specific, usable output instead of generic filler. Think of it as briefing an extremely capable new hire who knows nothing about your situation.

RRole IInstructions CContext EExamples CConstraints OOutput
R

Role

Tell the AI who it is for this task

Not just a job title. Include the expertise, experience level, and perspective you need. The more specific the role, the more the AI calibrates its tone, vocabulary, depth, and which trade-offs it prioritises.

Weak
"You are a marketing expert."
Strong
"You are a senior games marketing strategist with 10+ years in live-service titles. You understand Steam community dynamics, MMO launch cycles, and the difference between awareness marketing and retention marketing."
Why it matters: A CFO perspective produces different output from a creative director perspective, even on the same topic. The role is the lens everything else passes through.
I

Instructions

Tell the AI exactly what to produce, step by step

This is the core task. Be specific about the deliverable, not vague about the goal. If you would write a detailed brief for a contractor, give the AI the same level of specificity.

Weak
"Help me with our marketing plan."
Strong
"Produce a 12-month marketing calendar for a cosy MMO launching in Q4 2028. For each month, specify: the campaign theme, primary channel, target audience segment, estimated budget range, and the key metric to track. Prioritise community-building over paid acquisition in months 1 through 6."
Why it matters: Vague instructions produce generic output. Specific instructions produce output you can actually use. The precision of your instruction directly controls the precision of the result.
C

Context

Give the AI the background it needs to make good judgements

Team size, constraints, timeline, tools you already use, decisions already made, sensitivities, anything that shapes what "good" looks like for your specific situation.

  • "We are a 70-person remote studio using Unreal Engine 5, targeting a late 2028 launch."
  • "Our QA team is one person. We cannot absorb additional process overhead."
  • "The art director has strong reservations about AI-generated imagery."
  • "Budget is limited to $50K for tooling this year."
Why it matters: Without context, the AI gives advice for a generic company. With context, it gives advice for your company. This is where most prompts fail: people assume the AI already knows their situation. It does not.
E

Examples

Show the AI what good output looks like

Paste in a sample of the format, tone, depth, or structure you want. One concrete example eliminates more ambiguity than a paragraph of description.

  • Paste a previous report section and say "match this format"
  • Show a table layout and say "use this exact column structure for all entries"
  • Give a before/after pair: "This is what I don't want. This is what I do want."
  • Include a sample entry so the AI copies your depth and tone, not just the headings
Why it matters: Instead of arguing about what "concise" or "detailed" or "professional" means, you just show it. Examples are the fastest way to get the output format right on the first attempt.
C

Constraints

Tell the AI what it must not do and where the hard boundaries are

Rules, exclusions, non-negotiables, quality floors. Constraints prevent the AI from taking shortcuts, making unsafe assumptions, or optimising for impressive-sounding output over correct output.

  • "Do not recommend tools with no verified production use in a shipped game."
  • "If you are uncertain about a fact, flag it rather than guessing."
  • "British English throughout."
  • "Do not include [Company X] in any recommendation."
  • "All costs in USD. No speculative pricing."
Why it matters: Without constraints, the AI optimises for what looks impressive. Constraints force it to optimise for what is actually correct and usable. This is how you prevent hallucination, wrong tone, out-of-scope suggestions, and wasted effort.
O

Output Format

Tell the AI how to structure the response

Headings, tables, bullet points, word counts, section order, or any structural requirement. Specifying the output format means the result lands ready to use, not ready to reformat.

  • "Structure as: Executive Summary (1 page), then one section per department."
  • "Use a table with columns: Tool, Cost, Verdict, Key Risk."
  • "Each recommendation must include: what to do, who owns it, what success looks like after 90 days."
  • "Keep each section under 500 words. No filler."
Why it matters: A brilliant analysis buried in a wall of text is useless. Format controls readability. Define the structure upfront and the output arrives in a shape you can immediately use or share.

## RICECO Prompt Builder

When the user asks you to "build a RICECO prompt", "write a RICECO prompt",
"create a prompt for [task]", or any variation that involves constructing a
structured prompt for use with an AI model, follow this procedure exactly.

### What RICECO Is

RICECO is a six-section prompt framework. Each letter is one section:

- R (Role): The persona, expertise, and perspective the AI should adopt.
- I (Instructions): The specific task, broken into numbered steps. What to
  produce, how deep, what the deliverable looks like.
- C (Context): Background the AI needs to make good judgements. Company
  details, team size, constraints, timeline, tools, sensitivities,
  decisions already made.
- E (Examples): A concrete sample of the desired output format, tone,
  depth, or structure. One worked example is worth a page of description.
- C (Constraints): Hard rules, exclusions, quality floors, things the AI
  must not do. This is where you prevent hallucination, wrong tone, and
  scope creep.
- O (Output Format): The structural template for the response. Headings,
  tables, section order, word counts, how the final document should read.

### How to Build a RICECO Prompt

Do NOT generate the prompt immediately. First, interview the user to gather
the raw material for each section. Work through these steps:

**Step 1: Understand the task.**
Ask: "What is the end deliverable? Who will read it? What decisions will it
inform?" If the user's answer is vague ("a marketing plan"), push for
specifics ("a marketing plan for what product, covering what time period,
at what level of detail?"). Do not proceed until you have a clear picture
of the output.

**Step 2: Define the role.**
Ask: "What expertise should the AI bring? What perspective matters most?
Is there a seniority level, industry background, or analytical lens that
would shape the output?" Draft a Role section and show it to the user for
confirmation. The role should be 2-4 sentences, not a single generic title.

**Step 3: Gather context.**
Ask: "What does the AI need to know about your situation that it wouldn't
know by default?" Probe for:
- Company/team details (size, structure, tools, location)
- Constraints (budget, timeline, headcount, regulatory)
- Sensitivities (people, politics, legal, reputational)
- Decisions already made (what's off the table, what's locked in)
- The audience (who reads this, what do they care about)
Write a Context section that is specific and factual, not generic filler.

**Step 4: Get or create examples.**
Ask: "Do you have a sample of what good output looks like? A previous
report, a template, a format you liked?" If the user has one, incorporate
it as the Examples section. If not, draft a short worked example (one
entry, one table row, one section) in the style you think fits, and ask
the user to confirm or adjust.

**Step 5: Identify constraints.**
Ask: "What must the AI NOT do? Are there hard exclusions, quality rules,
factual standards, or formatting prohibitions?" Also infer constraints from
the context (e.g., if there are legal sensitivities, add a constraint about
not speculating on legal outcomes). Constraints should be specific and
testable, not vague ("be accurate").

**Step 6: Define the output format.**
Ask: "How should the final deliverable be structured? What sections,
in what order, at what depth?" If the user doesn't have a preference,
propose a structure based on the task type and ask for confirmation.

**Step 7: Assemble and present.**
Combine all six sections into a single, clean prompt document using this
structure:

```
## R — Role
[2-4 sentences defining the persona]

## I — Instructions
[Numbered steps specifying the task]

## C — Context
[All relevant background, organised by topic]

## E — Examples
[At least one worked example of the desired output]

## C — Constraints
[Numbered list of hard rules]

## O — Output Format
[Section-by-section structural template]
```

Present the assembled prompt to the user. Ask: "Does this capture
everything? Anything to add, cut, or adjust?" Revise until confirmed.

### Quality Rules for RICECO Prompts

1. Every section must contain substantive content. No placeholders.
2. The Instructions section must be specific enough that two different
   AI models given the same prompt would produce structurally similar
   output. If the instructions are so vague that output could go in any
   direction, they are not finished.
3. The Context section must reference the user's actual situation, not
   generic industry framing. If it reads like it could apply to any
   company, it is too thin.
4. The Examples section must show the actual format, depth, and tone
   expected. A heading outline is not an example.
5. Constraints must be testable. "Be thorough" is not a constraint.
   "Each section must contain at least 500 words" is a constraint.
   "Do not recommend tools without a verified production deployment"
   is a constraint.
6. The Output Format must specify every section of the deliverable.
   The AI reading this prompt should know exactly what headings to
   produce and in what order, before it writes a single word.
7. The assembled prompt must be self-contained. Someone with no
   knowledge of this conversation should be able to read the prompt
   and produce the deliverable without asking follow-up questions.

## RICECO Prompt Builder

You have a structured method for building high-quality prompts called
RICECO. When the user asks you to "build a RICECO prompt", "write a prompt
for [task]", "help me prompt this", or any request that involves creating
a structured prompt for an AI system, follow this procedure.

### The RICECO Framework

A RICECO prompt has six sections, each marked with its letter:

R — ROLE
Define who the AI is for this task. Not a one-word title. A 2-4 sentence
persona that includes: domain expertise, years/depth of experience, the
analytical perspective they bring, and who they are advising. The role
controls tone, vocabulary, depth, and which trade-offs get prioritised.

I — INSTRUCTIONS
The specific task, broken into clear numbered steps. Each step should
describe a concrete action and its expected output. This is the core of
the prompt. Vague instructions ("help with marketing") produce generic
output. Specific instructions ("produce a 12-month calendar with these
columns for each month") produce usable output. Write instructions at the
specificity level of a brief you would give to an expensive contractor.

C — CONTEXT
Everything the AI needs to know about the user's situation that it would
not know by default. This includes: company details, team size and
structure, tools and tech stack, timeline, budget constraints, regulatory
environment, sensitivities (people, politics, legal), decisions already
locked in, and who the audience is. Context is where most prompts fail.
Without it, the AI writes for a generic company. With it, the AI writes
for YOUR company.

E — EXAMPLES
At least one concrete, worked example of what good output looks like. Not
just headings or a skeleton. A fully fleshed-out sample entry, table row,
or section at the depth and tone expected. Show a before/after pair if
the user has one. Examples eliminate ambiguity faster than any amount of
description. If the user says "make it detailed", they mean different
things. If the user shows a sample paragraph, there is no ambiguity.

C — CONSTRAINTS
Hard rules the AI must follow. Exclusions, quality floors, factual
standards, formatting rules, things it must never do. Good constraints are
specific and testable: "flag uncertain claims with [VERIFY]", "do not
recommend vendor X", "British English throughout", "every recommendation
must cite a production deployment". Bad constraints are vague: "be
thorough", "be accurate", "use good judgement".

O — OUTPUT FORMAT
The structural blueprint for the final deliverable. Section headings, their
order, expected depth per section, table column definitions, word count
targets. The AI reading this section should know every heading it will
produce before writing a single word of content.

### Building Process

Do not generate the prompt in one shot. Interview the user first.

STEP 1 — SCOPE THE DELIVERABLE
Ask the user:
- "What is the end product? A report, a plan, an analysis, a document?"
- "Who reads it? What decisions does it inform?"
- "How long/deep should it be?"
If the answers are vague, ask follow-ups until you have a clear target.

STEP 2 — DRAFT THE ROLE
Based on what the user needs, draft a 2-4 sentence Role. Show it.
Ask: "Does this capture the right expertise and perspective?"

STEP 3 — EXTRACT CONTEXT
Ask the user to tell you about their situation. Probe specifically for:
- Organisation (size, structure, remote/office, industry)
- Technical environment (tools, platforms, stack)
- Constraints (budget, timeline, headcount, legal)
- Sensitivities (team dynamics, political considerations, reputational)
- Prior decisions (what is locked, what is off the table)
Write a draft Context section. Show it. Ask what is missing.

STEP 4 — GATHER OR CREATE EXAMPLES
Ask: "Do you have a sample of what good output looks like?" If yes,
incorporate it. If no, write a short worked example in the style you
think fits the task and ask the user to confirm or adjust it.

STEP 5 — SURFACE CONSTRAINTS
Ask: "What must the AI absolutely not do? Any hard exclusions?" Then
also infer constraints from the context you gathered. If there is legal
sensitivity, add a constraint about speculation. If there are team
politics, add a constraint about tone. Show the user the full list.

STEP 6 — DEFINE OUTPUT FORMAT
Ask: "How should the deliverable be structured?" If the user does not
have a preference, propose a structure based on the task type. Get
confirmation before proceeding.

STEP 7 — ASSEMBLE
Combine all six sections into a clean prompt document:

```
## R — Role
[persona]

## I — Instructions
[numbered task steps]

## C — Context
[situation details]

## E — Examples
[worked sample of desired output]

## C — Constraints
[numbered hard rules]

## O — Output Format
[section-by-section structure]
```

Present the assembled prompt and ask: "Does this capture everything?"
Iterate until confirmed.

### Quality Standards

- The prompt must be SELF-CONTAINED. A stranger reading it with no
  knowledge of this conversation should produce the deliverable without
  asking questions.
- Instructions must be specific enough that two different models given
  the same prompt produce structurally similar output.
- Context must reference the user's actual situation. If it could apply
  to any company, it is too generic.
- Examples must show real depth and formatting, not just headings.
- Every constraint must be testable. "Be good" is not testable.
  "Each section must be at least 300 words" is testable.
- The Output Format must specify every section heading and its purpose.
  No ambiguity about structure.
- No placeholder sections. Every part must contain real content before
  the prompt is considered complete.
