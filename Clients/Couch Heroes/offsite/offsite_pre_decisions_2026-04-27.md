# Couch Heroes Offsite - Pre-Decisions Glen Lays Down Before Apr 27

**Purpose:** Six decisions stated up front so the room doesn't relitigate. Each decision lists Glen's chosen position, the alternatives considered, the reason this position wins, and the constraint it places on the rest of the offsite. Attendees can disagree at the offsite (must disagree if they disagree per the rules) but the burden of proof shifts onto the disagreement.

---

## Decision 1 - Pricing Model

**Position:** F2P with cosmetic monetisation as the primary model. Optional Founder's Pack / Early Adopter tier for pre-launch revenue and premium positioning.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Premium box / one-time purchase (FFXIV, GW2 hybrid)
- Subscription (WoW)
- Hybrid premium + cosmetic shop

**Why this wins:**
1. The game's design pillar is **gear has no stats; cosmetics are the aspirational currency**. Charging for the aspirational currency aligns the business model with the design intent. Premium box collides with this because players expect "I bought it, I get all of it".
2. Soft-class via weapon + off-hand means players stay engaged horizontally (build variety) rather than vertically (gear ladder). Horizontal engagement maps to F2P retention curves better than premium.
3. Partner game integration funnels traffic in; F2P removes the price barrier for that traffic to convert.
4. Young adult audience trends F2P-heavy; subscription specifically is out of favour with under-25s outside FFXIV.
5. The Platform (web + mobile) layer is naturally F2P-shaped - cosmetics, social, partner content all live there.

**What it constrains downstream:**
- Live services lane on Day 2 must include a real monetisation model (battle pass yes/no, store rotation, founder's pack design, currency design).
- Steam page work assumes F2P + cosmetic shop language, not "buy now £39.99".
- GTM positioning treats wishlist, demo, and Founder's Pack as the conversion funnel, not a launch-day box drop.
- Investor narrative: "F2P MMORPG with partner game integration" is the pitch shape.

**Open inside the room:**
- Founder's Pack price band and contents (mid Day 2)
- Whether subscription tier is offered as optional convenience layer (probably no, but listen)

## Decision 2 - Publisher vs Self-Publish

**Position:** Self-publish core distribution. Targeted publishing relationships for specific capabilities (regional, marketing, hardware partnerships, IP cross-promotion).

**Alternatives considered:**
- Full publisher deal (lose IP control + take rate but get capital and marketing muscle)
- Pure self-publish (full upside, full risk, requires capability we are still building)
- Co-publish (shared credit, shared upside)

**Why this wins:**
1. Partner game integration is the differentiator. A traditional publisher would view partner-IP relationships as competition or contamination. Self-publishing preserves the freedom to negotiate partner deals on our terms.
2. Couch Heroes is fundraising direct equity. Publishing capital becomes redundant if equity closes.
3. Glen has the BD network to negotiate targeted deals (Microsoft Game Pass first-party, Sony EU push, regional partners in EU/Asia, hardware partnerships).
4. Self-publishing keeps the live services lane intact. Publisher deals usually carve out live ops in ways that constrain post-launch flexibility.
5. The **community** strategy is core to the game's design (social systems, partner cross-promo). Publishers tend to standardise community handling; we need our own voice.

**What it constrains downstream:**
- BD lane on Day 2 frames partner conversations as targeted capability deals, not full-game licensing.
- Marketing budget on the LRP must include self-publishing spend (PR firm, paid acquisition, events).
- Studio Business backlog "Publisher conversations" become "Strategic capability conversations" - different scope.
- Steam, Epic, console first-party relationships are direct, not via publisher.

**Open inside the room:**
- Specific targeted partnerships to pursue first (one platform-aligned, one regional, one IP cross-promo as a starter pattern)
- Marketing capability we need to build vs buy

## Decision 3 - Demo, Beta, Early Access

**Position:** Closed beta at vertical slice / mid-production. No public early access launch. Public demos only at major beats (Steam Next Fest if achievable, GDC, partner showcase events).

**Alternatives considered:**
- Public early access at alpha (revenue early, risk of first-impression burn)
- No demos, full launch only (low risk, low buzz building)
- Open beta from alpha onwards (community building, server load testing)

**Why this wins:**
1. MMORPGs need **server load and social system testing** at scale. Closed beta delivers that without burning launch energy.
2. Early access launches tend to hollow out launch buzz - press treats EA as "the launch" and the actual launch becomes a non-event.
3. Closed beta with NDA-light controls protects the partner game integration moments (don't give away the surprise of partner reveals).
4. Public demo at major beats (Steam Next Fest, GDC) gives the marketing team real assets to wishlist-convert without committing to a permanent demo build.
5. Founder's Pack tier (Decision 1) can grant beta access as the value proposition - this is the conversion path, not EA.

**What it constrains downstream:**
- Production gate criteria (Day 2 Session 1) must include "closed beta ready" as an alpha-gate criterion.
- Live ops tooling must be ready for beta, not for launch (earlier than usual).
- GTM lane on Day 2 plans the beta wave (size, criteria, NDA terms, comms plan) and the public demo beats separately.
- Wishlist campaign anchors on beta-access-as-Founder's-Pack-perk.

**Open inside the room:**
- Beta size and gating criteria (closed-but-large vs invite-only)
- Whether to publish a permanent free play island (small, sandboxed, partner-content-only) as a constant marketing surface

## Decision 4 - Studio Name vs Game Name

**Position:** Studio name and game name are separate. "Couch Heroes" stays as the game name. Studio name TBD - resolve at Day 1 of offsite or in week after.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Same name (Couch Heroes Studios makes Couch Heroes the game) - marketing efficiency, but ties studio fortunes to a single game and complicates a second project
- Separate names (Mojang / Minecraft, Larian / Baldur's Gate) - protects studio identity across multiple projects
- Studio as parent + game brands (Annapurna pattern)

**Why this wins:**
1. The studio plans to make games beyond this one (per vision doc strategic intent). Locking the studio identity to "Couch Heroes" caps the brand at one game's lifecycle.
2. Investor narrative reads better when the studio has its own identity. "Couch Heroes Studios making Couch Heroes" can sound like a one-shot.
3. Trademark/domain hygiene easier with separate names.
4. The Platform (web/mobile companion product) needs its own identity that isn't game-bound; the studio name is a natural carrier for the Platform brand.

**What it constrains downstream:**
- Brand identity work on Day 2 GTM lane treats the studio name as a separate work item (can be deferred without blocking game marketing).
- Domain and social handle portfolio (Studio Operations Legal feature) doubles - both names need protection.
- Investor materials and Studio Operations / Investor pipeline need both names treated as parallel artefacts.

**Open inside the room:**
- Studio name shortlist (could be a 30-minute exercise on Day 2 GTM lane if energy allows)
- Whether the studio launch comms include the studio name reveal alongside game branding or sequentially

## Decision 5 - Cross-play and Unified Account Model

**Position:** Unified account is mandatory at design level (every player has one identity across game, web, mobile, partner content). Cross-play between platforms is a Critical ACT launch goal: PC + mobile + web at launch; consoles when first-party negotiations land.

**Alternatives considered:**
- PC-only at launch, console later (lowest tech complexity, but cuts the player base and contradicts the Platform pillar)
- No cross-play but unified account (data layer aligned but players can't co-play across platforms)
- Cross-play but separate accounts per platform (player frustration; data fragmentation)

**Why this wins:**
1. The **Platform** is a separate product surface in the design. Web and mobile aren't just companion apps - they are first-class surfaces. Players move between PC client, mobile app, web dashboard. A unified account is the spine that makes this work.
2. Cross-play across PC + mobile is required by partner game integration design. Players are pulled in via partner content; that funnel can't bottleneck on platform choice.
3. Console cross-play is harder (first-party negotiations, certification) but the game design supports it. Saying "yes at launch" for PC + mobile, "yes when first-party allows" for console is a clean line.
4. Single account = single source of player data = easier KYC/age gating, GDPR compliance, anti-cheat ban enforcement, and economy controls.

**What it constrains downstream:**
- Tech architecture (Mustafa) must lock the account / identity / cross-platform sync system as Foundation-layer at the gate-criteria session on Day 2.
- IT and Systems: identity provider decision is upstream of game backend platform decision.
- Live services lane treats "single live ops surface across all platforms" as a constraint.
- Pricing / monetisation: store rotation must work across platforms; Apple / Google fees apply on mobile, store rules apply on console.

**Open inside the room:**
- Console launch list (Switch 2 / PS5 / Xbox Series) and order of priority
- Mobile app scope at launch (full play vs companion only)

## Decision 6 - Studio 12-Month Goal

**Position:** A primary outcome plus two supporting outcomes. All three are concrete, verifiable, time-bounded by "12 months from offsite Day 1".

**Primary:** Close the institutional funding round and ship an internal vertical slice the leadership room is proud of.

**Supporting:**
1. Hit a sustainable production cadence - on-time gate transitions, predictable velocity, stable team.
2. Build the studio core team to working capacity per the hiring plan (priority hires landed, not gaps blocking the roadmap).

**Why this wins:**
1. Without the funding close, every other goal is contingent. Naming it explicitly forces the room to keep funding work prioritised.
2. Vertical slice is the demonstrable product progress that funding closes against. It's also the artifact that drives investor follow-on, publisher conversations, and beta-tester quality. Multi-purpose anchor.
3. "Sustainable cadence" forces the room to value boring delivery as much as exciting progress. Couch Heroes' historical pattern (per Granola context) has been more excitement than cadence.
4. Hiring goal ties the HR project to the roadmap; the gates can't transition without the right people.
5. All three are measurable. "Close round" = wired funds. "Vertical slice" = playable internal build hitting agreed criteria. "Cadence" = three consecutive gate transitions on-time. "Hiring" = priority roles per gate target landed.

**What it constrains downstream:**
- Studio-down goals cascade (Day 2 Session 6) can attribute every feature's "dream of success" back to one of these three.
- Investor reporting cadence (Studio Operations Fundraising feature) reports against these three.
- HR hiring priority order driven by the roadmap, not by org chart aesthetics.
- Anything that isn't moving any of the three is a candidate for parking.

**Open inside the room:**
- Concrete vertical slice criteria (which features, which zones, what scope) - this is Day 1 Session 4 work.
- Funding round size and use of funds (if not yet locked at deck level).
- Hiring priority list confirmation against the new gate placements.
