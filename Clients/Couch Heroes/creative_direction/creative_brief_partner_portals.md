# Creative Brief: Partner Portals - How Players Interact with Portals from Other Games

**Project:** Couch Heroes MMO
**Owner:** Creative Director
**Date:** 2026-05-11
**Classification:** Internal - Creative Direction

---

## 1. What Are Partner Portals?

Partner Portals are Couch Heroes' signature differentiator. No other MMO has this. Partner studios license their IP into the CH world, where their games appear as physical portals players can discover and step through. CH becomes the "home" players return to between adventures in other games.

This is not a launcher. This is not a menu. This is a diegetic, in-world system where portals are physical objects in the game space - doorways, arcade cabinets, dimensional rifts - that players walk up to and step through. The byte-punk aesthetic provides a natural creative frame: portals are digital gateways in a world built on an AI proving ground. They make lore sense.

**Partner content types:**
- Full portal experiences (roguelikes, mini RPGs, narrative shorts, racing games)
- Arcade cabinets (retro arcade, puzzle games, quick-play experiences)
- Partner item shops (themed items sold in CH world)
- Quest rewards featuring partner IP
- Guild TV and guild portal access

## 2. The Player Experience - Beat by Beat

### 2.1 Discovery

**Where portals live:**
- **Portal Peak** - the primary portal zone, a mountain biome where dimensional energy concentrates. This is the "portal district" of the world. Players travel here specifically to find new portal experiences. The zone's identity is built around the portal concept - crystalline formations, dimensional shimmer in the air, glimpses of other worlds visible through rock formations.
- **Downtime (main city)** - arcade cabinets in social spaces, partner shops in commercial districts. These are the casual, walk-past-and-notice portal touchpoints. A player heading to the Barbersmith passes an arcade cabinet glowing with a new game. A player browsing the market notices a partner shop with unfamiliar items.
- **User Space (housing)** - personal portals earned or unlocked. Having a portal in your User Space is a statement of identity ("I'm the person who plays X"). Visitors to your User Space can see and use your portals.
- **Guild spaces** - shared guild portals for group adventures. Guilds can collectively unlock and curate their portal collection. Guild TV (screens showing partner content) creates ambient awareness.
- **World scattered** - rare, hidden portals in the open world. Discovery of these is an exploration reward - stumbling across a glowing rift in a corrupted forest that leads to a partner game nobody in your guild has found yet.

**How players find them:**

The Hero Gadget (the player's personal portable device) picks up "dimensional signatures" as the player explores. A subtle UI pulse, a directional indicator, a faint audio cue. The player follows the signal and discovers the portal. First discovery of any portal is a curated moment:

1. **Signal detection** - Hero Gadget picks up an anomaly. Subtle. Easy to miss if you're rushing, impossible to miss if you're exploring. This rewards CH's core explorer audience.
2. **Approach** - As the player gets closer, the world changes. The air shimmers with byte-punk digital particles. The ambient audio shifts - fragments of the partner game's soundtrack bleed through. The portal isn't fully formed yet; it's resolving, like a signal being tuned.
3. **Reveal** - The portal stabilises and the player sees through it for the first time. A window into another world. The art style of the partner game is visible through the portal frame, but contained within CH's byte-punk portal architecture. It's a window, not a hole - CH's world frames it.
4. **Invitation** - An NPC or in-world prompt explains what this portal is. Who created it. What's on the other side. This is narrative context, not a menu. "This rift has been stable for three days. Whatever's on the other side... it's been calling to adventurers."

### 2.2 Approach & Entry

**The portal as physical object:**

Portals are not identical. Each portal reflects the game on the other side while maintaining CH's byte-punk architectural language:

- **Full dimensional portals** (Portal Peak, open world) - Large, architectural structures. Think: stone archways with digital energy flowing through carved channels. The portal surface is a shimmering membrane showing glimpses of the partner world. Each portal has unique framing that hints at the content - a portal to a roguelike might have carved dungeon motifs; a portal to a racing game might have speed lines etched into the arch.

- **Arcade cabinets** (Downtime, social spaces, User Spaces) - Physical arcade machines in the byte-punk style. Chunky, stylised cabinets with screens showing the partner game in attract mode. Players walk up, interact, and the screen expands to fill their view as they "enter" the cabinet. This is the casual portal type - quick play, no commitment, social (friends can watch over your shoulder).

- **Partner shops** (Downtime commercial districts) - Storefronts with partner branding adapted to CH's byte-punk style. A medieval market stall, but the goods on display are items from another world. The shopkeeper might be an NPC from the partner IP, transplanted into CH's world and speaking in CH's voice.

- **User Space portals** (housing) - Smaller, personal portal frames. Players choose the frame style. These are display pieces - a conversation starter when friends visit. "Oh, you've got the portal to [game]? Is it good?"

**Entry sequence (full portals):**

This is a signature creative moment. Not a loading screen. Not a fade to black.

1. **Player approaches** - The portal reacts to the player's proximity. The digital energy in the frame intensifies. The partner game's audio bleeds louder. The membrane surface ripples.
2. **Player interacts** - The membrane parts like a curtain. The player sees a clear view of the partner world. A beat of stillness - the threshold moment.
3. **Player steps through** - The byte-punk frame stretches and warps around the player's peripheral vision as they cross. CH's world falls away behind them. For 2-3 seconds, the player is between worlds - surrounded by flowing digital energy, fragments of both art styles swirling. Then the partner world resolves around them.
4. **Arrival** - The player is in the partner game's world/level/experience. Behind them, the portal frame persists as a small visual anchor - a reminder that CH is home and they can return.

**Entry sequence (arcade cabinets):**

Faster, more casual. The player interacts with the cabinet. The screen zooms in, the cabinet frame becomes the game border. The player is playing within the arcade aesthetic - it's a game-within-a-game. Other players nearby can see the cabinet's screen showing what the player is doing.

### 2.3 Inside the Portal

**The partner experience:**

Inside the portal, the partner game runs with its own art style, mechanics, and rules. CH doesn't override the partner's identity - that would defeat the purpose. However, CH provides a consistent frame:

- **Portal HUD overlay** - A subtle CH-branded border or corner element that persists during partner content. Not intrusive - just a reminder of where you came from. Shows your CH identity, any active quests related to the portal, and the "return home" prompt.
- **CH rewards tracking** - As the player achieves things in the partner game, CH-side rewards accumulate. A small notification: "Portal Loot: +1 [item]". The player knows their time in the partner game counts back in CH.
- **Session framing** - Partner experiences have natural endpoints. A roguelike run ends at death or boss kill. A racing game ends at the finish line. A narrative short has a conclusion. CH provides a "results screen" at the end that shows what the player earned in CH terms.

**What the player earns:**

- **Partner-themed items** for CH (adapted to byte-punk style) - cosmetic armour, housing decorations, weapon crystals with partner-themed visual effects
- **Portal reputation** - Repeat visits to the same portal increase the player's standing with that "dimension", unlocking exclusive rewards
- **Stories to tell** - "I beat the final boss in [partner game] and got this sword skin" is a social currency in CH. The reward is visible to other players.
- **Portal mastery** - Players who complete all challenges in a partner portal get a permanent visual mark (a subtle glow, an aura, a title) that shows their achievement in the CH world

### 2.4 Return to Couch Heroes

**Coming home:**

The return is as intentional as the entry. This is not a disconnect - it's a homecoming.

1. **Player triggers return** - Via the portal HUD "return home" option, or by reaching the partner experience's natural endpoint.
2. **Transition** - The partner world dissolves into digital energy. The byte-punk frame re-establishes itself. The player passes back through the between-worlds moment, but this time CH's world is resolving ahead of them.
3. **Arrival home** - The player steps back out of the portal into CH's world. They're standing exactly where they left. Their friends might be waiting. The portal behind them pulses once - acknowledging their return.
4. **Rewards summary** - The Hero Gadget displays what they earned. Items appear in inventory. Reputation updates. If they completed something notable, there might be a world reaction - an NPC commenting, a visual change to the portal (it glows differently for portals you've mastered).

**The "home" feeling:**

This is the emotional core of the system. CH is home. Other games are adventures. The player's User Space, their friends, their faction, their Batsuits, their crafting stations - that's all here. Portals are exciting because they're departures from home, and returns are satisfying because home is rich and personal.

### 2.5 Repeat Visits & Long-Term Engagement

**How portals evolve:**

- **Portals update** - Partner studios can push new content to their portal (new levels, new challenges, seasonal events). The portal in CH reflects this - it might glow differently, the NPC might have new dialogue, the preview through the membrane shows new content.
- **Portal events** - Limited-time events where a portal offers exclusive rewards. Creates urgency and social buzz ("The [partner game] portal has a limited boss this week").
- **Portal Peak evolves** - As new partner deals close, new portals appear in Portal Peak. This is a world event - dimensional energy spikes, the zone changes, players gather to see what's new. New portal arrivals are live service content without CH's team building new game content.
- **Community around portals** - Players who love a specific partner game form communities within CH. They hang out near that portal. They share strategies. The portal becomes a social landmark.

## 3. Creative Constraints

### What portals must always be:
- **Diegetic** - Physical objects in the world with lore justification, never menu entries
- **Framed by CH** - Partner content appears within CH's byte-punk portal architecture, never raw/unframed
- **Voluntary** - Players choose to enter portals; they're never forced or gated by main progression
- **Rewarding in CH terms** - Time in partner games always yields something meaningful back in CH
- **Respectful of partner identity** - Partner games keep their own art style and personality inside the portal

### What portals must never be:
- **Loading screens** - The transition must feel like a physical journey, not a technical pause
- **Advertisements** - Portals are content, not ads. Players should want to enter, not feel sold to
- **Required for CH progression** - No CH story quest should gate behind a partner portal completion
- **Visually jarring** - The style bridge (byte-punk frame) must make even wildly different art styles feel intentional
- **Low quality** - A bad partner experience reflects on CH. Quality gate before any portal goes live

### Tone:
- **Young adult, edgy, fun** - Portal discovery should feel like finding a secret passage in a library
- **Witty, not corporate** - NPC dialogue about portals should be curious and playful, not transactional
- **Mysterious, not explained** - Portals exist because the world's dimensional fabric is thin. Don't over-explain
- **Social, not solo** - Portals should be things players tell friends about, show off, explore together

## 4. Portal Visual Taxonomy

| Portal Type | Location | Visual Form | Entry Style | Session Length |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dimensional Portal | Portal Peak, open world | Stone archway with digital energy, full-body walk-through | Full cinematic transition | 10-60 min |
| Arcade Cabinet | Downtime, social spaces, User Space | Byte-punk arcade machine, attract mode screen | Screen zoom, cabinet-frame gameplay | 2-15 min |
| Partner Shop | Downtime commercial districts | Themed storefront adapted to byte-punk | Walk in, browse, purchase | 1-5 min |
| User Space Portal | Player housing | Personal-scale portal frame, chosen style | Abbreviated transition (familiar portal) | Variable |
| Guild Portal | Guild space | Large shared portal, guild-customised frame | Group transition (party enters together) | Variable |
| Hidden Rift | Open world, corrupted zones | Raw dimensional tear, unstable, flickering | Discovery-first, unstable entry effect | Variable |

## 5. Lore Integration

The world of Couch Heroes was created as an AI proving ground (Darwin/Digit1). The dimensional fabric of this world is inherently thin - it was designed to be a sandbox, and sandboxes have edges. Portals are places where the edges of the sandbox show through. Other "simulations" (partner games) are visible through these thin spots.

Drystan's corruption virus exploits the same dimensional weakness. Corruption spreads through the fabric of the world; portals are natural weak points that corruption gravitates toward. This creates a gameplay tension: portals are desirable (fun content, rewards) but also vulnerable (corruption targets them). Players who invest in restoration near portals are protecting their favourite adventures.

Portal Peak is the zone where the dimensional fabric is thinnest. It's a mountain where reality itself is perforated. The zone's visual identity reflects this: crystalline formations that refract views of other dimensions, floating fragments of terrain from partner worlds suspended in the air, a sky that shifts between CH's sky and glimpses of partner worlds.

## 6. Key Creative Questions for Leadership

1. **Corruption and portals** - Can corruption close or degrade a portal? If so, this creates a powerful restoration motivation ("save the portal to my favourite game"). If not, portals are safe havens from corruption. Both are valid - but the choice affects the entire system.

2. **Portal permanence** - When a partner deal ends, what happens to the portal? Does it close? Does it remain but lock? Does it become a "memorial" (inactive but visible)? This has massive player trust implications.

3. **Portal progression gating** - Should some portals require a minimum CH level or quest completion to enter? This creates aspiration ("I can't wait to be level 30 so I can enter the [game] portal") but also gates content.

4. **Competitive partner content** - If a partner game has competitive elements (leaderboards, PvP), do CH stats/items affect it? The answer should almost certainly be no (partner content is a level playing field), but this needs to be explicit.

5. **Partner NPC integration** - How far do partner characters go into CH's world? Just shopkeepers? Quest givers? Random encounters in Downtime? Each level deepens integration but increases creative control complexity.

6. **User Space portal limit** - How many portals can a player have in their User Space? Unlimited cheapens them. One is too restrictive. Three to five creates a curation decision ("which portals define me?").

7. **Portal discovery spoilers** - Hidden portals in the open world are exploration rewards. Should the community be encouraged to share discoveries, or should spoiler culture be managed? This affects community design.

## 7. Reference Points

| Reference | What to learn | What to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Roblox portal/game discovery | Seamless transition between experiences within one platform | Chaotic quality variance, no consistent framing, disorienting transitions |
| Fortnite Creative / UEFN | User-created content alongside curated content, social hubs | Content feeling disconnected from the core game, quality floor too low |
| Ready Player One (Oasis) | The fantasy of a hub world connecting many game experiences | Over-promising scope, tech complexity eclipsing creative quality |
| Kingdom Hearts (Disney worlds) | Visiting recognisable IPs within a host game's mechanics | Feeling like tourism rather than meaningful integration |
| Super Smash Bros (character crossovers) | Respectful IP integration that celebrates both sources | Reducing partner IPs to cosmetic skins without depth |
| Steam/Epic (storefronts) | Discovery and recommendation systems | Being a launcher, not a game. Transactional, not experiential |
| Animal Crossing (island visiting) | Social visiting, personal space as identity expression | Limited interaction options, novelty wearing off quickly |
