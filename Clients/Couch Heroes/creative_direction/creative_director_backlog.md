## Feature 1: Creative Vision & Pillars

**Description:** Establish and document the creative north star for Couch Heroes MMO. Define the core creative pillars that every discipline uses to make judgment calls - what makes this game feel like *this game* and not a generic MMO. This is the foundational document everything else references.

### Story 1.1: Define Core Creative Pillars

**Description:** Identify and articulate 3-5 creative pillars that define the Couch Heroes experience. These are the non-negotiable truths about the game's identity - the lens through which every feature, asset, and design decision is evaluated.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, when I watch a trailer or play for 5 minutes, I can immediately tell this game apart from every other MMO on the market. It has a clear identity I can describe to a friend in one sentence."*

**Success Factors:**
- Pillars are distinct, memorable, and not interchangeable with competitor MMOs
- Each pillar has a "this, not that" contrast (e.g., "Whimsical danger, not cartoon safe")
- Every discipline lead can recite the pillars and give an example of how they apply to their work
- Pillars are validated against the existing feature set - nothing in the V2 feature list directly contradicts them

**Tasks:**
- Audit competitor MMO creative identities (WoW, FFXIV, GW2, New World, Palia) to identify white space
- Workshop with discipline leads to surface what they believe makes CH unique
- Draft pillar candidates with "this, not that" definitions
- Validate pillars against the V2 feature list for contradictions
- Write the Creative Pillars document with rationale and examples per discipline
- Present to studio leadership for sign-off

---

### Story 1.2: Creative Vision Document (CVD)

**Description:** Write the master Creative Vision Document that translates the pillars into actionable guidance for every department. This is the single reference document that answers "what does Couch Heroes feel like?" across art, audio, narrative, design, and UX.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, every part of the game - the combat, the world, the menus, the music, the story - feels like it was made by people who agreed on what they were making. Nothing feels out of place or bolted on."*

**Success Factors:**
- Document covers visual tone, narrative tone, gameplay tone, audio tone, and UX tone
- Each section includes concrete reference material (moodboards, audio references, gameplay clips)
- Document is usable by a new hire on day one to understand the game's creative direction
- Reviewed and endorsed by Art Director, Audio Director, Lead Designer, and Narrative Lead

**Tasks:**
- Write visual tone section with moodboards and reference art
- Write narrative tone section with sample dialogue and world voice
- Write gameplay tone section with feel targets and pacing references
- Write audio tone section with sonic palette references
- Write UX tone section with interaction philosophy
- Compile reference media library (images, clips, audio samples)
- Review cycle with all discipline leads
- Final sign-off and distribution

**Description:** Map Couch Heroes' creative positioning against the MMO market. Identify where CH occupies unique creative space and where it deliberately overlaps with proven approaches. This informs both game direction and marketing messaging.

**Player Story:** *"As a player choosing my next MMO, I can see exactly what Couch Heroes offers that I can't get elsewhere. It's not trying to be WoW or FFXIV - it's doing something I haven't seen before."*

**Success Factors:**

**Tasks:**

- Plot CH features against competitor feature sets on a differentiation matrix

- Identify proven patterns to adopt (not reinvent)
- Write competitive differentiation summary
- Review with BD and marketing teams

## Feature 2: World Identity & Lore

**Description:** Define the world of Couch Heroes - its history, mythology, geography, cultures, and the central corruption/restoration dynamic. This is the narrative and environmental foundation that gives every zone, NPC, quest, and faction meaning and coherence.

### Story 2.1: World Bible - History & Mythology

**Description:** Write the foundational world bible covering the history, creation mythology, and major historical events of the Couch Heroes world. Establish why corruption exists, what restoration means, and what the player's role is in this world's story.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, I feel like this world existed before I arrived. The ruins, the NPCs' stories, the corruption spreading across the land - it all connects to something bigger. I want to learn more."*

**Success Factors:**
- History is internally consistent with no timeline contradictions
- Corruption/restoration dynamic is baked into world history, not bolted on
- History creates natural hooks for zone themes, faction motivations, and quest lines
- Lore is layered - surface-level accessible to casual players, deep enough for lore hunters
- Document is structured so quest writers and level designers can reference it without reading the whole thing

**Tasks:**
- Draft creation mythology and the origin of corruption
- Write major historical eras (3-5 ages/epochs)
- Define the restoration force/magic and its relationship to corruption
- Map historical events to geographic locations (future zone theming)
- Create a timeline with key dates and events
- Write a "lore cheat sheet" (1-page summary for quick reference)
- Review with narrative team for consistency

---

### Story 2.2: Zone Identity Guide

**Description:** Define the creative identity for each major zone/region in the game world. Each zone needs a distinct visual theme, narrative purpose, corruption state, and emotional tone that fits within the broader world while feeling unique to explore.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, every time I enter a new zone, I feel like I've travelled somewhere genuinely different. The colours change, the music shifts, the NPCs talk differently, and the corruption looks and feels different here. I want to explore every corner."*

**Success Factors:**
- Every zone in the V2 World Systems feature set has a defined identity
- Zones have clear visual, audio, and narrative differentiation
- Corruption manifests differently per zone (not copy-paste corruption overlays)
- Zone identities support the progression curve (starter zones feel safe and inviting; endgame zones feel dangerous and alien)
- Each zone identity doc includes enough detail for environment artists and level designers to work from

**Tasks:**
- Define zone count and geographic layout (continent/island structure)
- Write identity briefs per zone: visual theme, narrative hook, corruption state, emotional tone, ambient audio direction
- Define corruption visual language per zone (how corruption looks different in a forest vs desert vs urban)
- Create zone mood boards with reference imagery
- Map zone identities to the player progression curve
- Review with environment art lead and level design lead

---

### Story 2.3: Faction & Culture Design

**Description:** Design the playable and NPC factions of the world, including their cultures, visual identities, motivations, and relationships. Factions drive player choice, quest content, reputation systems, and PvP/PvE narrative hooks.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, I feel genuine loyalty to my faction. They have a clear identity, a cause I believe in, and enemies I understand. Choosing sides matters because the factions feel real and different."*

**Success Factors:**
- Factions are distinct in visual identity, motivation, and gameplay niche
- No faction is objectively "the good one" - each has strengths, flaws, and moral grey areas
- Faction designs support the reputation/faction system from the V2 feature list
- Faction relationships create natural PvP and PvE content hooks
- Cultural details are deep enough for quest writers to build on without inventing new lore

**Tasks:**
- Define faction count and high-level roles (defender, aggressor, neutral, corrupted, etc.)
- Write faction identity briefs: origin story, visual identity, values, relationship to corruption
- Design inter-faction relationship matrix (allies, rivals, neutral, hostile)
- Define faction visual markers (armour styles, colour palettes, architecture, banners)
- Create faction leadership characters (named NPCs with personality briefs)
- Map factions to zones (home territories, contested zones, neutral ground)
- Review with narrative team and game design lead

### Story 2.4: NPC Archetype Library

**Description:** Define the recurring NPC archetypes across the world - quest givers, merchants, faction leaders, ambient townsfolk, enemies. Each archetype needs visual, behavioural, and narrative guidelines so NPCs feel consistent within their faction/zone while being individually memorable.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, NPCs feel alive. The blacksmith in a corruption-free village acts differently from the one in a besieged outpost. I can tell a character's faction and role at a glance, but they still surprise me."*

**Success Factors:**
- Archetypes cover all NPC types in the V2 feature list (quest givers, vendors, faction NPCs, combat NPCs)
- Each archetype has visual, behavioural, and dialogue guidelines
- Archetypes adapt to zone and corruption state (same archetype, different flavour)
- Archetype library is structured as a toolkit for quest designers and character artists

**Tasks:**
- Catalogue all NPC types from the V2 feature list
- Define archetype categories (quest giver, merchant, faction officer, ambient, hostile, boss)
- Write archetype guides: visual template, behaviour patterns, dialogue tone, corruption variants
- Create naming convention guidelines per faction/culture
- Design "ambient life" guidelines (what townsfolk do, patrol routes, idle behaviours)
- Review with character art lead and quest design team

## Feature 3: Art Direction

**Description:** Establish the visual identity of Couch Heroes across all art disciplines - character, environment, UI, VFX, and technical art. The Art Direction feature ensures every pixel on screen feels like the same game and serves the creative pillars.

### Story 3.1: Art Style Guide

**Description:** Define and document the overarching art style for Couch Heroes. This is the visual north star that all art production references - proportions, colour philosophy, rendering approach, level of detail, and stylistic boundaries.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, the game's art style is distinctive and appealing. I can recognise a Couch Heroes screenshot instantly. The style works across different content types - cute housing decorations and terrifying bosses both feel like they belong."*

**Success Factors:**
- Style guide defines proportions, silhouette philosophy, colour palette approach, and rendering style
- Includes "do" and "don't" examples for common art decisions
- Style scales from small UI icons to massive boss encounters without breaking
- Style supports the corruption/restoration dynamic visually (clean vs corrupted states need to coexist)
- Art team can use the guide to self-review work before CD review

**Tasks:**
- Define character proportion standard (realistic, stylised, chibi spectrum)
- Establish colour philosophy (palette structure, saturation rules, corruption colour language)
- Define rendering approach (PBR, stylised shading, outline treatment)
- Create silhouette standards (readability at distance, class identification)
- Produce "do/don't" reference sheets per art discipline
- Write material and texture guidelines
- Review with art team and get sign-off

### Story 3.2: Character Design Direction

**Description:** Set the creative direction for all player and NPC character designs - body types, facial features, armour/clothing philosophy, class visual identity, and customisation boundaries. This feeds directly into the character creation, garment system, and combat ability visuals from the V2 feature list.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, my character looks amazing and unique. I can see my class fantasy in my armour. The character creator gave me real choices, not just slider variations of the same face. Other players' characters all look different."*

**Success Factors:**
- Direction supports the character creation & management features (race/species, appearance customisation, garment system)
- Class/archetype visual identities are distinct at a glance (a tank looks different from a healer at 50 metres)
- Armour progression is visually meaningful (starter gear looks starter; endgame gear looks endgame)
- Customisation direction ensures player expression within the art style boundaries
- Corruption effects on characters are defined (what does a corrupted player/NPC look like?)

**Tasks:**
- Define body type range and proportions for all playable races/species
- Establish class visual identity guidelines (silhouette, colour, material per class archetype)
- Design armour progression visual language (how gear improves visually across tiers)
- Set garment system visual rules (layering, dye system, transmog boundaries)
- Define corruption effects on character models (visual corruption states)
- Create character design review checklist
- Produce reference sheets for character artists

**Description:** Define the visual approach to world environments - terrain, architecture, vegetation, lighting, weather, and the corruption/restoration visual system. This ensures zones are visually distinct, navigable, and emotionally resonant.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, the world is breathtaking. Every zone has its own colour palette and architecture. I can tell I've crossed into a new area without checking the map. The corruption spreading across the landscape is genuinely unsettling, and seeing a restored zone is satisfying."*

- Corruption visual system is defined with multiple stages (pristine → touched → consumed → void)

- Day/night cycle and dynamic weather interact with the art direction intentionally

**Tasks:**

- Design corruption visual progression system (4-5 stages of corruption per biome)
- Design restoration visual system (what cleansed areas look like)
- Establish architecture style guides per faction/culture
- Define day/night and weather visual impact guidelines
- Set vegetation and terrain detail standards
- Create environment art review checklist

### Story 3.4: VFX Direction

**Description:** Define the visual effects philosophy - how combat abilities, environmental effects, corruption/restoration magic, and UI feedback look and behave. VFX is critical for combat readability, emotional impact, and the corruption fantasy.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, I can read combat clearly even in a 20-person boss fight. My abilities feel powerful and distinct. Corruption effects are menacing, and restoration abilities feel cleansing and heroic. Nothing is visual noise."*

**Success Factors:**
- VFX direction supports combat readability (player abilities, enemy telegraphs, AoE indicators)
- Corruption and restoration VFX are visually distinct families (you can tell at a glance which is which)
- VFX scales appropriately (solo play vs group content vs massive raids)
- Direction includes performance budgets per VFX type
- Status effects (buff/debuff framework) have clear, non-overlapping visual language

**Tasks:**
- Define VFX style (painterly, particle-heavy, geometric, elemental)
- Create combat VFX readability hierarchy (player > party > enemy > environmental)
- Design corruption VFX language (spreading, pulsing, decay)
- Design restoration VFX language (cleansing, growing, light)
- Define status effect VFX library (buffs, debuffs, crowd control indicators)
- Set VFX performance budgets per content type (solo, group, raid, world event)
- Create VFX review checklist

### Story 3.5: UI Art Direction

**Description:** Define the visual identity of all user interfaces - menus, HUD, inventory, character sheet, map, chat, and all other UI surfaces. The UI art direction must serve usability first while reinforcing the game's visual identity and adapting to the corruption theme.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, the UI is beautiful without getting in my way. It feels like it belongs in this world. Important information is easy to find. The menus don't look like a spreadsheet - they feel like part of the game."*

**Success Factors:**
- UI art direction covers all UI surfaces from the V2 feature list (character sheet, inventory, crafting, map, combat HUD, housing, social)
- Direction prioritises readability and usability over decoration
- UI adapts to corruption state where appropriate (corrupted zone map edges, tainted inventory items)
- Consistent iconography system across all features
- Accessible to colourblind players and supports text scaling

**Tasks:**
- Define UI visual language (frame styles, backgrounds, typography, icon style)
- Create colour system for UI states (default, active, disabled, corrupted, highlighted)
- Design iconography standards and icon grid
- Set typography hierarchy (headers, body, numbers, labels)
- Define HUD layout philosophy (minimal, contextual, always-on elements)
- Create accessibility guidelines (colourblind modes, scaling, contrast)
- Produce UI art direction reference sheet

## Feature 5: Player Fantasy & Experience Design

**Description:** Define what fantasy the player lives in Couch Heroes, and how the experience evolves from first login to endgame mastery. This is the emotional and experiential architecture that connects the game's systems into a coherent player journey.

### Story 5.1: Core Player Fantasy Definition

**Description:** Articulate the core player fantasy - what does it feel like to be a hero in this world? What power fantasies does the game deliver? How does the corruption/restoration dynamic create a fantasy that's unique to Couch Heroes? This is the emotional contract with the player.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, I feel like a hero whose actions matter. I'm not just levelling up - I'm pushing back the corruption and restoring the world. My choices in how I fight and what I restore define my personal story."*

**Success Factors:**
- Fantasy is specific to Couch Heroes, not generic "be a hero in a fantasy world"
- Fantasy connects directly to the corruption/restoration mechanic as a player-driven narrative
- Fantasy scales from early game ("I'm learning to fight the corruption") to endgame ("I'm the vanguard of restoration")
- Fantasy supports multiple playstyles (combat, crafting, social, exploration) without favouring one
- Fantasy is testable - playtesters can articulate it unprompted

**Tasks:**
- Write core fantasy statement (one paragraph, evocative not mechanical)
- Define fantasy arc per game phase (tutorial, levelling, midgame, endgame)
- Map fantasy delivery to specific game systems (which features deliver which parts of the fantasy)
- Define how corruption/restoration creates a personal narrative for each player
- Write fantasy validation questions for playtest surveys
- Review with game design and narrative teams

- Fantasies are achievable through the combat and ability systems in the V2 feature list

**Tasks:**
- Define class/role archetypes and count
- Write fantasy statement per class/role

- Define class visual identity hooks (what makes each class visually recognisable)
- Define class audio identity hooks (signature sounds per class)
- Create class fantasy validation criteria for playtesting
- Review with combat design, art direction, and audio direction

## Feature 6: Corruption & Restoration Creative System

**Description:** The corruption/restoration dynamic is Couch Heroes' signature creative system. This feature defines how corruption and restoration look, sound, feel, and behave as a unified creative experience across all disciplines. This is the creative system that makes CH an *experience*, not just a set of mechanics.

### Story 6.1: Corruption Visual & Audio Language

**Description:** Define the comprehensive sensory language of corruption - what it looks like at every stage, what it sounds like, how it affects the environment, characters, and UI. Corruption should be immediately recognisable and viscerally unsettling without being gratuitous.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, corruption terrifies me in a compelling way. I can see it spreading across the landscape. The sounds change - birdsong fades, replaced by low rumbles and distortion. Even the UI starts to feel wrong. I want to fight it."*

**Success Factors:**
- Corruption has a clear visual progression (5 stages from pristine to consumed)
- Corruption looks and sounds different per biome (not one-size-fits-all purple fog)
- Corruption affects all sensory channels (visual, audio, UI, gameplay feel)
- Corruption is unsettling but not horror-genre (appropriate for the game's target rating)
- All disciplines have clear guidelines for implementing corruption in their domain

**Tasks:**
- Design 5-stage corruption visual progression with reference art per biome
- Design corruption audio progression (ambient sound changes, music distortion)
- Define corruption effects on NPC behaviour and dialogue
- Define corruption effects on UI elements (subtle visual corruption on menus/HUD in affected zones)
- Create corruption VFX library specifications (particles, shaders, post-processing)
- Set corruption intensity standards per content type (open world vs dungeon vs raid)
- Produce corruption reference bible (one document all disciplines reference)

---

### Story 6.2: Restoration Visual & Audio Language

**Description:** Define the sensory language of restoration - the counterpoint to corruption. Restoration should feel heroic, satisfying, and earned. This is the emotional payoff for the game's core loop.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, restoring a corrupted zone is one of the most satisfying things I've ever done in a game. The corruption peels back, colour returns, creatures come back, and the music swells. I feel like I made a real difference."*

**Success Factors:**
- Restoration has a clear visual progression that mirrors and reverses corruption stages
- Restoration is emotionally uplifting and satisfying (not just "corruption goes away")
- Restored zones feel alive and vibrant, not just "default"
- Restoration VFX and audio create memorable "hero moments"
- Player actions visibly drive restoration (not automatic timers)

**Tasks:**
- Design restoration visual progression (reversal of corruption stages, plus "thriving" state beyond pristine)
- Design restoration audio progression (music restoration, ambient life returning)
- Define restoration "climax moments" (what happens at the moment of full restoration)
- Create restoration VFX specifications (growth, light, renewal effects)
- Define player feedback for restoration actions (visual and audio rewards per contribution)
- Design persistent restoration markers (how players see their contribution over time)
- Produce restoration reference bible

---

### Story 6.3: Corruption/Restoration Integration with Game Systems

**Description:** Define how the corruption/restoration creative system integrates with every major game system - combat, crafting, progression, housing, PvP, economy, and questing. This ensures corruption/restoration isn't a skin - it's woven into the game's creative DNA.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, corruption and restoration affect everything, not just the scenery. My weapons can become corrupted. My crafting materials change in corrupted zones. My housing can be threatened. It's not a gimmick - it's the world."*

**Success Factors:**
- Integration points are defined for every major feature area in the V2 list
- Integration adds depth without adding friction (enhances gameplay, doesn't gate it)
- Integration creates meaningful player choices (embrace corruption for power? Resist for purity?)
- Creative direction for each integration point is documented (not left to individual designers to invent)
- Corruption/restoration integration doesn't contradict or complicate existing game system designs

**Tasks:**
- Audit V2 feature list for all corruption/restoration integration points
- Define corruption effects on combat (corrupted weapons, abilities, enemy behaviours)
- Define corruption effects on crafting (corrupted materials, tainted recipes, purification crafts)
- Define corruption effects on player progression (corruption XP track? Restoration milestones?)
- Define corruption effects on housing/user spaces (can corruption threaten player homes?)
- Define corruption effects on economy (corrupted item market, restoration bounties)
- Write creative integration briefs per feature area
- Review with game design leads for each system

## Feature 7: Content Direction & Quality Standards

**Description:** Establish what content gets made, to what quality standard, and in what order. The Creative Director sets the quality bar and content strategy - not the production schedule, but the creative priorities and quality gates that production works within.

### Story 7.1: Content Quality Standards

**Description:** Define the minimum quality bar for all content types - environment art, character models, animations, VFX, audio, quests, dialogue, and UI. These standards are the creative quality gate that all content must pass before it ships.

**Player Story:** *"As a player, nothing in this game feels unfinished or low-effort. Every quest has genuine writing. Every zone has detail. Every enemy has personality in its animations. The quality is consistent throughout."*

**Success Factors:**
- Quality standards are defined per content type (art, animation, audio, narrative, UI)
- Standards are objective enough for reviewers to apply consistently
- Standards distinguish between "minimum shippable" and "target quality"
- Standards are achievable within production constraints (not aspirational to the point of blocking shipping)
- A review checklist exists per content type

**Tasks:**
- Define quality standards per content type with exemplar references
- Create review checklists per content type (what to check, what fails, what passes)
