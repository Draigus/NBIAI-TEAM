# 1. Insurance Requirements for UK Companies

If you run a business in the UK, you will be liable to hold certain insurances, depending on the business sector. Firstly, you will need Employers’ Liability Insurance, especially if you are hiring in the UK, but also if you are hiring from overseas. The Home Office will need to see your Employers’ Liability Insurance when applying for a Sponsor Licence.

## 1.1 Legally Required: Employers’ Liability Insurance

You must have this if you employ anyone. It covers employee injury or illness at work.

Minimum cover: £5 million

Required under the Employers’ Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969

You can be fined £2,500 for every day you are not properly insured

You must display your EL certificate where employees can access it

Source: https://www.gov.uk/employers-liability-insurance

NOTE: This applies even if staff are part-time or work remotely (in most cases). You do not need EL insurance only if you exclusively employ close family members or staff based entirely outside England, Scotland and Wales.

Employers’ Liability Insurance costs are approximately £250 to £800 per year for a small employer.

# 2. Setting Up a UK Pension Scheme

To set up a workplace pension scheme in the UK, employers must meet legal auto-enrolment requirements. These are set out by The Pensions Regulator and apply to any employer with at least one worker.

## 2.1 You Must Provide a Qualifying Pension Scheme

The scheme must be a registered UK pension scheme

It must allow employees to join without barriers (no forms or choices required to stay in)

It must not include prohibited charges or restrictions

Source: https://www.thepensionsregulator.gov.uk/en/employers/new-employers/im-an-employer-who-has-to-provide-a-pension

## 2.2 Automatically Enrol Eligible Employees

You must enrol staff if they meet all three criteria:

Aged 22 to State Pension age

Earn £10,000 or more per year (this threshold is confirmed at £10,000 for the 2026/27 tax year)

Work in the UK

Sources: GOV.UK: AE Thresholds 2026/27 |  GOV.UK: Joining a Workplace Pension

## 2.3 Make Minimum Contributions

Employer minimum: 3% of qualifying earnings

Employee minimum: 5% of qualifying earnings

Total minimum: 8% of qualifying earnings

Qualifying earnings band for 2026/27: £6,240 to £50,270 per year

You can pay more, but not less.

Source: https://www.thepensionsregulator.gov.uk/en/employers/new-employers/im-an-employer-who-has-to-provide-a-pension/choose-a-pension-scheme

## 2.4 Ongoing Duties

Assess workers continuously: check staff eligibility every pay period (e.g. monthly payroll)

Allow opt-ins and opt-outs: employees can opt out within 1 month and receive a refund, or opt in if not automatically enrolled. You must still provide a scheme if they request it.

Communicate with employees: you must write to staff explaining their pension rights within 6 weeks of assessing them.

Declare compliance: submit a declaration of compliance to The Pensions Regulator within 5 months of starting duties, even if no staff qualify yet.

Re-enrol employees: anyone who opted out must be automatically re-enrolled approximately every 3 years.

Keep records: contributions, enrolment decisions, and communications, typically for at least 6 years.

Start on time: duties begin when you hire your first eligible employee. You usually have up to 6 weeks to enrol them (or up to 3 months if using postponement).

Sources: The Pensions Regulator: Employer Duties |  The Pensions Regulator: Earnings Thresholds

## 2.5 Common Mistake to Avoid

You cannot wait until probation ends to offer a pension (except for limited postponement rules of up to 3 months). This is a legal requirement, not discretionary.

## 2.6 Forthcoming Change: Pensions (Extension of Automatic Enrolment) Act 2023

This Act received Royal Assent in September 2023 and gives the government the power (not yet exercised) to lower the age threshold from 22 to 18 and to remove the lower earnings limit for contributions. These powers have not yet been commenced, but Couch Heroes should be aware that they may affect future obligations. The government launched the Pensions Commission in July 2025 to consider wider pension adequacy, including auto-enrolment thresholds.

Source: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/36/enacted

## 2.7 Approximate Costs

These are indicative and will depend on the pension provider chosen. Note that NEST (the government-backed scheme) has no setup fees and no annual employer charges, making it a low-cost option for small employers.

Set up cost (one-off fee): approximately £3,250 (no VAT) for a private scheme

Annual cost: approximately £2,750 + VAT for a private scheme

# 3. Setting Up a UK Sponsor Licence

Setting up a UK Sponsor Licence (visa sponsorship capability) allows your company to legally hire workers from outside the UK. It is regulated by the UK Home Office and involves strict compliance requirements.

## 3.1 Check Your Business is Eligible

To apply for a Sponsor Licence, your company must:

Be a genuine UK business (registered with Companies House if applicable)

Have a UK presence (trading or operating lawfully)

Have no serious immigration or criminal breaches

NOTE: Even small startups can apply if they meet these criteria.

Source: https://www.gov.uk/uk-visa-sponsorship-employers

## 3.2 Decide Which Licence You Need

Skilled Worker licence (main route for hiring overseas employees): covers long-term skilled roles

Temporary Worker licence: covers short-term and Global Business Mobility routes

## 3.3 Put Key People in Place

You must appoint responsible staff to manage the licence:

Authorising Officer: senior person responsible for compliance

Key Contact: main contact with the Home Office

Level 1 User: manages the online Sponsor Management System (SMS)

The Level 1 User must have settled status — they must either be a British citizen or have indefinite leave to remain.

These roles are critical. You are legally accountable for what they do.

Source: https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/690c8dafb5e5a06aefd46b29/Sponsor-guidance-Part-1-Apply-for-licence-11-25-v1.0.pdf

## 3.4 Prepare HR Systems (Very Important)

Before applying, you must show you can manage sponsored workers properly. You need systems to:

Track employee attendance

Keep copies of passports/visas

Monitor visa expiry dates

Report changes (salary, role, absence) to the Home Office

NOTE: This is where many applications fail. The Home Office may audit you before granting the licence and at any time afterwards.

## 3.5 Choose Eligible Job Roles

You can only sponsor jobs that:

Meet the required skill level (RQF Level 3+)

Meet minimum salary thresholds: the general threshold for the Skilled Worker route is currently £41,700 per year or the going rate for the specific occupation code, whichever is higher. Lower thresholds may apply in limited circumstances (e.g. new entrants: £33,400).

Match official occupation codes from the Home Office (SOC 2020 codes)

Source: https://www.gov.uk/skilled-worker-visa/your-job

Sources: GOV.UK: Skilled Worker Visa — Your Job |  GOV.UK: Immigration Fees from 8 April 2026 |  GOV.UK: Pre-Licence Priority Service

## 3.7 Additional Costs Per Sponsored Worker

Sources: GOV.UK: Skilled Worker Visa Costs |  GOV.UK: Sponsor Guidance Part 2 (CoS fees) |  GOV.UK: Visa Fee Transparency Data

## 3.8 Critical Rule: You Must Not Recover Costs from Workers

Since 31 December 2024, UK employers are prohibited from passing any sponsorship-related costs on to workers. This includes the CoS fee, sponsor licence fee, Immigration Skills Charge, and associated administrative costs. The Home Office has stated it will normally revoke a sponsor licence where an employer attempts to recoup these costs through salary deductions, repayment clauses, or any other mechanism.

Source: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/workers-and-temporary-workers-sponsor-a-worker

## 3.9 Ongoing Responsibilities

Report changes (e.g. employee leaves, role changes, salary changes, extended absences)

Keep accurate records of all sponsored workers

Renew your licence every 4 years

Comply with all immigration rules at all times

Failure can lead to licence suspension or revocation, fines, or bans from hiring overseas workers.

# 4. Setting Up Payroll for a New UK Company

This involves registering with HMRC, choosing a system to run payroll, and meeting tax, reporting, and UK employment law obligations.

## 4.1 Register as an Employer

Before paying anyone, you must register with HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC).

Do this before your first payday

HMRC will issue a PAYE reference number and an Accounts Office reference

NOTE: You typically need to register even if you are only paying yourself as a director.

Source: https://www.gov.uk/register-employer

## 4.2 Choose How You Will Run Payroll

Payroll software (most common if you have a UK Finance person): must support Real Time Information (RTI) reporting

Accountant or payroll provider

Manual payroll (rare and risky)

Popular HMRC-recognised options include Xero, QuickBooks, and Sage Payroll.

## 4.3 Collect Employee Information

For each employee, you need:

Full name, address, date of birth

National Insurance number

Starter checklist (or P45 from previous job)

Bank details

Salary and contract details

## 4.4 Set Up PAYE (Pay As You Earn)

PAYE is the system used to deduct income tax, National Insurance contributions (NICs), and student loan repayments (if applicable). Your payroll system calculates these automatically.

## 4.5 Run Payroll Each Pay Period

Every time you pay staff (weekly/monthly), you must calculate gross pay, deduct tax and NICs, and generate payslips (legally required).

## 4.6 Report to HMRC (RTI Submissions)

Each payday, you must send a Full Payment Submission (FPS) to HMRC including pay, tax, and deductions for each employee. This must be sent on or before payday.

You may also need to submit an Employer Payment Summary (EPS), e.g. for reclaiming statutory pay.

## 4.7 Pay HMRC

You must pay HMRC what you have deducted, usually monthly by the 22nd if paying electronically. This includes income tax and both employer and employee NICs.

NOTE: From April 2025, employer NICs increased from 13.8% to 15%, and the secondary threshold dropped from £9,100 to £5,000. This significantly increases payroll costs and should be budgeted for.

Source: https://www.gov.uk/running-payroll

## 4.8 Statutory Sick Pay (SSP) — Major Changes from 6 April 2026

From 6 April 2026, under the Employment Rights Act 2025, SSP is changing significantly:

The three-day waiting period is being removed. SSP will be payable from the first full day of sickness absence.

The Lower Earnings Limit is being removed. All employees will be eligible for SSP regardless of their earnings level.

The rate of SSP will be the lower of 80% of the employee’s average weekly earnings or the flat weekly rate (£123.25 per week for 2026/27, subject to parliamentary approval).

Average weekly earnings are calculated based on the 8 weeks before the sickness absence.

NOTE: Payroll systems must be updated to reflect these changes before 6 April 2026. Contracts and sickness policies must also be reviewed to remove references to waiting days or earnings thresholds.

Sources: Business.gov.uk: SSP Changes |  ACAS: Statutory Sick Pay |  GOV.UK: SSP Transitional Guidance

## 4.9 Other Statutory Payments

You may need to process Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP), Statutory Paternity Pay (SPP), Statutory Adoption Pay (SAP), Statutory Shared Parental Pay (ShPP), Statutory Parental Bereavement Pay (SPBP), and Neonatal Care Pay (from April 2025). Your payroll system should calculate these.

## 4.10 National Minimum Wage Rates from 1 April 2026

Source: https://www.gov.uk/national-minimum-wage-rates

## 4.11 Workplace Pension

You must comply with auto-enrolment rules from The Pensions Regulator (see Section 2 above). Payroll software usually integrates with pension schemes.

## 4.12 Benefits in Kind (P11D) Reporting

Important: Mandatory payrolling of benefits in kind was originally planned for April 2026 but has been delayed to 6 April 2027. P11D and P11D(b) forms remain required for the 2025/26 and 2026/27 tax years. From 6 April 2027, most benefits in kind (except employment-related loans and accommodation) must be reported via payroll through RTI. Voluntary payrolling is available from April 2026 for employers who wish to test their systems.

Sources: GOV.UK HMRC: Mandatory Payrolling Guidance |  GOV.UK HMRC: Original Policy Paper

## 4.13 Keep Payroll Records

You must keep records for at least 3 years, including payments and deductions, RTI submissions, and employee details.

## 4.14 Year-End Tasks

At the end of the tax year (April), you must provide P60s to employees, submit final reports to HMRC, and handle any benefits (P11D if applicable for 2025/26 and 2026/27).

## 4.15 Payroll Provider Fees

If you opt for a payroll provider, a typical service includes performing calculations, reconciliation, report and payslip preparation, Full Payment Submission to HMRC, processing net salary payments, advising of PAYE/NIC liability, managing auto-enrolment duties, undertaking workforce assessments, enrolling eligible workers, processing opt-outs, providing employee communication letters, creating contribution schedules, and liaising with your pension provider.

# 5. Steps for Hiring in the UK

Hiring in the UK follows a structured process governed by employment law and compliance requirements.

## 5.1 Define the Role

Job title, responsibilities, and reporting line

Required skills, experience, and qualifications

Salary range and benefits (ensuring the salary meets National Living Wage/National Minimum Wage requirements from 1 April 2026: £12.71 per hour for workers aged 21+)

## 5.2 Check Your Legal Obligations

Confirm the role meets minimum wage requirements

Understand working time rules (Working Time Regulations 1998)

Prepare to follow anti-discrimination rules under the Equality Act 2010

Be aware of the Employment Rights Act 2025 and its phased implementation (see Section 6 below)

## 5.3 Advertise the Job

Post the role on job boards (Indeed, LinkedIn, etc.), your company website, and through recruitment agencies if needed. Make sure the advert is clear, fair, and not discriminatory.

## 5.4 Review Applications and Interview

Screen CVs and cover letters against objective criteria

Keep records to ensure fairness and compliance

Conduct structured interviews with consistent questions for all candidates

Avoid questions that could be discriminatory (e.g. about age, family plans, religion)

You may include skills tests and assessment tasks

## 5.5 Make a Job Offer

Offer the role to your chosen candidate (verbally first, then in writing). Include salary, start date, job title, and conditions (e.g. references, background checks, right to work verification).

## 5.6 Carry Out Right to Work Checks

This is a legal requirement. You must verify the candidate’s legal right to work in the UK by checking documents (passport, visa, etc.) and following guidance from the UK Home Office.

Source: https://www.gov.uk/check-job-applicant-right-to-work

## 5.7 Provide a Written Employment Contract

You must give a written statement of employment particulars on or before day one. This is required under Section 1 of the Employment Rights Act 1996 (as amended). See the contract template in Appendix Two for the required contents.

## 5.8 Register as an Employer (if not already)

Register with HMRC and set up PAYE for tax and National Insurance (see Section 4 above).

## 5.9 Onboard the Employee

Provide training and induction

Set up payroll and pension (auto-enrolment is required)

Introduce policies: health and safety, data protection, whistleblowing, anti-harassment, flexible working

Inform the employee of their right to request flexible working (a day-one right since 6 April 2024)

## 5.10 Maintain Ongoing Compliance

Keep employee records

Follow rules on sick pay, holidays, and dismissal

Stay compliant with employment law updates, particularly the Employment Rights Act 2025 changes (see Section 6)

Comply with UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018 for all employee data

NOTE: Hiring must comply with UK GDPR. The best way to be compliant is to have a robust HRIS platform with a recruitment module.

# 6. Employment Rights Act 2025: Key Changes Timeline

The Employment Rights Act 2025 received Royal Assent on 18 December 2025. It is the most significant reform to UK employment law in a generation. Its provisions are being phased in from April 2026 through 2027. Couch Heroes must plan for these changes.

Source: https://www.acas.org.uk/employment-rights-bill

## 6.1 Changes Taking Effect April 2026

Paternity leave becomes a day-one right (previously required 26 weeks’ service)

Ordinary (unpaid) parental leave becomes a day-one right (previously required 1 year’s service)

SSP: waiting period removed, lower earnings limit removed, new 80%-of-AWE rate (see Section 4.8)

Collective redundancy protective award doubled from 90 to 180 days’ pay

Whistleblowing protections extended to sexual harassment disclosures

Fair Work Agency established (enforcement body for NMW, holiday pay, SSP)

Trade union recognition simplified; electronic balloting introduced

## 6.2 Changes Taking Effect October 2026

Fire and rehire (dismissal and re-engagement on worse terms) becomes automatically unfair

Employer duty to prevent sexual harassment tightened from ‘reasonable steps’ to ‘all reasonable steps’

Third-party harassment liability reintroduced

Employment tribunal time limits extended from 3 months to 6 months

Employers must inform workers of their right to join a trade union

Trade union right of access to workplaces introduced

## 6.3 Changes Taking Effect January 2027

Unfair dismissal qualifying period reduces from 2 years to 6 months

The compensation cap on unfair dismissal awards is removed

NOTE: Anyone hired from approximately July/August 2026 could gain unfair dismissal protection on 1 January 2027. This has major implications for probation design, performance management, and dismissal procedures.

## 6.4 Changes Taking Effect Later in 2027

Zero-hours contract workers gain the right to guaranteed hours after a reference period

Workers gain the right to compensation for cancelled shifts

Mandatory gender pay gap and menopause action plans (for employers with 250+ employees)

Strengthened protections for pregnant workers and those returning from maternity leave

New right to statutory bereavement leave

Sources: ACAS: Employment Rights Act 2025 |  GOV.UK: Employment Changes for Employers |  UK Parliament: Employment Rights Act 2025
